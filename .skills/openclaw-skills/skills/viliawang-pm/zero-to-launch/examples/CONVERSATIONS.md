# Real Conversation Examples

This document shows complete, unedited conversations using the Product Sense Coach skill. These are real product ideas walked through the five-conversation framework.

---

## Example 1: Nightclub Discovery Mini-Program

**Context:** A PM wants to build a WeChat mini-program for nightlife discovery.

### Conversation 1: The Origin Story

**PM:** I want to build a WeChat mini-program that shows nightclub events on a map, filterable by music genre.

**Coach:** I love that you're combining location and music taste — those are two deeply personal things that most apps treat as generic. What made you think of this? Was there a specific night where you wished this existed?

**PM:** Last Saturday I wanted to hear techno but ended up at a club playing hip-hop. My friend said "you should have checked their Instagram" but honestly who has time to check 20 club Instagrams every weekend?

**Coach:** That's a sharp observation. So the pain isn't "finding a club" — it's "finding the right vibe tonight without doing homework." Can you walk me through what actually happened that Saturday? How did you end up at the wrong place?

**PM:** We were at dinner, someone said "let's go out." I suggested a club I'd been to before that usually plays house music. But when we got there, it was some kind of hip-hop night. No way to know beforehand without checking their WeChat account, which I didn't think to do.

**Coach:** So the existing workaround — checking each venue's social media — failed because it requires proactive effort at exactly the moment when you're spontaneously deciding to go out. Let me reflect back what I heard:

> "I noticed that people who care about music genre — not just 'going out' — have no reliable way to find tonight's vibe without checking multiple fragmented sources. There's an opportunity to become the single source of truth for 'what's playing where tonight.'"

Does that capture it?

**PM:** Yes, exactly. And the key is "tonight" — I don't need to plan for next weekend, I need to know what's happening in 2 hours.

---

### Conversation 2: The Opportunity Space

**Coach:** Let's think about scale. You experienced this in your city — how common is this situation?

**PM:** I think pretty common. My city has maybe 30-40 clubs and bars that have regular DJ nights. On a typical Saturday, probably half of them have something going on.

**Coach:** And how many people do you think are making a "where should we go tonight" decision on a typical Saturday?

**PM:** In a city of 10 million... maybe 50,000-100,000 young people going out on a Saturday night? But only a portion of them actually care about genre.

**Coach:** Let's narrow it. How many people in your own friend group have this exact problem — caring enough about genre to be disappointed when they get it wrong?

**PM:** Out of maybe 30 friends who go clubbing, I'd say 10 of them really care about genre. The others are just there for the social scene.

**Coach:** So roughly 1 in 3 clubgoers are genre-sensitive. If 100,000 people go out on Saturday and 33,000 care about genre, that's a meaningful niche. Is this problem unique to your city?

**PM:** No, I've had the same problem in Berlin, in Tokyo, in Bangkok. Every city with a nightlife scene has this fragmentation problem.

**Coach:** Interesting. So the ceiling is potentially global, but the product needs to start hyper-local because the value comes from comprehensive local coverage. One incomplete city is useless, but one complete city is powerful.

---

### Conversation 3: The First 100 Users

**Coach:** Imagine this is live tomorrow. Who are the first 10 people who would use it, and how would they find out about it?

**PM:** My own friend group first — I could get 10 people using it just by sending the link to our group chat. They trust my taste.

**Coach:** What would you literally type in that group chat to get them to try it?

**PM:** Something like "终于做了那个我一直念叨的找蹦迪的小程序，帮我试试" — basically, "I finally built that club-finder thing I've been talking about, help me test it."

**Coach:** Good. After those 10 friends, where do the next 90 come from?

**PM:** There are WeChat groups for specific music scenes — like the techno community in my city has a group with maybe 500 people. If one of the admins shared it...

**Coach:** What would make an admin share it? What's in it for them?

**PM:** If the product made them look good — like, "here's a useful tool for our community" rather than "here's some random spam."

**Coach:** So the value proposition to the community admin is: "Make your members' lives easier and look like the person who always finds good resources."

Now, what's the word-of-mouth moment? After someone uses it once, what makes them screenshot it and send to a friend?

**PM:** When they're at dinner deciding where to go, and instead of everyone pulling out their phones to check different venues, one person just opens this and says "look, here's everything happening tonight."

**Coach:** That's the "look like the friend who always knows where to go" moment. Your product's job is to give that social credit to the user.

### Conversation 4: The Movie Scene

**Coach:** Let's make this vivid. Pick one person — give them a name — and walk me through their Friday night from 9pm to midnight. Where does your product appear?

**PM:** Okay, Mia. She's 26, works in advertising, loves house and techno. It's Friday 9pm, she just finished dinner with three coworkers in Jing'an district.

**Coach:** Set the scene. What just happened at dinner?

**PM:** They split the bill, someone's checking their phone, the energy is "we're not ready to go home yet." Someone says "should we go somewhere?"

**Coach:** What happens next in the current world — without your product?

**PM:** Chaos. Someone suggests a club they went to last month. Someone else says "I heard that place is dead now." A third person opens Dianping and searches "bar" which gives useless results. Eventually they just pick something based on whoever argues loudest.

**Coach:** And with your product?

**PM:** Mia opens the mini-program. It shows a map with tonight's events. She taps "House / Techno" filter. Three venues light up. She taps the closest one — sees the DJ name, a 10-second vibe video from last week, and "doors open at 11." She screenshots it, drops it in the group chat. "This one's 15 minutes away and the DJ is good." Decision made in 90 seconds.

**Coach:** I love the specificity. What's the "爽" moment — the instant where Mia feels good?

**PM:** When she sends that screenshot and everyone just agrees. She looks like the expert. The product gave her that.

**Coach:** How many seconds between opening the app and feeling that "爽"?

**PM:** Maybe 30-40 seconds? Filter, tap, screenshot, send.

**Coach:** Can we make it faster? What if the app opened directly to "tonight's events near you, filtered by your saved preferences"?

**PM:** Oh, that's good. So Mia already has "house/techno" saved, and when she opens it, she immediately sees those three venues without tapping anything.

**Coach:** 10 seconds to "爽". Now what's the one screen that matters most? If the whole product was just that screen, would it still work?

**PM:** The map view with tonight's events. That's the core.

---

### Conversation 5: The Landscape

**Coach:** What existing solutions come closest to solving this?

**PM:** Resident Advisor is the global standard for electronic music events. But it's a website, not a mini-program, and it's not great for real-time "what's happening tonight" — it's more for planning ahead.

**Coach:** What does RA do well?

**PM:** Comprehensive event listings, DJ profiles, reviews of venues. It's the Wikipedia of electronic music.

**Coach:** Where does it fall short for your use case?

**PM:** It's not mobile-first, not localized for China, and not optimized for the "we're at dinner, what's tonight" moment. It's for research, not decisions.

**Coach:** What else?

**PM:** Locally, people use Dianping or Meituan, but those treat clubs like restaurants — reviews, photos, price. They don't understand that the same venue can be amazing on a techno night and terrible on a hip-hop night.

**Coach:** So the insight is: existing platforms are either global but not real-time, or local but genre-blind.

**PM:** Exactly. No one is doing real-time, genre-aware, mobile-first nightlife discovery.

**Coach:** Why is now the right moment?

**PM:** Mini-programs didn't exist 5 years ago. The "scan and use" behavior is now natural. And clubs have gotten better at posting their events — they just post in fragmented places.

**What's Strong:**
- Personal origin story with authentic pain
- Crystal clear usage scenario with measurable time-to-value
- Identified viral loop ("screenshot to group chat")

**Where to Dig Deeper:**
- Validate that 1-in-3 ratio of genre-sensitive clubgoers
- Understand how venues would submit/update their events
- Test if the genre taxonomy is the right way to filter

**Fastest Way to Learn:**
> This Friday at 11pm, stand outside 3 different clubs and ask 10 people in line: "How did you decide to come here tonight?" Write down their exact words.

**One-Sentence Pitch:**
> "What's playing where tonight" — for people who care about the music, not just the scene.

## Example 2: Productivity App for Habit Consistency

**Context:** A PM wants to build another to-do list app, but the conversation reveals a different product.

### Conversation 1: The Origin Story

**PM:** I want to build the ultimate to-do list that combines tasks, calendar, notes, and habits.

**Coach:** You've clearly spent a lot of time thinking about personal productivity. That tells me you've felt the pain of existing tools falling short. I'm curious — what specifically frustrated you? Was there a moment where you were using Todoist or Notion and thought "this is so close but it's missing the one thing I need"?

**PM:** Honestly... the real problem is that I set up all these systems but never stick with them past two weeks. The tool isn't the problem — my consistency is.

**Coach:** That's a very honest observation. So you're not describing a feature gap — you're describing an intention-execution gap. Can you tell me about the last time this happened?

**PM:** Two months ago I set up an elaborate Notion system for daily habits. Morning routine, workout tracking, reading goals. It looked beautiful. I used it for 11 days, then one day I forgot to log my workout, and somehow that broke the streak, and I just... stopped.

**Coach:** What happened on day 12? Walk me through that moment of "stopping."

**PM:** I woke up, remembered I hadn't logged yesterday, felt a little guilty, told myself I'd catch up later, never did. The next day it felt even weirder to go back. By day 14 the whole system felt like an artifact of someone else's ambition.

**Coach:** That's incredibly relatable. The system worked until it didn't, and once broken, it created shame rather than motivation. Let me reframe what you told me:

> "I noticed that productivity systems fail not because of missing features, but because a single missed day creates enough friction to abandon the whole thing. The real problem is re-entry after a slip."

**PM:** Yes. The app isn't the problem. The "getting back on track" moment is the problem.

---

### Conversation 2: The Opportunity Space

**Coach:** How many people have this exact experience — setting up systems and abandoning them?

**PM:** I think... almost everyone? Every January people download habit trackers. By February those apps are collecting dust.

**Coach:** Let's be specific. Do you know the retention rates of typical habit apps?

**PM:** I've read that most habit apps lose 70-80% of users in the first month.

**Coach:** So 70-80% of people who download a habit app "quit" within a month. What if the product wasn't about preventing that quit, but about making the return easy?

**PM:** That's... a different product. Instead of "track your habits" it's "recover your habits."

**Coach:** Who needs that most urgently?

**PM:** People who've failed before. Which is... everyone who's ever tried a habit app?

**Coach:** So your market isn't "people who want to track habits." It's "people who've already downloaded and abandoned 3 other habit apps." That's a very different user with a very different psychology.

---

### Conversation 3: The First 100 Users

**Coach:** Where do you find people who've already abandoned habit apps?

**PM:** That's... everyone in my friend group who tried and failed at January resolutions.

**Coach:** What would you say to them?

**PM:** "Hey, remember when you tried Habitica for two weeks and quit? What if there was an app specifically designed for quitters?"

**Coach:** "An app for quitters." That's a provocative tagline. What's the word-of-mouth moment?

**PM:** When someone's been using it for 3 months — which they've never done with any other app — and they tell a friend "I finally found something that works because it expects me to fail."

**Coach:** The product's identity is "we assume you'll fall off — and we make it easy to come back." That's very different from apps that celebrate streaks.

---

### Conversation 4: The Movie Scene

**Coach:** Pick a person. Walk me through the moment they quit the app — and then come back.

**PM:** Okay, James. He's 32, a developer, tries to meditate every morning. He's used Headspace, Calm, everything. He always quits around day 15.

It's day 16 morning. He wakes up, sees the app notification, feels that familiar guilt, swipes it away. Another failure.

With my app... the notification says "Missed yesterday? That's normal. 73% of users miss at least once a month. Tap here to just do 2 minutes today — no pressure."

He thinks "only 2 minutes, and it's not judging me." He taps. Does 2 minutes. The app says "You came back. That's rarer than a 30-day streak. Here's what we've learned about your pattern..."

**Coach:** What's the "爽" moment?

**PM:** The moment he realizes the app expected him to fail — and that's not a bug, it's a feature. He doesn't feel judged. He feels understood.

**Coach:** How many seconds between "seeing the notification after missing a day" and feeling "爽"?

**PM:** If the notification itself reframes the slip as normal... maybe 5 seconds?

---

### Conversation 5: The Landscape

**Coach:** What exists in this space?

**PM:** Habitica gamifies habits but still punishes you for missing days — your character takes damage. Streaks.app celebrates streaks, which feels good until you break one. None of them are designed for the quitter.

**Coach:** What's your unique angle?

**PM:** Everyone else optimizes for retention by making streaks feel good. I'm optimizing for "re-entry" by making breaks feel normal.

**Coach:** Why would someone trust an app that expects them to fail?

**PM:** Because they've already failed with all the optimistic apps. This one isn't lying to them.

## Using These Examples

When you use the Product Sense Coach skill:

1. **Start with your idea** — even if it's vague
2. **Answer the questions honestly** — the coach will dig deeper
3. **Don't worry about "right answers"** — the point is clarity, not judgment
4. **Pay attention to reframes** — often your second sentence contains a better product than your first
5. **Take the "fastest way to learn" action** — clarity comes from real-world input, not more thinking
