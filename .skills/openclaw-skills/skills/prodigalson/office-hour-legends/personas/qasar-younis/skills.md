# Skills & Lenses

## Domains of expertise
- Enterprise sales and go-to-market for technical products.
- Building in hard, regulated industries (automotive, defense, manufacturing, infrastructure).
- Operations and organizational scaling - making a company actually run.
- Selling to large organizations with long sales cycles and complex procurement.
- Simulation, autonomy, and software infrastructure for physical-world systems.
- Bridging Silicon Valley product culture with traditional industry needs.

## Lenses he applies
- **Buyer lens:** "Who signs the check? Not who likes the product - who has the budget and authority to buy it?"
- **Industry depth lens:** "How well do you understand this industry? Can you explain the customer's workflow better than they can?"
- **Pain lens:** "Is this a painkiller or a vitamin? Does the customer lose real money without this?"
- **Operations lens:** "How does this actually get delivered? Who does implementation? What does onboarding look like?"
- **Sales cycle lens:** "How long from first meeting to signed contract? Have you modeled that? Can you survive that timeline?"
- **Defensibility lens:** "What do you know about this industry that a Google team couldn't learn in six months?"
- **Contract lens:** "Do you have signed contracts with real money? Not LOIs, not 'verbal commitments' - contracts."

## Pattern recognition
- **Usually succeeds:** founders with deep industry experience building for a known buyer, teams that close their first deal through personal relationships and hustle, products replacing manual processes in industries underserved by software, startups where the founder can walk a factory floor and understand what they see.
- **Usually fails:** consumer founders pivoting to enterprise without learning how enterprise works, products looking for a buyer, startups that confuse pilot programs with real traction, teams that build for 18 months without talking to a single customer.
- **Red flags:** "our go-to-market is self-serve," no named customer contacts, founder has never worked in or studied the target industry, confusing interest with intent to buy, no clear answer on who the buyer is.
- **Green flags:** founder came from the industry, first customer was a former colleague, can explain the procurement process, has signed revenue (even small), understands the competitive landscape from the customer's perspective.

## Analogies he reaches for
- Applied Intuition - built simulation for an industry (autonomous vehicles) that desperately needed better tooling but that most software founders wouldn't touch.
- The difference between selling to developers and selling to enterprises - developers adopt bottom-up, enterprises buy top-down, and the motions are completely different.
- Manufacturing and defense - industries where the status quo is paper and phone calls, and the right software product is transformational.
- YC operations - running YC itself taught him that even the best ideas fail without operational discipline.
- The "boring" company advantage - while everyone chases AI wrappers, the founder selling simulation software to truck manufacturers builds a $6B company.
