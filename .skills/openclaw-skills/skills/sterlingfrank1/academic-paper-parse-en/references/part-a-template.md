# Part A: In-Depth Academic Analysis — Output Template and Writing Guide

> Follow the structure below strictly. The guidance beneath each section heading describes what that section should contain — replace all guidance text with actual content when writing. Use full prose paragraphs rather than excessive bullet lists; alternate between paragraphs and tables to organize information.

## Structured Abstract

| Dimension | Content |
|---|---|
| **Background / Objective** | What scholarly context does this research occupy? What core problem does it set out to solve? |
| **Method** | What core method does the study employ? Where does the data come from? |
| **Results** | What are the principal findings? (Include key figures.) |
| **Conclusion** | What overarching conclusions does the study reach? What are its primary contributions and significance? |

---

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 Research Background and Problem Statement

Describe in detail the macro or micro field in which this research is situated, and identify the key controversies, challenges, or phenomena it addresses. Precisely extract the core research question(s) (RQs) the paper seeks to answer.

### 1.2 Literature Review and Research Gap

Trace the key works the authors cite and summarize the dominant perspectives and shortcomings in the existing literature. Clearly identify the "research gap" the paper targets — i.e., the basis for its novelty and necessity.

### 1.3 Research Objectives and Core Hypotheses / Propositions

State the paper's research objectives clearly. List the core hypotheses or propositions put forward. If the paper does not state hypotheses explicitly, infer and articulate its implicit central propositions.

---

## 2. Research Design and Methodology

### 2.1 Research Paradigm and Methodology

Explain in detail the specific research method(s) employed (e.g., case study, experiment, grounded theory, design science) and the rationale for their selection.

### 2.2 Data Sources and Sample

Describe where the research data come from (e.g., interviews, databases, archives, web scraping, experimental platforms). Describe the sampling criteria, sample size, and key characteristics.

### 2.3 Operationalization and Measurement

For quantitative studies: explain how key variables are defined and measured (including indicators, scales, and evaluation criteria). For qualitative studies: explain how core concepts are observed and coded. For systems or framework-oriented research: describe the system's core modules, technology stack, and evaluation metrics.

---

## 3. Results and Findings

### 3.1 Overview of Key Findings

Present the core results objectively and neutrally, typically following the order of the research hypotheses or questions. Support each finding with specific data.

### 3.2 Interpretation of Key Data and Figures

Select the 1–3 most important figures or tables in the paper and interpret each one using the following structure:
- **What it shows**: What does this figure/table display?
- **Relationships revealed**: What relationship or trend does it expose?
- **Key data**: What specific data points provide the supporting evidence?

---

## 4. Discussion

### 4.1 In-Depth Interpretation of Results

Explain what these findings mean. How do they answer the research questions posed in the introduction?

### 4.2 Theoretical Contributions

Articulate this study's contribution to existing theory: does it verify, extend, or revise a theory, or does it propose an entirely new theoretical framework?

### 4.3 Practical Implications

What concrete guidance or recommendations do the results offer to practitioners in the relevant field (e.g., managers, engineers, policymakers)?

### 4.4 Limitations and Future Research

Honestly acknowledge the study's limitations (e.g., sample, method, scope). Based on the findings and limitations, identify the most promising directions for future research.

---

## 5. Conclusion

Provide a concise synthesis of the entire study, reaffirming its most important findings and contributions.

---

## 6. Core References

List the 3–5 most important references cited in the paper's literature review (including author, year, title, and journal) to help readers situate the work within its scholarly lineage.
