# Stakeholder Communication & Internalization Templates

## Research Foundation

Two research findings shape everything in this reference:

1. **:** The primary *outcome* of their scenario planning exercise was not the document — it was organizational dialogue, increased tool adoption, and bringing diverse people together to think strategically. The document was a by-product. The conversations were the value.

2. Leadership and dialogue are the primary influencing factors in whether scenario planning actually changes decisions. A scenario plan that is not internalized by decision-makers is not a plan.

**Implication:** Communication is not the last step of scenario planning — it is *ongoing throughout* the process. Stakeholders should be involved in building scenarios, not just presented with them at the end.

---

## Stakeholder Involvement Levels by School

| School | When to Involve Stakeholders | How |
|--------|------------------------------|-----|
| **ILM** | Throughout — from Driving Forces brainstorm through strategic options | Facilitated workshops; participants co-create scenarios |
| **LP** | At key stages — variable identification, actor analysis, scenario validation | Expert interviews + structured workshops |
| **PMT** | Primarily at input stages — event list and probability estimation | Expert panels; probabilistic surveys |

**The Independent Auditor role :** For high-stakes engagements, appoint an external person — not a participant, not an internal facilitator — whose role is to ensure all voices are heard, dominant personalities don't hijack the process, and scenarios are genuinely plausible rather than reflecting the convenient consensus of the group. This is especially important when participants have a vested interest in a particular scenario.

## Stakeholder Communication Plan Template

| Stakeholder Group | Key Concern | Message Emphasis | Format | Frequency | Owner |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Executive Sponsors | Strategic viability, ROI, decision authority | No-regret moves, Decision Gate authority, escalation path | 1-page summary + decision register | At each gate | PM |
| Steering Committee | Governance, risk exposure, budget | Scenario activation thresholds, pre-authorized decisions, gap analysis | Dashboard + exception report | Monthly or at milestone | PM |
| Project Core Team | Their role, what to watch for | Early warning indicators, signal monitoring, workstream implications | Team briefing + monitoring checklist | Weekly standup | Team Lead |
| Functional Leads | Resource and capacity impact | Scenario-specific resource needs, advance notice of pivots | Briefing document | At planning and at gates | PM + Sponsor |
| External Vendors/Partners | Contract and scope implications | Scope flexibility, escalation process, mutual obligations | Targeted conversation | As scenarios activate | PM |
| Clients / End Users | Delivery confidence | Adaptive plan framing; what "on track" looks like under each path | Progress update with adaptive framing | At milestone | PM or Client Lead |

## Framing Language for Resistant Stakeholders

**When stakeholders resist scenario planning as "speculative":**
> "We're not predicting the future — we're making sure we've already thought through the key decision points before we're under pressure to act. Every good general plans multiple campaign strategies before the battle begins. The value is in the preparation, not in being right."

**When executives resist as "too much overhead":**
> "This exercise produces three immediate outputs that save time later: a list of no-regret moves we should be doing anyway, a set of pre-authorized decisions that will spare us an emergency meeting when conditions shift, and a clear escalation path so you're not surprised. It pays for itself the first time a signal appears."

**When team members worry scenarios will "become self-fulfilling":**
> "Documenting a Disrupted scenario doesn't make disruption more likely — it means we already know what we'll do if it happens. That's the difference between preparedness and prediction."

**When quantitative stakeholders want probabilities:**
> "Attaching probabilities would actually reduce the value of this exercise. Research shows that once people see a number like 15% on a scenario, they mentally discount it. All four scenarios are equally worth preparing for — that's the point. The scenario we call 'unlikely' is always the one that surprises us most."

**When a client asks how this affects their deliverables:**
> "This adaptive planning approach is designed to protect your deliverables, not create uncertainty. By identifying our response strategies now, we can move faster when conditions change — rather than losing weeks to deliberation. The core deliverables you depend on are the first things we protect under every scenario."

## The Ongoing Communication Rhythm

Scenario plans die when they are filed. Keep them alive:

| Cadence | What Happens | Who |
|---------|-------------|-----|
| Weekly | Signal review: have any early warning indicators moved? | PM + Team Lead |
| Bi-weekly | Early warning summary shared with sponsor | PM → Sponsor |
| At each milestone | Full Decision Gate review: confirm or pivot path | PM + Sponsor |
| Monthly | Scenario currency check: are scenarios still plausible? Any new drivers? | PM + Core Team |
| At scenario activation | Formal briefing: which scenario is confirmed; full strategy pivot | PM → Sponsor → All stakeholders |
| Post-project | Lessons learned: which scenario actually unfolded? Why? | Full team |

**The most important question to ask monthly:**
*"Has anything happened in the last 30 days that would change which scenario we think is most likely, or that would change any of our early warning indicator thresholds?"*

This is the question that keeps scenario planning alive and useful rather than archival.
