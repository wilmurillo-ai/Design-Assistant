# School Selection Guide & Methodology Reference

## The Three Schools: Detailed Comparison



---

## School 1: Intuitive Logic Method 

**Origin:** Herman Kahn (US defense planning) → Shell (1970s, Pierre Wack) → , Schwartz, Schoemaker

**Philosophy:** The future is non-deterministic. No probability can be assigned. The goal is to stretch thinking, challenge mental models, and prepare for multiple equally plausible futures.

**When to use:**
- Long-term horizon (3–20 years)
- Strategic organizational uncertainty
- Qualitative, judgment-based drivers
- Teams with no scenario experience (accessible entry point)
- Corporate strategy, digital disruption, market entry, regulatory shift
- When the team IS the primary data source

**Process (ILM standard steps):**
1. Define the Key Focal Issue and time horizon
2. Identify Driving Forces via PESTLE brainstorm (20–50 forces)
3. Rank forces by impact and uncertainty
4. Select 2 Critical Uncertainties (most uncertain + most impactful)
5. Build 2×2 scenario matrix from those 2 axes
6. Develop vivid, narrative scenarios for each quadrant
7. Write scenario stories (present tense, as if already happened)
8. Identify early warning indicators per scenario
9. Develop strategic options for each scenario
10. Wind-tunnel strategies against all scenarios to find robust options

**Participants:** Internal team (knowledgeable about the environment) + external domain experts. The facilitator is critical. ILM requires genuine dialogue — not a solo exercise.

**Output form:** Qualitative narratives, no probabilities. 4 differentiated scenarios.

**Quality criteria for ILM scenarios:**
- Plausible (could credibly happen)
- Internally consistent (elements reinforce each other)
- Challenging (breaks conventional wisdom)
- Differentiated (structurally different, not variations)
- Actionable (leads to strategic options)

**Known limitations:**
- Risk of group think if facilitation is weak
- Dominant personalities can distort outputs (use an Independent Auditor for high-stakes engagements —)
- Scenario quality depends on participant knowledge and imagination
- No probability weighting — may feel unsatisfying to quantitatively-oriented stakeholders

## School 2: La Prospective 

**Origin:** Gaston Berger (France, 1950s) → Michel Godet

**Philosophy:** The future is partially shaped by human choices. Structural analysis of the current system reveals which variables are most influential. Actors have agency. Both qualitative and quantitative inputs matter.

**When to use:**
- Medium-term horizon (3–20 years)
- Public sector planning, national strategy, large institutions
- When actor behavior (stakeholders, competitors, regulators) is a key uncertainty
- When time series data exists alongside judgment
- When you want to model interdependencies between variables

**Process (LP standard steps):**
1. Define the problem scope and system boundary
2. Structural analysis: identify key variables and map their influence on each other (MICMAC analysis — identifying which variables are most influential and most dependent)
3. Actor analysis : map stakeholder objectives, alliances, and conflicts
4. Build scenarios from combinations of key variable trajectories
5. Assign probabilities to variable trajectories using expert panels
6. Develop morphological cross-impact analysis to find coherent combinations
7. Select 3–4 plausible scenario paths
8. Write narratives and identify strategic implications

**Participants:** Organizational team (usually led by expert consultant) + external experts for specific variables. More expert-driven than ILM.

**Output form:** Descriptive or normative scenarios; may include probabilities; actor behavior modeling.

**Quality criteria for LP scenarios:**
- Plausibility and verifiability (can be tested retrospectively)
- Coherence of variable combinations
- Coverage of the scenario space
- Comprehensiveness (major driving forces represented)

**Known limitations:**
- More complex and time-consuming than ILM
- Requires expert facilitation (MICMAC, MACTOR tools)
- Risk of overly deterministic framing despite qualitative inputs
- Less accessible for teams new to scenario planning

## School 3: Probabilistic Modified Trends 

**Origin:** Theodore Gordon & Olaf Helmer (Futures Group, 1960s–70s)

**Philosophy:** The future is largely an extension of the present, modified by specific disruptive events. Statistical methods can model base trends; expert judgment adjusts for event impacts. Probabilities are meaningful and should be used.

**Two main PMT techniques:**

### Trend Impact Analysis 
1. Identify key performance indicators  affected by uncertainty
2. Extrapolate historical trends for each KPI (base forecast)
3. Brainstorm discrete future events that could disrupt each trend
4. Expert panel assigns probabilities and timing to each event
5. Model how each event, if it occurs, would shift the trend
6. Generate scenario space from combinations of high/low trend disruptions
7. Select representative scenarios (typically 3: baseline, pessimistic, optimistic)

**Best for:** Financial planning, project schedule risk, market forecasting with historical data

### Cross-Impact Analysis 
Like TIA but adds: conditional probabilities. Ask not just "what is the probability of Event A?" but "what is the probability of Event A *given that* Event B has already occurred?" This captures causal relationships between events.

**Best for:** Complex systems where events are interdependent (regulatory + market + technology changes happening together)

**Participants:** Outside experts + statistical analysts. PMT is the most expert-dependent school.

**Output form:** Scenarios with probabilities attached; sensitivity analysis; uncertainty ranges. May include Monte Carlo simulation.

**Quality criteria for PMT scenarios:**
- Plausibility and verifiability
- Quantitative rigor (internally consistent probability structure)
- Sensitivity analysis (what changes if one assumption shifts?)
- Coverage (are the main uncertainty drivers represented?)

**Known limitations:**
- Requires historical data; poor for genuinely novel uncertainties
- Probability estimation is prone to bias (overconfidence, availability heuristic)
- "Probability = meaningless in radical uncertainty" — do not use PMT when true uncertainty (not risk) is the primary condition 
- Monte Carlo simulation is powerful but requires modeling expertise
- The most demanding school in terms of data and expertise

**When NOT to use PMT:**
- When the future is genuinely discontinuous from the past
- When the main uncertainty is about *which world we're in*, not *how much of a known variable*
- For megaprojects with radical uncertainty about end-product definition (use ILM instead)

## Hybrid Approach: Combining Schools

In practice, many high-quality scenario exercises combine elements:

| Combination | When to Use |
|---|---|
| **ILM + PMT sensitivity testing** | Build qualitative scenarios via ILM, then use sensitivity analysis on the financial/schedule implications of each scenario |
| **LP structural analysis + ILM narrative** | Use LP's MICMAC to identify the most influential variables, then build ILM-style narrative scenarios around those variables |
| **PMT for risk, ILM for uncertainty** | Use PMT (risk register + probability) for quantifiable known unknowns; use ILM scenarios for the genuinely uncertain strategic environment |
| **ILM + Gap Analysis** |  hybrid: use qualitative scenario construction, then quantify the Planning Gap per scenario using financial modeling |

## Decision Tree: Which School?

```
Is the primary uncertainty quantifiable with historical data?
├── YES → Is the uncertainty about event impacts on trends, or about structural change?
│         ├── TRENDS → Use PMT (TIA or CIA)
│         └── STRUCTURE → Use LP (structural + actor analysis)
└── NO  → Is the horizon short (<1 year) or long (1+ years)?
          ├── SHORT → Use simplified ILM (3-scenario set: pessimistic/baseline/optimistic)
          └── LONG  → Use full ILM (2×2 matrix from 2 critical uncertainties)

Is this a megaproject with radical uncertainty about the end product?
└── YES → Use Turner's (2022) scenario planning framework: 
          - Narrative construction 
          - Innovation checkpoints
          - Principal-steward governance framing
          - Keep options open; the Skeptic's approach
```

## Scenario Planning for Megaprojects 

Megaprojects require a different orientation because they are **complex** (non-linear, small input changes produce large output changes), not merely **complicated** (difficult but solvable by deductive methods).

**Key principles:**
- **Principal-steward governance** — Client and contractors both work toward the project's best outcome, not their individual contract maximization. Conventional principal-agent contracting creates moral hazard, adverse selection, and information asymmetry that destroy megaproject performance.
- **Innovation over control** — Conventional project management emphasizes control and closes options early (the Optimist). Megaprojects require keeping options open and innovating toward the best configuration (the Skeptic).
- **Scenario planning as narrative** — The scenarios are not probability models; they are *stories about possible project worlds* that help the team respond as the project evolves.
- **Future-perfect planning** — Work from a desired future state backward to the present. "If we imagine the project is complete and successful — what did that look like? What decisions led there?" 

**Megaproject scenario process:**
1. **Preparation** — Define the project purpose (not just scope), multiple possible outcome configurations, stakeholder landscape, and team roles.
2. **Uncertainty analysis** — Identify internal and external forces (PESTLE + competitive forces + project-specific technical uncertainties).
3. **Scenario creation** — Write scenario stories; rank forces by uncertainty and impact; involve stakeholders in co-creation.
4. **Scenario analysis** — Refine, quantify where possible, develop strategies for each scenario.
5. **Planning & implementation** — Build an adaptive roadmap; constantly question assumptions; watch for signals; reduce options deliberately at Decision Gates.
6. **Lessons learned** — Which scenario unfolded? Why? What signals were accurate?

**Influencing factors :** Dialogue, Learning, Decision-making, Mental Models, Leadership — these must all be actively cultivated in megaproject scenario planning, not just left to chance.
