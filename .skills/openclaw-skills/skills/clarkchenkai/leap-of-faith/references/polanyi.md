# Polanyi and Tacit Knowledge

## Core Concept

**Michael Polanyi** (1891–1976), Hungarian-British physical chemist and philosopher. He articulated one of epistemology's most practically valuable concepts:

> "We can know more than we can tell."

This is the core proposition of **Tacit Knowledge**.

## Tacit vs. Explicit Knowledge

| Dimension | Explicit Knowledge | Tacit Knowledge |
|-----------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Nature | Can be expressed in language, formulas, processes | Cannot be fully verbalized |
| Transfer | Transmissible through documents, teaching | Only acquired through practice, demonstration, imitation |
| Example | The physics of riding a bicycle | The actual "feel" of riding a bicycle |
| In decisions | Data, analysis reports, due diligence results | Intuition, "something feels off," bodily reactions |

## Subsidiary Awareness

Polanyi distinguished two modes of awareness:

### Focal Awareness
- What you consciously attend to
- Example: Reading the numbers in a business plan

### Subsidiary Awareness
- What you don't deliberately focus on, but which shapes your judgment in the background
- Example: Micro-expressions during a meeting, shifts in tone, your own bodily tension

**Key Insight**: When we force subsidiary awareness into focus, it breaks down. Just as consciously thinking about how to ride a bicycle makes you ride worse.

## The Role of Tacit Knowledge in Decision-Making

### Why Tacit Knowledge Is a Legitimate Signal

1. **Crystallized experience** — Your intuition doesn't come from nowhere. It's the compressed encoding of all past experience.
2. **Pattern recognition** — The brain identifies patterns before the rational mind has time to analyze them.
3. **Bodily wisdom** — Physical reactions (tension, excitement, unease) are an early warning system refined over millions of years of evolution.
4. **Relational intuition** — Your "feel" for people is often more accurate than background checks.

### How to Surface Tacit Knowledge

Polanyi argued that tacit knowledge cannot be directly "told," but can be awakened through indirect methods:

1. **Body scan** — Imagine the decision outcome, observe bodily reactions
2. **Reversal test** — If the option were removed, what's your first reaction?
3. **Trust projection** — What would you tell the person you trust most?
4. **Time lens** — Five years from now, will you regret doing it or not doing it?
5. **Metaphor association** — Describe your situation as an image or story

### The Pitfalls of Tacit Knowledge

Tacit knowledge is not infallible:

- **Cognitive biases** — Intuition can be distorted by anchoring effects, sunk cost fallacy, and other psychological biases
- **Emotional interference** — Fear, greed, and anxiety can masquerade as "intuition"
- **Experience blind spots** — In entirely new domains, tacit knowledge may not exist or may mislead

Therefore, tacit knowledge should be used in conjunction with explicit analysis, not as a replacement.

## Application in Leap of Faith

Polanyi's tacit knowledge theory and Kierkegaard's leap of faith are perfectly complementary:

- Kierkegaard tells us: **Reason has limits. At the boundary, you must leap.**
- Polanyi tells us: **Beyond the boundary, you are not empty-handed — you carry tacit knowledge.**

The leap of faith is not a blind jump. You leap carrying everything you know, including what you can't put into words.
