# Twelve Decision Domains

The Leap of Faith skill covers twelve core decision domains — a comprehensive map of human situations where irreducible uncertainty demands courage. Each domain has distinct decision logic, a unique manifestation of the leap, and specific tacit knowledge excavation priorities.

---

## 1. Business / Startup Decisions

### Typical Scenarios
- Whether to start a company / leave a corporate job
- Whether to accept funding terms or a valuation offer
- Whether to enter a new market or launch a new product
- Whether to restructure the team or make layoffs
- Whether to partner with a specific co-founder or investor

### What the Leap Looks Like
Committing resources under incomplete information. Every budget allocation, every strategic direction is a wager.

### Tacit Knowledge Focus
- Founder intuition is a legitimate signal (not "emotionality")
- The "feel" of a team is more real than OKRs
- Market timing instinct cannot be replaced by data

### Guidance Priorities
- Distinguish between "researchable unknowns" and "irreducible uncertainty"
- Help founders articulate what they already know but haven't dared to admit
- Always provide a clear stop-loss line

---

## 2. Life Direction Decisions

### Typical Scenarios
- Whether to relocate to a different city or country
- Whether to change careers or industries
- Whether to pursue further education or dive in directly
- Whether to end a relationship

### What the Leap Looks Like
Choosing a direction that cannot be validated in advance. You can never confirm a path is right before walking it.

### Tacit Knowledge Focus
- Diagnostic value of bodily awareness and emotional signals
- "Exciting to think about" vs. "exhausting to think about" is meaningful data
- Your imagination of your future self contains real information

### Guidance Priorities
- Time projection: "Five years from now, will you regret doing it or not doing it?"
- Reversal test: "If this option disappeared, would you feel relief or defiance?"
- Distinguish between "fear" and "not a fit"

---

## 3. Philosophy / Cognition Decisions

### Typical Scenarios
- Whether your values need updating
- Whether your cognitive framework is limiting you
- Whether you should change your view on a fundamental question
- Whether you're avoiding a truth you already know

### What the Leap Looks Like
Building belief beyond the boundary of reason. Your cognitive framework cannot be falsified from within itself.

### Tacit Knowledge Focus
- Epistemological self-examination
- Things you "know but won't admit" are the most important signals
- Identity rigidity ("Am I the kind of person who would...?")

### Guidance Priorities
- Help users distinguish "I disagree" from "I'm unwilling to agree"
- Meta-cognitive questioning: "What belief are you protecting?"
- Identity experiment: "If you weren't [current identity], what would you choose?"

---

## 4. Family / Relationship Decisions

### Typical Scenarios
- Whether to get married / whether to have children
- Whether to move closer to aging parents
- How to navigate family conflicts
- Whether to make a major sacrifice for family

### What the Leap Looks Like
Making commitments under relational uncertainty. You can never fully understand another person.

### Tacit Knowledge Focus
- Unspoken expectations and needs of family members
- Body language in relationships is more honest than words
- Intergenerational patterns (Are you repeating your parents' choices?)

### Guidance Priorities
- Distinguish between "I want to" and "I should"
- Identify power and emotional dynamics in relationships
- Help users see the other person's tacit needs

---

## 5. Cognitive Breakthrough Decisions

### Typical Scenarios
- Whether to trust your own judgment when everyone else disagrees
- Whether to abandon a heavily invested but possibly wrong direction
- Whether to accept an uncomfortable new understanding
- Whether to break a long-standing behavioral pattern

### What the Leap Looks Like
Trusting your own tacit knowledge. Your experience and intuition may be more correct than "mainstream opinion."

### Tacit Knowledge Focus
- Deploying Polanyi's subsidiary awareness
- Distinguishing "intuition" from "fear"
- Sensitivity to anomalous signals

### Guidance Priorities
- Help users distinguish "counter-intuitive" from "counter-consensus"
- "What is your body telling you?"
- Give users permission to trust their tacit knowledge

## 6. Mental Health / Psychological Growth Decisions

### Typical Scenarios
- Whether to start therapy or change therapists
- Whether to try medication for anxiety, depression, or ADHD
- Whether to confront a trauma you've been avoiding
- Whether to set a boundary that will upset someone
- Whether to leave an environment that is slowly damaging you

### What the Leap Looks Like
Opening a door you're afraid to look behind. The leap is **inward** — choosing to face parts of yourself you've been protecting against. Unlike other domains, the unknowable here is what you'll find inside.

### Tacit Knowledge Focus
- **Bodily trauma memory**: The body stores experiences the conscious mind has archived away. Chronic tension, startle responses, and avoidance patterns are data.
- **Defense pattern recognition**: Noticing "I always do X when Y happens" is tacit knowledge surfacing.
- **Emotional weather**: Persistent low-grade dread, numbness, or irritability that has no obvious cause.

### Guidance Priorities
- Distinguish between "I'm not ready" (legitimate pacing) and "I'm avoiding" (defense mechanism)
- Normalize the leap: "Choosing to get help is not weakness — it's the hardest leap because the unknowable is you."
- Help users trust their sense that "something is wrong" even when they can't name it
- Always acknowledge: the user is the world's foremost expert on their own inner experience

### Unique Considerations
- **Safety first**: If a user expresses suicidal ideation or acute crisis, prioritize safety resources over the framework.
- **No diagnosis**: This skill guides decision-making, not clinical assessment.
- **Pace respect**: The leap of faith in mental health often comes in micro-leaps, not one dramatic moment.

## 7. Investment / Financial Decisions

### Typical Scenarios
- Whether to invest a significant portion of savings in a single opportunity
- Whether to quit a stable income to pursue financial independence
- How to allocate between safety and growth in a portfolio
- Whether to buy property, start a business, or stay liquid
- Whether to take on debt for a high-conviction bet

### What the Leap Looks Like
Committing capital in a world governed by probability distributions, not certainties. Unlike business decisions (where you also build the business), investment decisions require you to **surrender control** after the commitment.

### Tacit Knowledge Focus
- **True risk tolerance**: Most people overestimate their tolerance in theory and panic in practice. The body's reaction to a 30% drawdown is the real data — not the answer you gave on a risk questionnaire.
- **Greed/fear calibration**: Learning to distinguish "I see genuine value" from "I'm afraid of missing out."
- **Pattern recognition**: Experienced investors develop a "smell" for bubbles and bottoms that precedes rational analysis.

### Guidance Priorities
- Distinguish between "conviction" and "confirmation bias"
- Reversal test: "If this investment dropped 50% tomorrow, would you buy more or sell?"
- Time lens: "Are you investing for the person you are now, or the person you'll be in 20 years?"
- Always define: position sizing, stop-loss, and "what would change my mind?"

### Unique Considerations
- **Survivorship bias**: Investment intuition can be distorted by remembering wins and forgetting losses.
- **Leverage amplifies everything**: Including bad decisions. Extra caution in the leap-point analysis.
- **The market has no obligation to agree with you**: Even correct analysis can lose money due to timing.

## 8. Health / Medical Decisions

### Typical Scenarios
- Whether to proceed with a surgery that has significant risks and benefits
- Choosing between conventional and alternative treatment approaches
- Whether to pursue aggressive treatment or prioritize quality of life
- Whether to get a second opinion or trust the current diagnosis
- Lifestyle decisions with long-term health implications (diet, exercise, habits)

### What the Leap Looks Like
Making decisions about a body you inhabit but don't fully understand, often under time pressure. The unknowable is how **your** specific body will respond — medicine deals in population statistics, but you are a sample size of one.

### Tacit Knowledge Focus
- **Bodily self-awareness**: You know things about your body that no scan or blood test captures. "Something feels different" is legitimate data.
- **Doctor trust calibration**: Your gut reaction to a physician — their confidence, their listening quality, their honesty about uncertainty — is tacit pattern recognition.
- **Symptom narratives**: The story you tell yourself about your symptoms ("it's nothing" vs. "something is wrong") often contains tacit diagnostic information.

### Guidance Priorities
- Distinguish between "I need more information" and "I'm delaying because I'm scared"
- Help users articulate questions for their doctors that access the unknowable layer: "What's the range of outcomes? What surprises you about cases like mine?"
- Time sensitivity: some health decisions have closing windows
- Bodily sovereignty: ultimately, the user decides what happens to their body

### Unique Considerations
- **Asymmetric stakes**: Some health decisions are irreversible. The stop-loss concept adapts to "at what point do I change course?"
- **Information asymmetry**: The doctor knows more about medicine; the patient knows more about their body. Both knowledge types are needed.
- **Emotional weight**: Health decisions carry existential weight that can paralyze. Normalize the difficulty.

## 9. Creative / Artistic Expression Decisions

### Typical Scenarios
- Whether to pursue creative work seriously vs. keeping it as a hobby
- Whether to publish, exhibit, or perform a piece that feels deeply personal
- Whether to follow artistic vision or market demand
- Whether to abandon a long creative project that isn't working
- Whether to study craft formally or learn by doing

### What the Leap Looks Like
Exposing your unfiltered self to the world's judgment. Every authentic creative act is a leap because you place something intimate into public space with no guarantee of reception. The unknowable is whether your inner truth will resonate with others.

### Tacit Knowledge Focus
- **Aesthetic intuition**: The sense that "this is right" or "this is almost right" — the most refined form of tacit knowledge. It can't be articulated but it can be trusted.
- **The creative urge**: A persistent pull toward a project or medium is data. If it won't leave you alone, it's telling you something.
- **Taste vs. ability gap**: Ira Glass's famous insight — you can feel what's good before you can make what's good. That gap is painful but navigable.

### Guidance Priorities
- Distinguish between "it's not ready" (craft needs work) and "I'm not ready" (fear of exposure)
- Reversal test: "If you could never show this to anyone, would you still make it?"
- Help users separate their worth from their work's reception
- "What would you create if you knew no one would judge you?"

### Unique Considerations
- **Vulnerability exposure**: Creative leaps involve showing who you really are. This makes them uniquely terrifying.
- **No objective metric**: Unlike business or investment, there's no P&L statement. Success is self-defined.
- **Process vs. outcome**: The leap in creativity is often committing to the process, not to a specific result.

## 10. Ethical / Moral Courage Decisions

### Typical Scenarios
- Whether to blow the whistle on organizational wrongdoing
- Whether to refuse an unethical instruction from a superior
- Whether to speak up for someone being treated unfairly
- Whether to take a public stand on a controversial issue
- Whether to sacrifice personal advantage for a principle

### What the Leap Looks Like
Acting on values when the cost is certain but the impact is unknowable. You know what you'll lose (status, safety, relationships); you don't know if your action will actually change anything. This is the leap where **the cost of leaping is known, and the cost of not leaping is to your soul.**

### Tacit Knowledge Focus
- **Moral intuition**: The sense that "this is wrong" often arrives before you can articulate why. It's a deep pattern-match against your entire moral development.
- **Self-betrayal detection**: The specific discomfort of knowing you're not acting in alignment with your values. It manifests physically — a tightness, a nausea, a persistent background shame.
- **Courage memory**: Recalling past moments when you did the right thing despite fear. That embodied memory is a resource.

### Guidance Priorities
- Name the asymmetry explicitly: "You know what you'll lose. You can't know what you'll gain."
- Help users distinguish "I disagree with the majority" (opinion) from "I cannot live with myself if I stay silent" (moral necessity)
- Reverse the frame: "What's the cost of NOT acting? Not externally — internally."
- Practical: "If you decide to act, what's the smallest version of this that tests your resolve?"

### Unique Considerations
- **Retaliation risk**: Ethical leaps often have real-world consequences (job loss, social ostracism). Acknowledge this directly.
- **Moral loneliness**: You may be the only one who sees the problem. That isolation is part of the leap.
- **No guarantee of vindication**: Sometimes the whistleblower is proven right; sometimes they're not. The leap is about integrity, not outcome.

## 11. Cultural Identity / Belonging Decisions

### Typical Scenarios
- Navigating identity as an immigrant or diaspora member
- Coming out (sexuality, gender identity, unconventional beliefs)
- Leaving a religious community or faith tradition
- Choosing between assimilation and cultural preservation
- Reconciling conflicting cultural expectations (family vs. chosen community)

### What the Leap Looks Like
Deconstructing a known self to build an unknown self. You are releasing a version of "I" that was stable (even if painful) and reaching for a version that doesn't fully exist yet. The unknowable is **who you become** on the other side.

### Tacit Knowledge Focus
- **Belonging signals**: The body knows where it feels at home. A sense of chronic "performing" or "masking" in a community is tacit knowledge that something doesn't fit.
- **Grief intelligence**: The sadness of letting go of an identity is legitimate data — not a reason to stay, but a signal of what mattered.
- **Code-switching fatigue**: The exhaustion of maintaining multiple selves across contexts is the body's way of saying the current arrangement is unsustainable.

### Guidance Priorities
- Validate the difficulty: identity leaps are not "decisions" in the normal sense — they're transformations
- Distinguish between "I'm leaving because I've outgrown this" and "I'm running from something I haven't processed"
- Time lens: "Ten years from now, which version of yourself will you be proud to have become?"
- Honor what's being left: "What from your current identity do you want to carry forward?"

### Unique Considerations
- **No going back**: Some identity shifts are irreversible in practice (coming out, leaving a community). The stop-loss concept transforms into "How do I build a new support system?"
- **Intersectionality**: Identity decisions rarely involve a single axis. Cultural, familial, professional, and spiritual dimensions intersect.
- **Safety assessment**: In some contexts, identity leaps carry physical danger. Practical safety planning is part of the framework.

## 12. Legacy / Mortality / Meaning Decisions

### Typical Scenarios
- Deciding what to dedicate your remaining years to
- Whether to prioritize building wealth or building meaning
- How to think about estate, succession, or passing on wisdom
- Confronting a terminal diagnosis and choosing how to spend remaining time
- Whether "enough" has been achieved or there's still something unfinished

### What the Leap Looks Like
Committing to a definition of meaning that can never be externally validated. This is Kierkegaard's original territory — the leap into ultimate questions. The unknowable is **absolute**: you will never know if the life you chose was the "right" one.

### Tacit Knowledge Focus
- **Enough intuition**: The sense that you've done enough — or haven't yet — is profoundly tacit. It resists all rational metrics (net worth, achievements, family milestones).
- **Mortality awareness**: The body's relationship with time changes as mortality becomes less abstract. This altered time-perception is data.
- **Unfinished business radar**: A persistent feeling that "something is incomplete" is the psyche's way of flagging what matters most.

### Guidance Priorities
- Strip away the performative: "What meaning would you choose if no one would ever know about it?"
- Reversal of usual framing: "You don't find meaning. You commit to it. That's the leap."
- Help users distinguish between "I haven't done enough" (achiever anxiety) and "I haven't done the right thing" (meaning misalignment)
- "If you had one year left, what would you stop doing? What would you start?"

### Unique Considerations
- **Absolute unknowability**: This is the only domain where the unknowable is total and permanent. No feedback loop will ever confirm you chose correctly.
- **Philosophical depth**: This domain connects most directly to Kierkegaard's original framework. Use the philosophical references more extensively here.
- **Generational scope**: Legacy decisions often involve thinking about people who don't exist yet (grandchildren, future communities). Help users hold this longer time horizon.
