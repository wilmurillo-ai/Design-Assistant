# Standard Clauses Reference

Balanced clause examples and contract-type-specific mini-checklists. Loaded during Phase 2 for comparison against the contract under review.

---

## Part 1: What Balanced Clauses Look Like

For each of the 14 checklist categories, this section provides:
- Key elements that should be present
- What a fair/balanced version looks like
- Solo-entrepreneur-friendly variations

### 1. Parties and Roles

**Key elements**:
- Full legal names of all parties
- Business entity type (LLC, Inc., sole proprietor)
- Roles clearly defined (e.g., "Client" and "Contractor")
- Address and notice information for each party

**Balanced example**:
> This Agreement is entered into between [Your Entity Name], a [State] limited liability company ("Contractor"), and [Counterparty Name], a [State] [entity type] ("Client").

**Solo-entrepreneur note**: Ensure your business entity (not your personal name) is the contracting party. This preserves limited liability protection.

### 2. Payment Terms

**Key elements**:
- Amount or rate clearly stated
- Payment schedule / frequency
- Invoice procedure
- Payment due date (net-X from invoice)
- Late payment consequences
- Currency specification
- Expense reimbursement terms

**Balanced example**:
> Client shall pay Contractor at a rate of $[X] per [hour/project/milestone]. Invoices shall be submitted [monthly/upon milestone completion]. Payment is due within thirty (30) days of invoice receipt. Late payments shall accrue interest at 1.5% per month. All amounts are in USD.

**Solo-entrepreneur note**: Net-30 is standard. Push for milestone payments on longer projects to avoid cash flow gaps. Include a right to pause work if payment is 15+ days overdue.

### 3. Scope of Work / Deliverables

**Key elements**:
- Specific deliverables enumerated
- Acceptance criteria defined
- Revision/feedback process
- Change order procedure
- Out-of-scope items listed

**Balanced example**:
> Contractor shall deliver the items listed in Exhibit A ("Deliverables"). Client shall review each Deliverable within ten (10) business days. If Client does not provide written objections within the review period, the Deliverable is deemed accepted. Changes to scope require a written Change Order signed by both parties.

**Solo-entrepreneur note**: Always use a separate SOW or Exhibit for scope. Cap revisions. Define what "done" means.

### 4. Term and Termination

**Key elements**:
- Start and end date
- Renewal mechanism (auto vs. manual)
- Termination for cause (with cure period)
- Termination for convenience (mutual, with notice)
- Effect of termination (payment for WIP, wind-down)

**Balanced example**:
> This Agreement begins on [date] and continues for [X] months. Either party may terminate for convenience upon sixty (60) days' written notice. Either party may terminate for material breach if the breach is not cured within thirty (30) days of written notice. Upon termination, Client shall pay for all work completed through the termination date.

**Solo-entrepreneur note**: Always ensure payment for completed work survives termination. See `references/termination-for-convenience.md` for detailed TFC guidance.

### 5. Liability and Indemnification

**Key elements**:
- Mutual liability cap
- Exclusion of consequential/indirect damages
- Mutual indemnification
- Carveouts clearly listed
- Insurance coordination

**Balanced example**:
> Each party's total aggregate liability under this Agreement shall not exceed the total fees paid or payable in the twelve (12) months preceding the claim. Neither party shall be liable for indirect, consequential, or punitive damages. Each party shall indemnify the other against third-party claims arising from its own breach, negligence, or willful misconduct.

**Solo-entrepreneur note**: Liability cap at 1-2x annual fees is standard. Ensure indemnification is mutual and included within the cap. Reject uncapped indemnity.

### 6. IP Ownership and Licensing

**Key elements**:
- Clear distinction between background IP and project IP
- Ownership of deliverables specified
- License grants for background IP incorporated in deliverables
- License-back for contractor portfolio use
- Open-source/third-party component disclosure

**Balanced example**:
> Contractor assigns to Client all rights in the Deliverables upon full payment. Contractor retains all rights in pre-existing intellectual property ("Background IP"). Contractor grants Client a non-exclusive, perpetual license to use Background IP solely as incorporated in the Deliverables. Client grants Contractor a non-exclusive right to use non-confidential elements of the Deliverables for portfolio and marketing purposes.

**Solo-entrepreneur note**: Never assign background IP. Always get a license-back. Disclose open-source dependencies upfront.

### 7. Confidentiality / NDA

**Key elements**:
- Definition of confidential information
- Clear exclusions (public knowledge, prior possession, independent development, compelled disclosure)
- Obligations of receiving party
- Term of confidentiality (not perpetual for non-trade-secrets)
- Return/destruction upon termination
- Residual knowledge clause

**Balanced example**:
> Each party shall maintain the confidentiality of the other's Confidential Information for a period of three (3) years from disclosure. Confidential Information does not include information that: (a) is or becomes publicly available; (b) was known prior to disclosure; (c) is independently developed; or (d) is disclosed pursuant to legal requirement. Upon termination, each party shall return or destroy all Confidential Information upon request.

**Solo-entrepreneur note**: Ensure residual knowledge is protected — you should be able to use general skills and knowledge gained, just not specific confidential data. Watch for NDA terms that effectively create a non-compete.

### 8. Non-Compete / Non-Solicitation

**Key elements**:
- Narrowly scoped (specific clients, not entire industry)
- Reasonable time limit (6-12 months)
- Geographic limitation (if applicable)
- Distinction between non-compete and non-solicitation
- Carveout for existing relationships

**Balanced example**:
> For a period of twelve (12) months following termination, Contractor shall not directly solicit any client of Client with whom Contractor had direct contact during the engagement. This restriction does not apply to clients with whom Contractor had a pre-existing relationship, or to inbound inquiries.

**Solo-entrepreneur note**: Non-solicitation is more reasonable than non-compete for service providers. Reject blanket non-competes — they can shut down your business.

### 9. Dispute Resolution

**Key elements**:
- Escalation procedure (negotiation → mediation → arbitration/litigation)
- Neutral jurisdiction
- Governing law specified
- Fee allocation
- Interim relief preserved

**Balanced example**:
> The parties shall first attempt to resolve disputes through good-faith negotiation for thirty (30) days. If unresolved, the dispute shall be submitted to mediation under [rules]. If mediation fails, the dispute shall be resolved by binding arbitration under [rules] in [neutral city]. Each party bears its own legal costs unless the arbitrator awards fees to the prevailing party.

**Solo-entrepreneur note**: Mediation-first clauses save significant costs. Negotiate virtual/remote proceedings. Avoid distant jurisdictions.

### 10. Force Majeure

**Key elements**:
- Defined triggering events
- Notification obligations
- Performance suspension (not termination) during event
- Right to terminate if event exceeds a threshold duration
- No financial penalty during force majeure

**Balanced example**:
> Neither party shall be liable for failure to perform due to events beyond its reasonable control, including natural disasters, pandemic, government action, or infrastructure failure. The affected party shall notify the other within five (5) business days. If the force majeure event continues for more than sixty (60) days, either party may terminate without penalty.

**Solo-entrepreneur note**: Ensure force majeure explicitly covers scenarios relevant to solo operators (personal illness, key-person incapacity). The threshold for termination right should be reasonable.

### 11. Data Protection / Privacy

**Key elements**:
- Data processing roles (controller vs. processor)
- Permitted processing purposes
- Sub-processor controls
- Data subject rights procedures
- Breach notification timeline
- Cross-border transfer mechanisms
- Data return/deletion on termination

**Balanced example**:
> If Contractor processes personal data on behalf of Client, the parties shall execute a Data Processing Addendum in compliance with applicable data protection laws. Contractor shall process data solely for the purpose of performing the Services, shall not engage sub-processors without prior written consent, and shall notify Client of any data breach within forty-eight (48) hours.

**Solo-entrepreneur note**: If you handle any customer data, insist on clear DPA terms. You're personally exposed if data handling goes wrong.

### 12. Insurance Requirements

**Key elements**:
- Required coverage types and amounts
- Proportional to contract value
- Certificate of insurance timing
- Additional insured provisions
- Waiver of subrogation (if applicable)

**Balanced example**:
> Contractor shall maintain professional liability insurance with minimum coverage of $[amount proportional to contract]. Contractor shall provide a certificate of insurance upon request. Coverage shall be maintained for the term of the Agreement and for twelve (12) months thereafter.

**Solo-entrepreneur note**: Negotiate coverage requirements down to proportional levels. $1M-$2M general liability is standard for most consulting. Push back on requirements exceeding 2-3x contract value.

### 13. Amendment Procedures

**Key elements**:
- Written agreement required from both parties
- No unilateral modifications
- Process for proposing amendments
- Integration with change order process

**Balanced example**:
> This Agreement may be amended only by a written instrument signed by both parties. No waiver of any provision shall be effective unless in writing and signed by the waiving party.

**Solo-entrepreneur note**: Simple and standard. Reject any "we can update terms by posting on our website" language.

### 14. Governing Law

**Key elements**:
- Specific jurisdiction named
- Consistent with dispute resolution clause
- Practical for both parties

**Balanced example**:
> This Agreement shall be governed by and construed in accordance with the laws of the State of [State], without regard to its conflict of laws provisions.

**Solo-entrepreneur note**: Your home state is ideal. If counterparty insists on theirs, ensure you understand the legal implications. Some states are more favorable for contractors (e.g., California limits non-competes).

## Part 2: Contract-Type Mini-Checklists

### NDA — Top Issues to Prioritize

1. **Scope of confidential information** — Is it reasonably bounded? Does it include only genuinely confidential material?
2. **Exclusions** — Are standard exclusions present (public domain, prior knowledge, independent development, compelled disclosure)?
3. **Term** — How long does confidentiality last? 2-5 years is standard for business info. Trade secrets may be perpetual.
4. **Residual knowledge** — Can you use general skills and knowledge gained? Or does the NDA effectively prevent you from working in the field?
5. **Injunctive relief** — Is it mutual? Does it preserve your right to contest in court?
6. **Return/destruction** — What happens to confidential info at termination? Is the process practical?
7. **Non-compete disguised as NDA** — Does the confidentiality definition effectively prevent you from competing?

### MSA / Services Agreement — Top Issues to Prioritize

1. **Scope creep protections** — Is there a change order process? Is "scope" clearly defined or open-ended?
2. **Acceptance criteria** — How are deliverables approved? Is there deemed acceptance on silence?
3. **Payment terms** — Net-30 or better? Milestone payments for long projects? Late payment consequences?
4. **IP ownership** — Who owns the work product? Is background IP protected? License-back?
5. **Indemnity and liability cap** — Mutual? Capped at contract value? Are carveouts reasonable?
6. **Liability cap** — Does it cover all claims including indemnity? Is it proportional?
7. **Termination** — Mutual TFC? Adequate notice? Payment for WIP on termination?

### SaaS / Software License — Top Issues to Prioritize

1. **Usage restrictions** — Are user/seat limits reasonable? Can you scale without renegotiating?
2. **Data ownership** — Who owns data you input? Can you export it? In what format?
3. **Service levels (SLA)** — Uptime commitments? Remedies for downtime? Credits?
4. **Security** — SOC 2, encryption at rest/in transit? Incident response plan?
5. **DPA** — Is there a compliant Data Processing Addendum? Sub-processor list?
6. **Audit rights** — Can you verify security claims? Access logs?
7. **Renewal and pricing** — Auto-renewal terms? Price increase caps? Cancellation notice window?

### Contractor Agreement — Top Issues to Prioritize

1. **IP assignment scope** — Work-for-hire vs. assignment? Background IP protected? License-back?
2. **Independent contractor status** — Is the classification defensible? Look for control indicators that suggest employment.
3. **Non-solicitation/non-compete** — Scope, duration, geography. Is it proportional?
4. **Payment milestones** — Clear triggers? Retainer or milestone payments to avoid cash flow gaps?
5. **Scope of engagement** — Specific deliverables or open-ended "services as requested"?
6. **Termination and WIP** — Payment for work completed? Notice period? Wind-down?
7. **Tax obligations** — Clear that contractor handles own taxes? 1099 / W-9 provisions?

### Partnership / JV-lite — Top Issues to Prioritize

1. **Governance** — Who makes decisions? What requires unanimous consent?
2. **Deadlock resolution** — What happens when partners disagree? Buy-sell provisions?
3. **Ownership split** — Revenue, IP, and asset ownership clearly defined?
4. **Exit rights** — How does a partner leave? Buyout terms? Valuation method?
5. **Decision authority** — Day-to-day vs. major decisions. Spending limits?
6. **Capital contributions** — Who contributes what? How are additional contributions handled?
7. **Liability allocation** — Joint and several? Or proportional? Personal guarantees?

### Statement of Work (SOW) — Top Issues to Prioritize

1. **Scope and deliverables** — Specific, measurable, bounded. No "including but not limited to."
2. **Acceptance process** — Clear criteria, timeline for review, deemed acceptance on silence.
3. **Timeline and milestones** — Realistic deadlines? Consequences for delays (both sides)?
4. **Payment triggers** — Tied to milestones? Clear invoicing schedule?
5. **Change order process** — How are additions handled? Who approves? At what cost?
6. **Dependencies** — What do you need from the client? What happens if they delay?
7. **Relationship to MSA** — How does this SOW interact with the master agreement? Which prevails on conflict?
