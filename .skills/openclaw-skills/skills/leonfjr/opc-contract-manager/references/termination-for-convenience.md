# Termination for Convenience — Deep Dive

A comprehensive reference on Termination for Convenience (TFC) clauses and their impact on solo entrepreneurs. Loaded when a TFC clause is detected during contract review.

---

## Why TFC Is Critical for Solo Entrepreneurs

A Termination for Convenience clause allows one or both parties to end the contract without cause — no breach required. For large companies, this is a routine risk management tool. For a solo entrepreneur, it can be an existential threat.

**The core problem**: If a major client can terminate your contract at any time with minimal notice and no financial consequence, your business operates on borrowed time. Revenue that looks stable on paper is actually contingent.

---

## Analysis Framework

When a TFC clause is detected, evaluate it across these 11 dimensions:

### 1. Notice Period

| Notice Length | Risk Level for Solo Entrepreneur |
|---|---|
| 0-7 days | **Critical** — virtually no time to find replacement revenue |
| 8-14 days | **High** — insufficient for meaningful business development |
| 15-29 days | **Medium-High** — tight but possible to activate pipeline |
| 30-59 days | **Medium** — standard and generally manageable |
| 60-89 days | **Low-Medium** — reasonable buffer for transition |
| 90+ days | **Low** — adequate time to replace revenue |

**Counter-proposal**: Push for 60 days minimum. For contracts representing >20% of your revenue, push for 90 days.

### 2. Mutual vs. One-Sided

| Structure | Risk Level |
|---|---|
| One-sided (only counterparty can terminate for convenience) | **High** — you're locked in while they're not |
| Mutual (both parties can terminate for convenience) | **Medium** — at least you can exit too |
| Mutual with asymmetric notice (they get 30 days, you get 90) | **High** — technically mutual but practically one-sided |

**Counter-proposal**: TFC must always be mutual with equal notice periods. If counterparty insists on shorter notice for themselves, demand a corresponding termination fee.

### 3. Payment for Work in Progress (WIP)

| WIP Treatment | Risk Level |
|---|---|
| No mention of WIP payment | **Critical** — you could lose weeks of unbilled work |
| "Reasonable" compensation for WIP (undefined) | **High** — will be disputed |
| Payment for all work completed through termination date | **Low** — standard and fair |
| Payment for WIP + committed costs + reasonable profit on completed phases | **Very Low** — ideal protection |

**Counter-proposal**: At minimum: "Upon termination, Client shall pay for all Services performed and expenses incurred through the effective date of termination, plus any non-cancellable third-party commitments."

### 4. Wind-Down / Transition Provisions

| Provision | Risk Level |
|---|---|
| No transition period | **High** — abrupt cutoff |
| Defined transition period (e.g., 2-4 weeks after notice) | **Low** — allows orderly handoff |
| Transition period with separate compensation | **Very Low** — you're paid for the handoff work |

**Counter-proposal**: Include a paid transition period of 2-4 weeks beyond the notice period for knowledge transfer and handoff. Transition work should be billable at your standard rate.

### 5. Committed Spend / Reserved Capacity Compensation

| Provision | Risk Level |
|---|---|
| No minimum commitment | **High** — you may have turned down other work for nothing |
| Minimum monthly/quarterly commitment | **Low** — baseline revenue is protected |
| Reserved capacity fee (use-it-or-lose-it) | **Very Low** — ideal for retainer-style engagements |

**Counter-proposal**: If you're dedicating significant capacity to this client, negotiate a minimum commitment or reserved capacity fee. For example: "Client commits to a minimum of [X] hours per month. Unused hours are not refundable or rollable."

### 6. Non-Cancellable Third-Party Costs

| Provision | Risk Level |
|---|---|
| No mention of third-party costs | **High** — you eat the cost of tools, subcontractors, etc. |
| Client reimburses committed third-party costs | **Low** — standard protection |

**Counter-proposal**: "Client shall reimburse Contractor for all non-cancellable costs committed to prior to the termination notice, including subcontractor commitments, tool licenses, and reserved resources."

### 7. Minimum Fee / Early Termination Fee

| Provision | Risk Level |
|---|---|
| No termination fee | **Medium-High** — counterparty has no financial friction to terminate |
| Termination fee equal to X months of fees | **Low** — provides a financial buffer |
| Remaining contract value payable on early termination | **Very Low** — full protection (rare, but negotiable for fixed-term contracts) |

**Counter-proposal**: For fixed-term contracts, propose a termination fee equal to the lesser of: (a) 3 months of average monthly fees, or (b) the remaining contract value. This gives the counterparty an exit while protecting your investment in the relationship.

### 8. Dependency Risk Assessment

**Key question**: How much of your total revenue does this client represent?

| Revenue Concentration | TFC Risk Multiplier |
|---|---|
| < 10% | Low — TFC is manageable |
| 10-25% | Medium — TFC needs reasonable protections |
| 25-40% | High — TFC needs strong protections (60+ day notice, termination fee) |
| > 40% | Critical — TFC combined with high concentration is an existential risk |

**Guidance**: If a single client is >25% of your revenue and has a one-sided TFC with <30 days notice, this is a **Critical** finding regardless of other factors. Your business survival depends on this clause.

### 9. Interaction with Renewal / Auto-Renewal

**Watch for these scenarios**:
- Contract auto-renews for 12 months, but counterparty can TFC with 30 days notice → you're locked into renewal obligations while they can leave anytime
- Renewal price increases combined with TFC → they can terminate if they don't like the new price, but you can't
- TFC resets the notice period on renewal → counter-intuitively, renewal may weaken your position

**Counter-proposal**: If there's auto-renewal, ensure TFC notice periods apply within renewal terms too. Or: non-cancellation period for the first [X] months of each renewal term.

### 10. Whether TFC Bypasses Breach Cure Periods

**Watch for this**: Some contracts allow TFC to be invoked as a shortcut when the counterparty doesn't want to go through the formal breach/cure process. Effectively, they can terminate "for convenience" when the real reason is a dispute, avoiding their obligation to let you cure.

**Counter-proposal**: Add language that TFC cannot be invoked during a pending breach notice/cure period. If there's a dispute, the dispute resolution process should apply, not TFC.

### 11. Hedging Strategies

Beyond clause-level negotiation, these business-level strategies reduce TFC risk:

1. **Revenue diversification** — Never let one client exceed 30% of revenue
2. **Retainer structures** — Monthly retainers with minimum commitments are harder to TFC
3. **Phased contracts** — Shorter contract terms with explicit renewal decisions (instead of long terms with TFC)
4. **Pipeline maintenance** — Always keep 2-3 warm prospects active, even when fully booked
5. **Savings buffer** — Maintain 3-6 months of operating expenses in reserve
6. **Staggered terms** — Time your contracts to start/end at different dates so they don't all TFC at once

## Counter-Proposal Templates

### Template 1: Balanced Mutual TFC

> Either party may terminate this Agreement for convenience upon sixty (60) days' prior written notice to the other party. Upon such termination, Client shall pay Contractor for: (a) all Services performed through the effective date of termination; (b) all non-cancellable third-party costs committed to prior to the date of the termination notice; and (c) a transition fee equal to [two weeks'] fees for knowledge transfer and handoff activities.

### Template 2: TFC with Termination Fee (for fixed-term contracts)

> Either party may terminate this Agreement for convenience upon sixty (60) days' prior written notice. If Client terminates for convenience during the initial term, Client shall pay Contractor: (a) all amounts due for Services performed through the termination date; (b) a termination fee equal to the lesser of three (3) months of average monthly fees or the remaining contract value; and (c) all non-cancellable committed costs.

### Template 3: TFC with Minimum Commitment

> Client may terminate this Agreement for convenience upon ninety (90) days' prior written notice, provided that Client has fulfilled the minimum commitment of [X] hours per month through the effective date of termination. Upon termination, Client shall pay for all Services performed and expenses incurred through the termination date.

## Quick Decision Guide

| If the TFC clause is... | And the client is... | Then... |
|---|---|---|
| Mutual, 60+ days, pays WIP | Any size | **Acceptable** — standard terms |
| One-sided, 30+ days, pays WIP | < 15% of revenue | **Negotiable** — push for mutual but acceptable as-is |
| One-sided, 30+ days, pays WIP | 15-30% of revenue | **Negotiate** — must add termination fee or longer notice |
| One-sided, < 30 days, no WIP | Any size | **Reject** — propose Template 1 or 2 |
| Any TFC, no WIP payment | > 30% of revenue | **Critical** — do not sign without significant changes |
| One-sided, < 30 days, no WIP | > 30% of revenue | **Walk away** — unless terms are fundamentally restructured |
