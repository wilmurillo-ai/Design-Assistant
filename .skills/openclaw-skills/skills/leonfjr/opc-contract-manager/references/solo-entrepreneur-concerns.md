# Solo Entrepreneur Concerns Guide

Risks and considerations specific to one-person companies. Loaded during Phase 3 (Risk Assessment) to evaluate findings through the lens of a solo founder.

---

## 1. Personal Liability Exposure

**The risk**: As a solo entrepreneur, the line between personal and business liability is thin — especially for sole proprietors, single-member LLCs in states with weak veil protection, or anyone who signs personal guarantees.

**What to watch for**:
- Contracts that name you personally (not your entity) as a party
- Personal guarantee clauses buried in the agreement
- Indemnification obligations that pierce the corporate form
- Contracts that require you to be "personally responsible" for performance

**Guidance**: Always contract through your business entity. Reject personal guarantees unless the contract value justifies the risk and you negotiate a cap. If you're a sole proprietor, consider forming an LLC before signing significant contracts.

---

## 2. Capacity Constraints — You Are the Business

**The risk**: Penalty clauses for delays, guaranteed response times, and SLAs are far riskier when you are the only person who can do the work. Illness, emergency, or even a vacation can trigger breach.

**What to watch for**:
- Guaranteed turnaround times under 48 hours
- Penalty clauses for missed deadlines without force majeure coverage
- SLA commitments (e.g., 99.9% uptime) that you can't guarantee alone
- "Key person" requirements that name you specifically with no substitute provision

**Guidance**: Build buffer into deadlines. Negotiate force majeure to include personal illness or incapacity. For SLAs, ensure the commitment matches what a single-person operation can realistically deliver. Consider adding a "reasonable efforts" qualifier.

---

## 3. Cash Flow Sensitivity

**The risk**: Solo entrepreneurs typically lack a cash reserve. A single late-paying client or an unexpected gap between projects can create a survival crisis.

**What to watch for**:
- Net-60 or net-90 payment terms
- No deposit or upfront payment on long projects
- Pay-when-paid clauses
- Large final payment tied to subjective acceptance criteria
- Auto-renewal that locks you into below-market rates

**Guidance**: Always push for milestone payments or monthly billing. Require a deposit (25-50% for project work). Include right to pause work if payment is 15+ days overdue. Negotiate shorter payment terms — net-30 is reasonable and should be your standard.

---

## 4. IP Is Your Primary Asset

**The risk**: Your intellectual property — methods, tools, frameworks, reputation, portfolio — is likely your most valuable business asset. Contracts that transfer too much IP can erode your competitive position.

**What to watch for**:
- Assignment of all IP, including background IP
- No license-back for portfolio use
- Work-for-hire classification for everything you create "in connection with" the engagement
- Confidentiality terms so broad they prevent you from demonstrating your expertise
- IP warranties that don't carve out open-source or pre-existing tools

**Guidance**: Protect your background IP aggressively. Get a license-back for non-confidential work. Clearly define what "deliverables" are assigned vs. what you retain. Maintain a record of your pre-existing IP that you can reference in each engagement.

---

## 5. Non-Compete Can Shut Down Your Business

**The risk**: A broad non-compete doesn't just limit one client relationship — it can make it impossible for you to earn a living, since you likely operate in a specialized niche.

**What to watch for**:
- Industry-wide non-competes (not limited to named competitors)
- Duration exceeding 6-12 months
- Geographic scope beyond where you actually served
- Non-solicitation so broad it covers the counterparty's entire client base
- Stacking multiple contracts with non-competes that overlap

**Guidance**: Reject industry-wide non-competes outright. Accept only narrow non-solicitation of clients you directly served. Maximum 6 months duration. If you must accept a non-compete, demand a "garden leave" payment (they pay you during the restricted period).

---

## 6. Insurance Requirements Can Exceed Contract Value

**The risk**: Enterprise clients often have standard insurance requirements designed for large vendors. A $10M E&O policy requirement for a $30K project is disproportionate and potentially unprofitable.

**What to watch for**:
- Professional liability (E&O) requirements exceeding 3x contract value
- Cyber liability requirements for non-technology projects
- Naming counterparty as additional insured without clear scope limitation
- Insurance certificate requirements that must be renewed annually even for short engagements

**Guidance**: Negotiate insurance requirements proportional to your contract value. Standard for solo consultants: $1-2M general liability, $1M professional liability. Push back on requirements that would cost more than 10% of your contract profit.

---

## 7. Jurisdiction Matters More When You're Small

**The risk**: Litigating in a distant jurisdiction is disproportionately expensive for a solo entrepreneur. A $50K contract dispute isn't worth $30K in travel and local counsel fees.

**What to watch for**:
- Governing law and venue in a distant state or country
- No provision for virtual proceedings
- Mandatory in-person arbitration in counterparty's city
- Forum selection clauses that waive your right to object

**Guidance**: Negotiate for your home jurisdiction, or at minimum a neutral location. Push for virtual arbitration/mediation as the first step. Include a mediation-first clause to resolve disputes before incurring litigation costs.

---

## 8. Key Person Dependency — What If You Can't Work?

**The risk**: If you're incapacitated, there's no team to pick up the work. Contracts without provisions for this scenario can create breach liability during a medical emergency.

**What to watch for**:
- No force majeure or personal emergency provisions
- Penalties triggered by your personal unavailability
- No right to subcontract or bring in a substitute
- Client expecting continuity that only you can provide

**Guidance**: Include force majeure coverage for personal illness/incapacity. Negotiate a right to engage qualified subcontractors with client approval. Consider what happens to the contract if you can't work for 30+ days — there should be a graceful exit path.

---

## 9. Tax Implications of Contract Structure

**The risk**: Contract language that treats you like an employee (fixed hours, employer-provided tools, exclusive engagement) can trigger employment reclassification, resulting in back taxes, penalties, and loss of independent contractor benefits.

**What to watch for**:
- Requirements to work specific hours at the client's location
- Client provides all tools and equipment
- Exclusivity combined with long-term engagement
- "At-will" termination language (an employment concept)
- Benefits or PTO provisions (these suggest employment)
- Supervision and reporting requirements that look like employment

**Guidance**: Ensure the contract reflects genuine independent contractor status: you control how and when work is done, you use your own tools, you can work for other clients, and you bear your own business expenses. If a contract has too many employment indicators, it may need restructuring — and possibly a lawyer's review.

---

## 10. Termination for Convenience — The Cash Flow Killer

**The risk**: For a solo entrepreneur, a one-sided TFC clause from a major client is an existential threat. If 40%+ of your revenue can disappear with 7 days' notice, your business is always one phone call from crisis.

**What to watch for**:
- Unilateral TFC (only counterparty can invoke)
- Short notice period (under 30 days)
- No payment for work in progress
- No wind-down or transition period
- No termination fee or committed spend minimum
- Combined with exclusivity (you gave up other clients for this one)

**Guidance**: See `references/termination-for-convenience.md` for detailed analysis and counter-proposal strategies. Core principle: the larger the client is as a percentage of your revenue, the stronger your TFC protections should be.

---

## 11. Assignment and Change of Control

**The risk**: Your counterparty gets acquired by a company you'd never choose to work with. Without assignment protections, you're stuck in a contract with a new entity.

**What to watch for**:
- Counterparty can freely assign the contract
- No notification requirement for change of control
- Your obligations transfer to an unknown third party
- No termination right upon assignment or change of control

**Guidance**: Require written consent for assignment. Include a termination right (with payment for WIP) if the counterparty is acquired or assigned to a competitor. At minimum, require notification of any assignment or change of control.

---

## 12. Accumulating Obligations Across Contracts

**The risk**: As a solo entrepreneur managing multiple clients, contractual obligations accumulate. Five NDAs, three non-solicitation clauses, and two exclusivity provisions can collectively make it nearly impossible to operate freely.

**What to watch for**:
- Overlapping non-solicitation clauses that together cover most of your potential clients
- Confidentiality obligations from one client that conflict with transparency obligations to another
- Exclusivity or non-compete clauses that, when stacked, cover your entire operating domain
- Insurance requirements from multiple clients that exceed what a single policy covers

**Guidance**: Maintain a running inventory of your active obligations (this is what the contract archive system is for). Before signing a new contract, check for conflicts with existing commitments. Flag any new restriction that, when combined with existing ones, creates an unreasonable total burden.
