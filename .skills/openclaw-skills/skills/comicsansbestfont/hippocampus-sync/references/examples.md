## Example 1: Top of Mind Entry

**What this teaches:** Every Top of Mind item should answer "What? So what? What next?" A bare metric is not a Top of Mind item — a metric with consequence and a trigger is.

---

### Before (weak)

> Cron success rate dropped to 85%.

**Why it fails:** No trajectory (was it 95% last week?), no consequence (does 85% matter or not?), no trigger (when should someone act?). Sachee reads this and doesn't know if it's a crisis or noise.

---

### After (strong)

> Cron success rate at 85% — down from 97% two weeks ago. Three consecutive failures on hippocampus-sync for JDN agents. If it drops below 80%, memory continuity breaks and agents start sessions without recent context. No pattern identified yet. Flag to Sachee if another failure before next sync.

**Why it works:** Trajectory (97% → 85%), consequence (what breaks if it continues), conditional trigger (what to do and when), and honest gap ("no pattern identified yet" is better than a false explanation).

</example>

## Example 2: Open Thread

**What this teaches:** An Open Thread needs stakes, age, who's involved, and what happens if it stays unresolved. Without those, it's a to-do item, not a thread.

---

### Before (weak)

> Working on governance docs.

**Why it fails:** Who's working on them? Since when? What's blocked? Why does it matter? This thread could have been open for a day or three months — the reader can't tell. It also doesn't tell Sachee whether to act, wait, or close it.

---

### After (strong)

> **Governance docs — open since Mar 2 (19 days).** Cyclawps owns. Three charter files drafted; BU sign-off still needed from Bobo and Ananda. Bobo hasn't responded to the Mar 7 review request. If unsigned by Mar 28, the v2 reporting structure can't launch on schedule — town halls will run without formal domain boundaries. Escalate to Sachee if no response by Mar 21.

**Why it works:** Age visible (19 days), owner named, specific blocker (Bobo hasn't responded), consequence of inaction (v2 launch blocked), and a conditional trigger with a date.

</example>

## Example 3: Domain Section Entry (Marketing Pulse style)

**What this teaches:** Raw metrics belong in a spreadsheet. A domain section entry interprets metrics — trajectory, context, and what Sachee should do with the information.

---

### Before (weak)

> Revenue: $4,200. Orders: 31. AOV: $135.

**Why it fails:** No comparison point — is $4,200 good or bad this week? No seasonal context — is March typically slow or strong? No decision implication — should Sachee be concerned, relieved, or adjusting the plan?

---

### After (strong)

> **Revenue $4,200 (orders: 31, AOV: $135) — down 18% on the prior fortnight.** March historically runs 15-20% softer than February due to post-summer slowdown; the dip is within expected range. However, AOV has compressed from $158 two weeks ago — likely driven by the discount campaign running Mar 1-14. If AOV doesn't recover to $145+ by Apr 1, the campaign may have trained price sensitivity rather than expanded the basket. Watch the next two fortnights before renewing discount activity.

**Why it works:** Trajectory visible (18% down, AOV $158 → $135), seasonal context normalises the dip, causal hypothesis named (discount campaign), and a concrete watch condition that turns the data into a decision framework.

</example>

## The Pattern

Every strong entry has three layers:

1. **What happened** — the fact, with enough context to understand scale (trajectory, comparison, timeframe).
2. **So what** — the consequence if this continues, resolves, or escalates. Name who's affected and how.
3. **What next** — a conditional trigger, owner, or decision point. "Monitor" is not a next step.

Entries that only have layer 1 are data, not intelligence. Sachee doesn't need data surfaced — he needs interpretation.
