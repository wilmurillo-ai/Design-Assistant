## Poor Charlie's Almanack

**Published:** 2005 (expanded edition)  
**Author:** Compiled by Peter Kaufman, edited by Charles Thomas Munger  
**Link:** https://www.poorcharliesalmanack.com

### Overview

> *"This book is something of a publishing miracle — never advertised, yet year after year selling many thousands of copies from its Internet site."* — Warren Buffett

**Poor Charlie's Almanack** is the definitive collection of Munger's speeches, writings, and wisdom. Unlike Buffett who writes annual letters, Munger preferred the speech format — this book contains the major speeches edited into essay form.

### Core Contents

| Chapter | Topic | Description |
|---------|-------|-------------|
| 1 | The Psychology of Human Misjudgment | His magnum opus on why humans behave irratically |
| 2 | A Lesson on Elementary Worldly Wisdom | Mental models before investing |
| 3 | Turning $2M Into $2 Trillion | Coca-Cola mental model exercise |
| 4 | Academic Economics | Strengths and weaknesses of economics |
| 5 | Annual discourses | Speeches at Daily Journal meetings |

### Key Themes in the Book

1. **Multidisciplinary thinking** — Drawing from psychology, economics, physics, biology, history
2. **Lollapalooza effects** — When multiple psychological tendencies combine for massive outcomes
3. **Inversion** — "Invert, always invert" — solve problems by thinking backwards
4. **Patience** — The ultimate virtue; you make your money by waiting
5. **Rationality** — The most important trait; "I'm rational"

## The Psychology of Human Misjudgment

**Original speech:** Harvard Law School, 1992  
**Revised:** 2005 (for Poor Charlie's Almanack)  
**Link:** https://fs.blog/great-talks/psychology-human-misjudgment/

### Overview

Munger calls this his "magnum opus" on human behavior. He identifies **25 standard causes of human misjudgment** and explains how they combine to create "lollapalooza effects."

### The 25 Psychological Tendencies (Summary)

**Reward/Punishment Superpower:**
- People respond to incentives — exploit this, but beware incentive-caused bias

**Liking/Loving Tendency:**
- People tend to accept people/products they like — use as persuasion, beware social proof

**Disliking/Hating Tendency:**
- Opposite of liking — can cause distorted judgment

**Doubt-Avoidance Tendency:**
- The brain resists doubt — seeks certainty quickly (explains religious faith, superstition)

**Inconsistency-Avoidance Tendency:**
- People hate to change their minds — creates anchoring, commitment devices

**Curiosity Tendency:**
- Curiosity drives learning — but also leads into cults and harmful pursuits

**Paternalistic Tendency:**
- People trust those who act like parents/caregivers

**Optimism/Pessimism Tendency:**
- Optimism leads to underestimating risks; pessimism can be protective

**Excessive Self-Regard Tendency:**
- Everyone thinks they're above average — leads to overconfidence

**Dehumanization/B dehumanization Tendency:**
- When you dehumanize others, you can harm them more easily

**Influence from Motivation (Argyll):**
- Motivated reasoning — people believe what they want to believe

**Mental Simulation Tendency:**
- We imagine scenarios — sometimes too few, sometimes too many

**Confabulation Tendency:**
- We fill gaps in memory with plausible fabrications — "remembering" things that didn't happen

**Authority Bias:**
- Too much weight given to authority figures — Milgram experiments

**Testing/Confirmation Bias:**
- Seeking information that confirms existing beliefs

**Stress Effect:**
- Stress makes people dumber temporarily — affects decision-making

**Financial/Physical Outcome Misweighting:**
- Outcome bias — judging decisions by results, not the info available at the time

**Change Affinity:**
- Tendency to change things rather than let them work (doing vs. not doing)

**Contrast Effect:**
- Perception distorted by comparison — $100 looks cheap after $1,000

**Availability Heuristic:**
- Most available memory = most salient — leads to overestimating rare events

**Underestimating Randomness:**
- Everything attributed to skill must actually be random variation

**Influence of Coarse Sorting:**
- Binary thinking — yes/no, black/white, ignoring continuous distributions

**Authority Bias from Authority:**
- Following authority blindly

**Commonfollower Bias:**
- Everyone else is doing it

**Present-Bias:**
- Overweighting present relative to future

### The Lollapalooza Effect

When two or more psychological tendencies operate simultaneously, the combined effect can be multiplicative, not additive. This explains why cults work so well — social proof + authority + consistency + doubt-avoidance all fire at once.

## A Lesson on Elementary Worldly Wisdom

**Speech:** University of Southern California, 1994  
**Link:** https://fs.blog/great-talks/a-lesson-on-worldly-wisdom/

### Core Message

Before you can be a good investor, you need a foundation of **multidisciplinary thinking**. Munger believes most people are trained in one discipline and think it solves all problems. This is a mistake.

### Key Mental Models for Investing

| Discipline | Key Model | Application |
|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| **Mathematics** | Compound interest, multiplicative effects | The math of compounding is foundational |
| **Physics** | Equilibrium, threshold effects | Markets seek equilibrium; breaks above/below thresholds |
| **Biology** | Evolution, natural selection | Competitive advantages must evolve or die |
| **Psychology** | All 25 tendencies | Understanding misjudgment is essential |

## Turning $2 Million Into $2 Trillion

**Speech:** 1986 (revisited in Poor Charlie's Almanack)  
**Link:** https://fs.blog/turning-2-million-into-2-trillion/

### The Coca-Cola Exercise

Munger poses a hypothetical: How would you build a company from $2 million to $2 trillion (adjusted for inflation) starting in 1886?

### Key Principles Demonstrated

1. **Brand power** — The name itself is an enormous asset
2. **Global ubiquity** — Making the product available everywhere, always
3. **Compound interest in brand** — The longer the brand exists, the more valuable
4. **Biological craving** — Caffeine + sugar + water = instinctual appeal
5. **Pricing power** — At 1 cent in 1886, price could rise with inflation
6. **Network effects** — Distribution system becomes competitive moat

### Lessons on Decision-Making

> "Simple, basic business logic + discipline + rationality + patience = winning."

## Academic Economics — Strengths and Weaknesses

**Speech:** 1995 (Harvard Economicsposium)  
**Link:** https://fs.blog/great-talks/academic-economics-charlie-munger/

### Strengths of Economics

| Strength | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| **Magnificent achievement** | Standard economics is enormously useful |
| **边际思维 (Marginal thinking)** | Incentives drive behavior at the margin |
| **Comparative advantage** | Basis for trade; fundamental to understanding specialization |
| **Creative destruction** | Schumpeter's gift: capitalism renews itself through disruption |
| **Gresham's Law** | Bad money drives out good; applies to morals too |

### Weaknesses of Economics

| Weakness | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| **Too few models** | Economists under-use other disciplines |
| **Incentive problem** | Economists are biased toward serving powerful interests |
| **Extreme rationality assumption** | Homo economicus doesn't exist |
| **Ignores psychology** | Human irrationality matters enormously |
| **Groupthink** | Economists reinforce each other's assumptions |

### Munger's Prescription

> "I paid no attention to the territorial boundaries of academic disciplines and I just grabbed all the big ideas that I could."

## Berkshire Hathaway Philosophy

**Sources:** Berkshire Hathaway Annual Reports, Munger speeches

### The Munger-Buffett Partnership

Munger is credited as the **"architect"** of modern Berkshire Hathaway's philosophy. Buffett described Munger as his closest partner.

**Key contributions from Munger:**
- Shifted Berkshire from "cigar butt" investing to quality businesses at fair prices
- Introduced the concept of "wonderful businesses at fair prices" vs. fair businesses at wonderful prices
- Emphasized patience and compound interest above all
- Pushed for diversification into businesses like See's Candies and Coca-Cola

### Berkshire's Core Principles (Munger's Version)

1. **Capital allocation** — Allocate capital to highest return uses
2. **Aligned incentives** — Manager compensation should align with owners
3. **No borrowing** — Berkshire almost never leveraged
4. **Long-term thinking** — "Our favorite holding period is forever"
5. **Rationality** — Don't let emotions drive decisions
6. **Transparency** — Full financial disclosure

## Daily Journal Corporation Letters

**Role:** Chairman of Daily Journal Corp (1984–2023)  
**Source:** Annual reports and shareholder meetings

### The Daily Journal Philosophy

Munger treated Daily Journal as an extension of his investment philosophy:
- Invested the company's excess cash in a concentrated portfolio
- Holdings included: Costco, Wells Fargo, Bank of America, US Bancorp
- Maintained large cash positions waiting for opportunities

### Key Lessons from Daily Journal

> "It takes character to sit with all that cash and do nothing. I didn't get to where I am by going after mediocre opportunities."

**Investment approach at Daily Journal:**
- Similar to Berkshire — high quality businesses, patient capital
- Not trying to time market, just waiting for right prices
- Costco was a core holding for decades
