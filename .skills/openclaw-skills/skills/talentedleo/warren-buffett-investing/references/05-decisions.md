## Case 1: Coca-Cola (KO) — The Definitive Moat Investment

**Entry:** 1988–1989
**Initial Cost:** ~$1.0 billion (first purchase)
**Total Cost by 1994:** $1.3 billion
**Shares:** 6.2% of outstanding shares
**Holding Period:** 1988–present (36+ years)
**Current Value (2024):** ~$25 billion+ (cost basis ~$1.3B)

### Decision Chain

**Step 1: Moat Identification**
> "Coca-Cola had the most powerful brand in the world. People don't switch from one soft drink to another. They have a favorite."

**Step 2: ROE Check**
- ROE in 1988: **31.8%** (well above 15% threshold)
- Operating margin: 19.2%
- International growth potential: massive (only 25% of world consumption was outside US)

**Step 3: Management Quality**
- Roberto Goizueta (CEO): Focused business, sold non-core assets
- New CEO: David Ogden (finance background, disciplined capital allocation)
- Initiated dividend increases

**Step 4: Price/Value**
- 1987 crash: stock dropped ~25%
- Valuation: P/E ~15x earnings at purchase
- "The market mispriced Coca-Cola's intangibles."

### Buffett's Own Assessment (2007 Letter)
> "The Coca-Cola investment taught me about the power of brand and patience. I should have put more money in earlier."

### Key Metrics

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Initial ROE | 31.8% |
| Holding Period | 36+ years |
| Original Investment | $1.0B |
| Current Value (est.) | $25B+ |
| Annual Dividend (2024) | ~$800M |

## Case 2: American Express (AXP) — Crisis Investing

**Entry:** 1964 (formal accumulation begins)
**Holding Period:** 1964–2005 (41 years)
**Profit:** ~$3.7 billion
**Return:** ~40x on original investment

### Background
The "Salad Oil Scandal" — American Express subsidiary was found to have fraudulent inventory records.

**Impact:** Stock dropped from ~$60 to ~$35 in 18 months.

### Decision Logic

**Step 1: Core Business Intact?**
> "The fundamental business — charge cards and travelers' checks — was not impaired. The scandal was in a subsidiary."

**Step 2: Competitive Position?**
- American Express: irreplaceable brand
- Competitors (Diners Club): weakened
- Network effects strengthening

**Step 3: Management Response**
- New leadership acknowledged problems
- Stock-based compensation tied to performance
- Aggressive cost reduction

**Buffett on the Decision (1991 Letter)**
> "Sometimes a crisis creates the opportunity. American Express had demonstrated that people would pay for trust. The stock mispriced that trust."

## Case 3: Apple Inc. (AAPL) — Evolving the Circle of Competence

**Entry:** 2016–2018 (large accumulation)
**Initial Cost:** ~$36 billion
**Shares:** ~5.4% of outstanding
**Holding Period:** 2016–present (8+ years)
**Current Value (2024):** ~$175 billion

### The Evolution of His Thinking

**2013 (Before Buying):**
> "I don't understand Apple well enough. Tim Cook is a brilliant manager, but I don't know if I understand the product cycle."

**2016 (First Purchase):**
> "I finally figured it out. This is not a tech stock. It's a consumer products company with a technology component."

### The Key Insight

**What Changed:**
- iPhone ecosystem = conversion costs
- Users don't switch because of app investment, data, hardware integration
- "Once you're in the Apple ecosystem, leaving is expensive."

**Decision Logic:**
> "The phone is like a utility — people need it. And Apple has pricing power. The key is the ecosystem."

### Quantified Metrics

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| ROE (2016) | 36.1% |
| Cash/Debt | $256B cash vs $95B debt |
| Services Revenue Growth | 15%+ annually |
| Customer Retention | Industry-leading |

## Case 4: 2008 Financial Crisis — "Be Fearful When Others Are Greedy"

**Capital Deployed:** ~$15 billion in September–October 2008

### Goldman Sachs (GS)

**Date:** September 2008
**Investment:** $5 billion in preferred stock
**Dividend:** 10% annual
**Warrants:** Additional equity at favorable price

**The Position:**
> "We will never borrow money for investment purposes. But Goldman Sachs is not a speculation. It's a fundamental bet on America."

**Outcome:** Preferred shares redeemed 2011. Total profit ~$1.8B + warrant gains.

### GE (General Electric)

**Date:** October 2008
**Investment:** $3 billion in preferred stock
**Dividend:** 10% annual
**Outcome:** Redeemed 2013. Profit ~$1B+

## Case 5: GEICO — Float as a Competitive Moat

**Acquisition:** 1996 (full ownership); initial stake 1970s
**Investment:** ~$2.3B for 100% ownership
**Float at Acquisition:** ~$9 billion
**Current Float (2024):** ~$190 billion+

### Why GEICO Is Buffett's Moat

**The Business Model:**
- Direct auto insurance (lower costs than agents)
- 15-minute quote capability
- Low expense ratio

**The Float Mechanics:**
> "GEICO holds premiums today and pays claims tomorrow. The float is essentially free money when loss ratios are controlled."

**Key Metric:**
> "GEICO's combined ratio (losses + expenses / premiums) has been below 100% for 20+ years. That means the float costs us nothing."

### Decision to Buy Remainder (1996)

> "The 1996 acquisition was simple: it was the best insurance business I knew. I knew the management. I knew the float. I knew the brand."

## Case 6: Dexter Shoe — The "Moat That Wasn't There"

**Year:** 1993
**Cost:** $433 million (in Berkshire stock)
**Outcome:** Worthless by 2003
**Total Loss:** ~$4.5 billion in shareholder value (including stock given up)

### What Happened

**The Thesis:**
- US-made high-quality shoes
- Strong management (Harold Goldstein)
- Brand loyalty in work shoes
- "People don't switch shoe brands."

**The Reality:**
- Chinese import shoes were 80% cheaper
- No moat: shoes are a commodity
- The management was good — but the business had no protection

### Buffett's Own Words (2002 Letter)

> "I used the stock of Berkshire — which was worth far more than I knew — to pay for a business that was worth far less than I thought. This is the worst deal I ever made."

### Lessons

| Lesson | Application |
|--------|-------------|
| Management ≠ Moat | Good managers don't create a moat in a bad business |
| Commodity businesses have no moats | Competition will eventually destroy margins |
| Stock as currency amplifies mistakes | Berkshire stock was high when he traded it |

## Case 7: US Air / Airlines — Twice Burned

### First Investment: 1989

**Outcome:** 75% loss on preferred stock
**Buffett's Comment:**
> "If there was an capitalist on that board, he should have shot down the Wright Brothers."

**What Happened:** Airlines have no pricing power, high fixed costs, union problems.

### Second Investment: 2016

**Position:** Initiated stakes in all four major carriers (American, Delta, United, Southwest)
**Why:** Low oil prices, consolidation, management changes

**Exit:** March 2020 (COVID crash)
> "I made a mistake. The airline business changed. I was wrong."

**Loss:** ~$5 billion write-off

### Buffett's Reflection

> "Every decade or so, the airline business will destroy capital. The business model is fundamentally flawed. The problem is: when you have too much capital and not enough opportunities, you make mistakes."

## Case 8: ConocoPhillips — Misreading Oil Prices

**Years:** 2006–2008
**Position:** Large stake, accumulated at high oil prices
**Peak Oil Price:** $147/barrel (July 2008)

**What Happened:**
- Buffett bought more as oil rose ("momentum" thinking)
- Oil crashed to $32 by December 2008
- Berkshire lost ~$2.6 billion on the position

**Buffett's Assessment:**
> "I should have recognized that oil prices were unsustainable. I was thinking about what was happening, not what would happen."

## Case 9: Kraft Heinz — Overpaying for the Moat

**Year:** 2015 (3G Capital partnership)
**Cost:** ~$22 billion (Berkshire share)
**Current Value (2024):** ~$11 billion (half of original)

### The Thesis

- Strong brands: Kraft, Heinz, Oscar Mayer
- Pricing power in consumer staples
- 3G Capital's cost-cutting expertise

### What Went Wrong

- Consumer habits changed (healthier eating)
- Private label competition
- Berkshire paid for "moats" that were actually shrinking

**Buffett (2023 Letter):**
> "We overpaid. The brands were not as durable as I thought."

## Case 10: Tesco — Delayed Exit

**Entry:** 2006–2007
**Position:** 5% stake (~$2.3B)
**Exit:** 2014
**Loss:** ~$800M

### The Story

**2008:** Buffett admits he should have sold earlier when accounting problems emerged.

> "I knew something was wrong. I should have acted. Instead, I waited. Waiting cost us."

**Lesson:**
> "When you realize you've made a mistake, exit immediately. Don't wait for confirmation."

## Case 11: Salomon Inc. — Governance Failure

**Investment:** $700M in preferred stock, 1987
**Crisis:** Treasury auction manipulation by Salomon trader

**Buffett's Role:** Interim chairman to save the company

**On Why He Took the Role:**
> "I couldn't let 8,000 people lose their jobs because of one rogue trader."

**Outcome:** Salomon survived. Buffett's $700M earned 9%/year. Not a financial failure — but an enormous personal time sink.

## Decision Framework Summary

### The Buffett Decision Checklist

```
Before Any Investment:

1. MOAT CHECK
   - Can the business sustain pricing power for 10+ years?
   - What specifically protects it from competitors?

2. MANAGEMENT CHECK
   - Are they honest? (No Integrity = No Deal)
   - Are they good capital allocators?
   - Do they think like owners?

3. NUMBERS CHECK
   - ROE > 15% for 5+ years?
   - Free Cash Flow > Net Income?
   - Debt < 50% of capital?

4. PRICE CHECK
   - Is there a margin of safety?
   - Would I buy at this price if the market closed 10 years?

5. OPPORTUNITY COST
   - Is this the best use of capital right now?
   - What am I giving up by deploying here?
```
