## 1. Berkshire Hathaway Letters to Shareholders (1957–Present)

**Official Source:** [berkshirehathaway.com/letters](https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/letters/letters.html)

Annual letters from 1957 to present. These are the single most important primary source for understanding Buffett's investment philosophy. Compiled in book form by Lawrence Cunningham.

### Key Letters by Era

#### Partnership Era Letters (1957–1970)

**1957 Letter** — First letter. Describes market conditions and early partnership performance.
> "The Dow entered 1957 in a declining market and continued lower through the first two months... Our whole philosophy in buying these working capital situations is to get bargains—if the valuation standards we apply to a diversified list of 20 of these situations doesn't work out, we would expect the average to be satisfactory."

**1964 Letter** — Buffett closes his investment partnership, citing inability to find undervalued situations at his capital scale.
> "My own opinion is that the investment responsibility that I have is too great to be mixed up with a large number of outstanding people who have entrusted their savings with me — particularly when I am having trouble doing a better job than the Dow."

**1965–1969 Letters** — Describes acquisition of Berkshire Hathaway and transition from textile business to investment holding company.

#### Berkshire Transformation Letters (1970–1989)

**1977 Letter** — First systematic articulation of "owner earnings" concept.
> "Our goal is to maximize the average annual rate of gain in intrinsic value. We believe the test should be applied over a period of years, not a single year."

**1987 Letter** — Market commentary. Introduced "Mr. Market" explicitly.
> "The analogy I like to use is the imaginary investor I call Mr. Market. He is very obliging... Every day he names a price at which he will either buy your interest or sell you his."

**1989 Letter** — On intrinsic value and the nature of business valuation.
> "The value of any business is the present value of all future cash flows... This equation, frequently used, is not merely academic, but is the standard by which every proposition in this letter should be judged."

#### Modern Era Letters (1990–2007)

**1991 Letter** — On risk, diversification, and the nature of investment risk.
> "Investment risk... is most often a function of both the price paid and the quality of the business."

**1996 Letter** — First explicit articulation of the Circle of Competence principle.
> "What counts for most people is not how smart they are or how fast they move, but whether they can stay in the game for the long term."

**2005 Letter** — On derivatives and the risks they pose to financial system.
> "Derivatives are weapons of mass destruction... they have dramatically increased the leverage and risk in the financial system."

**2007 Letter** — Full articulation of the "Economic Moat" concept.
> "A truly great company must have a durable 'moat' that protects its high investment returns... A moat that must be continuously rebuilt will eventually be no moat at all."

#### Recent Letters (2008–Present)

**2008 Letter** — On the financial crisis, market panic, and Berkshire's response.
> "We will never borrow money for investment purposes... We simply wait for opportunities that are clearly唾手可得."

**2015 Letter** — Buffett and Munger's 50-year anniversary at Berkshire. Reflections on compounding and partnership with Munger.

**2020 Letter** — On COVID-19, market volatility, and long-term optimism.
> "American miracles continue... The American economy has demonstrated over time its ability to weather difficult cycles."

**2021 Letter** — On "The Big Four" banks and their role in Berkshire's portfolio.
> "Never bet against America" — a theme throughout his post-COVID writings.

**2022 Letter** — On the importance of American dynamism and capitalism.

**2023 Letter** — On Charlie Munger, who died in November 2023.
> "Charlie was the architect of Berkshire Hathaway. I was merely the contractor."

**2024 Letter** — On succession planning (Greg Abel as successor) and Berkshire's future.

**2025 Letter** — Buffett's last regular letter. Announces plan to step back.
> "I will be quiet going forward... Greg Abel will be CEO before year-end."

## 2. Books by Warren Buffett

### "The Snowball: Warren Buffett and the Business of Life" (2008)

**Author:** Alice Schroeder  
**Pages:** 641  
**Publisher:** Bantam Press  
**ISBN:** 978-0553806266  
**Link:** [amazon.com](https://www.amazon.com/Snowball-Warren-Buffett-Business-Life/dp/0553806266)

**Summary:** The only fully-authorized biography of Buffett. Schroeder spent hundreds of hours with Buffett and his family to write this comprehensive account of his life, philosophy, and investing career.

**Core Ideas:**
- Buffett's childhood influences: his father's investment philosophy, his mother's personality
- Early business ventures: selling gum, delivering papers, pinball machine business
- Partnership years under Graham's mentorship
- Acquisition of Berkshire Hathaway — initially a mistake, transformed into the holding company
- Relationship with Charlie Munger and how Munger changed Buffett's approach from "cigar butt" investing to quality
- Personal life: two marriages, family relationships, philanthropy

**Key Quote from the Book:**
> "Life is like a snowball. The important thing is finding wet snow and a really long hill."

---

### "The Making of an American Capitalist" (1999)

**Author:** Roger Lowenstein  
**Pages:** 380  
**Publisher:** Broadway Books  
**ISBN:** 978-0767906895  
**Link:** [amazon.com](https://www.amazon.com/Making-American-Capitalist-Warren-Buffett/dp/0767906894)

**Summary:** An unauthorized biography that traces Buffett's life from his Omaha childhood through his building of Berkshire. Known for its detailed reporting on the financial mechanics of Buffett's deals.

**Core Ideas:**
- Warren's unusual childhood relationship with money
- His partnership years and the Graham-and-Dodd influenced approach
- The acquisition of Berkshire and subsequent transformation
- Detailed explanation of how he uses insurance float
- Critical of some of Buffett's mistakes (Dexter Shoe, US Air)

---

### "Tap Dancing to Work" (2012)

**Author:** Carol Loomis  
**Pages:** 256  
**Publisher:** Portfolio  
**ISBN:** 978-1591846245  
**Link:** [amazon.com](https://www.amazon.com/Tap-Dancing-Work-Stories-Magazine/dp/1591846247)

**Summary:** A collection of Fortune magazine articles about Buffett spanning 50 years (1966–2012), edited by Carol Loomis who has known Buffett for decades.

**Core Ideas:**
- Evolution of Buffett's public image over five decades
- His philosophy as expressed in interviews and profiles
- The consistency of his core principles despite changing market conditions
- His relationship with the press and public

## 3. Books About Buffett's Philosophy

### "The Intelligent Investor" by Benjamin Graham (1949)

Buffett wrote the updated foreword for the 1972 edition (revised 2003).

**Buffett's Foreword Key Points:**
> "Investment is most intelligent when it is most businesslike... The investor's chief problem — and even his worst enemy — is likely to be himself."

**Why It Matters:** Graham was Buffett's teacher at Columbia Business School. Buffett calls this "the best book on investing ever written."

**Original:** [amazon.com](https://www.amazon.com/Intelligent-Investor-Definitive-Investing-Essentials/dp/0060555661)

---

### "Security Analysis" by Benjamin Graham & David Dodd (1934)

Buffett studied this book at Columbia. The 1940 edition has a special place in his approach. This is the foundational text of value investing.

**Link:** [amazon.com](https://www.amazon.com/Security-Analysis-Foreword-Warren-Buffett/dp/0071592531)

---

### "The Warren Buffett Way" by Robert G. Hagstrom (1994)

The first comprehensive book explaining Buffett's investment methodology. Essential reading for understanding the synthesis of Graham's value investing with Buffett's quality focus.

**Link:** [amazon.com](https://www.amazon.com/Warren-Buffett-Way/dp/0471585790)

---

### "Warren Buffett's Letter to Shareholders" — Annual Compilation Book

**Publisher:** Lawrence Cunningham  
Published annually since 2001  
**Link:** [cunningham.substack.com](https://cunningham.substack.com)

Contains the full unedited text of every Buffett letter to shareholders.

## 4. Public Speeches & Testimonies

### Florida University Business School Speech (October 15, 1998)

**Link:** [berkshirehathaway.com/lectures](https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/lectures)

**Summary:** Widely considered the best public speech Buffett has ever given. Known as "The Superinvestors of Graham-and-Doddsville" lecture.

**Core Ideas:**
- "The Superinvestors of Graham-and-Doddsville" — presents the argument that value investing outperforms because of behavior, not just analysis
- The difference between price and value
- Why investors need an "inner scoreboard" rather than seeking external validation
- On risk: "The risk of being wrong is when you don't know the facts"

**Key Quotes from the Speech:**
> "You don't need to be an expert in every company or every industry. You only need to be able to recognize a few situations where you have an edge."

> "The most important investment you can make is in yourself."

> "The difference between someone who knows a lot and someone who doesn't is often the ability to distinguish what they know from what they don't know."

---

### Congressional Testimony (1991)

Buffett testified before the Senate Banking Committee on the savings and loan crisis. He was called as an expert witness to explain the failure of his own savings and loan investment.

**Key Point:**
> "I was wrong... the whole premise of my investment was flawed."

This demonstrates Buffett's willingness to publicly acknowledge mistakes.

## 5. Key Speeches Referenced by Buffett

### "What Makes a Good CEO?" — Fortune CEO Conference

Buffett has repeatedly given a version of this talk emphasizing that CEOs should:
1. Run the business as if they own 100% of it
2. Be honest with shareholders
3. Focus on long-term value, not earnings management
