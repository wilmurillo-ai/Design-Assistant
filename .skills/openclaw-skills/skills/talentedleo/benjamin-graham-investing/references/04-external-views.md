# Benjamin Graham - External Views

## Overview

Benjamin Graham is widely regarded as the father of value investing, but his approach has both admirers and critics. This document compiles external perspectives on Graham's methodology.

---

## Admirers and Followers

### Warren Buffett
**The Greatest Admirer and Student**

Buffett studied under Graham at Columbia Business School and worked at Graham's partnership before starting Berkshire Hathaway.

**Key Admiration Points:**
- Called Graham "the second most influential person in my life, after my father"
- The Intelligent Investor is "the best book on investing ever written"
- Adopted and adapted Graham's margin of safety concept
- Eventually moved beyond Graham's "cigar butt" investing to "wonderful businesses at fair prices"

**Buffett's Refinement:**
> "We paying much more attention to qualitative factors... Graham's approach worked fine when markets were inefficient. That's changed."

---

## Critics and Skeptics

### 1. Market Efficiency Critics

**Argument:** In modern markets, Graham's quantitative screens find fewer opportunities.

**Counterpoints:**
- Markets still become irrational (2008 Financial Crisis, 2000 Dot-com Bubble)
- Quantitative approaches have evolved but Graham's principles remain valid
- New tools (AI, big data) still use Graham's foundational concepts

### 2. Growth Investing Critics

**Argument:** Graham's approach misses high-growth companies like Amazon, Apple, Google.

**Counterpoints:**
- Graham specifically said defensive investors should focus on large, stable companies
- Growth investing requires different skills and risk tolerance
- Many Graham followers have still achieved excellent returns

### 3. Complexity Critics

**Argument:** Graham's approach is too mechanical and ignores qualitative factors.

**Counterpoints:**
- Graham acknowledged qualitative factors in later editions
- The "cigar butt" approach was just one part of his strategy
- Modern value investing combines quantitative and qualitative analysis

## Comparisons with Other Approaches

### Graham vs. Modern Portfolio Theory

| Aspect | Graham | MPT |
|--------|--------|-----|
| Focus | Intrinsic value | Market beta |
| Risk Definition | Permanent loss of capital | Volatility |
| Return Expectation | Margin of safety | Market return |
| Diversification | Defensive focus | Broad diversification |

### Graham vs. Technical Analysis

| Aspect | Graham | Technical |
|--------|--------|----------|
| Price Source | Secondary | Primary |
| View of Market | Service to use | Signal to follow |
| Time Horizon | Long-term | Short-term |
| Analysis Type | Fundamental | Price patterns |

## Modern Adaptations

### Quantitative Value (Towers Watson, O'Shaughnessy)
- Systematic screens based on Graham's criteria
- Book value, earnings yield, dividend yield
- Automated rebalancing

### Quality + Value (Cliff Asness, AQR)
- Combines Graham's value metrics with quality factors
- Addresses "value trap" problem
- More sophisticated than pure quantitative

### Warren Buffett's Evolution
- Started with pure Graham approach ("cigar butts")
- Evolved to "wonderful businesses at fair prices"
- Incorporated moat analysis from Phil Fisher
- Still acknowledges Graham as foundation

## Legacy and Impact

### On Financial Education
- Columbia Business School's value investing program
- MBA programs worldwide teach Graham's principles
- CFA Institute incorporates value investing in curriculum

### On Investment Industry
- Created the securities analysis profession
- Influenced institutional investing
- Founded the concept of "intelligent investor"

### On Individual Investors
- Democratized sophisticated investment analysis
- Emphasized long-term, rational approach
- Provided frameworks for evaluating risk

---

## Controversies and Criticisms

### Main Criticisms

1. **"Graham's approach only works in inefficient markets"**
   - Counter: Markets still have irrational periods
   
2. **"It produces too many 'value traps'"
   - Counter: Margin of safety protects against permanent loss
   
3. **"Misses the big winners"
   - Counter: Graham targeted consistent gains, not home runs

4. **"Too conservative for long-term wealth building"
   - Counter: Graham's approach has preserved capital through multiple crashes
