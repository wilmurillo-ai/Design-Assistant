# Benjamin Graham - Investment Decisions and Cases

## Overview

While Graham is best known for his theoretical contributions, he also managed money through partnerships. Here are documented investment decisions and cases.

---

## 1. GEICO Insurance - The Famous Investment

### Background
Graham discovered GEICO in 1948 through his student and friend, and made it one of the most famous investments in history.

### Key Facts:
- **Year Discovered:** 1948
- **Initial Investment:** ~$1 million for 50% stake
- **Why Graham Bought:** The company was trading below its net current asset value
- **Warren Buffett's Role:** Later became Graham's student and worked at Graham's partnership, later took over GEICO

### The Story:
Graham noticed GEICO was selling for less than its working capital. He investigated and found that the company had a unique business model with significant upside potential.

**Quote from Graham:**
> "The purchase was made at the suggestion of a student and friend who was familiar with the insurance business and who believed that the stock was underpriced."

### Outcome:
- Graham's partnership made approximately $500,000 profit
- Buffett later made GEICO a core holding of Berkshire Hathaway
- The investment became a case study in value investing

## 2. Northern Pipeline (Northern Natural Gas)

### Background
One of Graham's documented investments in Security Analysis.

### Key Facts:
- **Purchase Price:** Below intrinsic value
- **Holding Period:** Multiple years
- **Strategy:** Classic Graham approach - bought below intrinsic value and waited

### Graham's Analysis:
He looked at the company's assets, earnings power, and dividend yield to determine intrinsic value.

## 3. The "Cigar Butt" Approach

Graham's early approach involved finding cheap companies with "one more puff left in them."

### Characteristics:
- Companies trading below net current asset value
- Turnaround situations
- Distressed businesses
- Little fundamental quality required

### Example Characteristics:
- P/E ratio below 10
- Price below 2/3 of book value
- Dividend yield above 5%
- Working capital > long-term debt

---

## 4. Defensive Investor Screen (From The Intelligent Investor)

Graham provided specific criteria for defensive investors:

### Required Criteria:
1. **Size:** At least $100 million in annual revenue
2. **Financial Strength:** Current ratio > 2
3. **No Debt:** Long-term debt < book value
4. **Earnings Stability:** Positive earnings in each of past 10 years
5. **Dividend History:** Continuous dividends for past 20 years
6. **Earnings Growth:** At least 1/3 growth in EPS (10-year period)

### Why These Criteria:
- Large enough to survive economic downturns
- Financially strong to weather recessions
- Conservative to minimize risk
- Consistent to provide reliability
- Growing to maintain purchasing power

## 5. Graham's Partnership Performance

### Facts:
- Graham managed money through investment partnerships from 1936-1956
- Average annual return of approximately 20%
- Beat the Dow Jones in almost every year

### Key Characteristics of His Approach:
- Conservative, value-oriented investments
- Margin of safety on every purchase
- Diversification (typically 15-30 positions)
- Long holding periods

---

## 6. Notable Investment Principles Applied

### From His Documented Decisions:

**1. Margin of Safety Always**
Graham never invested without a margin of safety. The difference between purchase price and intrinsic value provided protection against errors in analysis.

**2. Diversification for Defensive Investors**
Graham believed defensive investors should own 10-30 stocks to reduce risk of permanent capital loss.

**3. Long Holding Period**
Once a stock reached intrinsic value, Graham would sell. But he was patient - he expected to hold for years if necessary.

**4. Separate Investment from Speculation**
Graham kept his investment capital separate from speculative capital and never mixed the two.
