## Investment Philosophy: Margin of Safety

**Core Foundation:** Burry's entire approach is built on one principle:

> "All my stock picking is 100% based on the concept of a margin of safety."
> — Michael Burry, multiple sources

**Source:** Benjamin Graham & David Dodd, *Security Analysis* (1934) — the textbook Burry returns to constantly.

**What this means in practice:**
- Only buy when price is significantly below intrinsic value
- Margin of safety absorbs calculation errors, bad luck, or market irrationality
- Burry: "The purpose of the margin of safety is to render unnecessary an accurate estimate of the future."
- Unlike classic Graham, Burry applies it to growth stocks too — but only when the price discount is extreme

#### Post 2: "The Cardinal Sign of a Bubble: Supply-Side Gluttony"

**Core thesis — AI is the new dot-com bubble:**

> "A key issue with the dot-com bubble was catastrophically overbuilt supply and nowhere near enough demand. And once again there is a Cisco at the center of it all, with the picks and shovels for all and the expansive vision to go with it. Its name is Nvidia."

**The "Five Horsemen" of today's AI boom:**
- Microsoft, Google (Alphabet), Meta, Amazon, Oracle
- Plus "several adolescent startups" including OpenAI

**Key argument:** 25 years ago, the Nasdaq was driven by "highly profitable large caps" including Microsoft, Intel, Dell, Cisco — yet the bubble still burst because of oversupply.

**Current AI capex:** Big tech has pledged to invest over $1 trillion in AI infrastructure (chips, data centers) over the next few years.

**His short position:** Burry's Scion held bearish put options on Nvidia and Palantir with a combined notional value of **$1.1 billion** as of September 30, 2025.

**Palantir feud:**
- Palantir CEO Alex Karp called Burry's puts "batshit crazy"
- Burry responded: "Karp couldn't crack a 13F"
- Scion exited Palantir puts in October 2025 before the stock dropped 26%

> "Supply-side gluttony" — his term for the cardinal sign of a bubble

> "The market regularly overreacts to both good and bad news. This creates mispricing — opportunities for those willing to be against consensus."

> Burry has noted that his neurology training affects how he thinks about markets — pattern recognition, understanding systems, and not being swayed by emotion.

### On Short Selling

> "Being short a bubble is like being long a value stock — you need patience, and the market can stay irrational longer than you can stay solvent."

From the NY Times op-ed: He believed by 2003-2004 that subprime mortgages and their bonds would begin losing value when original "teaser" rates reset to higher rates — often within 2 years of initiation.

## Vanity Fair: "Betting on the Blind Side" (March 2010)

**Source:** Vanity Fair — March 2010 (excerpt from *The Big Short* by Michael Lewis)  
**Link:** https://www.vanityfair.com/news/2010/04/wall-street-excerpt-201004  
**Author:** Michael Lewis

### On Discovering Subprime (2004)

> "In early 2004 a 32-year-old stock-market investor and hedge-fund manager, Michael Burry, immersed himself for the first time in the bond market. He learned all he could about how money got borrowed and lent in America. He didn't talk to anyone about what became his new obsession; he just sat alone in his office, in San Jose, California, and read books and articles and financial filings."

**His method:** Reading actual mortgage bond prospectuses — 130 pages each, from 10kWizard.com for $100/year.

**Key insight on lenders:**
> "What you want to watch are the lenders, not the borrowers. The borrowers will always be willing to take a great deal for themselves. It's up to the lenders to show restraint, and when they lose it, watch out."

### On "The Extension of Credit by Instrument"

In his quarterly letters, Burry coined this phrase to describe what he saw:

> "It was a clear sign that lenders had lost it, constantly degrading their own standards to grow loan volumes."

### On How He Found CDS

> "Credit-default swaps remedied the problem of open-ended risk for me. If I bought a credit-default swap, my downside was defined and certain, and the upside was many multiples of it."

**The insight:** He realized Wall Street would eventually create CDS on subprime bonds — and he needed to get in before that market materialized.

### On His First Silicon Investor Post (1996)

From the Vanity Fair article:

> "Late one night in November 1996, while on a cardiology rotation at Saint Thomas Hospital, in Nashville, Tennessee, he logged on to a hospital computer and went to a message board called techstocks.com. There he created a thread called 'value investing.'"

### On Why He Became a Value Investor

> "The late 90s almost forced me to identify myself as a value investor, because I thought what everybody else was doing was insane."

**On Buffett:**
> "If you are going to be a great investor, you have to fit the style to who you are. Warren Buffett, though he had every advantage in learning from Ben Graham, did not copy Ben Graham, but rather set out on his own path, and ran money his way, by his own rules."

### On His Disability and Personality

> "Before he was two years old he was diagnosed with a rare form of cancer, and the operation to remove the tumor had cost him his left eye."

> "His glass eye, he assumed, was the reason that face-to-face interaction with other people almost always ended badly for him. He found it maddeningly difficult to read people's nonverbal signals."

> "I don't really know how to stop it, so people just keep moving left until they're standing way to my left, and I'm trying not to turn my head anymore. I end up facing right and looking left with my good eye, through my nose."

### On Obsessiveness

> "His mind had no temperate zone: he was either possessed by a subject or not interested in it at all."

> "Even as a small child he had a fantastic ability to focus and learn, with or without teachers."

### On Why He Left Medicine

> "The actual practice of medicine, on the other hand, either bored or disgusted him."

> "One scene with people carrying legs over their shoulders to the sink to wash out the feces just turned my stomach, and I was done."

### On Friendship and Marriage

> "My nature is not to have friends. I'm happy in my own head."

On Match.com profile: "a medical resident with only one eye, an awkward social manner, and $145,000 in student loans."
