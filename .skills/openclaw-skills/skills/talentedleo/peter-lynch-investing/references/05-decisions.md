# Peter Lynch - Investment Decisions and Cases

## Overview

Peter Lynch is famous for his stock-picking success. His documented investment cases provide insight into his decision-making process.

---

## Famous Investment Cases

### 1. Chrysler (1980s)

**Situation:**
- Company near bankruptcy
- Stock price: $10
- Everyone expected bankruptcy

**Lynch's Decision:**
- Saw management turnaround efforts
- Understood the story
- Bought heavily despite criticism

**Outcome:**
- Stock rose significantly
- Turnaround succeeded

**Lesson:**
> "How could you recommend Chrysler? Don't you know they're going bankrupt?" - Relatives

> "Sometimes you have to be willing to look foolish."

---

### 2. Taco Bell (Non-First-Hand)

**Situation:**
- Small restaurant chain
- Professional investors ignored it
- Lynch noticed it in his neighborhood

**Lynch's Decision:**
- Researched the business model
- Saw growth potential
- Invested early

**Outcome:**
- Company grew into major chain
- Significant gains

**Lesson:**
> "Look for companies in your own backyard."

---

### 3. Hanes (Non-First-Hand)

**Situation:**
- Lynch noticed his wife bought Hanes pantyhose
- Product was popular
- Stock was ignored by professionals

**Lynch's Decision:**
- Used personal observation
- Researched the company
- Invested based on consumer insight

**Outcome:**
- Major winner

**Lesson:**
> "Invest in what you know."

---

### 4. Flying Tiger (First Ten-Bagger)

**Situation:**
- Lynch's first stock purchase in college
- Air freight industry analysis
- Vietnam War boosted the stock

**Lynch's Decision:**
- Researched air freight sector
- Bought $1,000 worth
- Stock rose 9-10x

**Outcome:**
> "I had my first ten-bagger. I sold all the way up to 80 and helped pay for graduate school."

**Lesson:**
> "You need only a few ten-baggers in your lifetime."

---

### 5. McDonald's (Long-Term Winner)

**Situation:**
- Some said McDonald's growth was over
- Lynch disagreed

**Lynch's View (2025):**
> "People said 'McDonald's is done.' They just simply didn't think it through. Sometimes you don't have to be in the first inning."

**Lesson:**
> "Great companies can grow longer than you think."

## Investment Criteria

### Lynch's Checklist

1. **Understand the Business**
   - Can you explain it simply?
   - Do you know how it makes money?

2. **Check the Story**
   - Is there a compelling narrative?
   - Can the company execute?

3. **Financial Health**
   - Debt levels reasonable?
   - Cash flow positive?

4. **Valuation**
   - Is it fairly priced?
   - PEG ratio under 1?

5. **Category Fit**
   - Which of the six types?
   - What are your expectations?

## Stock Categories and Strategies

### 1. Fast Growers

**Characteristics:** 20%+ growth, P/E 20-80

**Strategy:** Buy early, hold through growth

**Examples:** Tech startups in expansion phase

### 2. Stalwarts

**Characteristics:** 3-5% growth, mature companies

**Strategy:** Steady holding, benefit from dividends

**Examples:** Coca-Cola, Johnson & Johnson

### 3. Slow Growers

**Characteristics:** 1-2% growth, mature industries

**Strategy:** Income through dividends

**Examples:** Utilities, railroads

### 4. Asset Plays

**Characteristics:** Hidden asset value

**Strategy:** Buy when market recognizes value

**Examples:** Companies with valuable real estate

### 5. Cyclicals

**Characteristics:** Economy-dependent

**Strategy:** Time entry and exit

**Examples:** Auto manufacturers, steel

### 6. Turnarounds

**Characteristics:** Distress, recovery potential

**Strategy:** Buy when turnaround begins

**Examples:** Chrysler 1980s

## Decision-Making Framework

### Before Buying

1. **Understand the story** - Can you tell it in one sentence?
2. **Check fundamentals** - Debt, cash flow, margins
3. **Know the category** - What type of stock is this?
4. **Set expectations** - What return are you looking for?

### After Buying

1. **Monitor the story** - Has anything changed?
2. **Check fundamentals** - Quarterly results
3. **Rebalance if needed** - Don't over-concentrate
4. **Hold winners** - Let them run

### When to Sell

1. **Story breaks** - Company strategy fails
2. **Fundamentals deteriorate** - Debt increases, margins shrink
3. **Better opportunity** - Found a better stock
4. **Category shift** - Company changes type

### When NOT to Sell

1. **Price drops** - Unless story changed
2. **Short-term volatility** - Accept as normal
3. **Market panic** - Unless you need money
4. **Boredom** - Don't sell a good stock because you're bored

> "The person who turns over the most rocks wins."

> "Buy companies, not stocks."

> "Never invest more than 5% in any single stock."

## Portfolio Construction

### Lynch's Approach

1. **Diversification:** 10-30 stocks across categories
2. **Position Sizing:** Max 5% per stock
3. **Rebalancing:** Annual review
4. ** winners:** Let them run 3-10 years

### Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Selling winners too early**
2. **Holding losers too long**
3. **Ignoring the story**
4. **Over-concentrating**
5. **Trying to time markets**
