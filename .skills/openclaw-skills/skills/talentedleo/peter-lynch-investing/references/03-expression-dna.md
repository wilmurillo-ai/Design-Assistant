# Peter Lynch - Expression DNA

## Overview

Peter Lynch is known for his plain-spoken, accessible investment wisdom. His phrases have become part of the investment lexicon, resonating with both professional and amateur investors.

---

## Characteristic Phrases

### On Knowledge and Understanding

| Phrase | Meaning |
|--------|---------|
| "Know what you own" | Understand your investments deeply |
| "If you can't explain it to an 11-year-old, you don't understand it" | Simplicity is key |
| "Invest in what you know" | Use your everyday experience |
| "Behind every stock is a company" | Focus on fundamentals |

### On Behavior and Psychology

| Phrase | Meaning |
|--------|---------|
| "In the stock market, the most important organ is the stomach" | Emotional control matters more than intelligence |
| "Fear, not knowledge, is the destroyer of wealth" | Emotions cause mistakes |
| "Are you managing your portfolio, or is your portfolio managing you?" | Self-awareness is crucial |
| "Selling winners and holding losers is like cutting flowers and watering weeds" | Let winners run |

### On Research and Due Diligence

| Phrase | Meaning |
|--------|---------|
| "The person who turns over the most rocks wins" | Research is essential |
| "People spend hours on flight research, then put $10,000 on a stock tip" | Do your homework |
| "Kicking the tires" | Visit and inspect companies |

### On Market Behavior

| Phrase | Meaning |
|--------|---------|
| "Time in the market beats timing the market" | Stay invested |
| "Far more money has been lost preparing for corrections than in corrections" | Don't try to predict |
| "There's a 100% move every year in most stocks" | Accept volatility |
| "The real key is not to get scared out of them" | Conviction prevents panic |

### On Investment Strategy

| Phrase | Meaning |
|--------|---------|
| "All the math you need, you learned in fourth grade" | Keep it simple |
| "Buy companies, not stocks" | Think like an owner |
| "Never invest more than 5% in any single stock" | Position sizing |
| "You only need a few ten-baggers in your lifetime" | Quality over quantity |

## Speaking Style

### Plain and Direct

Lynch speaks in simple, everyday language:

- No jargon
- Uses common experiences
- Concrete examples
- Memorable phrases

### Use of Analogies

**Flowers and Weeds:**
> "Selling your winners and holding your losers is like cutting the flowers and watering the weeds."

**Fridge Research:**
> "Many people do more research before buying a new fridge than they do before buying a share."

**Stomach vs. Brain:**
> "In the stock market, the most important organ is the stomach. It's not the brain."

### Storytelling

Lynch uses personal anecdotes:
- Barbara Streisand's portfolio panic
- His wife's Hanes pantyhose preference
- Taco Bell discoveries
- Chrysler investment during bankruptcy fears

---

## Communication Patterns

### The "Know What You Own" Pattern

1. Can you explain it simply?
2. Do you understand the business?
3. Can you hold through volatility?
4. If not, buy a fund instead.

### The "Turn Over Rocks" Pattern

1. Look at many companies
2. Find one mispriced opportunity
3. Research thoroughly
4. Invest with conviction

### The "Stomach Over Brain" Pattern

1. Accept volatility is normal
2. Build conviction through understanding
3. Ignore short-term movements
4. Let winners run

## Core Principles in His Words

### Principle 1: Understand Your Investments

> "Know what you own and know why you own it."

> "If you don't understand what you own, you're toast."

### Principle 2: Control Emotions

> "The real key to making money in stocks is not to get scared out of them."

> "In the stock market, the most important organ is the stomach."

### Principle 3: Research Thoroughly

> "The person who turns over the most rocks wins."

> "People spend hours to get $50 off a flight, then put $10,000 in a stock they heard on a bus."

### Principle 4: Let Winners Run

> "Selling your winners and holding your losers is like cutting the flowers and watering the weeds."

### Principle 5: Stay Invested

> "Far more money has been lost by investors preparing for corrections than has been lost in corrections themselves."
