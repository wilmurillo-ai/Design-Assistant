## Common Stocks and Uncommon Profits (1958)

**Published:** 1958 (revised 1960)  
**Link:** https://www.amazon.com/Common-Stocks-Uncommon-Profits-Written/dp/0471648699  
**Recognition:** Called "a very, very good book" by Warren Buffett in 2018 Berkshire Hathaway annual meeting

### Core Thesis

> *"The best time to sell a stock is almost never."*

Fisher believed in buying **great companies at reasonable prices** and holding them for decades. His approach focused on identifying companies with superior growth potential and owning them through all market cycles.

### Key Distinction: Fisher vs. Graham

| Graham Approach | Fisher Approach |
|----------------|----------------|
| Buy cheap (cigar butt) companies | Buy great companies at fair prices |
| Quantitative (price vs. book value) | Qualitative (management, R&D, growth) |
| Diversified | Concentrated, few positions |
| Sell when fairly valued | Hold almost forever |

### Investment Performance

Fisher & Co. operated from 1931 to 1999 — a remarkable 68-year track record managing a small number of clients.

| Period | Return | Notes |
|--------|--------|-------|
| 1931-1999 | Extraordinary | One of the longest documented investment records |
| Specific returns | Not publicly disclosed | Fisher was extremely private about performance data |

> Note: Unlike modern hedge funds, Fisher did not publish detailed performance figures. The track record is known primarily through client testimonials and biographical accounts.

---

### The Synthesis

John Train famously described Warren Buffett as **"85% Benjamin Graham + 15% Philip Fisher"** — and by 2018, Buffett acknowledged his approach had shifted even more toward Fisher's quality focus, largely due to Charlie Munger's influence.

## The Scuttlebutt Method

**Definition:** The practice of gathering information about a company by talking to competitors, customers, suppliers, ex-employees, and industry experts — the "grapevine" approach to due diligence.

### How It Works

1. **Talk to competitors** — What do rivals say about this company?
2. **Talk to customers** — Are they satisfied or looking to switch?
3. **Talk to suppliers** — Is the company a reliable partner?
4. **Talk to ex-employees** — What's it really like inside?
5. **Talk to industry analysts** — What do the experts think?

### Why It Matters

> *"When you scuttlebutt, you make more informed decisions due to a better basis for analysis and valuation."*

Fisher believed that by gathering multiple independent perspectives, you could see through management's PR and get to the truth about a company's competitive position.

### Buffett's Endorsement

At the 2018 Berkshire Hathaway annual meeting, Buffett said:

> *"Using Fisher's scuttlebutt technique continues to be a good way of investing"* — and noted that Ted Weschler and Todd Combs still use this approach at Berkshire.

## Fisher's 15 Points for Evaluating a Company

In *Common Stocks and Uncommon Profits*, Fisher outlined **15 key questions** to ask when evaluating a growth company. These are organized into three categories:

### Category 1: Company Quality

| # | Point | Question |
|---|-------|----------|
| 1 | **Product margin** | Does the company have processes that can maintain above-average profit margins? |
| 2 | **Pricing power** | What is the company's pricing flexibility? Can it raise prices without losing customers? |
| 3 | **Service quality** | Does the company have strong customer service and product support? |
| 4 | **Sales organization** | Is the sales organization effective and growing? |

### Category 2: Growth Potential

| # | Point | Question |
|---|-------|----------|
| 5 | **R&D investment** | Is the company investing sufficiently in R&D? |
| 6 | **Product development** | Does the company have a track record of successful new products? |
| 7 | **Future growth areas** | Are the company's growth areas aligned with future market trends? |
| 8 | **Competitive advantage** | Does the company have a durable competitive moat? |

### Category 3: Management Quality

| # | Point | Question |
|---|-------|----------|
| 9 | **Manager competence** | Is management intellectually honest about problems? |
| 10 | **Depth of management** | Does the company have succession planning and depth? |
| 11 | **Employee relations** | Does management treat employees fairly? |
| 12 | **Executive compensation** | Is compensation aligned with shareholder interests? |
| 13 | **Capital allocation** | Does management allocate capital wisely? |
| 14 | **Insider ownership** | Do insiders own significant equity? |
| 15 | **Legal/regulatory issues** | Are there any potential legal or regulatory problems? |

> *Note: These 15 points are synthesized from Fisher's writings and secondary sources. Primary source is Common Stocks and Uncommon Profits (1958, revised 1960).*

## Growth at Reasonable Price (GARP)

Fisher's core contribution was bridging Graham's value approach with growth investing:

> *"I had made what I believe was one of the more valuable decisions of my business life. This was to confine all efforts solely to making major gains in the long-run."*

### The GARP Framework

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Great company** | Superior business with competitive moat |
| **Reasonable price** | Not too expensive, but not cheap either |
| **Long holding period** | Years or decades, not months |
| **Concentrated** | Few positions, deeply understood |

### Fisher on Diversification

> *"I don't want a lot of good investments; I want a few outstanding ones."*

> *"Usually a very long list of securities is not a sign of the brilliant investor, but of one who is unsure of himself."*

## Fisher's Investment Philosophy

### Core Principles

1. **Buy and hold** — "The time to sell is almost never"
2. **Quality over price** — Great business at fair price beats cheap business
3. **Scuttlebutt due diligence** — Talk to everyone connected to the company
4. **Concentrated positions** — Few stocks, deeply understood
5. **Long-term thinking** — Let compounding work over decades

### The Contrarian Element

> *"The wise investor can profit if he can think independently of the crowd and reach the rich answer when the majority of financial opinion is leaning the other way."*

### On Mistakes

> *"More money has probably been lost by investors holding a stock they really did not want until they could 'at least come out even' than from any other single reason."*

### On Future Earnings

> *"It is not the profit margins of the past but those of the future that are basically important to the investor."*

## Paths to Wealth through Common Stocks (1960)

**Published:** 1960  
**Link:** https://www.amazon.com/Paths-Wealth-Through-Common-Stocks/dp/0136747886

### Core Message

Follow-up to *Common Stocks and Uncommon Profits*, this book focused on practical wealth-building through stock market investment. Key themes:

- Compound interest as the investor's best friend
- The importance of patience
- Why most investors should use dollar-cost averaging
- Staying invested through market downturns

---

## Developing an Investment Philosophy (1980)

**Published:** 1980  
**Link:** Available through Financial Analysts Research Foundation  

### Core Message

This monograph was Fisher's most academic work — a formal framework for how investors should think about developing their own investment philosophy. Key themes:

- Why having a coherent philosophy matters more than specific techniques
- The dangers of not having a consistent approach
- How to develop conviction through research and experience
- The role of temperament vs. intelligence in investing

> *"The investor who has developed a sound investment philosophy and has the character to act consistently on it has a great advantage over the investor who jumps from technique to technique."*

---

## Conservative Investors Sleep Well (1975)

**Published:** 1975  
**Link:** https://www.amazon.com/Conservative-Investors-Sleep-Well/dp/0471252764

### Core Message

A defense of the growth investing approach against critics who called it too risky. Fisher argued:

- Growth stocks actually provide better downside protection than believed
- The volatility of growth stocks is misunderstood
- Long-term compounding in great businesses beats short-term trading

## Selected Quotes & Principles

### On Holding Period

> "If the job has been correctly done when a common stock is purchased, the time to sell it is almost never."

> "Usually, the big money isn't made in the buying or the selling, but in the waiting."

### On Quality

> "I don't want a lot of good investments; I want a few outstanding ones."

> "The wise investor can profit if he can think independently of the crowd."

### On Research

> "The successful investor is usually an individual who is inherently interested in business problems."

> "The stock market is filled with individuals who know the price of everything, but the value of nothing."

### On Mistakes

> "More money has probably been lost by investors holding a stock they really did not want until they could 'at least come out even' than from any other single reason."

### On Future

> "It is not the profit margins of the past but those of the future that are basically important to the investor."

### On Diversification

> "Investors have been so oversold on diversification that fear of having too many eggs in one basket has caused them to put far too little into companies they thoroughly know and far too much in others which they know nothing about."
