## Phil Fisher's Rare Public Appearances

### The Private Investor

Phil Fisher was famously private. He:
- Gave very few interviews throughout his career
- Was highly selective about which clients he took
- Remained unknown to the public until his first book in 1958
- Never sought public attention

### His Teaching at Stanford

Despite his private nature, Fisher taught at Stanford Graduate School of Business — and was one of only **three people ever** to teach the investment course there.

## Buffett on Fisher

### The Famous Quote

John Train described Warren Buffett's investment approach as:

> **"85% Benjamin Graham + 15% Philip Fisher"**

By 2018, Buffett himself acknowledged his approach had shifted more toward Fisher's influence, particularly due to Charlie Munger's counsel.

### 2018 Berkshire Hathaway Annual Meeting

At the 2018 Berkshire Hathaway annual meeting, Buffett praised Fisher:

> *"Common Stocks and Uncommon Profits is a very, very good book."*

He also noted that Fisher's scuttlebutt technique is still used at Berkshire:

> *"Using Fisher's scuttlebutt technique continues to be a good way of investing"* — and specifically mentioned that **Ted Weschler and Todd Combs** (Berkshire's portfolio managers) continue to use this approach.

### The 85% Graham + 15% Fisher

John Train first described Buffett's approach as "85% Benjamin Graham and 15% Philip Fisher." Buffett later acknowledged this had shifted:

> *"Charlie Munger pushed me in the direction of Fisher... I was 85% Graham and 15% Fisher in those days, but now I'm maybe 70% Graham and 30% Fisher."*

This shift was most visible in the **See's Candy acquisition (1972)** — the first time Berkshire paid a premium for a quality business rather than hunting for bargains. Pure Fisher.

### On Motorola

Buffett also commented on Fisher's Motorola hold:

> *"Phil bought Motorola in 1955 and held it until he died. That's what conviction looks like."*

### The Munger Connection

Charlie Munger pushed Buffett toward Fisher's quality-focused approach. The investment in **See's Candy** was an early example where Berkshire paid up for a quality business — pure Fisher.

## Fisher on His Own Philosophy

### On Why He Wrote Common Stocks

From his book, Fisher explained why he wrote publicly:

- He wanted to share what he had learned
- He believed the information could help serious investors
- He remained very selective about clients even after the book

### On His Investment Approach

From *Common Stocks and Uncommon Profits*:

> *"The best time to sell a stock is almost never."*

> *"Usually, the big money isn't made in the buying or the selling, but in the waiting."*

### On Finding Great Companies

From his writings, Fisher emphasized:

- Finding great companies takes hard work
- They cannot be found every day
- The investor must be willing to do the research

## Kenneth Fisher on His Father

### Son of Phil Fisher

**Kenneth Fisher** founded **Fisher Investments** in 1979 (the same year Berkshire hired Munger).

### What Kenneth Learned from His Father

Kenneth has spoken about growing up with Phil Fisher:

- His father's investment discipline was deeply ingrained
- The scuttlebutt approach was part of daily life
- Long-term thinking was fundamental to the family approach

### Fisher Investments Continues the Legacy

Kenneth built Fisher Investments into a major investment firm, applying many of his father's principles:
- Long-term thinking
- Focus on quality businesses
- Client-first mentality

## How Fisher's Ideas Spread

### Through Buffett

The biggest amplifier of Fisher's ideas was **Warren Buffett**. By praising Fisher's book at Berkshire meetings and crediting him as a major influence, Buffett ensured Fisher's ideas became part of the value investing canon.

### Through Charlie Munger

Munger pushed Buffett toward the Fisher quality approach. The synthesis of Graham (cheap) + Fisher (quality) + Munger's psychology = the modern Berkshire Hathaway approach.

### Through Academic Finance

Fisher's work influenced the development of:
- Growth investing as a formal category
- GARP (Growth at Reasonable Price) strategies
- Qualitative due diligence frameworks

## Direct Quotes from Phil Fisher

### From Common Stocks and Uncommon Profits

> *"There are two fundamental approaches to investment. There's the approach Ben Graham pioneered — find something intrinsically so cheap that there's little chance of it having a big decline. Then there's my approach — find something so good that if you don't pay too much for it, it will have very, very large growth."*

> *"If the job has been correctly done when a common stock is purchased, the time to sell it is almost never."*

> *"The big money isn't made in the buying or the selling, but in the waiting."*

> *"More money has probably been lost by investors holding a stock they really did not want until they could 'at least come out even' than from any other single reason."*

> *"The successful investor is usually an individual who is inherently interested in business problems."*

### On Finding Opportunities

> *"Great stocks are extremely hard to find. If they weren't, then everyone would own them. The record is crystal clear that fortune-producing growth stocks can be found. However, they cannot be found without hard work and they cannot be found every day."*

> *"Focus on buying these companies when they are out of favor — when either because of general market conditions or because the financial community at the moment has misconceptions of its true worth, the stock is selling at prices well under what it will be when it's true merit is better understood."*

## Notable Interview Themes

| Theme | Fisher's Position |
|-------|-----------------|
| **Buy and hold** | "The time to sell is almost never" |
| **Quality over price** | Great business at fair price |
| **Scuttlebutt** | Talk to competitors, customers, suppliers |
| **Concentration** | Few stocks, deeply understood |
| **Patience** | Big money in the waiting |
| **Diversification** | Over-sold; know something investors should concentrate |
