# 05-Decisions

> Major investment decisions and strategic choices that defined Phil Fisher's career.

## Iconic Decisions

### 1. Motorola — The 49-Year Hold (1955–2004)

| Detail | Value |
|--------|-------|
| **Bought** | 1955 |
| **Held** | Until his death in 2004 |
| **Duration** | ~49 years |
| **Company** | Started as radio manufacturer, evolved into telecommunications |

**Why it mattered:** Fisher bought Motorola in 1955 when it was still primarily a radio manufacturer. He held it through the entire evolution of the telecommunications industry — and never sold. This is the ultimate demonstration of his "buy and hold forever" philosophy.

---

### 2. Founding Fisher & Co. (1931)

| Detail | Value |
|--------|-------|
| **Founded** | 1931 |
| **Duration** | Managed until 1999 (68 years) |
| **Client approach** | Very selective; few clients, long relationships |

**Why it mattered:** Fisher built one of the most exclusive investment relationships in history. He managed money for a small number of clients for nearly seven decades, creating extraordinary aligned incentives.

---

### 3. Common Stocks and Uncommon Profits (1958)

| Detail | Value |
|--------|-------|
| **Published** | 1958 |
| **Impact** | Created the growth investing category |
| **Recognition** | Made Fisher famous; changed how investors thought about growth |

**Why it mattered:** Fisher had been running Fisher & Co. privately for 27 years before publishing his method. The book changed his status from private money manager to investment legend.

## Other Notable Investments

Beyond Motorola, Fisher was known for owning a very small number of positions. Some of his known investment themes:

### Technology and Growth Companies

Fisher specialized in innovative companies driven by research and development — decades before "Silicon Valley" became a known term. His focus on R&D-driven companies was unusual for his era.

### The Concentration Approach

Because Fisher concentrated so heavily, his known positions were few:
- **Motorola** — the most famous, held 49 years
- **Other growth companies** — names not publicly documented due to his extreme privacy

> *"I don't want a lot of good investments; I want a few outstanding ones."*

### Why So Few Positions?

With only a small number of clients and a research-intensive approach, Fisher could only deeply cover a handful of companies at a time. This forced concentration — and the compounding in those few positions was extraordinary.

## Notable Investment Principles

### On Selling

Fisher famously said: **"The time to sell is almost never."**

His approach was to buy great companies and hold them forever — or until the thesis fundamentally changed.

### On Diversification

> "I don't want a lot of good investments; I want a few outstanding ones."

### On Scuttlebutt Due Diligence

The scuttlebutt method was his primary research tool:
- Talk to competitors
- Talk to customers
- Talk to suppliers
- Talk to ex-employees

## Strategic Business Decisions

### 1. Client Selection

Fisher was extremely selective about clients. He took on very few and maintained long-term relationships — some lasting decades.

### 2. Private Practice

Unlike most money managers, Fisher never sought to scale or market himself. He maintained a small, exclusive practice.
