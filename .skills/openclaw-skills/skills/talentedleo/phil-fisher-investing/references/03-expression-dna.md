# 03-Expression-DNA

> The core character traits, personality, and psychological DNA that define Phil Fisher's approach to investing.

## Core Psychological Profile

| Trait | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Private** | Very selective about clients; rarely gave interviews |
| **Patient** | "The big money is in the waiting" — decades-long holding periods |
| **Research-intensive** | Deep due diligence via scuttlebutt method |
| **Long-term thinker** | Believed compound interest over decades is the game |
| **Quality-focused** | Great businesses at reasonable prices, not cheap businesses |
| **Independent** | Went against consensus when conviction was high |

## Personality Type

- **Scholar-investor** — More academic than most practitioners
- **Teacher** — One of only three people ever to teach Stanford's investment course
- **Private practitioner** — Never sought fame; let results speak
- **Patient capital allocator** — Would wait years for right opportunity

## Decision-Making Style

### Quality Before Price

Fisher would rather buy a great company at a fair price than a fair company at a cheap price. This was his key distinction from Graham.

### The Scuttlebutt Approach

> "When you scuttlebutt, you make more informed decisions due to a better basis for analysis and valuation."

### Patience as Competitive Advantage

> "Usually, the big money isn't made in the buying or the selling, but in the waiting."

## Psychological Strengths

1. **Extraordinary patience** — Held Motorola from 1955 to death (49 years)
2. **Intellectual honesty** — Would admit when he was wrong
3. **Research rigor** — Deep scuttlebutt before buying
4. **Independence** — Willing to hold when consensus was wrong

## Psychological Weaknesses (Acknowledged)

1. **Very private** — Hard for clients to understand his thinking
2. **Few positions** — Concentration amplifies both wins and losses
3. **Long holding period** — Requires extraordinary patience from clients

## Quote on Character

> "The wise investor can profit if he can think independently of the crowd and reach the rich answer when the majority of financial opinion is leaning the other way."
