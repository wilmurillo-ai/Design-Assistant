# Post-Filing Learnings — Real-World Incorporation Experience

_Lessons from filing OpenSource Media Corp (Nevada C-Corp, March 2026)_

## Filing Process

### SilverFlume (Nevada Online Filing)
- **URL:** https://esos.nv.gov/EntitySearch/OnlineEntitySearch (search) / SilverFlume portal (filing)
- **Process:** Account creation → Entity name search → Articles + Initial List → Payment → Confirmation
- **⚠️ Server errors at commit step are possible.** Payment may charge even if the system shows an error. Check your dashboard/email for confirmation before re-filing. Look for a Confirmation # and Job #.
- **Expedite options:** 24-hour ($500), 2-day ($250), standard (2-3 weeks). Most startups pick 24-hour.

### Registered Agent
- **Must be in place BEFORE filing.** SilverFlume asks for RA info during the Articles step.
- **Certificate of Acceptance:** Some RAs provide this automatically; others require you to request it. Have it ready.
- **Cost:** ~$100-150/year for a commercial RA (Northwest, Incorp, etc.)

## Post-Filing Steps (Learned the Hard Way)

### EIN (Employer Identification Number)
- **Online application (IRS Form SS-4):** Instant — but requires the applicant to have a US SSN or ITIN.
- **Non-US founders:** Cannot apply online. Must delegate to a US-based officer/director with SSN, or file by mail/fax (Form SS-4, 4-6 weeks).
- **Tip:** Identify who will apply for EIN BEFORE you file Articles. Don't discover the SSN requirement after the fact.

### 83(b) Elections — THE 30-DAY CLOCK
- **What:** IRS election to be taxed on stock value at grant (par value = ~$0) rather than at vesting. Critical for founders receiving stock for services.
- **Deadline:** 30 days from stock issuance. HARD DEADLINE. NO extensions.
- **Who files:** Each individual stockholder receiving shares for services. The company doesn't file it.
- **How:** Mail to IRS (certified mail with return receipt), keep copy for personal records, attach copy to personal tax return.
- **⚠️ Start this process the day stock is issued.** Don't wait.

### Stock Issuance
- **Restricted Stock Purchase Agreements (RSPAs):** Should be signed by each shareholder. Template not included in this skill — consult legal.
- **Board resolution authorizing issuance:** Included in the Organizational Resolutions template.
- **Vesting:** If using vesting (4yr/1yr cliff is standard), document it in the RSPA, not in the stock ledger or organizational resolutions.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Filing Articles before RA is confirmed.** SilverFlume will reject or stall.
2. **Not budgeting for expedite fees.** Nobody wants to wait 2-3 weeks. Budget $500+ for 24-hour.
3. **Assuming all founders can get EIN.** Non-US = manual process = weeks of delay.
4. **Missing the 83(b) window.** Set calendar reminders for day 1, day 15, and day 25 after stock issuance.
5. **Treating stock ledger as final before RSPAs are signed.** The ledger is a record, not a contract. RSPAs are the binding agreements.
6. **Not opening a bank account quickly.** Need EIN + Articles + Bylaws + Board Resolution. Some banks want Operating Agreement even for C-Corps (they're confused — push back).

## State-Specific Nevada Notes

- **No state income tax.** One of the main reasons to incorporate here.
- **Annual List of Officers:** Due within 60 days of anniversary of filing. $150/year.
- **Business License:** Required annually. ~$200.
- **Franchise tax:** Nevada has NO franchise tax (unlike Delaware's complex calculation).
- **Privacy:** Officers listed on Initial List are public record. Use registered agent address for principal office if privacy matters.

## Cost Summary: DIY vs. Lawyer

| Approach | Cost | Time |
|----------|------|------|
| **This skill (DIY)** | ~$525-750 (filing fees only) | 1-2 days |
| **Online service (LegalZoom, etc.)** | $1,000-2,500 | 1-2 weeks |
| **Startup lawyer** | $3,000-10,000+ | 2-4 weeks |

The skill generates the same documents a $5K lawyer would produce. The difference: a lawyer reviews your specific situation and catches edge cases. For straightforward formations, DIY works. For complex cap tables, multiple preferred series, or investor involvement — get a lawyer.
