# Evaluation Frameworks Reference

How to select and apply scoring frameworks in Phase 4. The principle: **one framework per round, applied consistently to all images.**

---

## Social Perception (Round 2) — Framework Selection

### Process

1. Search for current trends (AI avatars, digital identity, virtual persona aesthetics)
2. Read 3–5 sources, identify the dominant pattern
3. Distill into **one thesis statement**
4. Score all images from that thesis

### Example Theses (pick or formulate your own)

- "The 2026 trend has moved past stylized avatar aesthetics toward identity-driven representation — what matters is whether the avatar conveys who the agent is, not whether it follows a visual trend."
- "Platform thumbnail optimization is the primary constraint — at 40px, only high-contrast, front-facing, centered compositions survive."
- "Trust and transparency are the dominant design priorities — AI avatars should signal non-human origin while maintaining emotional warmth."

### Good vs. Bad Evaluation

**Good** (one through-line):
> Thesis: "Identity-driven representation matters more than stylistic trends."
> - #1 (8): Strong identity signal — the direct gaze and defined features immediately convey a specific personality, not a generic archetype.
> - #2 (7): The side-lit composition adds mystery but reduces identity clarity at small sizes — you sense a mood more than a person.
> - #3 (5): Too abstract to convey identity — at thumbnail size, this could be anyone or anything emerging from darkness.

**Bad** (cherry-picked sources):
> - #1 (8): Canva says "imperfect by design" is trending, and the painterly style fits.
> - #2 (7): Microsoft says thumbnails need clarity, and the side angle reduces that.
> - #3 (8): Branding Journal says "less is more" for avatars, so the minimalism works.

The bad version uses a different justification source for each image, making scores incomparable.

## Aesthetic Quality (Round 3) — Recommended Frameworks

Select ONE. List its criteria. Apply to all images in the same order.

### Option A: Digital Art Competition Standard (e.g., Stanford Digital Arts)

| Criterion | What to evaluate |
|-----------|-----------------|
| Composition | Visual balance, focal point placement, use of negative space |
| Color & Light | Palette harmony, intentional temperature shifts, value range |
| Technical Execution | Edge control (hard/soft/lost), detail hierarchy, rendering consistency |
| Emotional Impact | Does it evoke the intended feeling? How immediately? |
| Creative Risk | Does it attempt something beyond the obvious? |

### Option B: Portrait Painting Tradition (Sargent/Zorn/Loomis)

| Criterion | What to evaluate |
|-----------|-----------------|
| Value Structure | Clear light/shadow hierarchy, readable at a squint |
| Color Temperature | Warm lights vs. cool shadows (or intentional inversion) |
| Edge Hierarchy | Hard edges on focal points, soft/lost edges on periphery |
| Economy of Means | Maximum effect from minimum strokes — nothing wasted |
| Likeness & Presence | Does the portrait feel like a specific individual, not a type? |

### Option C: UX Avatar Scorecard

| Criterion | What to evaluate |
|-----------|-----------------|
| Readability | Recognizable at 32px, 64px, 128px |
| Emotional Clarity | Mood readable within 1 second |
| Distinctiveness | Stands out among 20 similar-sized avatars |
| Palette Consistency | Matches the stated color definition |
| Dark/Light Adaptability | Works on both dark and light backgrounds |

---

## General Rules

1. **State your framework before any scoring.** If you can't name it, you don't have one.
2. **3–5 criteria maximum.** More criteria dilutes judgment.
3. **Same criteria, same order, every image.** This is what makes scores comparable.
4. **Justify deductions with specifics.** Not "composition is weak" but "the face is offset 70% left, leaving dead space that doesn't serve the concept."
5. **Don't retroactively change your framework.** If you realize mid-scoring that your framework isn't working, restart the round — don't patch it.
