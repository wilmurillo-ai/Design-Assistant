# Color Harmony Quick Reference

## Common palette types

### Monochromatic
- One hue with value and saturation variation.
- Best for calm, controlled, premium, or product-heavy interfaces.
- Risk: can feel flat without a strong accent.

### Complementary
- Two hues opposite on the color wheel.
- Best for strong contrast, campaigns, sports, and CTA emphasis.
- Risk: becomes noisy if both colors are used at equal weight.

### Analogous
- Neighboring hues on the color wheel.
- Best for natural, smooth, atmospheric, or immersive visuals.
- Risk: may lack hierarchy without a contrast accent.

### Split complementary
- One base hue plus the two hues adjacent to its complement.
- Best for balanced contrast without the harshness of pure complements.

### Triadic
- Three evenly spaced hues.
- Best for playful, editorial, kids, and creative systems.
- Risk: over-saturation and visual clutter.

### Tetradic
- Two complementary pairs.
- Best for rich systems and complex storytelling.
- Risk: hard to balance; choose one dominant color.

## 60/30/10 rule
- 60% dominant color: background, large surfaces, overall tone
- 30% support color: secondary surfaces or supporting visual blocks
- 10% accent color: CTA, highlights, key information

## Mood associations
- Blue: trust, calm, competence
- Green: health, growth, nature
- Red: urgency, passion, danger
- Yellow: energy, optimism, caution
- Orange: warmth, friendliness, momentum
- Purple: imagination, luxury, mystery
- Black: authority, elegance, contrast
- White: simplicity, cleanliness, space
- Gray: restraint, utility, neutrality
- Pink: softness, intimacy, playfulness
- Brown: earthiness, heritage, craft

## Accessibility reminders
- For normal text, aim for at least 4.5:1 contrast.
- For large text, 3:1 may be acceptable.
- Do not rely on color alone to convey meaning.
- Safer chart pairings often include blue/orange or blue/brown.
- Avoid red/green dependency for critical states.

## Practical warnings
- Highly saturated palettes fatigue users quickly.
- Pure black on pure white is readable but can feel harsh at scale.
- Data visualizations need distinction before beauty.
- Screen color and print color will diverge; treat CMYK as approximate unless professionally managed.
