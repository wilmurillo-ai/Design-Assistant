# Opportunity Cost — Reference Guide

What to stop, what to delay, what to double down on. The discipline of understanding
that every yes to something is a no to something else.

---

## 1. THE OPPORTUNITY COST REALITY

Every hour spent on Initiative B is an hour not spent on Initiative A.
Every dollar spent on Tool X is a dollar not spent on Marketing Y.
Every week spent on Prayful is a week not spent on GND.

Opportunity cost is invisible but real. It's the biggest source of strategic waste
in founder-led companies — not bad decisions, but failing to notice the trade-offs.

---

## 2. THE OPPORTUNITY COST FRAMEWORK

### Calculating Opportunity Cost
```
For any initiative, ask:
  "If we spend [X hours/week] on this, what are we NOT doing?"
  "Is what we're NOT doing more valuable than what we ARE doing?"

EXAMPLE:
  Decision: Spend 20 hours building a new course (Teachable platform)
  Opportunity cost: 20 hours NOT spent on GND outreach
  
  Course revenue: $X in 6+ months (uncertain)
  GND outreach revenue: $99/mo/client × [1-2 clients from 20 hours] = $99-198/mo MRR
  
  Verdict: GND outreach has higher near-term ROI. Build course later.
```

## 3. THE STOP LIST

### What to Consider Stopping
```
STOP WHEN:
  - 3+ weeks of effort with no measurable progress
  - Underlying assumption proven false
  - Better opportunity identified that requires same resources
  - Quarterly OKR now irrelevant due to changed context
  - Execution keeps stalling for the same reasons

PAUSE WHEN:
  - Good idea but wrong timing (need revenue before investing in it)
  - Dependent on something that's not ready yet
  - Low priority vs. current top initiatives

DOUBLE DOWN WHEN:
  - Early signals of success
  - Faster progress than expected
  - Market response is positive
  - This is the constraint that's blocking other goals
```

### The Kill Decision Framework
```
Before killing an initiative, answer:

1. Has it had a fair trial? (Minimum: 4 weeks of consistent effort)
   NO → Give it a fair trial first
   YES → Continue to question 2

2. Is the fundamental assumption still valid?
   NO → Kill it (assumption was wrong)
   YES → Continue to question 3

3. Is the execution failing, or is the strategy failing?
   EXECUTION → Fix execution or change approach (don't kill)
   STRATEGY → Kill or fundamentally pivot

4. Is the time better spent elsewhere?
   YES, clearly → Kill or park
   MAYBE → Continue 2 more weeks with specific success metric
```

## 4. THE DOUBLING DOWN FRAMEWORK

### When to Double Down
Signs you should invest MORE in something:
```
POSITIVE SIGNALS:
  - Any sale on a new product in first 2 weeks → launch more marketing
  - A cold email gets a reply → double down on that template
  - A Gumroad product starts selling without promotion → list on more platforms
  - A specific audience converts better → focus all outreach on that audience
  
PRINCIPLE: Success signals should increase allocation, not maintain it.
  Most founders maintain effort on everything equally.
  Winners recognize signals and double the winning bet.
```
