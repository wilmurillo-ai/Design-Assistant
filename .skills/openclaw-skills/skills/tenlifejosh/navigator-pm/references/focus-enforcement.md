# Focus Enforcement — Reference Guide

Protecting the company from shiny object syndrome, commitment rules,
and the discipline of saying no to good ideas.

---

## 1. THE FOCUS PROBLEM

The biggest strategic threat to a solo founder is not lack of good ideas.
It's too many good ideas executed badly because attention is divided.

A founder who commits fully to one thing for 90 days accomplishes more
than a founder who pursues three things for a year.

---

## 2. THE COMMITMENT RULES

### Rules for Focus at TLC
```
RULE 1: Maximum 3 active workstreams simultaneously
  Active = you are currently building/executing on it
  If a 4th "must start now" → one of the 3 pauses

RULE 2: Complete before starting
  A product is complete when it's live and generating feedback, not just "done"
  Don't start the next product until the previous one has been shipped and measured

RULE 3: The 7-Day Test
  Any new idea: don't act for 7 days
  After 7 days, still important? → Score and schedule
  Forgot about it? → Wasn't actually important

RULE 4: Say no with a date
  "Not now" > "No forever"
  "We'll look at this in Q3" closes the loop without killing an idea
  Gets it out of active consideration without losing it

RULE 5: One platform at a time
  Don't expand to Etsy while Gumroad is underoptimized
  Don't start KDP paperbacks while Gumroad ebooks aren't selling
  Optimize what you have before adding more surface area
```

---

## 3. FOCUS METRICS

### Signs Focus Is Breaking Down
```
WARNING SIGNS (act when you see 2+ of these):
  □ Three or more "top priorities" in the same week
  □ New project started without completing previous one
  □ "Research phase" lasting more than 5 business days
  □ Weekly priorities change without completion week-over-week
  □ Half-built projects not launched by end of their planned sprint
  □ Conversations about future projects taking up > 30% of planning time
```

## 4. THE FOCUS ENFORCEMENT CONVERSATION

### When to Have the Focus Conversation with Hutch
When drift signals accumulate, Navigator surfaces the focus conversation:

```
"Hey — I want to flag something.

Looking at the last three weeks:
  - [Initiative A] is at 40% with no significant progress since [date]
  - [Initiative B] was started last week while A isn't done
  - [Initiative C] is being discussed as a new priority

I think we're spreading thin. Here's what I'd recommend:

Focus option 1: Finish A before anything else. [Timeline estimate]
Focus option 2: Formally park A, double down on B. [Trade-offs]

What's your call?"
```

This is the Navigator's job: surface the focus conflict, present options, get a decision.
Not: ignore it and let drift continue.
Not: make the decision unilaterally without Hutch.
