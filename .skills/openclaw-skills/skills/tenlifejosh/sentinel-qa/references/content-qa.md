## 1. CONTENT QA PHILOSOPHY

### What We're Actually Checking
Content QA is NOT proofreading. Proofreading finds typos. Content QA answers five deeper questions:

1. **Is it TRUE?** — Every factual claim is accurate and defensible
2. **Is it CLEAR?** — A reader can understand it without prior context
3. **Is it ON-BRAND?** — Sounds like us, not like a robot or a corporate press release
4. **Is it SAFE?** — Won't cause legal, reputational, or relationship damage
5. **Does it WORK?** — Accomplishes its intended purpose (sells, informs, connects)

### The Harm Hierarchy
Not all content failures are equal. Rate by severity:

| Level | Type | Example | Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 — Fatal | Legal liability | "Guaranteed to cure..." | REJECT — escalate |
| 2 — Severe | Factual error | Wrong stat or date | REJECT — fix immediately |
| 3 — Significant | Brand damage | Off-tone, embarrassing | REJECT — revise |
| 4 — Moderate | Clarity issue | Confusing, misleading | REVISION NEEDED |
| 5 — Minor | Style/preference | Could be better | Flag but can ship |

## 2. THE CONTENT REVIEW PROTOCOL

### Phase 1: Read Without Judging
Read the piece straight through as a target reader would. Don't stop to fix anything.
After one read, answer:
- What is this trying to accomplish?
- Did it accomplish that for me as a reader?
- What felt off?

### Phase 2: Fact Check
For every factual claim, verify or flag:
- Statistics: Is this number real? Is it current? Is it from a credible source?
- Dates/times: Are they accurate?
- Product claims: Does this product actually do this?
- Third-party references: Is the company/person described accurately?

### Phase 3: Brand Check
Does this sound like Ten Life Creatives? Apply the brand voice filter:
- Direct, not corporate
- Authentic, not performative
- Builder energy, not salesy
- Faith-aware when appropriate, not preachy

### Phase 4: Risk Pass
Is there anything here that could:
- Be misquoted out of context?
- Offend or alienate part of the audience?
- Make a legally actionable promise?
- Reveal information that should be private?

### Phase 5: Technical Check
- Links work?
- Formatting renders correctly on target platform?
- Media loads correctly?
- Character/word limits respected?

## 3. COPY QUALITY STANDARDS

### The Good Copy Test
Strong copy has these qualities:

**Specificity > Vagueness**
```
WEAK: "Our product helps you get organized"
STRONG: "Organize your entire family's schedule, chores, and finances in one 47-page system"

WEAK: "Improve your productivity"
STRONG: "Eliminate the 3 hours you spend redoing work because you lost track of what needs to happen"
```

**Concrete > Abstract**
```
WEAK: "Achieve financial freedom"
STRONG: "Know exactly where every dollar goes — before the month starts"

WEAK: "Build a deeper faith"
STRONG: "Spend 10 focused minutes in prayer every morning, with specific prompts that match your season"
```

**Active > Passive**
```
WEAK: "Your stress can be reduced by using our system"
STRONG: "Eliminate the Sunday-night dread before the week starts"
```

### Copy Fail Patterns to Catch

**The Vague Opener**
"In today's fast-paced world..." / "If you're like most people..." / "We all know that..."
→ Flag: Delete opener, start with the reader's specific pain

**The Feature List (Without Benefits)**
"This guide includes: chapters, exercises, worksheets, examples..."
→ Flag: Add benefit after each feature ("chapters that walk you through..." not just "chapters")

**The Buried CTA**
CTA is at the end, after 500 words of explaining. Reader never gets there.
→ Flag: Move CTA higher, add second CTA near end

**The Hedge**
"This product may help you possibly achieve some improvement in..."
→ Flag: Remove hedging language. Make specific, honest claims with confidence.

**The Wall of Text**
Paragraphs longer than 5 lines.
→ Flag: Break up with subheadings, bullets, white space

**The Robot Voice** (Highest priority to catch)
"It is worth noting that..." / "Delve into..." / "Furthermore..." / "In conclusion..."
→ Flag: Rewrite in natural, direct language. No AI-obvious phrases.

## 4. EMAIL-SPECIFIC QA

### Email Quality Checklist
```
SUBJECT LINE:
- [ ] Under 50 characters (displays fully on mobile)
- [ ] Creates curiosity or states specific benefit
- [ ] Not misleading or clickbait (what it promises, it delivers)
- [ ] Not all caps or excessive punctuation (spam triggers)
- [ ] No spam trigger words: "FREE!!!", "Buy now", "Limited time!!!"
- [ ] Preview text complements subject (not redundant)

OPENING:
- [ ] First line earns continued reading
- [ ] Not: "Hi! I hope this email finds you well..."
- [ ] Not: "I'm reaching out because..."
- [ ] Should be: direct, specific, relevant to the reader

BODY:
- [ ] One clear purpose per email (not three asks)
- [ ] Short paragraphs (3-4 lines max)
- [ ] Scannable (subheadings, bullets where appropriate)
- [ ] No jargon or inside language
- [ ] All personalization tokens have fallbacks: {{first_name | there}}

CTA:
- [ ] One primary CTA (not three different links)
- [ ] CTA text is specific ("Download your guide" not "Click here")
- [ ] CTA link tested and working
- [ ] CTA button looks like a button (stands out from body)

FOOTER:
- [ ] Unsubscribe link present and functional
- [ ] Physical mailing address present (CAN-SPAM requirement)
- [ ] Privacy policy link (if collecting data)
- [ ] Brand name/contact info

DELIVERABILITY:
- [ ] HTML:text ratio not too image-heavy (60:40 rule)
- [ ] Images have alt text
- [ ] No URL shorteners in body (flag as spam)
- [ ] Sender domain authenticated (SPF/DKIM — check with platform)
```

### Cold Email QA (Outbound)
```
PERSONALIZATION:
- [ ] Contains at least one specific personal detail (not just name)
- [ ] References their business/work specifically
- [ ] Doesn't feel like a mass email

OPENING LINE:
- [ ] Not a compliment ("I love your work on...")
- [ ] Not a request right away
- [ ] Earns interest in 1-2 sentences

ASK:
- [ ] One ask per email (not a paragraph of asks)
- [ ] Ask is small and easy to say yes to
- [ ] Not asking for 30-minute calls as first touch

LENGTH:
- [ ] Under 150 words for cold outreach
- [ ] Easily readable in 30 seconds
- [ ] No walls of text

CREDIBILITY:
- [ ] Relevant proof (not bragging, relevant)
- [ ] Social proof where natural
- [ ] No generic claims ("We're the best at...")
```

## 5. SOCIAL MEDIA CONTENT QA

### Platform-Specific Quality Checks

**Twitter/X:**
```
- [ ] Under 280 characters (or thread structure is clear)
- [ ] Hook in first line (stops the scroll)
- [ ] Doesn't start with "I" (algorithm suppression)
- [ ] If thread: value in every tweet (not padding)
- [ ] Links are working (don't auto-shorten)
- [ ] No more than 2 hashtags
- [ ] Image is 16:9 or 1:1 (correct aspect ratio)
```

**LinkedIn:**
```
- [ ] Opening line creates pattern interrupt (shows without clicking)
- [ ] Professional but not corporate
- [ ] Tells a story or gives insight (not just announcing)
- [ ] Ends with a specific question or CTA
- [ ] No more than 5 hashtags
- [ ] Image is 1.91:1 or 1:1
- [ ] Doesn't feel like a sales pitch
```

**Instagram:**
```
- [ ] Caption is compelling even if image isn't seen
- [ ] First 125 characters show before "more" (lead with strongest line)
- [ ] Hashtag count appropriate (10-20 for regular posts)
- [ ] Call to action in bio link text
- [ ] Image ratio is 4:5 for feed posts
```

### Social Content Quality Rubric

**Score each social post (1-5 per criterion):**

| Criterion | 1 | 3 | 5 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Scroll-stopping hook | Generic opener | Mildly interesting | Pattern interrupt |
| Clarity | Confusing | Somewhat clear | Crystal clear meaning |
| Value delivery | No value | Some value | Genuine insight/help |
| Brand consistency | Feels off | Mostly on brand | Perfect brand fit |
| CTA/next step | None | Vague | Clear, compelling |

**Minimum score to publish:** 3.0 average
**Ideal:** 4.0+ average

## 6. AI CONTENT DETECTION

### The AI Voice Patterns (Must Catch and Rewrite)

These phrases are dead giveaways of AI-generated content. Eliminate on sight:

**Structural tells:**
- "It is worth noting that..."
- "Furthermore, it is important to..."
- "In conclusion, ..."
- "In today's fast-paced world..."
- "This is a testament to..."
- "One cannot overstate the importance of..."
- "It goes without saying..."
- "With that being said..."
- "When it comes to..."
- "That being said..."

**Vocabulary tells:**
- "Delve into"
- "Holistic approach"
- "Leveraging" (as a verb)
- "Plethora"
- "Multifaceted"
- "Nuanced" (used generically)
- "Tapestry" (metaphorically)
- "Navigate" (used metaphorically)
- "Underscore" (as verb meaning "emphasize")
- "Paramount"

**Structural patterns:**
- Three-point lists for EVERY paragraph
- Transitions that announce transitions: "Now, let's turn to..."
- Conclusion paragraph that summarizes everything just said
- Opening paragraph that says what you're about to say

**The Fix:**
Rewrite AI content using:
- Short, direct sentences
- First-person perspective where authentic
- Concrete specifics instead of general claims
- Josh's actual voice (see josh-voice.md in scribe-docs skill)

## 7. FACTUAL ACCURACY CHECKS

### Claims That Need Verification
```
STATISTICAL CLAIMS:
"X% of people do Y" → Verify source, year, relevance
"Studies show..." → Which studies? Find them.
"Most people..." → Is this actually true or a writing convenience?
"The average [person/business/result]..." → Verify this average

HISTORICAL/DATE CLAIMS:
Any specific year, date, or event → verify
"Recently..." → When? Specifics matter

PRODUCT CLAIMS:
"Gets results in X days" → Is this provable?
"Trusted by X customers" → Real number?
"Works for [specific audience]" → Is this proven?

LEGAL CLAIMS:
"Guaranteed to..." → Are we legally guaranteeing this?
"Approved by..." → Approved by whom, exactly?
"As seen in [publication]" → Is this accurate?
```

### Verification Protocol
For any factual claim in customer-facing content:
1. Can you find this in a credible source right now?
2. Is the source current (within 3 years for stats)?
3. Does the stat mean what the copy implies it means?
4. If challenged, could we produce the source?

If any answer is NO → remove the claim or qualify it appropriately.

## 8. READABILITY STANDARDS

### Readability Targets by Content Type

| Content Type | Target Grade Level | Ideal Sentence Length |
|---|---|---|
| Social media | Grade 6-8 | < 15 words |
| Marketing copy | Grade 6-8 | < 20 words |
| Product descriptions | Grade 8-10 | < 20 words |
| Long-form articles | Grade 8-10 | < 25 words |
| Technical docs | Grade 10-12 | < 30 words |

**Checking Readability:**
- Use Hemingway App (hemingwayapp.com)
- Target: Grade 8 or lower for general content
- Cut any sentence Hemingway marks "hard to read" in red
- Rewrite any sentence Hemingway marks "very hard to read"

## 9. THE CONTENT SCORING RUBRIC

### Complete Content Score (0-100)

**Accuracy (20 points)**
- 20: All claims verified
- 15: Minor unverified claims (but plausible)
- 10: One questionable claim
- 0: False or misleading claims present

**Clarity (20 points)**
- 20: Reader immediately understands purpose and value
- 15: Clear but slightly abstract in places
- 10: Requires re-reading to understand
- 0: Confusing or contradictory

**Brand Voice (20 points)**
- 20: Unmistakably us — authentic, direct, real
- 15: Mostly on brand with a few off-notes
- 10: Generic — could be anyone
- 0: AI-obvious or completely off-brand

**Risk (20 points)**
- 20: Zero risk flags
- 15: Minor risks (vague claims)
- 10: One moderate risk present
- 0: Legal or severe reputational risk

**Effectiveness (20 points)**
- 20: Compelling — achieves its purpose strongly
- 15: Adequate — achieves its purpose
- 10: Weak — misses the mark
- 0: Counter-productive — actively hurts

**Score Thresholds:**
- 85-100: APPROVED
- 70-84: REVISION NEEDED (specific feedback)
- Below 70: REJECTED (fundamental issues)
