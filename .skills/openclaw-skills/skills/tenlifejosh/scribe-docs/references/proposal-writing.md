# Proposal Writing — Reference Guide

Client proposals, pitch documents, and business cases. The format and frameworks
for proposals that win GND clients and business relationships.

---

## 1. THE PROPOSAL PHILOSOPHY

A proposal is not a resume. It is not a price sheet. It is not a capabilities deck.
A proposal is a document that makes the client think: "This person understands my problem."

The proposal that wins is the one that:
1. Proves you understand their situation specifically
2. Shows clearly what you'll do and what they'll get
3. Makes the investment feel obvious relative to the value

## 3. PROPOSAL WRITING PRINCIPLES

### The Specificity Rule
Every proposal must have at least one thing in it that proves you looked at their specific business.
"Your reviews mention [specific thing]" or "I noticed your site [specific observation]."

Without specificity → it's a template. Templates don't win contracts.

### The Value Ladder
State the value before the price:
```
"Your website is costing you calls from people who find it and bounce
because it looks outdated. A $99/month investment in a professional site
is worth one new customer per month — for most local service businesses,
that's a 5-10x return."
```

### The Friction Removal Rule
Make saying yes as easy as possible:
- One CTA
- No complicated decision required
- Tell them exactly what happens after they say yes
- Remove uncertainty about the process

---

## 4. PROPOSAL QUALITY CHECKLIST

```
- [ ] Is the "Your Situation" section specific to THEM? (Not generic)
- [ ] Does it prove you understand their actual problem?
- [ ] Is what you'll deliver specific and clear?
- [ ] Is the price stated clearly? (No "starting from" ambiguity)
- [ ] Is the process explained clearly?
- [ ] Is the next step obvious and easy?
- [ ] Is it 1 page or close to it? (Shorter wins more often than longer)
- [ ] Is it written in confident, direct language?
```

---

## 5. FOLLOW-UP TIMING

```
Day 0: Send proposal
Day 3: Brief follow-up if no response:
  "Did the proposal land okay? Happy to answer any questions."

Day 7: Second follow-up with value-add:
  "One thing I forgot to mention: [specific observation or addition]"

Day 14: Final follow-up:
  "I have another local project starting soon that will take my bandwidth.
  Before I commit to that — is [Business Name] still interested?"
  (This is honest urgency, not manufactured urgency)
```
