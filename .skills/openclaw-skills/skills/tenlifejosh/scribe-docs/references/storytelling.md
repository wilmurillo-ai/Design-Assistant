# Storytelling — Reference Guide

Narrative arc, tension and payoff, personal story structures, and the mechanics
of stories that create connection, trust, and action.

---

## 1. WHY STORIES SELL

Stories work because:
- They bypass the skeptical mind (we receive stories as experience, not argument)
- They create emotional connection faster than any other format
- They're memorable (facts are forgotten, stories are retold)
- They build trust by showing vulnerability and authenticity
- They demonstrate competence without bragging

The best marketing is a story well told.

## 2. THE THREE-ACT BUSINESS STORY

### Act 1: The Before World
```
Set up the character's world as it was BEFORE the transformation.
Be specific. Be honest. Be relatable.

ELEMENTS:
- Where were they? (Context, situation)
- What was their status quo? (The "normal" that was actually not working)
- What small friction existed that they tolerated?

EXAMPLE:
"For three years, our family ran on group texts, sticky notes, and 
Sunday night anxiety. We weren't failing — we were functioning.
Just barely."
```

### Act 2: The Inciting Incident + Struggle
```
Something happens that forces a change. Then the struggle to figure it out.

ELEMENTS:
- What was the breaking point? (Specific event or accumulation)
- What did they try first? (And why it didn't work — this is important)
- What was the cost of trying and failing?

EXAMPLE:
"Then we missed [specific event]. Both of us thought the other was handling it.
Nobody was. That was the day I decided to actually fix this.

I tried apps. Nobody checked them. Shared calendars. Nobody updated them.
'Family meetings.' Never happened. Every solution required maintenance.
Nobody had time for maintenance."
```

### Act 3: The Transformation
```
The solution and its results. Be specific about what changed.

ELEMENTS:
- What was the actual insight or solution?
- What specifically changed?
- What does life look like now (the after state)?

EXAMPLE:
"Eventually I made a system that requires zero maintenance. Paper. 
Fill it in Sunday morning. Refer to it all week. That's it.
No app. No syncing. No notifications we ignore.

Our family actually knows what's happening each week now.
That sounds like a small thing. It isn't."
```

## 3. THE FOUNDER ORIGIN STORY

### Structure for Josh's Business Stories
```
THE PROBLEM BEFORE I SOLVED IT:
  [Specific situation, honest about the struggle]

WHAT I TRIED (AND WHY IT FAILED):
  [Shows credibility through trying — not a silver bullet solution]

THE THING THAT ACTUALLY WORKED:
  [The insight or solution, specific]

WHAT CHANGED:
  [Concrete, measurable or observable change]

WHY I'M SHARING THIS:
  [Natural bridge to product or recommendation]
```

### Story Length by Platform
```
Twitter: 3-5 tweets. Start in Act 2 (the struggle), resolve in Act 3.
LinkedIn: 200-500 words. Full 3-act structure possible.
Email: 100-200 words of story as opener, then transition to product.
Product description: 3-5 sentences of story setup only.
Video/script: Full story, 2-5 minutes.
```

## 4. TENSION AND PAYOFF

### Creating Narrative Tension
Tension is the gap between where the reader is and where they want to be.
No tension = no reason to keep reading.

```
WAYS TO CREATE TENSION:
  - State a problem before solving it (don't give the answer immediately)
  - Use cliffhanger transitions: "Here's what I found."
  - Reference a result without explaining it immediately: "The answer surprised me."
  - Open with a moment of failure before showing recovery
  - Ask a question the reader genuinely wants answered

EXAMPLE WITH TENSION:
  "I built five systems before one worked.
  Four of them failed in the same way.
  Here's what I got wrong every time — and what fixed it."

SAME STORY WITHOUT TENSION:
  "I've learned a lot about family organization. Here are my best tips."
```

## 5. PERSONAL STORY TYPES FOR TLC

### The Six Story Templates

**Story 1: The Failure Story**
What I tried, why it failed, what I learned
Best for: Building trust, showing authentic journey

**Story 2: The Discovery Story**
The unexpected thing I found when doing something specific
Best for: Sharing insights, building authority

**Story 3: The Before/After Story**
What my situation was, what I changed, what's different now
Best for: Product stories, transformation marketing

**Story 4: The "Nobody Told Me" Story**
A lesson learned the hard way that should be widely known
Best for: Thought leadership, viral content

**Story 5: The Contrast Story**
What everyone does vs. what actually works
Best for: Establishing a point of view, contrarian value

**Story 6: The Loss Story**
Something lost that led to a bigger gain or hard lesson
Best for: Vulnerable moments, faith-adjacent content
