## 1. COLD EMAIL ARCHITECTURE

### The 4-Line Cold Email Framework
```
LINE 1 — THE HOOK: A specific, personalized observation about them
  (Not a compliment. An observation that shows you've looked at their business.)
  
LINE 2 — THE RELEVANCE: Why you're mentioning it
  (One sentence connecting your observation to a specific problem you can solve.)
  
LINE 3 — THE PROOF: One piece of relevant credibility
  (Not your resume — one specific, relevant thing you've done.)
  
LINE 4 — THE ASK: One small, easy-to-say-yes ask
  (Not "30 minutes" — something that takes < 30 seconds on their end.)
```

### Cold Email Formula: GND (Good Neighbor Design)
```
Subject: [Business Name]'s website

[First Name],

I was searching for [service type] businesses in [City/Area] and found you.
Your [specific positive thing — reviews, reputation, how long they've been open].
Your website [specific honest observation — might be outdated, missing key info, etc.].

I build websites for local Colorado service businesses. $99/month, no contracts.
Usually takes 30 days.

Can I send you a quick example of what I'd change?

— Joshua Noreen
Good Neighbor Design
Parker, CO
[Phone number]
```

### What Makes Cold Email Work
```
RESEARCH MINIMUM (before sending):
  - Know their first name
  - Know their business category
  - Visit their actual website
  - Have ONE specific observation (not generic)

ONE PERSONALIZATION RULE:
  At least one line must be specific to THIS person.
  If it could be copy-pasted to 1,000 businesses unchanged → it's not personalized.

THE ASK PRINCIPLE:
  Ask for the smallest possible next step.
  NOT: "Would you like to schedule a 30-minute consultation?"
  YES: "Can I send you what I'd change?" or "Would a quick 5-min call work this week?"
  
  The smaller the ask, the higher the conversion.
  The goal of email 1 is not to close a deal — it's to start a conversation.
```

## 2. THE FOLLOW-UP SEQUENCE

### The 5-Touch Follow-Up
```
TOUCH 1 — Day 0: Initial email (the cold email above)

TOUCH 2 — Day 3: One-line follow-up
  "Hey [Name], following up on this. Still happy to share a quick look at what 
  I'd do differently for [Business Name]."

TOUCH 3 — Day 7: Value-add follow-up
  "I took a few minutes and put together one specific change I'd make to 
  [Business Name]'s site. Here's what I found: [specific observation]
  Happy to show you the full picture if useful."

TOUCH 4 — Day 14: Different angle
  "Different approach: Would it help to see an example of a similar business 
  we redesigned? [Specific business type]."

TOUCH 5 — Day 21: The breakup email
  "[First Name], I don't want to keep interrupting if this isn't relevant.
  I'll stop following up after this one.
  If the website ever becomes a priority — my door's open.
  Good luck with [business name]."

THE BREAKUP EMAIL:
  Paradoxically, this often gets the most replies.
  Final sends permission for them to say "actually, wait..."
```

## 3. TRANSACTIONAL EMAIL TEMPLATES

### Order Confirmation (Gumroad Products)
```
Subject: You're in — here's [Product Name]

[First Name],

Got it. Your order is confirmed.

[Product Name] is ready for download:
→ [Download link]

Your link is good for 30 days. If you need more time or have trouble downloading,
reply to this email and I'll sort it out.

Thanks for the support,
Joshua @ Ten Life Creatives

P.S. If [Product Name] helps, I'd love a quick review on Gumroad. Takes 30 seconds 
and it means a lot to an independent creator.
```

### Welcome Email (New Subscriber)
```
Subject: Welcome — here's what to expect

[First Name],

You're in.

Here's how I use this list:

I send useful stuff when I have useful stuff to send. I don't do weekly newsletters
just to stay in your inbox. So when you hear from me, it's worth reading.

Sometimes it's a new product. Sometimes it's something I learned the hard way.
Sometimes it's a resource I wish I'd had earlier.

If it's not useful, unsubscribe anytime. No hard feelings.

Good to have you,
— Joshua
```

## 4. NEWSLETTER WRITING

### The TLC Newsletter Framework
```
FREQUENCY: Only when there's something worth saying (not weekly by obligation)

STRUCTURE:
  Subject: [Specific hook — not "Newsletter #47"]
  
  Opening line: The most interesting thing you're sharing, stated immediately
  (NOT: "Happy [day]! Hope you're having a great week.")
  
  Body: 
    One thing (150-300 words max per item)
    2-3 items max per newsletter
    Each item: insight or resource or update
    
  CTA: One clear action (if relevant)
  
  Sign-off: Short, personal, no corporate warmth

GOOD NEWSLETTER SUBJECTS:
  "The mistake I see every local business make with their website"
  "Something I just released (and why I built it)"
  "3 things I learned this month"
  "Quick update + a question for you"
```

## 5. SUBJECT LINE FORMULAS

### High-Open Rate Subject Lines
```
CURIOSITY-BASED:
  "Something I've been thinking about"
  "Quick question"
  "The thing nobody tells you about [topic]"
  "This changed how I think about [topic]"

BENEFIT-BASED:
  "How to [specific outcome] in [timeframe]"
  "[Number] ways to [solve specific problem]"
  "[Specific result] without [common objection]"

PROBLEM-BASED:
  "Are you still [outdated approach]?"
  "[Problem] is costing you [specific cost]"
  "The [problem] you might not know you have"

PERSONAL/CONVERSATIONAL:
  "[First name], quick question"
  "Your [specific thing]"
  "RE: [relevant context]"
  "What happened when I tried this"

DIRECT:
  "New: [Product name]"
  "[Product] is live"
  "[Update] to [product]"
```

### Subject Line Rules
```
DO:
  ✓ Under 50 characters (fully visible on mobile)
  ✓ Start with the most interesting word
  ✓ Use specific numbers when relevant
  ✓ Match the tone of your relationship with the reader

DON'T:
  ✗ All caps: "BIG ANNOUNCEMENT"
  ✗ Excessive punctuation: "Amazing news!!!"
  ✗ Generic: "July Newsletter"
  ✗ Misleading: "Re: Our meeting" if there was no meeting
  ✗ Spam words: FREE, ACT NOW, LIMITED TIME (in caps)
```

## 6. PRODUCT LAUNCH EMAIL SEQUENCE

### 5-Email Launch Sequence
```
EMAIL 1 — Day -7 (Announcement/Teaser):
  Subject: "Something I've been building"
  
  Preview: You're building something. Let them know it's coming.
  Include: What problem it solves. Nothing else yet.
  CTA: "I'll share the link when it's live."

EMAIL 2 — Day -3 (The Story):
  Subject: "Why I built [product name]"
  
  Story: Why you needed this. What the problem cost you.
  Include: One specific experience that led to building this.
  CTA: "More on [launch day]."

EMAIL 3 — Day 0 (Launch):
  Subject: "It's live — [product name]"
  
  Direct: It's done. Here's the link. Here's what's in it.
  Include: Price, contents, download link
  CTA: "Download here: [link]"

EMAIL 4 — Day +3 (Follow-Up/Social Proof):
  Subject: "A few things people are saying about [product]"
  
  Include: Early reviews/reactions (real ones)
  Include: Reminder for anyone who missed launch
  CTA: "Still available: [link]"

EMAIL 5 — Day +7 (Last Chance / Extended Close):
  Subject: "Still here (and worth it)"
  
  For: People who opened but haven't bought
  Include: The specific reason to act now
  CTA: "[link] if you're ready"
```

## 7. EMAIL COPY STANDARDS

### The Three Email Tests
```
TEST 1: THE REPLY TEST
  Would a real person reply to this?
  Not every email needs a reply — but it should FEEL like something a real person sent.

TEST 2: THE FORWARD TEST
  Would someone forward this to a friend who'd find it useful?
  If no → the value isn't clear enough.

TEST 3: THE UNSUBSCRIBE TEST
  If this was the only email you ever sent to this list, would they unsubscribe?
  If yes → not enough value.
```

### Length Standards
```
Cold email: 75-150 words max
Follow-up: 30-75 words
Transactional (confirmation): 75-150 words
Newsletter: 200-500 words per item, 2-3 items max
Launch announcement: 200-400 words
```
