# Intelligence Briefing — Reference Guide

How to format research outputs for decision makers. The standard briefing format,
packaging research so founders can decide in minutes, not hours.

---

## 1. THE BRIEFING PHILOSOPHY

A research briefing fails if the decision maker:
- Has to dig for the key finding
- Can't act without asking follow-up questions
- Gets lost in detail before understanding the headline
- Doesn't know what you recommend

A research briefing succeeds if the decision maker can:
- Understand the key finding in 30 seconds
- Make a decision based on it in 2 minutes
- Get deeper if they want (evidence section)
- Know exactly what action you recommend

---

## 2. THE SEIAR FORMAT

Every research briefing uses this structure:

### S — Summary (30-second read)
```
The executive summary. What is the single most important finding?
What do you recommend?

FORMAT:
  FINDING: [One sentence — the key insight]
  RECOMMENDATION: [One sentence — what we should do]
  CONFIDENCE: [High / Medium / Low] — [One phrase explaining why]
```

### E — Evidence (2-minute read)
```
The specific findings that support your summary.
Numbered list. Each item includes source.

FORMAT:
  1. [Finding]: [Specific data point]. Source: [Where this came from]
  2. [Finding]: [Specific data point]. Source: [Where this came from]
  3-7 total evidence items for standard briefing
```

### I — Implications (1-minute read)
```
What does this evidence mean FOR US? Not just what's true in the world,
but what it means for our specific situation and priorities.

FORMAT:
  - If X is true, then we should/shouldn't [action]
  - This changes our [assumption / plan / priority] because [reason]
  - This opportunity is [better / worse / same] than we thought because [reason]
```

### A — Action (30-second read)
```
Specific, concrete next steps. Numbered. Assigned.

FORMAT:
  1. [Specific action] → [Who does it] → [By when]
  2. [Specific action] → [Who does it] → [By when]
  Maximum 3-5 actions. If more are needed, prioritize.
```

### R — Risk & Reliability
```
What could make this analysis wrong? What are we assuming?

FORMAT:
  Confidence in findings: [High/Medium/Low]
  Key assumption that could be wrong: [Specific assumption]
  What would change our recommendation: [Specific condition]
  Next validation step if confidence is low: [Specific action]
```

## 4. BRIEFING QUALITY STANDARDS

### The Five Tests of a Good Briefing

**Test 1: The 30-Second Test**
Can Hutch read the Summary section and know what you found and what he should do?
If not → rewrite the summary.

**Test 2: The Specificity Test**
Are all claims specific with sources?
"Many people want this" → FAIL
"47 Reddit posts about this problem in the last 30 days" → PASS

**Test 3: The "So What" Test**
Does every finding section answer "so what does this mean for us?"
Facts without implications → FAIL

**Test 4: The Action Test**
Could someone execute the recommended actions without asking follow-up questions?
Vague actions → FAIL
"Post in r/Parenting on [date] asking [specific question] to validate [specific assumption]" → PASS

**Test 5: The Confidence Test**
Does the briefing honestly state how confident we are and why?
Overconfident briefings lead to bad decisions.
Underconfident briefings lead to paralysis.
Calibrated briefings lead to good decisions.

---

## 5. BRIEFING ANTI-PATTERNS

### What Makes Briefings Fail

**The Data Dump**: Throwing all research at the decision maker without synthesis.
Fix: Synthesize first. Lead with the headline. Bury the details.

**The Buried Lede**: The most important finding is on page 3.
Fix: Summary section = most important finding + recommendation. Always.

**The Confidence Theater**: Projecting certainty that doesn't exist.
Fix: Label every finding with its confidence level honestly.

**The Answerless Brief**: Research without a recommendation.
Fix: Every brief ends with a recommended action. Always take a position.

**The Context-Free Number**: "$2 billion market!" without context.
Fix: Always explain what the number means for our specific opportunity.
