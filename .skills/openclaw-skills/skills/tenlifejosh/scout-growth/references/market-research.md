## 1. MARKET SIZING FRAMEWORKS

### The Two Approaches

**Top-Down Sizing** (Start with total market, work down)
```
Total market → Segment → Our addressable slice

Example: Digital productivity tools market
Total US digital tools market: $X billion
  × % that are productivity tools: Y%
  = Productivity tools segment: $Z billion
  × % accessible via Gumroad/direct: Q%
  = Our SAM: $W million
```

**Bottom-Up Sizing** (Start with unit economics, build up)
```
Price per unit × Units we could sell = Revenue opportunity

Example: Family organization PDF
Target buyers: Families with school-age children in the US
  US households with school-age children: ~33 million
  × % who actively seek organization tools: ~15%
  = Interest pool: ~5 million households
  × Realistic conversion at $47: ~0.002%
  = Annual sales potential: ~10,000 units
  × $47 = $470,000 annual revenue potential
  (Discount by 80% for reality: ~$94,000 attainable)
```

**Which to Use:**
- Top-down = validates the space is real and large enough
- Bottom-up = validates the specific opportunity for our business
- Always do both — they should roughly converge

## 2. TAM / SAM / SOM CALCULATION

### Definitions
```
TAM (Total Addressable Market):
  The total market if you captured every possible customer globally
  Use for: understanding the space size, investor conversations

SAM (Serviceable Addressable Market):
  The portion of TAM you can realistically reach with your product/model
  Constraints: geography, distribution, price point, platform
  Use for: realistic opportunity sizing

SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market):
  The portion of SAM you can realistically capture given your resources
  Typically 1-5% of SAM for early-stage businesses
  Use for: goal-setting, year-1 targets
```

### Calculation Template
```
Product: [Name]
Price: $[X]

TAM Calculation:
  - Who has this problem? [Description]
  - How many people is that? [Number + source]
  - What do they spend on solutions? [Average per year + source]
  - TAM = [People] × [Average spend] = $[X]

SAM Calculation:
  - Geography constraint: [US only? English-speaking?]
  - Platform constraint: [Can only reach via Gumroad/direct/KDP]
  - Price point constraint: [Who can afford $47?]
  - SAM = TAM × [Relevant %] = $[X]

SOM Calculation:
  - Our current audience/reach: [Number of people we can reach]
  - Realistic conversion rate: [1-3% for cold, 3-8% for warm]
  - SOM = Audience × Conversion = [Units/year] × $[Price] = $[Revenue]

Market Size Verdict:
  TAM: $[X] — [Interpretation: large enough to pursue?]
  SAM: $[X] — [Interpretation]
  SOM: $[X] — [First-year revenue potential]
```

## 3. MARKET TREND ANALYSIS

### Trend Research Sources
```
PRIMARY SOURCES:
  Google Trends: Direction and velocity of search interest over time
    → Use for: Is interest growing, peaking, or declining?
    → Key: Compare to 5-year trend, not just last year
    
  Amazon Best Sellers: What's selling in book/digital categories
    → Use for: What audiences are paying for right now
    → Key: New releases in top 100 = validated demand
    
  App Store Charts: Mobile category trends
    → Use for: Consumer app demand signals
    
  Gumroad Discover: What's trending in digital products
    → Use for: Direct market intelligence in our space

SECONDARY SOURCES:
  Reddit (r/productivity, r/personalfinance, r/entrepreneur, etc.):
    → Use for: What questions are people asking repeatedly?
    → Key: Questions asked 3+ times/month = real pain point
    
  Twitter/X Advanced Search:
    → "looking for [product category]" past week
    → "[problem] anyone know how to" past month
    
  Industry reports (Statista, Grand View Research, etc.):
    → Use for: Big picture numbers with citations
    → Key: Always check methodology and date
```

### Trend Velocity Assessment
```
For any trend, classify as:
  EMERGING: < 2 years old, growing rapidly, limited solutions exist
    → High risk, high potential, early mover advantage
    
  GROWING: 2-5 years old, steady growth, growing competition
    → Sweet spot — market proven, not yet saturated
    
  MATURE: 5+ years, stable or slow growth, heavy competition
    → Need strong differentiation to compete
    
  DECLINING: Clearly shrinking search/sales interest
    → Avoid unless specific niche contrarian opportunity
```

### Google Trends Analysis Protocol
```
1. Search the core problem/solution term
2. Switch to "5 years" timeframe (not 12 months)
3. Look for:
   - Overall direction (up/down/flat)
   - Seasonality patterns (when does it peak?)
   - Geographic concentration (US-heavy or global?)
4. Use "Related queries" → Rising section for adjacent opportunities
5. Compare to one obvious competitor term

INTERPRETING NUMBERS:
  100 = Peak popularity in the selected period
  50 = Half the peak popularity
  The numbers are relative, not absolute search volumes
  Use for direction and comparison, not absolute size
```

## 4. NICHE DISCOVERY METHODS

### The Niching Down Framework
```
START: Broad category
  → Narrow by audience: Who specifically benefits most?
  → Narrow by problem: Which specific pain point?
  → Narrow by context: In what specific situation?
  → Narrow by outcome: To achieve what specific result?

EXAMPLE:
  Broad: "Productivity tools"
  + Audience: "For parents"
  + Problem: "Who can't keep family schedules synced"
  + Context: "With kids in multiple activities"
  + Outcome: "Without spending 30 min on Sunday planning"
  
  Result: "Family activity coordination system for parents of multi-sport kids"
  → Specific enough to own, broad enough to be a real market
```

### Underserved Niche Signals
```
A niche may be underserved if:
  ✓ People ask the question repeatedly on Reddit/Quora
  ✓ Google search shows 1-2 quality results (low competition)
  ✓ Amazon has few books in the exact sub-category
  ✓ Existing solutions are generic, not specific to this audience
  ✓ The problem is well-defined but solutions are DIY or fragmented
  ✓ Practitioners in the field rely on manual processes or hacks
```

## 5. MARKET ENTRY RESEARCH

### The Market Entry Assessment
Before entering any market, answer:

```
MARKET ASSESSMENT:
1. Is the market large enough? (SOM > $100K potential is minimum threshold for digital products)
2. Is there demand? (Validated by keyword volume, community discussion, existing sales)
3. Is there competition? (Some competition = validated; no competition = no market or too early)
4. Can we differentiate? (What makes our version better for a specific audience?)
5. Can we reach buyers? (Do we have or can we build access to the target audience?)
6. Is timing right? (Growing trend, not peaking or declining?)

COMPETITIVE DENSITY SCORING:
  Low (0-3 direct competitors): Opportunity but may need market education
  Medium (4-10 competitors): Ideal — proven market with room
  High (10-30 competitors): Need strong differentiation
  Saturated (30+ competitors): Enter only with clear, defensible advantage
```

## 6. MARKET RESEARCH SOURCES

### Source Quality Matrix for Market Research
```
TIER 1 — Primary Research (do when decision is large):
  Customer interviews (5-10 target customers)
  Survey of existing audience
  A/B test on existing traffic
  "Smoke test" landing page with paid traffic

TIER 2 — Validated Data:
  Amazon sales rank + review analysis
  Google Keyword Planner (search volume)
  SEMrush / Ahrefs (competition + keywords)
  Statista reports

TIER 3 — Directional Signals:
  Reddit discussion frequency and sentiment
  Google Trends direction
  Twitter search for problem terms
  Facebook Groups: question frequency

TIER 4 — Anecdotal:
  Personal conversations
  Individual blog posts
  Single forum discussions
  
Weight decisions by tiers: Lead with Tier 1-2, use Tier 3-4 as directional support
```

## 8. COMMON MARKET RESEARCH MISTAKES

### Mistakes That Lead to Bad Decisions

**Mistake 1: Confusing Interest with Purchase Intent**
Many people are interested in a topic. Far fewer will pay money to solve it.
Fix: Look for purchasing signals (Amazon sales, Gumroad products selling), not just Google searches.

**Mistake 2: "There's No Competition = Opportunity"**
No competition usually means no market, not a wide-open opportunity.
Fix: Some competition validates demand. No competition → research why before entering.

**Mistake 3: Anchoring on TAM, Not SOM**
"This is a $50 billion market!" is meaningless unless you can actually reach buyers.
Fix: Build bottom-up SOM estimate. That's the number that matters for planning.

**Mistake 4: Using Old Data**
Market research from 2021 may be seriously misleading in 2024.
Fix: Always check publication date of data sources. Prefer data within 18 months.

**Mistake 5: Extrapolating From Non-Representative Samples**
"My Facebook friends all said they'd buy it" ≠ market research.
Fix: Find communities of the actual target audience, not your personal network.

**Mistake 6: Ignoring Seasonality**
A product that only sells in December has a different business model than one that sells year-round.
Fix: Check Google Trends for seasonal patterns. Plan revenue projections accordingly.
