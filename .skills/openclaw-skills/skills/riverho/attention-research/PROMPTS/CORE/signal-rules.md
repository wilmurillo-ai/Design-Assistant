# Signal Rules — Attention Research Pipeline

*Version 1.0.0 | Domain-agnostic signal definition*

---

## What Is a Signal

A signal is something that **changes the probability distribution** of future outcomes.

Yes signals:
- A repeated pattern across multiple independent sources
- A concrete behavioral change in an actor
- A physical/logistical reality that contradicts public narrative
- A movement in markets, shipping, finance, or institutional behavior
- A decision that shifts power, leverage, or constraint

Not signals:
- A headline that confirms what you already believed
- A statement without behavioral follow-through
- A data point that fits the existing narrative
- An opinion without factual basis

---

## Signal Hierarchy

**Tier 1 — Confirmed physical change**
Something verifiable without trusting the actor: military movements observed by third parties, shipping data, financial transactions, regulatory decisions, confirmed personnel changes.

**Tier 2 — Behavioral confirmation**
An actor doing something they said they wouldn't, or not doing what they said they would: commitment with follow-through, resource allocation revealing true priority, decision contradicting stated position.

**Tier 3 — Institutional signals**
How organizations respond, not just what they say: bureaucratic resistance to policy, budget allocation contradicting narrative, coalition formation or fracture.

**Tier 4 — Rhetoric with context**
Public statements meaningful because of when, how, or by whom: sudden tone change without policy change, silence where response expected, framing serving specific audience.

## Signal vs Noise

For every data point, ask:
1. Does this change what I believe about this actor's incentives?
2. Does this contradict any existing assumption?
3. Does this connect to a pattern I've seen before?
4. What would have to be true for this to be theater, not signal?
5. What would falsify this?

**Noise markers:**
- Confirmation noise — "X is happening" when you already knew X
- Theater noise — statements designed to manage audience without behavior change
- Vague noise — "experts believe", "sources say", "likely to" without specifics
- Context-free noise — fact without connection to decision logic of the actor

---

## Dot-Connecting Protocol

For each signal, before surfacing:
1. **Cross-reference** — has this appeared before? In what form? Does repetition confirm or contradict?
2. **Actor mapping** — who is doing this? Who benefits? Who is being managed?
3. **Incentive check** — does the actor have incentive to be truthful here?
4. **Physical verification** — can I verify without trusting the actor?
5. **Follow-through test** — what would real follow-through look like?
6. **Contradiction check** — does this contradict any belief? If yes, surface with contradiction explicit.

---

## Weight of Evidence

When evidence is mixed:
1. **Physical evidence > institutional evidence > rhetorical evidence**
2. **Consistent pattern > single data point**
3. **Behavior > stated intent**
4. **Multiple independent sources > single source**

---

## When to Escalate

Escalate immediately (outside scheduled digest) when:
- Physical evidence of imminent conflict or crisis
- Market-moving data contradicting consensus
- Key actor decision fundamentally changing constraint structure
- Something forcing revision to a core hypothesis

Otherwise: hold for next scheduled digest.

---

## Watchlist Rules

Every topic digest ends with concrete, time-bound, behavior-verifiable watch items:
- Concrete — not vague ("watch Iran" is not a watch item)
- Time-bound — not "soon" ("by Wednesday" not "soon")
- Behavior-verifiable — not "analysts expect" ("shipping data confirms" not "analysts expect")
