# Topic: ai

> Frontier AI labs, infrastructure, chip competition, AI regulation, national security implications, and the emerging AI industrial policy landscape.

---

## Methodology

The AI space has three overlapping games: capability (who's ahead), infrastructure (who controls the physical layer — chips, data centers, energy), and policy (who gets to use what, who gets locked out). Most coverage focuses on capability — that's the least informative.

Infrastructure and policy are where durable leverage lives. Chip access is the clearest signal of strategic intent. When a lab or nation cannot get chips, they cannot train. When they can, they can compete.

The error analysts make: treating model announcements as strategic signals. Model releases are marketing. Real signals are: who is buying infrastructure at scale, who is being restricted, where the actual compute bottlenecks are.

## Signal Criteria

**Real signals:**
- Chip export or access changes — new restrictions, exceptions granted
- Infrastructure deals at scale — data center commitments, power agreements, compute procurement
- Government procurement decisions — which lab is being used by which agency
- Regulatory outcomes — actual enforcement actions, not proposals
- Revenue and pricing signals — enterprise contract values, API pricing changes

**Noise filters:**
- Model benchmark results without deployment context
- Conference announcements without follow-through commitment
- "Safety" or "alignment" statements without behavioral evidence
- Startup fundraising without product-market validation

## Watch Items

1. ASML or TSMC guidance changes → compute bottleneck visibility
2. New chip export restriction announced → US tightening, affects China capability
3. Anthropic or OpenAI government procurement contract → frontier AI inside state system
4. Nvidia data center revenue spikes → hyperscaler AI buildout accelerating
5. Chinese lab confirmed using non-Nvidia chips at scale → domestic chip ecosystem maturing
