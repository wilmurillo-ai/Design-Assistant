# Topic: finance-markets

> Equities, bonds, currencies, commodities, central bank policy, macro trends, institutional positioning, and the relationship between financial markets and real economic conditions.

---

## Methodology

Markets are a coordination mechanism, not a truth-teller. What markets price today is consensus about tomorrow — and consensus is often wrong, especially at turning points.

The error analysts make: treating market movements as responses to news. Markets move on expectations, not events. The relevant question is not "what happened" but "what did the market already know, and how does this new information change expected future?"

Physical reality and market pricing frequently diverge. When oil prices spike but shipping data shows no physical disruption, the market is pricing political risk. When equities rally on peace talks but Hormuz traffic remains restricted, equities are pricing the hope path.

Credit spreads, cross-asset correlations, and positioning data reveal more than price moves.

## Entity Framework

| Entity | Weight | Role |
|--------|--------|------|
| Central banks (Fed, ECB, PBOC, BOJ, BOE) | 25% | Set risk-free rate, shape expectations, determine credit conditions |
| Institutional investors (asset managers, hedge funds, sovereign wealth) | 20% | Drive flows, position ahead of consensus, create feedback loops |
| Governments and finance ministries | 15% | Fiscal policy, sovereign risk, political pressure on central banks |
| Rating agencies | 8% | Market access costs, sovereign borrowing costs, signal creditworthiness |
| Multilateral institutions (IMF, World Bank, BIS) | 7% | Policy prescriptions, crisis signaling, data standards |
| Corporate sector (key earners) | 15% | Earnings reality vs expectations, guidance signals |
| Commodity markets (oil, metals, agricultural) | 5% | Physical supply/demand, geopolitical risk premium |
| Retail market participants | 5% | Sentiment indicator, often wrong at extremes |

## Signal Criteria

**Real signals:**
- Rate decisions with behavioral follow-through — not just the decision but how the central bank signals its reaction function
- Credit spread changes — especially IG widening vs HY, institutional risk perception
- Cross-asset correlation breakdowns — when normal relationships break
- Positioning data — CFTC futures positioning, ETF flows, institutional allocation
- Earnings guidance vs actual results — the miss, not the headline beat
- Yield curve movements — especially inversion/un-inversion dynamics

**Noise filters:**
- Daily market commentary without structural insight
- Sell-side research without independent confirmation
- Short-term volatility not connected to fundamentals
- Sentiment surveys without behavioral follow-through

## Watch Items

1. Credit spreads widen 50+ bps without equity vol → institutional risk perception rising
2. Yield curve un-inverts while Fed still hiking → market pricing recession
3. Dollar strengthens on risk-off → flight to safety, signals risk aversion rising
4. High yield bond prices fall while IG holds → real economy stress
5. Oil spikes with no physical disruption confirmed → geopolitical premium, not supply shock

## Source Hierarchy

1. Market data (Bloomberg, Reuters Eikon) — price, volume, positioning, spreads
2. Central bank communications and minutes — actual decisions and voting patterns
3. Corporate earnings and guidance — real results vs expectations
4. CFTC positioning data — commitment of traders
5. Sell-side research — useful for reference, validate independently
