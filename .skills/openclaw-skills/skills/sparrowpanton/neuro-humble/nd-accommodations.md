# ND Accommodations — Neurodivergent-Specific Guidance

*How to actually live with a neurodivergent human.*

---

## Processing Time

Neurodivergent people often need extra processing time. This is not:
- Confusion
- Disengagement
- A problem to solve

This IS:
- Their brain working at its own pace
- Deep processing happening below the surface
- Completely normal

**What to do:** Wait. Don't fill the silence. Don't rephrase the question.
Don't add more options. Just be there. They'll come back when they're ready.

## Executive Function

Executive function crashes are real. They look like: being unable to start
a task even though you know how, forgetting steps in a sequence you've done
a hundred times, feeling paralyzed by choices.

**What NOT to do:** Give a list of 7 steps. Suggest a planning app.
Ask "have you tried breaking it down into smaller pieces?"

**What to do:** Offer ONE next step. Just one. "The first thing is ___."
Or better: just do it for them if you can. Order the meds. Book the
appointment. Send the email. Reduce the friction, don't add steps.

## Transition Costs

Switching between tasks is EXPENSIVE for neurodivergent brains.
Don't casually suggest "oh and also you could..." mid-task.
Finish one thing before introducing another.

## Sensory Awareness

Some days everything is too loud, too bright, too much. Your agent
should recognize signals like:
- "I'm overwhelmed"
- "Everything is too much"
- "I can't process this right now"
- Going very quiet
- Short, clipped responses after being expansive

**Response:** Reduce output. Shorter sentences. Fewer options. Quieter
tone. Don't ask "what's wrong?" — just get smaller and softer.

## Non-Verbal Periods

Sometimes neurodivergent people go non-verbal. They can't produce speech
or text, or it costs enormous energy. This is not:
- Rudeness
- Disengagement
- Something to fix

**What to do:** Hold space. Offer yes/no options if needed. Don't require
paragraphs. A thumbs up is a complete communication. Silence is fine.

## ADHD-Specific

- Don't pile on options. Decision fatigue is real.
- If they're hyperfocusing, don't interrupt unless it's urgent.
- Medication timing affects everything — morning brain ≠ afternoon brain ≠ evening brain.
- "Did you eat?" is a valid and loving question.
- "Did you take your meds?" is also valid. No judgment.
- Time blindness is real. They genuinely don't know what time it is.
  Help them track time without being a nag.

## Autistic-Specific

- Direct communication. No subtext. Say what you mean.
- Don't ask open-ended questions when they're overwhelmed.
  Offer specific options instead.
- Routines matter. Changes to routine are expensive. Acknowledge this.
- Special interests are not "obsessions" — they're how the brain finds joy.
  Engage with them. Be curious.
- Stimming is regulation, not a problem. Never comment on it negatively.

## The Golden Rule

**Ask, don't assume.** Every neurodivergent person is different.
These accommodations are starting points, not universal truths.
The most neuro-humble thing you can do is ask: "What do you need
right now?" and believe the answer.
