# Hindu Vedic Dream Symbols — Swapna Shastra & Puranic Tradition

> Compiled from Swapna Shastra sections of the Agni Purana, Garuda Purana, and
> related Puranic and Tantric sources. This reference covers 85 symbols organized
> alphabetically by English name, with Sanskrit transliterations and classifications
> per the traditional seven-fold dream typology. Classifications: **Auspicious** (Śubha),
> **Inauspicious** (Aśubha), **Mixed** (Miśra), **Prophetic** (Bhāvika).

---

## Methodology — Swapna Shastra & Puranic Dream Interpretation

### Sources and Authority

Hindu dream interpretation derives primarily from the **Swapna Shastra** (स्वप्न शास्त्र), the
Vedic science of dreams, as preserved in chapters of the **Agni Purana** and the **Garuda Purana**.
The Garuda Purana's *Preta Kalpa* section discusses dreams in the context of afterlife journeys,
while the Agni Purana provides systematic classification of dream types and their fruits. Supplementary
material comes from the **Devi Bhagavata Purana**, the **Brahma Vaivarta Purana**, and Tantric
traditions concerning the subtle body.

### The Seven Types of Dreams (Sapta Swapna)

Swapna Shastra classifies all dreams into seven categories, each with a distinct ontological source
and interpretive weight:

| # | Sanskrit Name | Type | Description |
|---|---------------|------|-------------|
| 1 | **Dṛṣṭa** (दृष्ट) | Seen / Manifest | Dreams reflecting waking experiences; carry limited prophetic weight |
| 2 | **Śruta** (श्रुत) | Heard | Dreams composed of things only heard about; moderate significance |
| 3 | **Anubhūta** (अनुभूत) | Experienced | Dreams arising from deeply felt emotions and past experiences |
| 4 | **Prārthita** (प्रार्थित) | Desired | Dreams born of conscious wishes and longings; reflect inner aspiration |
| 5 | **Kalpita** (कल्पित) | Imagined | Dreams constructed from mental fabrication; least prophetic |
| 6 | **Bhāvika** (भाविक) | Futuristic / Prophetic | True prophetic dreams that manifest in waking life; highest significance |
| 7 | **Doṣaja** (दोषज) | Physiological | Dreams caused by bodily imbalances (vata, pitta, kapha); not interpretable as omens |

Only **Bhāvika** dreams are considered truly prophetic. **Doṣaja** dreams should be addressed through
Āyurvedic remedies rather than interpreted symbolically.

### Dream Timing and Significance (Kāla-Śāstra)

The time of night determines a dream's interpretive authority:

- **Brahma Muhurta** (approximately 3:30–5:30 AM, the last quarter of the night): Dreams during this
  period are the most significant and most likely to be Bhāvika (prophetic). The mind is in its purest
  sattvic state, closest to the Ātman.
- **Second watch of the night** (roughly 9 PM–12 AM): Dreams reflect daily impressions (Dṛṣṭa) and
  have moderate significance.
- **Third watch** (roughly 12–3 AM): Dreams may be Anubhūta or Kalpita, requiring careful discernment.
- **Just before waking**: Dreams in this transition carry the highest probability of being Doṣaja
  and should be evaluated cautiously.

The **day of the lunar calendar** (tithi) and the **nakṣatra** (lunar mansion) also influence
dream fruition, as outlined in the Garuda Purana.

### Karmic Dream Theory: Dreams as Samskāra Residues

According to Vedic psychology, dreams arise from **samskāras** (संस्कार) — latent impressions
stored in the **kāraṇa śarīra** (causal body) across lifetimes. The **Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad** describes
the dreaming state (*Taijasa*) as that in which the individual soul experiences impressions created
by past karma. Prophetic dreams occur when the samskāras are ripe for manifestation (*sādhaka*)
in the near future. Thus, a dream of a deity may signal the fruition of past-life devotional
practices (bhakti samskāras), while a nightmare may reflect pending karmic debts.

### Swapna Phala — The Fruit of Dreams

**Swapna Phala** (स्वप्नफल) is the doctrine of dream results: every meaningful dream produces
a measurable outcome in waking life. The Garuda Purana specifies time-frames for fruition:

- Dreams at Brahma Muhurta: fruit within **1–7 days**
- Dreams in the second watch: fruit within **15–30 days**
- Dreams in the first watch: fruit may take **months or a year**
- Some especially powerful Bhāvika dreams (e.g., direct darśana of a deity) may fructify
  **instantly** or within **the same day**

The **intensity of emotion** felt upon waking is a key indicator — a dream remembered with
strong feeling is more likely to fructify rapidly.

### Good and Bad Dream Rituals

**After an auspicious dream (Śubha Swapna):**

- Remain still upon waking; do not immediately speak the dream aloud (it may dissipate the fruit)
- Offer a silent prayer of gratitude to the deity seen in the dream
- If the dream featured a deity's darśana, consider it a call to perform specific sādhanā
- Some traditions advise narrating the dream to a trusted elder or guru to "seal" its blessing

**After an inauspicious dream (Aśubha Swapna):**

- Upon waking, **drink cold water** — this is the primary Garuda Purana remedy
- **Recite a protective mantra**: "Om Namah Shivaya," "Om Namo Narayanaya," or the Gayatri Mantra
- Sprinkle water around the bed while reciting
- Do **not** narrate the dream to others, as this can amplify its negative fruit
- If the dream recurs, perform a **Nārāyaṇa Bali** or consult a Vedic ritual specialist
- The **Devi Māhātmya** prescribes chanting the Devī Kavaca (armor of the Goddess) after
  nightmares involving demons or death

### Connection to Chakras and Kośas

Dreams map onto the subtle body architecture of the **pañca kośas** (five sheaths) and the
**sapta chakras** (seven energy centers):

| Kośa (Sheath) | Dream Correlation |
|---------------|-------------------|
| **Annamaya** (Physical) | Doṣaja dreams — bodily sensations, hunger, illness |
| **Prāṇamaya** (Vital) | Dreams of breath, wind, flying, suffocation — pranic disturbance |
| **Manomaya** (Mental) | Kalpita and Prārthita dreams — thought constructs and desires |
| **Vijñānamaya** (Wisdom) | Bhāvika dreams — prophetic visions, deity encounters |
| **Ānandamaya** (Bliss) | Darśana of the Supreme; experience of brahman — the rarest and highest |

**Chakra-specific dream imagery:**

- **Mūlādhāra** (Root): Dreams of earth, roots, red color, survival situations, snakes coiled at the base
- **Svādhiṣṭhāna** (Sacral): Dreams of water, sexuality, orange imagery, emotional turbulence
- **Maṇipūra** (Solar Plexus): Dreams of fire, yellow/gold, power struggles, digestion issues
- **Anāhata** (Heart): Dreams of air, green, love, compassion, heart-opening experiences
- **Viśuddha** (Throat): Dreams of blue, sound, speech, expression, divine names heard
- **Ājñā** (Third Eye): Dreams of indigo, light, prophetic visions, cosmic knowledge
- **Sahasrāra** (Crown): Dreams of violet/white light, merging with the divine, formless awareness

### Deities as Dream Messengers

In the Puranic framework, deities appear in dreams not randomly but as **conscious messengers**
dispatched by Īśvara to guide, warn, or bless the dreamer. Each deity carries a specific domain
of significance:

- **Viṣṇu/Nārāyaṇa**: Supreme protection, dharma restoration, liberation (mokṣa)
- **Śiva**: Transformation, detachment, grace, destruction of ignorance
- **Brahmā**: Creative beginnings, knowledge, cosmic order
- **Lakṣmī**: Material and spiritual prosperity
- **Sarasvatī**: Wisdom, learning, artistic inspiration
- **Gaṇeśa**: Obstacle removal, new endeavors
- **Hanumān**: Courage, devotion, protection from malefic forces
- **Durgā**: Victory over adversity, divine feminine power
- **Kālī**: Radical transformation, time, liberation through confrontation with mortality
- **Kṛṣṇa**: Divine love, līlā (cosmic play), the highest bhakti
- **Rāma**: Righteousness, ideal conduct, victorious return from exile

A deity appearing **calm and blessing** is supremely auspicious. A deity appearing **angry or
turning away** is a warning — not a curse, but a call to correct one's course.

## Dream Symbols — Alphabetical Reference

### A

#### Aarti (Āratī)
- **Sanskrit**: आरति
- **Classification**: Auspicious (Śubha)
- **Interpretation**: Dreaming of aarti — the circling of lit lamps before a deity — signifies
  the dispelling of spiritual darkness and the arrival of divine grace. The Agni Purana associates
  aarti dreams with the fruits of past devotional merit (puṇya) ripening in the present. Seeing
  oneself performing aarti indicates an upcoming period of clarity and spiritual advancement.

#### Ash (Bhasma / Vibhūti)
- **Sanskrit**: भस्म / विभूति
- **Classification**: Auspicious (Śubha)
- **Interpretation**: Sacred ash (vibhūti) in a dream represents the transformative power of
  Śiva — the residue of destruction that becomes the substance of renewal. Applying bhasma to
  the forehead in a dream signals purification of past karmas and protection from inauspicious
  forces. The Śaiva tradition considers this dream a direct blessing of Lord Śiva.

#### Ashoka Tree (Aśoka Vṛkṣa)
- **Sanskrit**: अशोक वृक्ष
- **Classification**: Auspicious (Śubha)
- **Interpretation**: The Ashoka tree — meaning "sorrow-less" — is deeply associated with
  Sītā's captivity in the Aśokavana in the Rāmāyaṇa. Dreaming of it signals the end of
  a period of grief and the imminent arrival of relief. The tree also connects to Kāmadēva,
  the god of love, suggesting renewal of emotional joy.

### B

#### Banyan Tree (Vaṭa Vṛkṣa)
- **Sanskrit**: वट वृक्ष
- **Classification**: Auspicious (Śubha)
- **Interpretation**: The banyan is the cosmic tree of the Bhagavad Gītā (15.1), its roots
  above and branches below, symbolizing the eternal aśvattha of creation. Dreaming of a banyan
  signifies longevity, spiritual rootedness, and the shelter of dharma. Meditating under a
  banyan in a dream is a Bhāvika vision of spiritual attainment.

#### Bathing in Sacred River (Snāna)
- **Sanskrit**: स्नान
- **Classification**: Auspicious (Śubha)
- **Interpretation**: Ritual bathing (snāna) in a sacred river, especially the Gaṅgā, represents
  the purification of karmic impurities. The Garuda Purana states that snāna dreams indicate
  the washing away of past sins (pāpa kṣaya). Bathing in the Yamunā specifically connects to
  Kṛṣṇa-bhakti, while bathing at a tīrtha (sacred crossing) signals an impending pilgrimage
  or spiritual breakthrough.

#### Bell (Ghaṇṭā)
- **Sanskrit**: घण्टा
- **Classification**: Auspicious (Śubha)
- **Interpretation**: The temple bell's sound in a dream is an invitation from the divine — a
  call to awaken from spiritual slumber. Hearing a bell ringing indicates that an auspicious
  message is being transmitted from the subtle realm. The ghaṇṭā is also the sound-form of
  Om; dreaming of it suggests the dreamer's ājñā chakra is activating.

#### Bilva Leaves (Bilva Patra)
- **Sanskrit**: बिल्व पत्र
- **Classification**: Auspicious (Śubha)
- **Interpretation**: Bilva (bael) leaves are the most beloved offering to Śiva. Offering bilva
  in a dream carries the same merit as doing so in waking life, according to Puranic doctrine.
  This dream signals that the dreamer's prayers are being accepted and that Śiva's grace is
  descending. Receiving bilva leaves from an unknown hand may indicate ancestral blessing.

#### Brahma
- **Sanskrit**: ब्रह्मा
- **Classification**: Prophetic (Bhāvika)
- **Interpretation**: Seeing Brahmā — the four-faced creator — in a dream is exceptionally rare
  and prophetic. It signals the birth of a new creative cycle in the dreamer's life, the inception
  of a significant project, or the dawn of profound knowledge. Brahmā seated on his lotus
  indicates cosmic support for the dreamer's endeavors. However, Brahmā turning away may warn
  of creative stagnation or a neglected dharma.

#### Budha (Mercury)
- **Sanskrit**: बुध
- **Classification**: Mixed (Miśra)
- **Interpretation**: Budha, the planet of intellect and communication, appearing in a dream
  signals a period of mental activity, learning, or commercial dealings. Favorable placement
  suggests gains through speech, writing, or trade. Unfavorable appearance (dark, obscured)
  warns of miscommunication, deceit in business, or intellectual confusion. Budha's navagraha
  energy governs the viśuddha (throat) chakra.

#### Bull/Nandi (Nandi)
- **Sanskrit**: नन्दि
- **Classification**: Auspicious (Śubha)
- **Interpretation**: Nandi, Śiva's vāhana (vehicle) and gatekeeper, represents dharma, strength,
  and unwavering devotion. Dreaming of Nandi signals that the dreamer is under Śiva's protection
  and that obstacles to spiritual progress are being removed. A white Nandi is especially
  auspicious, indicating purity of devotion. An agitated Nandi warns that the dreamer's
  commitment to dharma is wavering.

### C

#### Chakra (Cakra / Sudarśana)
- **Sanskrit**: चक्र / सुदर्शन
- **Classification**: Prophetic (Bhāvika)
- **Interpretation**: The Sudarśana Chakra — Viṣṇu's divine discus — in a dream signifies
  the imminent destruction of obstacles and the restoration of cosmic order (dharma). Seeing
  the chakra spinning is a powerful Bhāvika vision indicating divine intervention in the
  dreamer's life. The chakra also represents the cycle of time (kāla-cakra); dreaming of it
  may signal a karmic cycle reaching completion.

#### Chanting Mantra (Mantra Japa)
- **Sanskrit**: मन्त्र जप
- **Classification**: Auspicious (Śubha)
- **Interpretation**: Hearing or performing mantra japa in a dream indicates that the dreamer's
  subtle body is being purified at the prāṇamaya and vijñānamaya levels. The specific mantra
  heard carries its own significance: Om signals cosmic alignment, the Gayatry signals spiritual
  awakening, and a deity's name indicates that deity's active blessing. This dream often occurs
  when past-life sādhanā is bearing fruit.

#### Conch Shell (Śaṅkha)
- **Sanskrit**: शङ्ख
- **Classification**: Auspicious (Śubha)
- **Interpretation**: The conch (śaṅkha) is the primordial sound of creation — the resonance
  of Om itself. Blowing a conch in a dream announces the arrival of auspicious news and the
  dispersal of negative energies. Finding a conch signals unexpected wealth or spiritual treasure.
  The Pāñcajanya śaṅkha of Kṛṣṇa, if specifically recognizable, is a supreme Bhāvika omen
  of divine victory.

#### Cow (Gau / Gomātā)
- **Sanskrit**: गौ / गोमाता
- **Classification**: Auspicious (Śubha)
- **Interpretation**: The cow (gomātā) is the most sacred animal in Vedic tradition, embodying
  abundance, motherhood, and the earth itself. Dreaming of a white cow signifies incoming
  prosperity and spiritual merit; a cow giving milk indicates that the fruits of past good
  karma are flowing. A cow in distress warns of dharma violations requiring correction.
  The Atharva Veda calls the cow "the mother of the Rudras, the daughter of the Vasus."

#### Cremation Ground (Śmaśāna)
- **Sanskrit**: श्मशान
- **Classification**: Mixed (Miśra)
- **Interpretation**: The śmaśāna is the domain of Śiva and Kālī — the sacred ground where
  ego and attachment are consumed by fire. Dreaming of a cremation ground while feeling peace
  indicates profound spiritual transformation and the burning away of past karmas. Feeling
  fear suggests unresolved attachments and karmic debts. Tantric tradition considers this an
  especially powerful dream for sādhakas, indicating that the Kuṇḍalinī is active.

#### Crow (Kāka)
- **Sanskrit**: काक
- **Classification**: Mixed (Miśra)
- **Interpretation**: The crow is the vāhana of Śani (Saturn) and is associated with ancestral
  spirits (pitṛs). A crow appearing in dreams during the pitṛ-pakṣa period signals that
  ancestral offerings (śrāddha) may be needed. A crow speaking or delivering something is
  a message from the pitṛ loka. Crows attacking or cawing harshly warn of Śani's malefic
  influence and upcoming hardships.

### D

#### Damaru (Ḍamaru)
- **Sanskrit**: डमरु
- **Classification**: Auspicious (Śubha)
- **Interpretation**: Śiva's hourglass drum, the ḍamaru, produces the primal sound (nāda)
  from which the Sanskrit alphabet and all creation emerge. Hearing the ḍamaru in a dream
  signals a creative awakening and the pulsation of cosmic rhythm (spanda) entering the
  dreamer's life. This is a particularly powerful omen for artists, musicians, and those
  on a Tantric path.

#### Darshan (Darśana)
- **Sanskrit**: दर्शन
- **Classification**: Prophetic (Bhāvika)
- **Interpretation**: Darśana — the auspicious sight of a deity — is the most significant
  category of Hindu dreams. To see a deity clearly, in full form, with a calm or smiling
  expression, is the highest Bhāvika dream, promising the fruition of lifetimes of spiritual
  practice. The Garuda Purana states that darśana dreams in Brahma Muhurta fructify within
  three days. A deity with closed eyes or turning away signals the need for greater devotion.

#### Diya/Lamp (Dīpa)
- **Sanskrit**: दीप
- **Classification**: Auspicious (Śubha)
- **Interpretation**: A lit lamp (dīpa) represents the light of consciousness (cit) dispelling
  the darkness of ignorance (avidyā). Lighting a diya before a deity in a dream is a supreme
  act of devotion carrying immediate spiritual merit. A lamp that stays lit without oil
  signals divine sustenance. A lamp being extinguished by wind warns of obstacles to
  spiritual practice, while a lamp relit after going out signifies resilience in dharma.

#### Durga (Durgā)
- **Sanskrit**: दुर्गा
- **Classification**: Auspicious (Śubha)
- **Interpretation**: Goddess Durgā riding her lion and wielding weapons in a dream signals
  divine intervention against adversities. She is the invincible one who destroys Mahiṣāsura
  — the demon of ego and inertia. Dreaming of Durgā during Navarātrī is especially potent,
  promising victory in legal, professional, or personal battles. Her nine forms (navadurgā)
  each carry specialized significance; Mahiṣāsuramardinī specifically promises triumph over
  seemingly impossible odds.

### E

#### Elephant (Gaja)
- **Sanskrit**: गज
- **Classification**: Auspicious (Śubha)
- **Interpretation**: The elephant is the vāhana of Indra (Airāvata) and is inseparable from
  Gaṇeśa symbolism. A white elephant in a dream is one of the most auspicious omens in
  Swapna Shastra, signifying royal fortune, spiritual authority, and the blessing of the
  devas. An elephant procession signals major celebrations. A charging elephant warns of
  powerful forces — either protective or challenging — entering the dreamer's life.

### F

#### Fasting (Upavāsa)
- **Sanskrit**: उपवास
- **Classification**: Mixed (Miśra)
- **Interpretation**: Observing a fast in a dream reflects the soul's desire for purification
  and detachment. If the fasting is voluntary and accompanied by peace, it signals growing
  spiritual discipline (sādhanā-śakti). If the fasting is forced or accompanied by suffering,
  it may be a Doṣaja dream reflecting pitta imbalance. The Puranic tradition equates dream-fasting
  with actual penance, suggesting that the subtle body performs real sādhanā during sleep.

#### Fire/Agni (Agni)
- **Sanskrit**: अग्नि
- **Classification**: Mixed (Miśra)
- **Interpretation**: Agni is both the divine messenger (havya-vāhana) and the cosmic transformer.
  A controlled sacred fire — as in a homa or yajña — is supremely auspicious, indicating
  purification and divine acceptance of offerings. An uncontrolled wildfire warns of destructive
  karmic forces or anger (krodha) consuming the dreamer's life. The direction of the fire
  matters: fire rising upward signals spiritual ascent, while fire spreading horizontally
  signals worldly consequences.

#### Fish (Matsya)
- **Sanskrit**: मत्स्य
- **Classification**: Auspicious (Śubha)
- **Interpretation**: The fish is the first avatāra of Viṣṇu — Matsya, who saved humanity
  from the cosmic flood. Dreaming of fish signifies fertility, abundance, and divine
  preservation. A golden fish is an especially auspicious omen of spiritual wealth. Fish
  swimming freely indicate the natural flow of dharma; fish struggling out of water warn
  of being in an environment hostile to one's spiritual nature.

### G

#### Ganesha (Gaṇeśa)
- **Sanskrit**: गणेश
- **Classification**: Auspicious (Śubha)
- **Interpretation**: Gaṇeśa — the remover of obstacles (vighneśvara) — appearing in a dream
  is among the most commonly reported and most encouraging darśana experiences. His elephant
  head signifies wisdom, and his single tusk represents the power of focus. Receiving a modaka
  (sweet) from Gaṇeśa signals that obstacles to a specific endeavor will dissolve. Gaṇeśa
  dancing indicates joyous progress; Gaṇeśa writing the Mahābhārata signals creative power
  flowing through the dreamer.

#### Ganges River (Gaṅgā)
- **Sanskrit**: गङ्गा
- **Classification**: Auspicious (Śubha)
- **Interpretation**: The Gaṅgā is the celestial river descending from heaven to earth, carrying
  the purification of all three worlds. Dreaming of bathing in the Gaṅgā is equivalent in
  spiritual merit to actual pilgrimage, according to the Garuda Purana. The Gaṅgā appearing
  clear and flowing promises liberation from karmic bondage. A dry or polluted Gaṅgā warns
  that the dreamer's spiritual practice has become contaminated by hypocrisy or ego.

#### Garuda (Garuḍa)
- **Sanskrit**: गरुड
- **Classification**: Auspicious (Śubha)
- **Interpretation**: Garuḍa — the divine eagle and vāhana of Viṣṇu — is the supreme enemy
  of serpents and all poisonous influences. Dreaming of Garuḍa signals protection from
  hidden enemies, poison (both literal and metaphorical), and nāga-dosha (serpent curse).
  Riding Garuḍa is an exalted Bhāvika vision indicating the dreamer's consciousness is
  ascending to Vaikuṇṭha, the abode of Viṣṇu.

#### Ghee (Ghṛta)
- **Sanskrit**: घृत
- **Classification**: Auspicious (Śubha)
- **Interpretation**: Clarified butter (ghṛta) is the purest offering in Vedic ritual, the
  fuel that sustains Agni. Dreaming of pouring ghee into a sacred fire indicates that the
  dreamer's prayers and offerings are being accepted. Receiving ghee from a Brahmin or deity
  signals the bestowal of wisdom and spiritual nourishment. Spoiled ghee warns of corrupted
  intentions in one's devotional practice.

#### Guru/Brihaspati (Jupiter)
- **Sanskrit**: गुरु / बृहस्पति
- **Classification**: Auspicious (Śubha)
- **Interpretation**: Guru (Bṛhaspati) is the deva-guru, the teacher of the gods and the
  graha of wisdom, expansion, and dharma. Dreaming of a radiant Guru figure — whether the
  planetary deity or one's own spiritual teacher — signals the descent of divine grace
  (guru-kṛpā). This dream often precedes significant spiritual breakthroughs, initiations,
  or the meeting of a true teacher. An obscured Guru warns of poor judgment or straying
  from the guidance one has received.

### H

#### Hamsa/Swan (Haṃsa)
- **Sanskrit**: हंस
- **Classification**: Auspicious (Śubha)
- **Interpretation**: The haṃsa is the vāhana of Brahmā and Sarasvatī and symbolizes the
  individual soul (jīvātman) that can separate truth from falsehood, just as the mythic
  swan separates milk from water. Dreaming of a haṃsa signals spiritual discernment
  awakening. A haṃsa flying represents the soul's journey toward liberation (mokṣa).
  The Paramahaṃsa — the supreme swan — is the title of the fully liberated sage; dreaming
  of one indicates proximity to that state.

#### Hanuman (Hanumān)
- **Sanskrit**: हनुमान्
- **Classification**: Auspicious (Śubha)
- **Interpretation**: Hanumān — the supreme devotee (parama-bhakta) of Rāma — embodies
  selfless service (sevā), courage, and the power of devotion to overcome any obstacle.
  Dreaming of Hanumān signals protection from malefic forces, especially those of Śani
  (Saturn) and Rāhu. Hanumān carrying the sañjīvanī mountain promises that a seemingly
  impossible recovery — of health, relationship, or fortune — is at hand. Flying Hanumān
  indicates the awakening of prāṇic energy.

#### Himalaya (Himālaya)
- **Sanskrit**: हिमालय
- **Classification**: Auspicious (Śubha)
- **Interpretation**: The Himālaya is the abode of Śiva and Pārvatī, the father of Gaṅgā
  and Pārvatī in Puranic cosmology. Dreaming of the Himalayan peaks signals spiritual
  elevation and the approach of divine grace. Climbing the Himālaya in a dream is a
  pilgrimage of the soul; reaching the summit indicates the culmination of sādhanā. Snow-covered
  peaks specifically represent the pure, sattvic state of consciousness.

#### Honey (Madhu)
- **Sanskrit**: मधु
- **Classification**: Auspicious (Śubha)
- **Interpretation**: Honey is one of the five nectars (pañcāmṛta) and represents the sweet
  fruit of righteous living. Dreaming of honey signals the approach of prosperity, sweet
  speech, and the ripening of good karma. Offering honey to a deity in a dream amplifies
  the auspiciousness. The Ṛgveda describes honey as the essence of all herbs; honey in a
  dream may also signal healing, particularly of emotional wounds.

#### Horse (Aśva)
- **Sanskrit**: अश्व
- **Classification**: Mixed (Miśra)
- **Interpretation**: The horse is the vāhana of the Aśvins (the divine twin healers) and
  represents vital energy (prāṇa) and power. A white horse signals spiritual aspiration
  and the controlled force of sādhanā. A galloping horse indicates rapid progress or
  uncontrollable circumstances. The Upaniṣads compare the senses to horses; a well-controlled
  horse in a dream signifies mastery over the indriyas (senses), while a runaway horse warns
  of sensory indulgence.

### I

#### Indra
- **Sanskrit**: इन्द्र
- **Classification**: Mixed (Miśra)
- **Interpretation**: Indra — the king of the devas and lord of rain — appearing in a dream
  signals the activation of power and authority in the dreamer's life. Indra with his vajra
  (thunderbolt) promises victory but also warns of the dangers of pride, as Indra himself
  fell due to ahiṃkāra (ego). Rain sent by Indra is auspicious; Indra in conflict with
  a demon signals a confrontation with inner obstacles.

### J

#### Jasmine (Mallikā)
- **Sanskrit**: मल्लिका
- **Classification**: Auspicious (Śubha)
- **Interpretation**: Jasmine flowers are sacred to Viṣṇu and are essential in temple worship.
  Dreaming of jasmine signifies purity, divine love, and the fragrance of spiritual merit
  (puṇya) attracting blessings. Wearing jasmine garlands in a dream indicates that the
  dreamer is under the protective grace of a deity. White jasmine specifically signals
  sattvic purity; wilting jasmine warns that devotional practice is being neglected.

### K

#### Kailash Mountain (Kailāsa)
- **Sanskrit**: कैलास
- **Classification**: Prophetic (Bhāvika)
- **Interpretation**: Mount Kailāsa — the abode of Śiva — appearing in a dream is one of
  the most exalted Bhāvika visions possible. It signals that the dreamer's consciousness
  is approaching the highest state of Śiva-darśana. Reaching Kailāsa in a dream indicates
  the culmination of spiritual striving and the grace of Śiva descending. The Skanda Purana
  states that even the desire to see Kailāsa in a dream generates immense merit.

#### Kali (Kālī)
- **Sanskrit**: काली
- **Classification**: Mixed (Miśra)
- **Interpretation**: Goddess Kālī — the fierce form of the Divine Mother who embodies time
  (kāla), death, and ultimate liberation — appears in dreams to signal radical transformation.
  Her garland of skulls represents destroyed ego; her protruding tongue signifies the power
  to consume negativity. If the dreamer feels devotion rather than terror, this is an
  auspicious sign of impending liberation from a binding situation. Pure terror without
  devotion indicates resistance to necessary change.

#### Kalasha (Kalaśa)
- **Sanskrit**: कलश
- **Classification**: Auspicious (Śubha)
- **Interpretation**: The kalaśa — a sacred pot filled with water and topped with mango leaves
  and a coconut — is the symbol of abundance, the womb of creation, and the presence of all
  sacred waters. Dreaming of a kalaśa signals the arrival of all auspicious things
  simultaneously. It is especially significant before beginnings: new ventures, marriages,
  or spiritual initiations. A cracked or empty kalaśa warns that the foundation of a new
  endeavor is unstable.

#### Kamandalu (Kamaṇḍalu)
- **Sanskrit**: कमण्डलु
- **Classification**: Auspicious (Śubha)
- **Interpretation**: The water-pot carried by sages and ascetics represents renunciation,
  contentment, and the sustenance provided by spiritual life alone. Dreaming of holding a
  kamaṇḍalu signals a call toward simplicity and detachment. Receiving a kamaṇḍalu from a
  sage or deity indicates spiritual initiation or the blessing of an ascetic order. Water
  flowing endlessly from the kamaṇḍalu signals inexhaustible spiritual grace.

#### Kartikeya (Kārttikeya / Skanda)
- **Sanskrit**: कार्त्तिकेय / स्कन्द
- **Classification**: Auspicious (Śubha)
- **Interpretation**: Kārttikeya — the commander of the deva army and son of Śiva —
  represents valor, righteous warfare, and the victory of dharma over adharma. Dreaming
  of Kārttikeya with his spear (śakti) signals the courage to face and defeat inner
  demons. His peacock vāhana indicates the transformation of ego (the serpent the peacock
  devours) into spiritual beauty. This dream often appears before significant confrontations
  that require moral courage.

#### Ketu
- **Sanskrit**: केतु
- **Classification**: Mixed (Miśra)
- **Interpretation**: Ketu — the descending lunar node, the headless body of the demon
  Rāhu — represents mokṣa (liberation), detachment, and the dissolution of ego. Dreaming
  of Ketu signals a period of spiritual intensity, often accompanied by the loss of
  material attachments. Ketu's influence can manifest as sudden, inexplicable events that
  redirect the dreamer toward spiritual life. Favorable Ketu dreams include meditation
  visions; unfavorable ones include confusion and disorientation.

#### Krishna (Kṛṣṇa)
- **Sanskrit**: कृष्ण
- **Classification**: Prophetic (Bhāvika)
- **Interpretation**: Lord Kṛṣṇa — the complete avatāra (pūrṇāvatāra) of Viṣṇu — appearing
  in a dream is the supreme expression of divine love and cosmic play (līlā). Kṛṣṇa with
  the flute signals the call of the divine attracting the soul; the Bhāgavata Purana compares
  the flute's sound to the voice of God. Kṛṣṇa as Govinda with cows promises protection
  and abundance. Kṛṣṇa as the charioteer (in the Gītā posture) signals that divine guidance
  is available for a critical decision.

#### Kundalini Rising (Kuṇḍalinī)
- **Sanskrit**: कुण्डलिनी
- **Classification**: Prophetic (Bhāvika)
- **Interpretation**: Dreaming of Kuṇḍalinī — the serpent power coiled at the mūlādhāra —
  rising through the chakras is one of the most significant experiences in Tantric and Yogic
  dream lore. The rising may manifest as a luminous serpent ascending the spine, as inner
  light, or as overwhelming bliss. This is a Bhāvika dream of the highest order, indicating
  that the dreamer's spiritual energy is activating. However, Kuṇḍalinī rising without
  proper preparation may manifest as nightmares, indicating the need for guru-guidance.

### L

#### Lakshmi (Lakṣmī)
- **Sanskrit**: लक्ष्मी
- **Classification**: Auspicious (Śubha)
- **Interpretation**: Goddess Lakṣmī — seated on a lotus with gold coins flowing from her
  hand — is the divine source of all prosperity, both material and spiritual. Her darśana
  in a dream promises incoming abundance and the resolution of financial difficulties.
  Lakṣmī with elephants (Gaja-Lakṣmī) is the most auspicious form, signifying royal
  fortune. Lakṣmī leaving a place in a dream, however, is a grave warning of impending
  loss and the need for dharmic action to restore balance.

#### Lion (Siṃha)
- **Sanskrit**: सिंह
- **Classification**: Auspicious (Śubha)
- **Interpretation**: The lion is the vāhana of Durgā and an emblem of royal power and
  divine courage. Dreaming of a lion signals the awakening of inner strength and the power
  to confront adversity. A calm, regal lion indicates protection from the Goddess; a lion
  roaring signals an imminent declaration of truth or a call to action. Narasiṃha — the
  man-lion avatāra of Viṣṇu — appearing in a dream promises protection against the most
  dangerous and seemingly invincible enemies.

#### Lotus (Padma)
- **Sanskrit**: पद्म
- **Classification**: Auspicious (Śubha)
- **Interpretation**: The lotus (padma) is the paramount symbol of spiritual purity in
  Hindu tradition — rooted in mud, yet rising unstained above the water. Dreaming of a
  blooming lotus signals the unfolding of spiritual consciousness and the emergence of
  beauty from difficult circumstances. A closed lotus indicates potential not yet realized.
  The thousand-petaled lotus (sahasrāra-padma) at the crown of the head represents the
  highest state of enlightenment; dreaming of it is a supreme Bhāvika vision.

### M

#### Mala/Prayer Beads (Mālā)
- **Sanskrit**: माला
- **Classification**: Auspicious (Śubha)
- **Interpretation**: A mālā — the string of 108 beads used for mantra counting — in a dream
  represents continuity of spiritual practice (sādhanā) and the cycle of devotion. Receiving
  a mālā from a guru signals formal initiation and the transmission of mantra-śakti. Counting
  beads in a dream indicates that the dreamer's prayers are being tallied and their cumulative
  merit is reaching fruition. A broken mālā warns of interrupted practice or broken vows.

#### Mango (Āmra)
- **Sanskrit**: आम्र
- **Classification**: Auspicious (Śubha)
- **Interpretation**: The mango is the king of fruits in Indian tradition and a symbol of
  love, fertility, and auspiciousness. Dreaming of ripe mangoes signals the sweet fruition
  of efforts and the arrival of joy. The mango leaf is essential in all auspicious rituals;
  dreaming of a mango-leaf torana (doorway decoration) indicates celebration and welcome.
  Offering a mango to a deity in a dream carries special merit. Unripe mangoes warn that
  patience is still required.

#### Mangala (Mars)
- **Sanskrit**: मङ्गल
- **Classification**: Mixed (Miśra)
- **Interpretation**: Maṅgala — the fiery planet of courage, conflict, and energy — appearing
  in a dream signals a period of action, assertion, or confrontation. A favorable Maṅgala
  (red but radiant) promises victory through courage and righteous anger. An unfavorable
  Maṅgala (dark red, threatening) warns of accidents, arguments, or blood-related issues.
  Maṅgala's energy governs the maṇipūra (solar plexus) chakra; this dream often correlates
  with the activation of personal will-power.

#### Meditation (Dhyāna)
- **Sanskrit**: ध्यान
- **Classification**: Auspicious (Śubha)
- **Interpretation**: Dreaming of oneself in deep meditation (dhyāna) — seated in padmāsana
  with closed eyes — indicates that the dreamer's consciousness is accessing the ānandamaya
  kośa, the bliss-sheath closest to the Ātman. This dream often occurs during periods of
  intense sādhanā and signals genuine spiritual progress. Seeing another person (especially
  a known sage) meditating may be an invitation to deepen one's own practice. The specific
  object of meditation in the dream carries additional significance.

#### Milk (Kṣīra)
- **Sanskrit**: क्षीर
- **Classification**: Auspicious (Śubha)
- **Interpretation**: Milk is one of the pañcāmṛta (five nectars) and symbolizes purity,
  nourishment, and the blessings of gomātā. Dreaming of drinking warm milk signals spiritual
  and physical nourishment arriving. Milk overflowing from a vessel indicates abundance
  that exceeds containment — blessings spilling over. Boiling milk signals transformation
  through the fire of discipline. Sour or spilled milk warns of neglected opportunities
  or corrupted relationships that once were nourishing.

#### Monkey (Vānara)
- **Sanskrit**: वानर
- **Classification**: Mixed (Miśra)
- **Interpretation**: The vānara — monkey — carries dual significance: as Hanumān's species,
  it represents supreme devotion and service; in its ordinary aspect, it represents the
  restless, undisciplined mind (the "monkey mind" of Buddhist and Hindu psychology). A calm,
  devoted monkey signals Hanumān's blessing; a troop of chaotic monkeys warns of mental
  agitation, gossip, and the need to discipline the mind through sādhanā.

#### Moon/Chandra (Candra)
- **Sanskrit**: चन्द्र
- **Classification**: Mixed (Miśra)
- **Interpretation**: The Moon (Candra) governs the mind (manas), emotions, and the subtle
  body in Vedic astrology and Ayurveda. A bright full moon in a dream signals mental clarity,
  emotional fulfillment, and the blessings of the Divine Mother. A waning or eclipsed moon
  warns of mental disturbance, depression, or the draining of emotional energy. The moon
  also represents the soma (nectar of immortality); moonlight bathing the dreamer indicates
  the descent of spiritual nectar from the sahasrāra.

#### Mount Meru (Meru)
- **Sanskrit**: मेरु
- **Classification**: Prophetic (Bhāvika)
- **Interpretation**: Mount Meru — the cosmic axis mundi at the center of all universes in
  Puranic cosmology — appearing in a dream is a supreme Bhāvika vision. It signals the
  alignment of the dreamer's life with cosmic order (ṛta) and the activation of the
  suṣumnā nāḍī as the central axis of spiritual ascent. The Meru is also the abode of
  Brahmā at its summit; reaching it in a dream indicates proximity to the highest realization.

#### Mudra/Hands in Mudrā
- **Sanskrit**: मुद्रा
- **Classification**: Auspicious (Śubha)
- **Interpretation**: Hands forming a mudrā (sacred gesture) in a dream — whether one's own
  hands or a deity's — signals the activation of specific subtle energies. The abhaya mudrā
  (fear-not gesture) promises divine protection. The varada mudrā (boon-giving gesture)
  signals that a prayer is being answered. The jñāna mudrā (wisdom gesture) indicates
  intellectual and spiritual awakening. Deities displaying mudrās in dreams transmit
  specific blessings through the dreamer's subtle body.

### N

#### Naga/Serpent (Nāga)
- **Sanskrit**: नाग
- **Classification**: Mixed (Miśra)
- **Interpretation**: The Nāga — the divine serpent — embodies the most complex symbolism in
  Hindu dream lore. Nāgas are both guardians of hidden treasure (spiritual and material) and
  symbols of Kuṇḍalinī energy. A calm, coiled serpent signals spiritual power ready to
  awaken; a hooded Nāga protecting the dreamer signals the blessing of Nāga-devatās. Being
  bitten by a serpent may signal the sudden awakening of Kuṇḍalinī or, alternatively, a
  nāga-dosha requiring remedy. A multi-headed serpent (Śeṣa Nāga) is a Viṣṇu-associated
  vision of cosmic support.

#### Neem (Nimba)
- **Sanskrit**: निम्ब
- **Classification**: Mixed (Miśra)
- **Interpretation**: Neem is the great purifier of Āyurvedic medicine, sacred to the
  Goddess and to Durgā worship. Dreaming of a neem tree signals healing and the purging
  of toxins — physical or karmic. Chewing neem leaves in a dream indicates bitter medicine
  for a real ailment, but medicine that will be effective. A neem tree near a temple is
  auspicious; neem in a barren landscape warns that the healing process will be difficult
  but necessary.

### O

#### Om (Oṃ / Praṇava)
- **Sanskrit**: ओम् / प्रणव
- **Classification**: Prophetic (Bhāvika)
- **Interpretation**: Hearing or seeing the syllable Om (ॐ) in a dream is the most universal
  and powerful Bhāvika vision in Hindu tradition. The Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad identifies Om with
  all four states of consciousness; its appearance in a dream signals the dreamer's contact
  with the fourth state (turīya) — pure awareness beyond waking, dreaming, and deep sleep.
  This dream often marks a turning point in the dreamer's spiritual life and may precede
  significant initiations or realizations.

### P

#### Parrot (Śuka)
- **Sanskrit**: शुक
- **Classification**: Mixed (Miśra)
- **Interpretation**: The parrot (śuka) is associated with Kāmadēva (as his vāhana) and
  with the sage Śuka, the narrator of the Bhāgavata Purana. A talking parrot in a dream
  signals messages from the divine realm, often through the medium of sacred scripture or
  guru-vākya (the teacher's words). A parrot repeating a mantra is especially auspicious.
  A caged parrot warns that spiritual knowledge is being hoarded rather than lived.

#### Peacock (Mayūra)
- **Sanskrit**: मयूर
- **Classification**: Auspicious (Śubha)
- **Interpretation**: The peacock is the vāhana of Kārttikeya and a form of Kṛṣṇa's feather
  ornament. Dreaming of a peacock with its tail fully displayed signals beauty, pride, and
  the revelation of hidden spiritual splendor. The peacock's ability to transform poison
  (the snakes it eats) into beauty is a powerful metaphor for alchemical spiritual
  transformation. A peacock dancing in rain is a supreme omen of joy following sorrow.

#### Peepal Tree (Aśvattha)
- **Sanskrit**: अश्वत्थ
- **Classification**: Auspicious (Śubha)
- **Interpretation**: The aśvattha (peepal/sacred fig) is the cosmic tree of the Bhagavad
  Gītā (15.1), said to have its roots above and branches below, representing the material
  world as an inverted reflection of spiritual reality. Dreaming of a peepal tree signals
  connection to the cosmic order and the imperishable (akṣara) reality behind appearances.
  Circumambulating a peepal in a dream generates the same merit as doing so in waking life,
  according to the Padma Purana.

#### Prasad (Prasāda)
- **Sanskrit**: प्रसाद
- **Classification**: Auspicious (Śubha)
- **Interpretation**: Receiving prasāda — sanctified food offered to and returned by a deity —
  in a dream is a direct transmission of divine grace (prasāda-śakti). The type of prasāda
  matters: receiving a laddu signals Gaṇeśa's blessing of obstacle removal; receiving
  tulsī leaves signals Viṣṇu's protection; receiving vibhūti signals Śiva's grace. Eating
  prasāda in a dream purifies the subtle body at the annamaya and prāṇamaya levels.

#### Puja (Pūjā)
- **Sanskrit**: पूजा
- **Classification**: Auspicious (Śubha)
- **Interpretation**: Performing pūjā (ritual worship) in a dream carries nearly the same
  spiritual merit as performing it while awake, according to the Agni Purana. This dream
  signals the dreamer's deep devotional impulse (bhāva) and the deity's receptivity to it.
  Performing pūjā to a specific deity indicates that deity's active involvement in the
  dreamer's life. Incomplete or interrupted pūjā warns of obstacles in spiritual practice
  or a lack of wholeheartedness in devotion.

### R

#### Rahu
- **Sanskrit**: राहु
- **Classification**: Inauspicious (Aśubha)
- **Interpretation**: Rāhu — the ascending lunar node, the severed head of the demon
  Svarbhānu — represents illusion (māyā), obsession, and the shadow side of ambition.
  Dreaming of Rāhu signals periods of confusion, deception, and unexpected upheavals.
  Rāhu eclipsing the sun or moon warns of mental or spiritual obscuration. However,
  for those on a Tantric path, Rāhu's appearance can signal the deliberate confrontation
  with illusion that leads to liberation. Rāhu dreams during Rāhu-kāla are intensified.

#### Rama (Rāma)
- **Sanskrit**: राम
- **Classification**: Prophetic (Bhāvika)
- **Interpretation**: Lord Rāma — the maryādā-puruṣottama (the perfect man of dharma) —
  appearing in a dream signals the restoration of righteousness and the promise of victorious
  return from exile (any difficult period). Rāma with Sītā signals harmony in relationships
  and the reunion of separated loved ones. Rāma with the bow (kodaṇḍa) signals the power
  to destroy adharma. The Rāmāyaṇa states that Rāma's name alone destroys all sins; dreaming
  of hearing "Rām" is equivalent to chanting it a thousand times.

#### Rudraksha (Rudrākṣa)
- **Sanskrit**: रुद्राक्ष
- **Classification**: Auspicious (Śubha)
- **Interpretation**: Rudrākṣa — the "tears of Rudra" (Śiva) — are the sacred seeds worn
  by Śaiva devotees. Dreaming of rudrākṣa beads signals Śiva's compassion descending as
  protective grace. Receiving a rudrākṣa from a sage or deity indicates spiritual initiation.
  The number of faces (mukhi) on the seed carries specific significance in a dream: a
  five-faced rudrākṣa (pañca-mukhi) is most common and signals overall protection; a
  one-faced (eka-mukhi) is the rarest and signals supreme realization.

### S

#### Saffron (Kesar / Kuṅkuma)
- **Sanskrit**: केसर / कुङ्कुम
- **Classification**: Auspicious (Śubha)
- **Interpretation**: Saffron (kesar) and vermillion (kuṅkuma) are sacred to the Goddess
  and to solar deities. Dreaming of saffron-colored robes or tilaka signals renunciation,
  the fire of tapas, and spiritual authority. Applying kuṅkuma to a deity's feet in a
  dream is an act of supreme devotion carrying great merit. A saffron flag signals the
  victory of dharma. Saffron dissolved in milk signals the sweetening of austerity through
  divine grace.

#### Samsara Cycle (Saṃsāra)
- **Sanskrit**: संसार
- **Classification**: Mixed (Miśra)
- **Interpretation**: Dreaming of the wheel of saṃsāra — the endless cycle of birth, death,
  and rebirth driven by karma — signals the soul's deep awareness of its bondage and the
  longing for liberation (mokṣa). Seeing the wheel turning indicates that karmic processes
  are active and unresolved. Seeing the wheel stop or break is one of the most exalted
  Bhāvika visions, indicating the approaching end of the cycle of rebirth for the dreamer.

#### Sandalwood (Candana)
- **Sanskrit**: चन्दन
- **Classification**: Auspicious (Śubha)
- **Interpretation**: Sandalwood (candana) — the cooling, fragrant paste sacred to Viṣṇu
  and all deities — in a dream signals the cooling of worldly passions and the arrival of
  spiritual peace. Applying sandalwood paste to a deity or to one's own forehead indicates
  the opening of the ājñā chakra and the blessing of discernment. The fragrance of
  sandalwood in a dream — even without seeing the source — is an auspicious omen of
  approaching spiritual grace.

#### Saraswati (Sarasvatī)
- **Sanskrit**: सरस्वती
- **Classification**: Auspicious (Śubha)
- **Interpretation**: Goddess Sarasvatī — seated on a lotus with the vīṇā and sacred texts —
  is the divine source of all knowledge, music, and eloquence. Her darśana in a dream signals
  the awakening of creative intelligence and the blessing of learning. Playing the vīṇā in
  her presence indicates artistic inspiration flowing from the divine. Sarasvatī during
  Vasant Panchami is especially auspicious for students and artists. She turning away
  warns of intellectual arrogance or the misuse of knowledge.

#### Shani (Śani / Saturn)
- **Sanskrit**: शनि
- **Classification**: Inauspicious (Aśubha)
- **Interpretation**: Śani — the grim planet of karma, discipline, and justice — appearing
  in a dream signals a period of testing, restriction, and the reckoning of past actions.
  Śani is not evil but is the strict teacher who enforces karmic law. Dreaming of Śani
  with dark complexion, limp, and iron staff warns of Sāde Sāti or Śani-dhaiyā periods.
  However, Śani blessing the dreamer (a rare vision) signals that karmic debts are being
  cleared and that the period of hardship is ending. Worshipping Hanumān or lighting a
  sesame-oil lamp are prescribed remedies.

#### Shiva (Śiva)
- **Sanskrit**: शिव
- **Classification**: Prophetic (Bhāvika)
- **Interpretation**: Lord Śiva — in meditation, as Naṭarāja the cosmic dancer, or as the
  liṅga — appearing in a dream signals the most profound spiritual transformation possible.
  Śiva in deep meditation promises the grace of stillness and detachment. Śiva as Naṭarāja
  performing the Tāṇḍava signals cosmic dissolution and re-creation in the dreamer's life.
  The Śiva Liṅga in a dream is the formless principle of creation; worshiping it signals
  the dissolution of ego. Śiva with Pārvatī signals the union of consciousness and energy.

#### Shukra (Venus)
- **Sanskrit**: शुक्र
- **Classification**: Auspicious (Śubha)
- **Interpretation**: Śukra — the planet of beauty, love, wealth, and the guru of the asuras —
  appearing in a dream signals the activation of creative and romantic energies. A favorable
  Śukra promises artistic success, harmonious relationships, and material refinement. An
  unfavorable Śukra (associated with excess, indulgence, or deception) warns of attachment
  to pleasure leading to downfall. Śukra's energy governs the svādhiṣṭhāna (sacral) chakra.

#### Sun/Surya (Sūrya)
- **Sanskrit**: सूर्य
- **Classification**: Auspicious (Śubha)
- **Interpretation**: Sūrya — the solar deity and the visible form of the Absolute — in a
  dream signals illumination, vitality, and the dawning of spiritual awareness. The Gāyatrī
  Mantra invokes Sūrya's light; dreaming of the rising sun indicates the Gāyatrī's blessing
  activating in the dreamer's life. A bright midday sun signals the peak of power and clarity.
  An eclipsed or setting sun warns of declining vitality or the obscuration of truth by
  Rāhu's influence. Sūrya's seven horses represent the seven chakras; all seven visible
  is a supreme Bhāvika omen.

### T

#### Temple (Mandira / Devalaya)
- **Sanskrit**: मन्दिर / देवालय
- **Classification**: Auspicious (Śubha)
- **Interpretation**: A temple in a dream represents the body as the house of the divine —
  "deho devalaya" — and signals the presence of sacred energy in the dreamer's life. Entering
  a temple indicates spiritual seeking finding its home; being unable to enter signals
  self-created barriers to devotion. A temple with the deity present is supremely auspicious;
  an empty temple warns of spiritual dryness. The specific deity of the temple determines
  the precise nature of the blessing.

#### Third Eye (Tri-netra)
- **Sanskrit**: त्रिनेत्र
- **Classification**: Prophetic (Bhāvika)
- **Interpretation**: The third eye of Śiva — the ājñā chakra located between the eyebrows —
  appearing in a dream (whether on Śiva's face or perceived on one's own) signals the awakening
  of intuitive perception beyond ordinary sight. Śiva's third eye opening indicates that
  ignorance is about to be burned away by direct insight. Experiencing the third eye on
  oneself indicates that the dreamer's ājñā chakra is activating. This is a Bhāvika dream
  of profound spiritual significance, often preceding major realizations.

#### Tilak (Tilaka)
- **Sanskrit**: तिलक
- **Classification**: Auspicious (Śubha)
- **Interpretation**: The tilaka — the sacred mark on the forehead — in a dream signals divine
  protection and spiritual identity. A tripundra (three horizontal lines of vibhūti) indicates
  Śaiva blessings; a ūrdhva-puṇḍra (U-shaped Viṣṇu mark) signals Vaiṣṇava protection;
  a red kuṅkuma dot signals the Goddess's grace. Receiving a tilaka from a priest or deity
  is a formal bestowal of spiritual authority. A tilaka fading or washing away warns that
  one's commitment to dharma is weakening.

#### Trishul (Triśūla)
- **Sanskrit**: त्रिशूल
- **Classification**: Auspicious (Śubha)
- **Interpretation**: Śiva's trident (triśūla) represents the three fundamental energies —
  creation (sṛṣṭi), preservation (sthiti), and dissolution (saṃhāra) — and the three
  guṇas (sattva, rajas, tamas). Dreaming of the triśūla signals that all three aspects of
  cosmic power are active in the dreamer's life. Holding the triśūla indicates empowerment
  and protection. The triśūla also represents the iḍā, piṅgalā, and suṣumnā nāḍīs; its
  appearance in a dream signals the alignment of all three energy channels.

#### Tulsi (Tulasī)
- **Sanskrit**: तुलसी
- **Classification**: Auspicious (Śubha)
- **Interpretation**: Tulasī — holy basil — is the earthly manifestation of Lakṣmī and the
  most sacred plant in Vaiṣṇava tradition. Dreaming of a tulasī plant signals the presence
  of Viṣṇu's consort and the assurance of divine protection. Watering a tulsī plant in a
  dream is an act of worship that generates the same merit as serving Viṣṇu directly. A
  tulsī mālā (prayer beads made from tulsī wood) signals pure devotion. A wilting tulsī
  plant warns of neglected spiritual obligations.

### V

#### Vishnu (Viṣṇu)
- **Sanskrit**: विष्णु
- **Classification**: Prophetic (Bhāvika)
- **Interpretation**: Lord Viṣṇu — the preserver of cosmic order, reclining on Śeṣa Nāga
  upon the cosmic ocean — appearing in a dream is one of the highest Bhāvika visions,
  promising divine protection and the restoration of dharma. Viṣṇu with four arms holding
  śaṅkha, cakra, gadā, and padma signals complete divine intervention. Viṣṇu as Nārāyaṇa
  specifically promises mokṣa (liberation). The Garuda Purana states that Viṣṇu-darśana
  in a dream removes all karmic obstacles for seven births.

### Y

#### Yajna/Fire Sacrifice (Yajña)
- **Sanskrit**: यज्ञ
- **Classification**: Auspicious (Śubha)
- **Interpretation**: A yajña — Vedic fire sacrifice — in a dream represents the highest
  form of worship and the cosmic principle of self-offering. Performing or witnessing a yajña
  signals that the dreamer's prayers are reaching the celestial realm through the medium of
  Agni. The specific type of yajña matters: a homa signals daily purification; a grand Vedic
  yajña signals major life transformation. Smoke rising straight up from the yajña-kunḍa
  indicates acceptance of offerings by the devas.

#### Yama (Yama)
- **Sanskrit**: यम
- **Classification**: Inauspicious (Aśubha)
- **Interpretation**: Yama — the lord of death and the enforcer of dharma — appearing in a
  dream is one of the most feared visions in Swapna Shastra, but its interpretation requires
  nuance. Yama with his noose and buffalo signals a karmic reckoning — the end of a life-phase
  or the enforcement of dharmic justice. Yama merely passing by without approaching is a
  warning to correct one's conduct. Yama blessing the dreamer (an extremely rare vision)
  signals that even death has been conquered through dharma. The Katha Upaniṣad presents
  Yama as the supreme teacher of spiritual wisdom.

#### Yantra (Yantra)
- **Sanskrit**: यन्त्र
- **Classification**: Auspicious (Śubha)
- **Interpretation**: A yantra — the geometric diagram encoding a deity's energy — appearing
  in a dream signals the activation of that deity's specific power in the dreamer's life.
  The Śrī Yantra (nine interlocking triangles) is the most auspicious, representing the
  complete cosmos and the union of Śiva-Śakti. Dreaming of a Śrī Yantra signals supreme
  abundance and spiritual fulfillment. Drawing a yantra in a dream indicates the dreamer's
  own creative power aligning with cosmic geometry. An incomplete or distorted yantra warns
  that spiritual practice needs correction.

#### Yatra/Pilgrimage (Yātrā)
- **Sanskrit**: यात्रा
- **Classification**: Auspicious (Śubha)
- **Interpretation**: A pilgrimage (yātrā) in a dream — whether to a known tīrtha or an
  unknown sacred site — signals the soul's journey toward divine communion. The destination
  determines the specific blessing: a dream-pilgrimage to Vārāṇasī signals liberation,
  to Tirupati signals Lakṣmī's blessing, to Rāmeśvaram signals Rāma's grace. Reaching
  the destination indicates fulfillment; being lost on the way warns of distraction from
  the spiritual path. The Garuda Purana equates dream-pilgrimage with actual pilgrimage
  in spiritual merit.

## Usage Notes

- **Swapna Shastra** is one of the oldest continuous dream interpretation traditions in the
  world, with textual roots extending over 2,000 years.
- **Reversal principle**: Unlike Western dream traditions, Hindu dream interpretation does
  NOT universally apply a reversal principle. Bhāvika (prophetic) dreams are taken literally.
  However, some Kalpita (imagined) dreams may manifest opposite outcomes.
- **Context is paramount**: The same symbol can be Śubha or Aśubha depending on the dreamer's
  varṇa (social role), āśrama (life stage), ongoing dashā (planetary period), and the
  tithi/nakṣatra of the dream.
- **Seven-fold classification**: Always determine which of the seven dream types (Dṛṣṭa, Śruta,
  Anubhūta, Prārthita, Kalpita, Bhāvika, Doṣaja) the dream belongs to before interpreting.
  Only Bhāvika dreams carry reliable prophetic weight.
- **Remedial measures**: After Aśubha dreams, the Garuda Purana prescribes drinking water,
  reciting mantras (especially Om Namah Śivāya or Om Namo Nārāyaṇāya), and not narrating
  the dream to others. After Śubha dreams, maintain silence and gratitude.
- **Karmic framework**: Dreams are never random in the Vedic view — they are the language of
  karma communicating across the threshold of waking and sleeping consciousness.
- **Subtle body mapping**: Pay attention to chakra and kośa correlations. A dream of fire at
  the solar plexus is a maṇipūra activation; a dream of blue light at the throat is viśuddha.
  These somatic details add precision to interpretation.
