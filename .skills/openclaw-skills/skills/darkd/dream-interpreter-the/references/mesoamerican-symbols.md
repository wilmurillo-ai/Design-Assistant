## Methodology of Mesoamerican Dream Interpretation

### The Daykeeper (Tonalpouhqui / Aj Q'ij)

In both Aztec and Maya traditions, dreams were not interpreted by the dreamer alone but by a trained ritual specialist — the **tonalpouhqui** (Nahuatl, "one who reads the day-signs") or **aj q'ij** (K'iche' Maya, "daykeeper"). The daykeeper consulted the sacred calendar to determine which day-sign governed the night of the dream, then read the dream's imagery through the lens of that day's deity, animal, and directional associations. A dream of water on the day-sign Atl carried very different weight than the same dream on the day-sign Ollin. The daykeeper also considered the trecena (13-day week) and the year-bearer to determine whether the dream was a personal omen, a community warning, or a message from a specific deity.

### The Sacred Calendars: Tonalpohualli and Tzolk'in

The **Tonalpohualli** (Aztec 260-day sacred calendar) and its Maya equivalent the **Tzolk'in** are interlocking cycles of 20 day-signs and 13 numbers. Each day is governed by a deity and carries a specific tonal (vital energy). Dreams occurring on a given day-sign are filtered through that sign's character:

- **Favorable day-signs** (e.g., Quetzalcoatl–Ehecatl, Xochipilli–Flower): dreams tend toward blessings, creative openings, and ancestral guidance.
- **Unfavorable day-signs** (e.g., Tezcatlipoca–Death, Mictlantecuhtli–Dog): dreams may carry warnings, underworld summons, or demands for offerings.
- **Portentous day-signs** (e.g., Ollin–Movement, Quiahuitl–Rain): dreams are charged with transformation and cannot be ignored without consequence.

The 20 day-signs are listed individually in this reference with their specific dream significance.

### Dream Incubation

Mesoamerican peoples practiced deliberate dream incubation at sacred sites:

- **Aztec**: The Temple of Huitzilopochtli in Tenochtitlan served as an incubation site where priests and nobles slept to receive guidance from the war god. The Templo Mayor's twin shrines (Huitzilopochtli and Tlaloc) each offered different dream channels — martial/tributary versus agricultural/atmospheric.
- **Maya**: Cenotes (sacred sinkholes) served as portals to Xibalba and sites of dream incubation. The Cenote of Sacrifice at Chichén Itzá was the most renowned. Dreamers descended to the water's edge or floated on rafts, seeking visions from the underworld lords. Caves (che'en) throughout the Maya highlands served the same function.
- **Shared practice**: Before incubation, the dreamer fasted, offered blood (auto-sacrifice via maguey thorns), and burned copal incense to open the channel between worlds.

### The Three Souls and Dream Travel

Mesoamerican anthropology recognized multiple soul-forces that could separate from the body during sleep:

- **Tonalli** (Nahuatl, from *tona* "to irradiate, to warm"): The day-soul residing in the head, linked to one's birth day-sign and destiny. During dreams, the tonalli may travel, encounter deities, or be stolen by supernatural beings. Loss of tonalli during sleep causes illness — the daykeeper must perform a *tonalli recovery* ritual.
- **Teyolia** (Nahuatl, "heart-soul"): Residing in the heart, this is the emotional and moral center. It remains in the body during ordinary dreams but may depart during prophetic visions or near-death experiences. The teyolia's departure signals a calling from the gods.
- **Ihiyotl** (Nahuatl, "breath-soul"): Residing in the liver, associated with courage, passion, and bodily vigor. The ihiyotl can animate the dream-body and is the force behind sleep-walking and sleep-talking. Sorcerers (*tlahuelpuchtin*) were said to send their ihiyotl abroad during sleep to harm others.

A dream in which all three souls are engaged — the dreamer sees with tonalli-clarity, feels with teyolia-depth, and acts with ihiyotl-force — is considered a true **temiktli**, a divinely sent dream.

### Underworld Journeys in Dreams

Dreams of descending into the earth were interpreted as actual underworld journeys:

- **Mictlan** (Aztec): The nine-layer underworld through which the dead travel for four years. Each layer presents a trial — the crashing mountains (Apanohuayan), the river of blood (Itzicuintli Yan), the obsidian-bladed wind (Itzehéyan), and the final realm of Mictlantecuhtli. Dreaming of Mictlan without dying was considered a shamanic initiation; the dreamer returned with knowledge of the afterlife.
- **Xibalba** (Maya): The underworld of seven houses described in the Popol Vuh — Dark House, Razor House, Cold House, Jaguar House, Bat House, and the house of the Xibalba lords. Dreaming of entering Xibalba mirrors the Hero Twins' descent; survival in the dream signals that the dreamer has the cunning of Hunahpu and the trickster wisdom of Xbalanque.

### Blood and Sacrifice Symbolism

In Mesoamerican dream theory, a dream that demands something of the dreamer establishes a **covenant with the gods requiring reciprocity**. A deity who appears in a dream and offers a gift or warning expects a return offering — typically blood (auto-sacrifice), copal, or flowers. To ignore such a dream is to break the covenant, inviting misfortune. The Florentine Codex records that Moctezuma I dreamed of Huitzilopochtli demanding greater tribute; the subsequent New Fire Ceremony and military campaigns were the reciprocal fulfillment. Dreams of bleeding, sacrifice, or being offered to the gods are not necessarily negative — they may signal a spiritual obligation that, once honored, brings favor.

### Popol Vuh Dream References and Hero Twin Mythology

The Popol Vuh, the K'iche' Maya creation narrative, contains several dream-related episodes:

- **One Hunahpu and Seven Hunahpu**: The father and uncle of the Hero Twins descend to Xibalba after being summoned by the lords of death — a journey that mirrors dream descent.
- **Blood Moon's dream-conception**: The maiden Blood Moon (Xkik') becomes pregnant after the severed head of One Hunahpu spits into her hand from the calabash tree — a supernatural conception that dream-interpreters linked to dreams of miraculous impregnation.
- **The Hero Twins' trickery**: Hunahpu and Xbalanque outwit the Xibalba lords through disguise, cunning, and self-sacrifice — a template for interpreting dreams where the dreamer must outsmart a threatening figure rather than flee.
- **The resurrection of the maize god**: The Twins' death and rebirth as the maize plant is the central dream archetype of transformation through sacrifice.

### Florentine Codex Dream Omens (Book 5)

Fray Bernardino de Sahagún's Florentine Codex, Book 5 ("The Omens"), records Aztec dream omens collected from indigenous informants. Key examples:

- Dreaming of a **jaguar** signaled that the dreamer would die in war or sacrifice — an honorable death.
- Dreaming of **drowning** while not near water meant Tlaloc had claimed the person for the Tlalocan paradise.
- Dreaming of a **feather falling from the sky** was an omen of divine election.
- Dreaming of **one's own house burning** portended ruin or transformation of the household.
- Dreaming of **singing in an unknown language** marked the dreamer as possessed by a deity.

These omens were categorized by the daykeeper as *tlacelel* (deserved fortune), *tlahuelliloc* (evil fortune), or *mahuiztilia* (miraculous/wonderous fortune).

### Nahuatl Dream Terminology

| Nahuatl Term | English Meaning | Usage |
|---|---|---|
| **temiktli** | dream | The general term for a dream experience |
| **temiktiani** | dreamer | One who dreams; also a title for those who receive prophetic dreams |
| **cozcacuauhqui** | dream interpreter | Specialist who reads dreams through the calendar; literally "one who wears the necklace of speech" |
| **temictiani** | one who causes dreams | A deity or supernatural being who sends dreams |
| **tonalco** | daytime dream | A vision received during waking hours (daydream/epiphany) |
| **yohualco** | nighttime dream | The ordinary dream received during sleep |
| **tlacatiliztemictli** | birth-dream | A dream occurring at the moment of birth, determining tonalli |
| **mictlantemictli** | underworld dream | A dream of journeying to Mictlan; a shamanic death-vision |
| **teotemictli** | god-dream | A dream in which a deity appears and speaks — the most portentous category |

### Acatl (Reed) — Day-sign

- **Nahuatl**: Acatl · **Maya**: Ben
- **Day-sign**: 13th of the Tonalpohualli (Reed)
- **Fortune**: Neutral
- **Interpretation**: Reed symbolizes the axis mundi — the vertical pillar connecting underworld, earth, and sky. Dreaming of reeds or cane on the day-sign Acatl signals a need for moral uprightness; the reed bends but does not break. The trecena 1-Reed was governed by Quetzalcoatl-Chalchiuhtlicue, linking reed dreams to creative partnership. A broken reed in a dream warns of ethical compromise. The daykeepers taught that reed dreams call the dreamer back to their founding purpose.

### Ah Puch (Maya Death God)

- **Maya**: Ah Puch / Ah Cimih / Kisin
- **Day-sign**: Cimi (Death) in Tzolk'in
- **Fortune**: Portentous
- **Interpretation**: Ah Puch, the Maya lord of death and decomposition, appears in dreams as a skeletal figure with dangling eyes or a bloated corpse. His presence signals an inevitable ending that cannot be delayed, but also the fertility that comes from decay — the Popol Vuh teaches that from the bones of the dead, the maize was born. Dreaming of Ah Puch on a Cimi day is a summons to complete something that has been lingering. Offer copal and chocolate at a crossroads to acknowledge the omen.

### Amaranth (Huauhtli)

- **Nahuatl**: Huauhtli
- **Day-sign**: Centeotl-Xochipilli trecena
- **Fortune**: Favorable
- **Interpretation**: Amaranth seeds were mixed with honey and shaped into images of Huitzilopochtli during the feast of Panquetzaliztli, then eaten communally — a ritual the Spanish friars condemned as idolatrous. Dreaming of amaranth signifies sustenance that is both physical and sacred; the dreamer is being nourished by the gods directly. A dream of amaranth statues being broken warns of suppressed traditions or cultural loss. The daykeepers read amaranth dreams as confirmation that the dreamer's offerings have been accepted.

### Atl (Water) — Day-sign

- **Nahuatl**: Atl · **Maya**: Muluc
- **Day-sign**: 9th of the Tonalpohualli (Water)
- **Fortune**: Neutral (unfavorable if combined with 4-Movement)
- **Interpretation**: Water as a day-sign governs all fluid forces — rain, blood, tears, and the flow of events. Dreaming of water on the day-sign Atl amplifies the dream's prophetic power; the daykeepers considered Atl-dreams especially clear because water is the medium of Tlaloc's communication. However, the Florentine Codex warns that a dream of turbulent water on the day 4-Atl portends flood or disease. Calm water in an Atl-dream is among the most favorable omens, signaling that Tlalocan's gates are open to the dreamer.

### Atlatl (Spear-Thrower)

- **Nahuatl**: Atlatl (literally "water-thrower," from the hunting tradition)
- **Day-sign**: Associated with Mixcoatl-Camaxtli trecena
- **Fortune**: Favorable
- **Interpretation**: The atlatl, a levered dart-thrower, was the weapon of the hunting god Mixcoatl and a symbol of amplified intention — the same force, directed with precision, goes much farther. Dreaming of an atlatl signals that the dreamer's efforts are about to receive divine leverage. If the atlatl breaks in the dream, the dreamer's aim is wrong and must be recalibrated. The daykeepers read atlatl dreams as encouragement to act decisively, for the gods are extending the reach of the dreamer's will.

### Ballcourt (Tlachtli)

- **Nahuatl**: Tlachtli · **Maya**: Pitz
- **Day-sign**: Associated with Ollin (Movement) and the Hero Twin mythology
- **Fortune**: Portentous
- **Interpretation**: The ballcourt is the threshold between the middle world and the underworld, where the Hero Twins descended to challenge the lords of Xibalba. Dreaming of a ballcourt signals that the dreamer is entering a contest with forces beyond ordinary perception — a trial of skill, courage, or wit. The Popol Vuh shows that victory in the ballcourt comes not from brute strength but from cunning and self-sacrifice. A dream of scoring a goal is profoundly favorable; a dream of losing the ball to the court's serpent-rings warns of self-defeating behavior.

### Bat (Tzinacan)

- **Nahuatl**: Tzinacan · **Maya**: Zotz
- **Day-sign**: Zotz in Tzolk'in (Bat)
- **Fortune**: Unfavorable
- **Interpretation**: The bat inhabits the Popol Vuh's House of Bats (Camalzotz), where One Hunahpu's head was bitten off by the death bat Camazotz. Dreaming of bats signals descent into the most dangerous layer of Xibalba — the realm where identity itself is dismembered. The daykeepers warned that bat dreams often precede illness or psychic attack. However, if the bat in the dream flies upward into daylight, it signals that the dreamer has survived the darkest trial and will emerge transformed. Offer maguey thorns and blood at a cave entrance to neutralize the omen.

### Blood Offering

- **Nahuatl**: Yollohuetl ("heart-blood") · **Maya**: K'ik'el
- **Day-sign**: All days; especially 4-Rain, 1-Death
- **Fortune**: Portentous
- **Interpretation**: Blood is the currency of the gods; without it, the sun would stop and the world would end. Dreaming of giving blood — whether from auto-sacrifice or being wounded — signals that the dreamer has entered a covenant demanding reciprocity. The Florentine Codex records that blood-dreams were classified as *teotemictli* (god-dreams), the most serious category. A dream of receiving blood from another is favorable — it means the gods are feeding the dreamer's tonalli. A dream of blood on the ground, soaking into earth, confirms that a sacrifice has been accepted.

### Bone Mountain (Mixcoatl's Realm)

- **Nahuatl**: Itzmictlan (Place of Obsidian Death) / Tzonmolco (Bone Heap)
- **Day-sign**: Mictlantecuhtli's trecena (6-Death)
- **Fortune**: Unfavorable
- **Interpretation**: Bone Mountain is the penultimate layer of Mictlan, where the dead must cross a mountain that crashes together like giant jaws. Dreaming of a mountain of bones signals that the dreamer faces a narrowing passage in life — a situation where timing is critical and hesitation is fatal. The Florentine Codex describes this as the "crushing place" where only those who move with precision survive. The daykeepers read bone-mountain dreams as urgent: act now or be crushed. Offerings of white flowers and chalk mark the dreamer's safe passage.

### Butterfly (Papalotl)

- **Nahuatl**: Papalotl · **Maya**: Pepen
- **Day-sign**: Associated with Xochipilli-Flower trecena; also the deity Papaztac
- **Fortune**: Favorable
- **Interpretation**: The butterfly is the soul in flight — the tonalli liberated from the body, dancing between worlds. Aztec warriors who died in battle or sacrifice were said to become butterflies and hummingbirds, accompanying the sun across the sky. Dreaming of butterflies signals that the dreamer's tonalli is traveling freely and receiving direct visions. A dark or tattered butterfly warns that the tonalli is weakened or under attack. The daykeepers considered butterfly dreams especially potent when they occurred during the feast of Miccailhuitontli (Little Feast of the Dead).

### Cacao

- **Nahuatl**: Cacahuatl · **Maya**: Kakaw
- **Day-sign**: Associated with Ek Chuah (Maya merchant god) and Mok (Tzolk'in)
- **Fortune**: Favorable
- **Interpretation**: Cacao was the drink of the elite and the currency of the gods — literally used as money and as the foam of divine communion. Dreaming of cacao beans or chocolate drink signals abundance, social elevation, and the favor of the merchant god Ek Chuah. The Maya associated cacao with the axis of the world tree, and cacao dreams were read as messages from the cosmic center. A dream of spilling or wasting cacao warns of squandered resources. The daykeepers counseled that cacao dreams call for generosity: share what the gods have given.

### Calendar Stone (Tonalpohualli)

- **Nahuatl**: Tonalpohualli (the 260-day count) / Cuauhxicalli (eagle vessel — sun stone)
- **Day-sign**: All; the stone encodes all 20 day-signs
- **Fortune**: Portentous
- **Interpretation**: Dreaming of the calendar stone or the tonalpohualli itself signals that the dreamer has reached a calendrically significant moment — a "year-bearer" shift, a New Fire ceremony, or the completion of a 52-year cycle. The stone's face is Tonatiuh (the sun god) with Ollin (Movement) at its center, and the four previous world-ages in the surrounding panels. The daykeepers interpreted calendar-stone dreams as summons to assume cosmic responsibility: the dreamer is being asked to carry a cycle, not merely live within one.

### Calli (House) — Day-sign

- **Nahuatl**: Calli · **Maya**: Ak'b'al
- **Day-sign**: 3rd of the Tonalpohualli (House)
- **Fortune**: Neutral
- **Interpretation**: House as a day-sign represents shelter, containment, and the boundary between inside and outside. The night Teotleco (Return of the Gods) fell on 2-House, making Calli a day of divine habitation. Dreaming of a house on the day-sign Calli asks the dreamer to consider what they are housing — which deities, which ancestors, which unresolved forces dwell within. A dream of an empty house warns that the dreamer's tonalli is absent; a dream of a crowded house signals that too many obligations compete for the dreamer's vital force.

### Chac (Maya Rain God)

- **Maya**: Chac / Chaac
- **Day-sign**: Caban in Tzolk'in; associated with directional color-variants (East=Red, North=White, West=Black, South=Yellow)
- **Fortune**: Portentous
- **Interpretation**: Chac is the Maya lord of rain, lightning, and agriculture — four gods in one, each guarding a cardinal direction. Dreaming of Chac signals that atmospheric and agricultural forces are at work in the dreamer's life. A dream of Chac with his axe raised is favorable for farmers and anyone seeking fertility; a dream of Chac with empty hands warns of drought — literal or metaphorical. The daykeepers noted that Chac dreams during the dry season were urgent petitions from the god himself, requiring a pilgrimage to a cenote with offerings of copal and maize.

### Chalchiuhtlicue (Jade Skirt)

- **Nahuatl**: Chalchiuhtlicue (She of the Jade Skirt)
- **Day-sign**: Governs the trecena 1-Reed alongside Quetzalcoatl; also associated with Atl (Water)
- **Fortune**: Favorable (unfavorable if dream involves drowning)
- **Interpretation**: Chalchiuhtlicue is the goddess of flowing water — rivers, lakes, and the sea — and the patroness of those born on the day-sign Atl. She is simultaneously the nurturing mother who washes away impurity and the drowning force that takes those Tlaloc claims. Dreaming of a woman in a jade skirt standing in water is among the most favorable omens: the dreamer is being cleansed and renewed. A dream of being pulled underwater by jade-skirted arms signals that Chalchiuhtlicue has chosen the dreamer for Tlalocan, the paradise of the water-dead.

### Cenote

- **Maya**: Ts'onot
- **Day-sign**: Associated with Muluc (Water) in Tzolk'in
- **Fortune**: Portentous
- **Interpretation**: The cenote is the Maya navel of the world — a wound in the earth's surface opening into the sacred waters of Xibalba. Dreaming of a cenote signals direct access to the underworld and its powers. Looking down into a cenote and seeing one's own reflection means the dreamer's tonalli is being called below; diving into a cenote is a shamanic initiation. The Cenote of Sacrifice at Chichén Itzá was the site where the Maya cast jade, gold, and human offerings — dreaming of throwing something into a cenote means the dreamer must make a real sacrifice to close a spiritual debt.

### Centeotl (Maize God)

- **Nahuatl**: Centeotl (Sacred Maize Cob) · **Maya**: Hun Hunahpu
- **Day-sign**: 7th of the Tonalpohualli (Flower) — Centeotl governs this sign
- **Fortune**: Favorable
- **Interpretation**: Centeotl is the divine maize — the sacrificed god whose body becomes the staff of life. The Popol Vuh tells how the bones of Hun Hunahpu were ground by the Hero Twins and mixed with water to create the maize that feeds humanity. Dreaming of Centeotl or a maize cob growing from the earth signals nourishment, lineage continuity, and the fruit of sacrifice. A dream of withered or diseased maize warns that the dreamer has neglected the reciprocal relationship with the earth. The daykeepers read Centeotl dreams as affirmations that the dreamer's "planting" — whether of children, projects, or prayers — will bear fruit.

### Cipactli (Crocodile) — Day-sign

- **Nahuatl**: Cipactli · **Maya**: Imix
- **Day-sign**: 1st of the Tonalpohualli (Crocodile/Alligator)
- **Fortune**: Favorable (for beginnings)
- **Interpretation**: Cipactli is the primordial sea-monster from whose body the earth was created — the original sacrifice that made the world possible. Dreaming of a crocodile or alligator on the day-sign Cipactli signals a new beginning charged with primordial power. The trecena 1-Cipactli was governed by Tonacatecuhtli (Lord of Sustenance), making it the most auspicious time for initiating any venture. A dream of Cipactli opening its jaws means the earth itself is receiving the dreamer's prayers; a dream of being swallowed by Cipactli signals a total life transformation — death of the old self.

### Coatlicue (Serpent Skirt)

- **Nahuatl**: Coatlicue (She of the Serpent Skirt) / Teteoinnan (Mother of the Gods)
- **Day-sign**: Associated with the trecena 1-Lizard
- **Fortune**: Portentous
- **Interpretation**: Coatlicue is the earth-mother in her most terrible aspect — a goddess of both birth and death, wearing a skirt of writhing serpents and a necklace of human hearts and hands. She is the mother of Huitzilopochtli and the Coyolxauhqui who was dismembered. Dreaming of Coatlicue signals a confrontation with the primal feminine — the force that gives life and consumes it. The daykeepers read Coatlicue dreams as initiatory: the dreamer is being claimed by the earth itself. Such dreams demand a blood offering at a mountain shrine. A dream of Coatlicue smiling beneath her serpent skirt is profoundly favorable: the earth has accepted the dreamer as her child.

### Copal (Incense)

- **Nahuatl**: Copalli · **Maya**: Pom
- **Day-sign**: All; especially associated with Tezcatlipoca trecena
- **Fortune**: Favorable
- **Interpretation**: Copal is the blood of the trees, the smoke that feeds the gods and carries prayers to the sky. Dreaming of burning copal or smelling its resinous perfume signals that a channel of communication with the divine is open. The daykeepers considered copal-dreams affirmations: whatever the dreamer was praying for has been heard. A dream of copal that will not light, or smoke that falls instead of rising, warns that the dreamer's petitions are blocked by unaddressed obligation. The Florentine Codex records that copal was offered to all deities, making it the universal mediator in dream-covenants.

### Coyote

- **Nahuatl**: Coyotl · **Maya**: Ch'am
- **Day-sign**: Associated with Huehuecoyotl (Old Coyote), trecena 1-Flower
- **Fortune**: Neutral
- **Interpretation**: The coyote is the animal of Huehuecoyotl, the trickster god of music, dance, and mischief — a deity who could bring either fortune or chaos depending on his mood. Dreaming of a coyote signals the presence of trickster energy: something in the dreamer's life is not what it seems. The daykeepers taught that coyote dreams are invitations to lighten up, to dance with uncertainty rather than resist it. A singing coyote is especially favorable for artists and lovers; a snarling coyote warns of deception by someone who wears a friendly mask.

### Cozcacuauhqui (Vulture) — Day-sign

- **Nahuatl**: Cozcacuauhqui (Vulture with the Necklace) · **Maya**: Cib
- **Day-sign**: 16th of the Tonalpohualli (Vulture)
- **Fortune**: Favorable (for longevity and authority)
- **Interpretation**: The vulture is the great cleanser — the bird that transforms death into life by consuming what would rot. Cozcacuauhqui also shares her name with the dismembered goddess of the moon, Coyolxauhqui, whose body was scattered at the foot of Coatepec. Dreaming of a vulture on this day-sign signals the transformation of decay into wisdom; the dreamer has completed a long process of digestion and is ready to claim authority. The trecena 1-Vulture was governed by Xolotl, the dog-god who guides the dead, linking this sign to both death-navigation and longevity.

### Cuetzpalin (Lizard) — Day-sign

- **Nahuatl**: Cuetzpalin · **Maya**: Men
- **Day-sign**: 4th of the Tonalpohualli (Lizard)
- **Fortune**: Neutral
- **Interpretation**: The lizard is the creature of the wall — motionless and watching, then suddenly swift. Dreaming of a lizard on the day-sign Cuetzpalin signals a period of patient observation before decisive action. The trecena 1-Lizard was governed by Coatlicue, making lizard-dreams particularly connected to the earth-mother's watchful aspect. A dream of a lizard losing its tail and regrowing it promises renewal after loss. The daykeepers read lizard dreams as counsel: watch carefully, conserve energy, and strike when the moment is right.

### Deer (Mazatl) — Day-sign

- **Nahuatl**: Mazatl · **Maya**: Manik
- **Day-sign**: 7th of the Tonalpohualli (Deer)
- **Fortune**: Favorable (for journeys and the hunt)
- **Interpretation**: The deer is the fleet-footed messenger of the forest — the animal of the hunt and of pilgrimage. Dreaming of a deer on the day-sign Mazatl signals a journey, either physical or spiritual, that requires grace and alertness. The trecena 1-Deer was governed by Tlaloc and Xochipilli, linking deer-dreams to both the rain-god's expansive generosity and the flower-prince's aesthetic sensibility. A dream of a deer standing at a crossroads means the dreamer must choose a direction; a dream of a deer being hunted warns that the dreamer is being pursued by obligations they cannot outrun.

### Dog (Itzcuintli) — Day-sign

- **Nahuatl**: Itzcuintli / Xoloitzcuintli · **Maya**: Oc
- **Day-sign**: 10th of the Tonalpohualli (Dog)
- **Fortune**: Favorable (for guidance); Unfavorable (for death-omens)
- **Interpretation**: The dog is Xolotl's animal — the guide who leads the dead through Mictlan's nine layers. The Xoloitzcuintli (hairless dog) was bred specifically for this spiritual function and was buried with the dead to ensure safe passage. Dreaming of a dog on the day-sign Itzcuintli signals that a guide has appeared — the dreamer is not alone, even in the darkest passage. The Florentine Codex records that a dream of a black dog was an omen of death, while a red dog signaled the fire-god's protection. The daykeepers counseled: follow the dog in the dream, wherever it leads.

### Drum (Huehuetl)

- **Nahuatl**: Huehuetl (Upright Drum) / Teponaztli (Horizontal Slit Drum) · **Maya**: Pombil
- **Day-sign**: Associated with the trecena 1-Flower and the god Xochipilli
- **Fortune**: Favorable
- **Interpretation**: The drum is the heartbeat of the cosmos — the huehuetl's deep voice sustains the rhythm of the sun's journey, while the teponaztli's melodic tones call the gods to attend the ritual. Dreaming of a drum signals that the dreamer is being called to participate in the cosmic rhythm; a time of ceremony and communal action is approaching. The daykeepers considered drum-dreams especially favorable for those beginning a new cycle of commitment. A dream of a broken or silent drum warns that the dreamer has fallen out of sync with the community's pulse.

### Ehecatl (Wind) — Day-sign

- **Nahuatl**: Ehecatl · **Maya**: Ik
- **Day-sign**: 2nd of the Tonalpohualli (Wind)
- **Fortune**: Neutral
- **Interpretation**: Ehecatl is the wind-god aspect of Quetzalcoatl — the breath that moves the world, the invisible force that carries seeds, songs, and souls. Dreaming of wind on the day-sign Ehecatl signals that invisible forces are reshaping the dreamer's life; the change is real even though it cannot be seen. The trecena 1-Wind was governed by Quetzalcoatl in his creator aspect, making wind-dreams auspicious for creative work. A dream of a whirlwind warns of confusion and scattered energy; a dream of a steady, warm breeze confirms that the dreamer's tonalli is aligned with its purpose.

### Eagle (Cuauhtli) — Day-sign

Warriors who died in battle or sacrifice became eagles and butterflies accompanying the sun from zenith to setting. Dreaming of an eagle on the day-sign Cuauhtli signals martial courage, elevated perspective, and the favor of Huitzilopochtli. The trecena 1-Eagle was governed by Xipe Totec, linking eagle-dreams to the flayed god's themes of renewal through sacrifice. A dream of an eagle perched and watching means the dreamer is being assessed by a powerful force; an eagle in flight means the dreamer's moment has come.

### Feather (Quetzalli)

- **Fortune**: Favorable
- **Interpretation**: The feather is the bridge between earth and sky — the quetzal feather descending from the cloud forest was the most precious object in the Aztec world, and a feather falling from the sky was recorded in the Florentine Codex as an omen of divine election. Dreaming of a feather signals that a message is descending from the gods; the dreamer is being chosen or called. A black feather warns of Tezcatlipoca's attention; a green feather signals Quetzalcoatl's blessing; a red feather marks Huitzilopochtli's claim. The daykeepers read feather dreams as direct communications that require acknowledgment through offering.

### Fire Serpent (Xiuhcoatl)

- **Fortune**: Portentous
- **Interpretation**: The Xiuhcoatl is the weapon of Huitzilopochtli — the fire-serpent that carries the sun's fire across the sky and marks the turning of the 52-year calendar cycle. Dreaming of a fire serpent signals a cataclysmic transformation, equivalent to the New Fire Ceremony when all fires were extinguished and relit from a single flame kindled on a captive's chest. The daykeepers considered Xiuhcoatl dreams among the most powerful: the dreamer is being forged in cosmic fire. A dream of holding the Xiuhcoatl means the dreamer has been chosen to carry transformative power; a dream of being bitten by it means resistance to necessary change.

### Flower (Xochitl) — Day-sign

Dreaming of flowers on the day-sign Xochitl signals the culmination of a cycle in beauty and joy. The Aztec "flower songs" (*xochicuicatl*) were poetic compositions about the brevity of life and the ecstasy of existence — flower-dreams carry this same bittersweet awareness. A dream of withered flowers warns that aesthetic and emotional sensitivity have been neglected; a dream of flowers blooming from stone confirms that beauty can emerge from the harshest conditions.

### Flower / Xochitl (Ritual Element)

- **Day-sign**: Xochitl (20th sign); also associated with Xochiquetzal
- **Fortune**: Favorable
- **Interpretation**: As a ritual element, flowers are the speech of the earth — the way the ground speaks to the sky. Flowers were offered to every deity, strewn on temple steps, worn by priests and sacrificial victims, and scattered in processions. Dreaming of offering flowers signals that the dreamer's prayers are being beautified before the gods. The Florentine Codex records that dream-incubation at the temple of Xochiquetzal required offerings of marigold (*cempasuchil*) and tuberose (*omixochitl*). A dream of flowers with no scent warns that the dreamer's worship is form without substance.

The daykeepers read frog dreams as favorable for agriculture, fertility, and any creative project that requires "watering." A dream of a dead or silent frog warns that the channels of nourishment are blocked; the dreamer must clear emotional or spiritual blockage before growth can resume.

Dreaming of Hunab Ku, often perceived as a luminous void or a galactic spiral, signals a direct encounter with the unmanifest source. The daykeepers considered such dreams indications that the dreamer's destiny involves teaching or healing at the highest level. No offering is sufficient; the appropriate response is to live the vision.

### Hummingbird (Huitzil)

Dreaming of a hummingbird signals the presence of Huitzilopochtli and the warrior ethos: fight without regard for the odds, fueled by the nectar of purpose. ### Huitzilopochtli (Hummingbird-South)

- **Interpretation**: Huitzilopochtli is the patron deity of Tenochtitlan, the war-god who demands tribute in blood to keep the sun moving across the sky. He was born fully armed from Coatlicue and dismembered his sister Coyolxauhqui at Coatepec. Dreaming of Huitzilopochtli signals a call to action — the dreamer must defend, conquer, or lead. The Florentine Codex records that Moctezuma I dreamed of Huitzilopochtli demanding greater conquests, and the subsequent wars fulfilled the dream-covenant. A dream of Huitzilopochtli's serpent-fire weapon (Xiuhcoatl) means the dreamer has been armed for a specific battle; a dream of the god weeping warns that the sun itself is imperiled.

- **Fortune**: Favorable

He is often depicted as an aged lord with a Roman-nosed profile or as the cosmic Itzam-Cab (iguana-earth). Dreaming of Itzamna signals that knowledge is being bestowed; the dreamer is being initiated into a deeper understanding of time, fate, or healing. A dream of Itzamna writing in a codex means the dreamer's destiny is being inscribed; a dream of Itzamna healing with his hands means the dreamer has the gift of curing.

- **Fortune**: Portentous

Dreaming of Ix Chel signals that the dreamer's cyclical nature is active; something is waxing or waning in their life. A dream of Ix Chel weaving at her loom means the dreamer's fate is being woven — cooperate with the pattern. A dream of Ix Chel overturning her water jar signals a flood of emotion or a cleansing that cannot be controlled. Offerings of woven textiles and blue flowers honor her.

The word *chalchihuitl* means "precious green stone" and also "drop of water," linking jade to the generative power of Chalchiuhtlicue. Dreaming of jade signals that the dreamer's tonalli is strong and well-nourished; the vital soul-force is intact. A dream of jade breaking warns of a fracture in the dreamer's essential identity. The daykeepers read jade-dreams as confirmations of spiritual wealth that transcends material circumstances.

Dreaming of a jaguar on the day-sign Ocelotl signals access to power that operates in the dark — intuition, stealth, and the ability to navigate the underworld. The Florentine Codex records that dreaming of a jaguar meant the dreamer would die in war, which was considered an honorable death. The daykeepers read jaguar dreams as both warning and blessing: the dreamer has been marked by Tezcatlipoca, the smoking mirror who walks as a jaguar through the night.

A dream of the sun-god's face means the dreamer is being seen — truly seen — by the cosmic eye; nothing hidden will remain so. The daykeepers read Kinich Ahau dreams as favorable for rulers, teachers, and anyone called to public responsibility. A dream of the obsidian teeth breaking off warns that brute force without strategy will shatter.

Dreaming of maguey signals endurance through sacrifice; the dreamer will be used and consumed, but from this consumption, sustenance for many will flow. The daykeepers read maguey dreams as favorable for those in service professions and anyone whose suffering feeds others. A dream of drawing thorns from maguey means the dreamer is preparing for necessary auto-sacrifice.

### Mictlan (Aztec Underworld)

- **Nahuatl**: Mictlan (Place of the Dead)

- **Fortune**: Unfavorable (for the living); Portentous (for shamanic calling)
- **Interpretation**: Mictlan is the nine-layer underworld where most of the dead travel for four years, crossing the river of blood, the crashing mountains, the obsidian wind, and the realm of Mictlantecuhtli before reaching final rest. Dreaming of descending into Mictlan signals either a shamanic initiation or a period of profound grief and transformation. The Florentine Codex records that only those who died by sacrifice, in war, by drowning, or in childbirth bypassed Mictlan; all others made the journey. A dream of ascending from Mictlan signals rebirth; a dream of being trapped there warns of depression or spiritual stagnation requiring a daykeeper's intervention.

### Mictlantecuhtli (Lord of Mictlan)

- **Nahuatl**: Mictlantecuhtli (Lord of the Place of the Dead) / Tzontemoc (He Who Lowers His Head)
- **Day-sign**: Governs the trecena 6-Death
- **Fortune**: Unfavorable
- **Interpretation**: Mictlantecuhtli is the lord of death — a skeletal god with gaping jaws, owl-feather headdress, and eyes that see every soul's final destination. He and his consort Mictecacihuatl rule the lowest layer of Mictlan. Dreaming of Mictlantecuhtli signals an encounter with finality, mortality, and the inexorable demand of death. The Florentine Codex records that Mictlantecuhtli dreams were classified as *mahuiztilia* (miraculous/wondrous fortune) if the dreamer received a gift from the death lord, and *tlahuelliloc* (evil fortune) if the god was angry. The daykeepers counseled that one should never lie to Mictlantecuhtli in a dream — he knows the truth of all things.

- **Fortune**: Unfavorable (generally); Favorable (for completions and transformations)
- **Interpretation**: Death as a day-sign is not merely an ending but a threshold — the moment when one cycle closes and another cannot yet be seen. The trecena 6-Death was governed by Mictlantecuhtli, making this the most solemn of all the trecenas. Dreaming of death-imagery (skulls, skeletons, funerals) on the day-sign Miquiztli amplifies the omen: something must die so that something else can live. The daykeepers taught that Miquiztli dreams are dangerous to ignore but powerful to work with; the dreamer must identify what is ending and consciously release it. A dream of death followed by rebirth is the most favorable form.

The daykeepers used actual obsidian mirrors in divination, gazing into the black glass to see beyond the visible world. The Florentine Codex records that moon-dreams were especially significant for women and were classified by the moon's phase: a waxing moon was favorable for growth and new projects, a full moon signaled the peak of power, and a waning moon warned of diminishment. Dreaming of a mountain signals a confrontation with immense, immovable forces; the dreamer must climb, circumnavigate, or receive what the mountain offers. The daykeepers taught that night-dreams demand courage: the dreamer must not flee but must wait in the darkness until Tezcatlipoca reveals what he wills. - **Interpretation**: The resplendent quetzal is the bird of the cloud forest — its iridescent green tail feathers were the most precious tribute item, worth more than jade, worn only by royalty and the god Quetzalcoatl himself. Dreaming of a quetzal signals the presence of Quetzalcoatl in his most beautiful, benevolent aspect: divine breath, creative inspiration, and the wind that carries prayers. A dream of a quetzal losing its feathers warns that beauty and spiritual treasure are being squandered; a dream of a quetzal flying freely confirms that the dreamer's spirit is unbound. The daykeepers considered quetzal dreams auspicious for artists, leaders, and anyone seeking the feathered serpent's guidance.

### Quetzalcoatl (Feathered Serpent)

- **Nahuatl**: Quetzalcoatl (Quetzal-Serpent / Precious Twin) / Ehecatl (Wind)
- **Maya**: K'uk'ulkan / Gucumatz
- **Day-sign**: Governs the trecena 1-Wind; also associated with the day-sign Coatl (5th sign)
- **Fortune**: Favorable
- **Interpretation**: Quetzalcoatl is the creator god, the wind, the morning star, the patron of priests and learning — the deity who gave humanity the calendar, maize, and the arts. He is the feathered serpent who unites earth (serpent) and sky (quetzal bird), body and spirit. Dreaming of Quetzalcoatl signals a visitation from the most benevolent of the gods: the dreamer is being offered knowledge, creative power, or a new cycle. The Popol Vuh's equivalent, Gucumatz, creates humanity alongside Tepeu. A dream of Quetzalcoatl as the morning star (Tlahuizcalpantecuhtli) warns against arrogance — even the feathered serpent was exiled from Tula for pride.

### Quiahuitl (Rain) — Day-sign

- **Nahuatl**: Quiahuitl · **Maya**: Kawoq
- **Day-sign**: 19th of the Tonalpohualli (Rain)
- **Fortune**: Unfavorable (for the day-sign itself, governed by Tonatiuh)
- **Interpretation**: Rain as a day-sign carries the paradox of Tlaloc's dual nature — rain brings life but also flood, hail, and lightning death. The trecena 1-Rain was governed by Tonatiuh (the sun), making rain-dreams on this day especially charged: the sun and rain must negotiate. Dreaming of rain on Quiahuitl signals a confrontation with forces beyond the dreamer's control. The Florentine Codex records that rain-dreams were classified by their quality: gentle rain was *tlacelel* (deserved fortune), while rain of fire or hail was *tlahuelliloc* (evil fortune). The daykeepers taught that Quiahuitl dreams call for humility before the atmospheric gods.

### Rabbit (Tochtli) — Day-sign

- **Nahuatl**: Tochtli · **Maya**: Lamat
- **Day-sign**: 8th of the Tonalpohualli (Rabbit)
- **Fortune**: Favorable (for fertility and pleasure); Unfavorable (for excess)
- **Interpretation**: The rabbit is the animal of the moon — the Maya saw a rabbit in the moon's face, and the Aztec linked the rabbit to Mayahuel and the Centzon Totochtin (Four Hundred Rabbits of drunkenness). Dreaming of a rabbit on the day-sign Tochtli signals fertility, abundance, and the pleasures of the flesh, but also the danger of overindulgence. The trecena 1-Rabbit was governed by Xochipilli and Mayahuel, making rabbit-dreams especially favorable for lovers and artists. A dream of a white rabbit is an omen of unexpected blessing; a dream of many rabbits warns that desires are multiplying beyond the dreamer's capacity to fulfill them.

### River of Blood (Underworld)

- **Nahuatl**: Itzicuintli Yan (River Where the Dogs Go) / Chocayán (Where One Weeps)
- **Day-sign**: Associated with Mictlan's third layer
- **Fortune**: Unfavorable
- **Interpretation**: The river of blood is the third obstacle in Mictlan that the dead must cross — a river of flowing blood, so wide and deep that only those who are guided by the Xoloitzcuintli dog can find the passage. Dreaming of a river of blood signals that the dreamer is confronting the accumulated consequences of sacrifice — their own or others'. The daykeepers read blood-river dreams as warnings that the dreamer is wading through inherited grief or collective trauma. A dream of crossing the river safely means the dreamer has a guide; a dream of being swept away means the dreamer must seek help from a daykeeper or spiritual elder.

### Scorpion (Colotl)

- **Nahuatl**: Colotl · **Maya**: Sina'an
- **Day-sign**: Associated with the goddess Itzpapalotl (Obsidian Butterfly) and the trecena 1-House
- **Fortune**: Unfavorable
- **Interpretation**: The scorpion is the creature of hidden venom — the sting that comes from beneath, from the dark, from what was stepped on unawares. Dreaming of a scorpion signals betrayal, hidden hostility, or the consequence of neglecting something dangerous. The daykeepers associated scorpion dreams with Itzpapalotl, the terrifying goddess who could appear as a beautiful woman or a skeletal butterfly with obsidian-blade wings. A dream of being stung by a scorpion warns of imminent psychic or emotional attack; a dream of killing a scorpion means the dreamer has neutralized a hidden threat. Offer chalk and white flowers to close the sting's channel.

### Serpent / Coatl (Day-sign)

- **Nahuatl**: Coatl · **Maya**: Chicchan
- **Day-sign**: 5th of the Tonalpohualli (Serpent)
- **Fortune**: Favorable (for wisdom and transformation)
- **Interpretation**: The serpent is the earth's vital force — the creature that moves between the underworld (where it burrows) and the surface (where it strikes). Coatl as a day-sign is governed by Chalchiuhtlicue, linking the serpent to the waters that flow through the earth. Dreaming of a serpent on the day-sign Coatl signals that transformative energy is moving through the dreamer's life. The Aztec understood the serpent as both poison and medicine — the same creature that kills also appears in the caduceus of healing. A dream of a serpent shedding its skin is favorable for personal transformation; a dream of a coiled serpent warns of latent power that must be respected.

### Stela

- **Maya**: Lakam Tun (Big Stone) · **Nahuatl**: Tetzahuatl (monument/omen stone)
- **Day-sign**: Associated with Itzamna and the Long Count
- **Fortune**: Neutral
- **Interpretation**: The stela is the Maya stone of record — a carved monument that simultaneously records historical events, divine prophecies, and the king's connection to the cosmic order. Dreaming of a stela signals that the dreamer's life is being inscribed into a larger narrative; personal events have cosmic significance. A dream of reading a stela means the dreamer is being given access to ancestral knowledge; a dream of a stela crumbling means a story or identity the dreamer has relied upon is losing its coherence. The daykeepers read stela dreams as calls to consider what legacy the dreamer is creating.

### Sun (Tonatiuh)

- **Nahuatl**: Tonatiuh (Sun / He Who Goes Shining) · **Maya**: Kin (Day/Sun) / Kinich Ahau
- **Day-sign**: Associated with the trecena 4-Ollin (current sun) and Cuauhtli (Eagle)
- **Fortune**: Favorable
- **Interpretation**: The sun is the supreme sacrifice — the god who throws himself into the fire at Teotihuacan to become the moving sun, requiring the blood of warriors and sacrifice to keep moving across the sky. Dreaming of the sun signals vitality, consciousness, and the demand for purposeful action. The Florentine Codex records that dreaming of looking directly at the sun was an omen of rulership, while dreaming of the sun setting signaled decline. The daykeepers read sun-dreams as the most authoritative category of omens: whatever the sun reveals in the dream is cosmic truth. A dream of the sun standing still at zenith means the dreamer has reached their peak moment — act now, for the sun will not wait.

### Sweat Bath (Temazcalli)

- **Nahuatl**: Temazcalli (House of Heat) · **Maya**: Pib (underground oven / sweat bath)
- **Day-sign**: Associated with the goddess Toci (Our Grandmother) and the trecena 1-Death
- **Fortune**: Favorable (for purification)
- **Interpretation**: The temazcalli is the womb of the earth goddess — a small domed structure of stone and adobe where steam is created by pouring water over heated rocks. The bather enters as if into the earth's body and emerges reborn. Dreaming of a sweat bath signals a need for purification at the deepest level; the dreamer is carrying physical or spiritual toxins that must be sweated out. The daykeepers prescribed actual temazcalli sessions for those who reported sweat-bath dreams, considering them prescriptions rather than predictions. A dream of emerging clean from the temazcalli is favorable; a dream of being trapped in the steam warns that the purification process has become overwhelming.

### Tecpatl (Flint) — Day-sign

- **Nahuatl**: Tecpatl · **Maya**: Etz'nab
- **Day-sign**: 18th of the Tonalpohualli (Flint Knife)
- **Fortune**: Unfavorable (for conflict); Favorable (for truth and sacrifice)
- **Interpretation**: Tecpatl is the flint knife — the blade of sacrifice, the tool of the priest, the shape of the northern star. The trecena 1-Flint was governed by Huitzilopochtli, the war god, making flint-dreams especially charged with martial and sacrificial energy. Dreaming of a flint knife signals that something must be cut away — a relationship, a habit, an illusion. The daykeepers read flint dreams as urgent calls for decisive action. A dream of using a flint knife for auto-sacrifice is favorable — the dreamer is willingly offering what the gods require; a dream of being cut by someone else's flint warns of conflict that cannot be avoided.

### Temalacatl (Ritual Stone)

- **Nahuatl**: Temalacatl (Round Stone for the Ball Game / Gladiatorial Stone)
- **Day-sign**: Associated with Xipe Totec and the feast of Tlacaxipehualiztli
- **Fortune**: Portentous
- **Interpretation**: The temalacatl is the circular stone on which captives were tied for gladiatorial sacrifice — they fought with feathered weapons against four armed warriors, and their blood fed the earth. Dreaming of the temalacatl signals that the dreamer is in a trial by ordeal: the outcome depends not on fairness but on courage and the quality of the dreamer's spirit. The daykeepers read temalacatl dreams as both warning and initiation — the dreamer must face something with whatever strength they possess, even if the odds are impossible. A dream of being untied from the temalacatl means the ordeal has been recognized and released.

### Tezcatlipoca (Smoking Mirror)

- **Nahuatl**: Tezcatlipoca (Smoking Mirror) / Titlacahuan (He Whose Slaves We Are) / Tlamatzincatl
- **Maya**: Tohil (K'iche' equivalent)
- **Day-sign**: Governs the trecena 1-Death; associated with the day-sign Ocelotl (Jaguar)
- **Fortune**: Portentous
- **Interpretation**: Tezcatlipoca is the most powerful and ambivalent of the Aztec gods — the lord of the night sky, the trickster, the giver and taker of fortune, the one who sees all through his obsidian mirror. He walks the earth as a jaguar and as a limping man, testing humanity. Dreaming of Tezcatlipoca or his smoking mirror signals a confrontation with destiny itself; the mirror shows the dreamer their true nature, stripped of illusion. The Florentine Codex records that Tezcatlipoca appeared in dreams to announce both rises and falls of rulers. The daykeepers considered Tezcatlipoca dreams the most difficult to interpret: they are always true, but never simple.

### Tlaloc (Rain God)

- **Nahuatl**: Tlaloc (He Who Makes Things Sprout) / Tlaloc Tlamacazqui
- **Maya**: Chac (in his rain aspect)
- **Day-sign**: Governs the trecenas 1-Quiahuitl (Rain) and 1-Mazatl (Deer)
- **Fortune**: Portentous
- **Interpretation**: Tlaloc is the ancient god of rain, thunder, and the mountains — a deity so old that his worship predates the Aztecs by millennia. He dwells in Tlalocan, the paradise of the water-dead, where those who drowned or died of water-related illness enjoy eternal spring. Dreaming of Tlaloc signals that atmospheric forces are directly involved in the dreamer's fate. The Florentine Codex records that drowning in a dream while not near water meant Tlaloc had claimed the person. A dream of Tlaloc offering water is among the most favorable omens; a dream of Tlaloc with empty gourds warns of drought and deprivation. The daykeepers taught that Tlaloc dreams require offerings of jade, shells, and blue copal at a mountaintop shrine.

### Tobacco (Picietl)

- **Nahuatl**: Picietl / Yetl · **Maya**: K'utz
- **Day-sign**: Associated with the goddess Cihuacoatl and the trecena 1-Lizard
- **Fortune**: Neutral
- **Interpretation**: Tobacco is the plant of communion — its smoke carries prayers, its juice is medicinal, and its spirit opens channels between the human and divine. The daykeepers inhaled tobacco smoke before interpreting dreams to sharpen their vision. Dreaming of tobacco signals that a ritual action is required: the dreamer must "smoke" on a decision, letting the smoke of contemplation rise before acting. A dream of offering tobacco is favorable — the channel is open; a dream of tobacco smoke descending instead of rising warns that the dreamer's prayers are not reaching the gods. The Maya daykeepers used tobacco as their primary divinatory aid.

### Tzompantli (Skull Rack)

- **Nahuatl**: Tzompantli (Skull Banner / Skull Rack)
- **Day-sign**: Associated with Mictlantecuhtli and Huitzilopochtli
- **Fortune**: Unfavorable (for the unprepared); Portentous (for warriors)
- **Interpretation**: The tzompantli is the rack upon which the skulls of sacrificial victims were displayed — a monument to the covenant between humans and gods, proof that the debt of blood had been paid. The Great Tzompantli of Tenochtitlan held thousands of skulls. Dreaming of a skull rack signals that the dreamer is confronting mortality at a collective level — the consequences of war, sacrifice, or systemic violence. The daykeepers read tzompantli dreams as stark reminders of the blood-covenant: existence requires exchange. A dream of one's own skull on the rack is the most serious omen, signaling that the dreamer's life is pledged and must be redeemed through sacrifice or service.

### Volcano

- **Nahuatl**: Popocatepetl (Smoking Mountain) / Xinantecatl
- **Day-sign**: Associated with Xiuhtecuhtli (Fire Lord) and the trecena 1-House
- **Fortune**: Portentous
- **Interpretation**: The volcano is the mountain of fire — the place where the underworld's heat breaks through to the surface, where creation and destruction are the same event. Popocatepetl was both a living deity and the source of the obsidian that armed Aztec warriors. Dreaming of a volcano signals that suppressed forces are erupting; the dreamer's inner fire can no longer be contained. The daykeepers read volcano dreams as calls to channel volcanic energy before it explodes destructively. A dream of lava flowing gently is favorable — creative force is moving; a dream of explosive eruption warns of emotional or spiritual crisis that will affect everyone nearby.

### Xibalba (Maya Underworld)

- **Maya**: Xibalba (Place of Fear) / Metnal
- **Day-sign**: Cimi (Death) in Tzolk'in
- **Fortune**: Unfavorable (for the unprepared); Portentous (for shamanic work)
- **Interpretation**: Xibalba is the Maya underworld of seven houses — Dark House, Razor House, Cold House, Jaguar House, Bat House, and the houses of the Xibalba lords Hun Came and Vucub Came. The Popol Vuh records the Hero Twins' descent into Xibalba, where they outwitted the lords of death through sacrifice and resurrection. Dreaming of Xibalba signals that the dreamer is being tested by the lords of fear; the challenge is not to avoid the underworld but to navigate it with cunning. The daykeepers read Xibalba dreams as shamanic initiations if the dreamer survives, and as death-omens if they do not. A dream of the Hero Twins appearing as guides means the dreamer will prevail.

### Xipe Totec (Flayed Lord)

- **Nahuatl**: Xipe Totec (Our Lord the Flayed One) / Tlatlauhqui Tezcatlipoca (Red Tezcatlipoca)
- **Day-sign**: Governs the trecena 1-Eagle; associated with the feast Tlacaxipehualiztli
- **Fortune**: Portentous
- **Interpretation**: Xipe Totec is the god of spring renewal — he wears the flayed skin of the sacrificial victim as a garment, symbolizing the earth putting on new vegetation after the dry season. The ritual of gladiatorial sacrifice and skin-wearing was the most dramatic ceremony of the Aztec year. Dreaming of Xipe Totec signals a period of radical renewal: the dreamer must shed their old self as a snake sheds its skin, but more violently — the old identity must be flayed, not merely outgrown. A dream of wearing another's skin means the dreamer has taken on a new identity; a dream of being flayed means the old self is being forcibly removed. The daykeepers counseled that Xipe Totec dreams, though painful, always precede growth.

### Xochiquetzal (Flower Feather)

- **Nahuatl**: Xochiquetzal (Flower-Quetzal)
- **Day-sign**: Associated with the day-sign Xochitl (Flower) and the trecena 1-Lizard
- **Fortune**: Favorable
- **Interpretation**: Xochiquetzal is the goddess of beauty, love, flowers, weaving, and the arts — the most seductive and creative of the goddesses, patroness of courtesans, artisans, and pregnant women. She was the original wife of Tlaloc before Tezcatlipoca abducted her to become his consort. Dreaming of Xochiquetzal signals beauty, sensuality, and creative power; the dreamer is being touched by the force that makes the world beautiful. The daykeepers read Xochiquetzal dreams as favorable for artists and lovers. A dream of Xochiquetzal weaving means the dreamer's fate is being made beautiful; a dream of Xochiquetzal weeping warns that beauty is being violated or neglected.

## Usage Notes

- **Day-sign primacy**: Always check which Tonalpohualli/Tzolk'in day the dream occurred on. The same symbol carries different fortune depending on its governing day-sign. A serpent dream on Coatl is favorable; a serpent dream on Miquiztli may signal poisoning.
- **Three souls**: Determine which soul is active in the dream. Tonalli-dreams (head/upper body imagery) concern destiny and identity. Teyolia-dreams (heart/chest imagery) concern emotion and moral calling. Ihiyotl-dreams (breath/abdomen imagery) concern vitality and courage.
- **Reciprocity principle**: Every Mesoamerican dream that involves a deity, an offering, or an underworld journey establishes a covenant. The dreamer must respond with real-world action — typically a blood offering (auto-sacrifice with maguey thorns), copal burning, or a pilgrimage to a sacred site.
- **Reversal is NOT standard**: Unlike Chinese dream tradition, Mesoamerican interpretation does not systematically reverse meanings. Death in a dream usually means death or ending; blood usually means sacrifice or covenant. However, some specific reversals exist — drowning dreams signal election to Tlalocan (paradise), not damnation.
- **Florentine Codex authority**: When a symbol appears in the Florentine Codex's omen lists (Book 5), that interpretation takes precedence over general symbolism.
- **Popol Vuh parallelism**: For Maya dreamers, the Hero Twins' journey through Xibalba provides the primary template for interpreting underworld dreams. Survival = favorable outcome; being caught = unfavorable.
- **Directional color symbolism**: East = Red (vitality, dawn), North = White (ancestors, bone), West = Black (underworld, death), South = Yellow (flower, abundance), Center = Blue-Green (water, jade, quetzal). These colors in dreams modify the symbol's interpretation.
