# Journalist Email Pitch Guide

Read this file when preparing an email to pitch a press release to journalists. The pitch email is often more important than the press release itself — a bad pitch means the release never gets read.

## Golden Rules

- **Paste the full press release in the email body.** Never attach it as a file. Journalists won't open attachments from unknown senders.
- **Keep the pitch above the release to 3-5 sentences max.** The pitch is the appetizer; the release is the main course.
- **One journalist, one email.** Never BCC a list. Personalize every pitch.
- **Research the journalist first.** Read their last 3-5 articles. Reference something specific they wrote.

## Subject Line Patterns

The subject line determines whether the email gets opened. 47% of journalists decide to open a pitch based on subject line alone.

### Proven Formulas

| Pattern | Example | When to use |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Data-driven** | "European SaaS churn drops 23% — new report" | You have a surprising number |
| **Exclusive offer** | "Exclusive: [Company] to acquire [Target] for $2B" | Offering an exclusive to one journalist |
| **Timely hook** | "Ahead of EU AI Act deadline: [Company] launches compliance tool" | Riding a news cycle or regulation |
| **Question** | "Why are 40% of French retailers switching to [X]?" | The question is genuinely interesting |
| **Name-drop** | "[Known Company] partners with [Your Company] on [outcome]" | The partner is more recognizable |
| **Local angle** | "Lyon startup raises EUR 15M to bring [X] to French hospitals" | Targeting a regional journalist |
| **Contrarian** | "Against the trend: [Company] hires 200 while sector cuts jobs" | The news defies expectations |
| **Simple announcement** | "[Company] launches [Product] — first [category] to [do X]" | The news is strong enough to stand alone |

### Subject Line Rules

- **Under 50 characters ideal, 60 characters hard maximum.** Mobile email clients (Gmail, Outlook) truncate at 41-50 characters on most phones. Desktop shows ~60. If the newsworthy element is cut off, the pitch fails before it starts.
- No ALL CAPS (spam filter trigger)
- No exclamation marks
- No "PRESS RELEASE:" prefix (obvious and wastes characters)
- Include the most newsworthy element in the first 40 characters
- Suggest 2-3 subject line variants so the user can A/B test or pick by feel

## Hook Types

The first 1-2 sentences of the pitch email must hook the journalist. Choose the hook type based on your strongest asset.

### 1. Data Hook

Lead with a surprising statistic or data point that creates a "wait, really?" reaction.

**Template:** `[Surprising stat]. [Company] just released [thing] that [addresses this]...`

**Example:** "47% of European e-commerce returns are now processed by AI, up from 12% two years ago. [Company] is launching the platform behind that shift."

**Best for:** Research reports, product launches with metrics, market trend stories.

### 2. Trend Hook

Connect your news to a broader trend the journalist is already covering.

**Template:** `You've been covering [trend]. [Company] just [action] that [connects to trend]...`

**Example:** "I saw your piece on the SaaS consolidation wave last week. [Company] just closed a $50M round specifically to acquire three smaller competitors in the [space]."

**Best for:** Funding, partnerships, market moves.

### 3. Contrarian Hook

Challenge conventional wisdom or highlight something that defies expectations.

**Template:** `Everyone says [common belief]. [Company]'s data shows the opposite...`

**Example:** "The narrative says remote work kills company culture. [Company] just hit 98% employee retention after three years fully remote — here's how."

**Best for:** Research, executive hires, culture stories, counter-trend announcements.

### 4. Exclusive Hook

Offer something no other journalist has: early access, exclusive data, first interview.

**Template:** `I'd like to offer you an exclusive on [news] before we go public on [date]...`

**Example:** "We're announcing a major product pivot on March 15. I'd like to offer you an exclusive briefing with our CEO this week, with an embargo until launch day."

**Best for:** Major launches, funding rounds, M&A, executive changes. Use sparingly — an "exclusive" offered to 20 journalists is not exclusive.

### 5. Local Angle Hook

Localize a national or global story for the journalist's geographic beat.

**Template:** `[Company]'s new [thing] will directly affect [local area/industry] by [specific impact]...`

**Example:** "The new logistics hub [Company] is opening in Toulouse will create 300 jobs in Occitanie and serve as the Southern European distribution center."

**Best for:** Expansion announcements, hiring, events, regional impact stories.

### 6. Human Interest Hook

Lead with a person, not a company. Journalists write about people.

**Template:** `[Person] spent [time] doing [thing]. Now they're [action that ties to news]...`

**Example:** "Marie Dupont spent 15 years as an ER nurse watching patient data get lost between systems. She just raised EUR 8M to fix the problem she lived every day."

**Best for:** Founder stories, executive hires, mission-driven companies, startup funding.

### 7. Timeliness Hook

Piggyback on breaking news, an upcoming event, or a calendar moment.

**Template:** `With [event/date] approaching, [Company] is [action]...`

**Example:** "Two weeks before Black Friday, [Company] is releasing data showing that 60% of European retailers still can't handle return surges — and launching a tool to fix it."

**Best for:** Seasonal products, event-adjacent launches, regulatory deadlines.

### 8. Problem-Solution Hook

Name a pain point the journalist's audience recognizes, then introduce the solution.

**Template:** `[Audience] has long struggled with [problem]. [Company] just launched [solution] that [specific result]...`

**Example:** "French SMBs spend an average of 12 hours per month on VAT compliance. [Company]'s new platform automates 90% of the process — verified in a pilot with 500 businesses."

**Best for:** Product launches, B2B tools, market entry.

## Email Body Structure

Keep the pitch to **3 paragraphs maximum** above the press release:

### Paragraph 1: The Hook (1-2 sentences)

Use one of the hook types above. This is the only sentence many journalists will read.

### Paragraph 2: The News Summary (2-3 sentences)

What is the news, in plain language? Why should this journalist care? What's the most compelling data point?

### Paragraph 3: The CTA (1-2 sentences)

What are you offering? Interview with the CEO? Exclusive access? A demo? Early data? Be specific.

**Example full pitch:**

> Subject: Lyon startup raises EUR 15M to automate hospital logistics
>
> Hi [First Name],
>
> French hospitals waste an estimated EUR 2.1B annually on manual supply chain management — a number that grew 18% post-COVID. [Company], based in Lyon, just raised EUR 15M in Series A to automate the process.
>
> The round was led by [Investor]. In a 6-month pilot with CHU de Lyon, [Company] reduced supply waste by 34% and freed 12 hours per week of nursing staff time. The funding will be used to expand to 50 hospitals across France by end of 2027.
>
> I can arrange a call with CEO [Name] this week if you're interested. Full details below.
>
> [FULL PRESS RELEASE PASTED HERE]

## Follow-Up Cadence

Most coverage comes from follow-ups, not initial pitches. But there's a line between persistent and annoying.

| Timing | Action | Tone |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Day 0** | Send initial pitch | Professional, concise |
| **Day 2-3** | First follow-up | Brief. "Wanted to make sure this didn't get buried. Happy to provide [additional asset]." |
| **Day 5-7** | Second follow-up (final) | Offer a new angle or asset. "Since I last wrote, [new development]. Would this change the story for you?" |
| **After 7 days** | Stop. Move on. | Do not send a third follow-up. If they haven't replied, they're not interested in this story. |

### Follow-Up Rules

- **Never** resend the same email with "bumping this" or "just following up"
- Each follow-up must add value: a new data point, a new angle, or an offer (interview, exclusive, demo)
- If the journalist says no, thank them and move on. Burning a relationship costs more than one story.
- Track pitches in a spreadsheet: journalist, outlet, date sent, follow-ups, response

## What NOT to Do

| Mistake | Why it fails |
| --- | --- |
| BCC a list of 200 journalists | Impersonal, often caught by spam filters, and journalists talk to each other |
| "Dear journalist" or "Dear editor" | Shows you didn't research who you're emailing |
| Attach the press release as a PDF/Word file | Won't be opened. Paste the full text in the body |
| Write a 500-word pitch email | The pitch is not the story. Keep it to 3-5 sentences |
| Send on Friday afternoon | Lowest open rates of the week. Tue-Thu mornings are optimal |
| Use "PRESS RELEASE" in subject line | Spam trigger. Let the news speak for itself |
| Pitch something the journalist doesn't cover | Research their beat. A fintech reporter won't cover your restaurant launch |
| Follow up 5+ times | Harassment. Two follow-ups max, then stop |
| Lie about exclusivity | If you offered an "exclusive" to multiple journalists, you've burned all those relationships permanently |
