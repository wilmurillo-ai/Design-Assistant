# Writing Principles

Read this file for detailed guidance on press release writing style, tone, and language quality.

## The News Test

If the first sentence doesn't contain news, rewrite it. "Company X, a leader in..." is not news. "Company X today launched the first..." is news.

## Kill the Jargon

Write so a general assignment reporter can understand paragraph one. Trade-specific terminology is acceptable only in trade press releases and only after the general news is established.

## One Page, One Story

Resist the urge to pack multiple announcements into one release. If there are two stories, write two releases. 70% of journalists won't read past 200 words.

## Data Beats Adjectives

"Revenue grew 47% year-over-year to EUR 12M" beats "Revenue grew substantially." Every claim must be backed by a number, a source, or a quote. Unsupported superlatives ("best-in-class," "revolutionary") trigger immediate deletion.

## Quotes Must Earn Their Place

Quotes should provide something the journalist cannot write themselves: opinion, vision, or context that would be inappropriate in narrative form. A quote that restates the lead paragraph wastes space.

**Bad:** "We are excited to announce this partnership," said the CEO. **Good:** "This changes how European retailers handle returns. Our pilot showed a 40% reduction in processing time," said [Name], CEO of [Company].

## Banned Phrases

These words and phrases signal marketing copy, not journalism. Avoid them entirely:

- "thrilled," "excited to announce," "proud to"
- "innovative," "cutting-edge," "world-class," "best-in-class"
- "synergy," "leverage," "disruptive," "game-changing"
- "revolutionary," "paradigm shift," "next-generation"
- "leading" (unless backed by market share data)
- "competitive pricing" (give the actual price)

## Attribution Rules

- Use "said" for all quote attribution — not "stated," "commented," "shared," "expressed," "noted"
- Full name + title on first reference: `said Jane Smith, Chief Executive Officer of Acme Corp`
- Last name only on subsequent references: `Smith said`
- Present tense "says" is acceptable for ongoing situations; past tense "said" for events

## Third Person Discipline

No first person ("we," "our," "us") or second person ("you," "your") outside of direct quotes. The release is a news document, not a letter to the reader.

## Lead Word Count

The lead paragraph must be 25-35 words. Count them. Under 25 lacks enough information to answer the 5W1H. Over 35 becomes a wall of text that buries the news. This is the single most important sentence in the release — get the word count right.

## Active Voice

Prefer active voice throughout. "The company launched the product" not "The product was launched by the company." Passive voice buries the actor and weakens the news.
