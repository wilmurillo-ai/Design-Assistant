## How Amazon Book Search Works

Amazon's book search algorithm (A9/A10) considers:

1. **Exact match in title/subtitle** — strongest signal
2. **Backend keywords** — 7 keyword fields, up to 50 characters each
3. **Book description text** — indexed but weaker signal than title or backend
4. **Category placement** — books in relevant categories rank better for related searches
5. **Sales velocity** — books that sell rank higher (flywheel effect)
6. **Review count and rating** — social proof influences ranking

**Key insight:** Amazon is a search engine first. Readers find books by typing queries. If your book doesn't appear for relevant queries, it doesn't exist to those readers. Keyword research is not optional — it is the foundation of discoverability.

## The 7 Backend Keywords

KDP provides 7 keyword fields during book setup. Each field accepts up to 50 characters.

### Rules
- **Use phrases, not single words.** "blood sugar control" not "blood" "sugar" "control"
- **No repetition of words in title/subtitle.** Amazon already indexes those. Using the same words wastes keyword slots.
- **No competitor names or trademarks.** Amazon may suppress your listing.
- **No subjective claims.** "bestselling" or "best" are not allowed.
- **No temporary statements.** "new" or "on sale" are not allowed.
- **Use all 7 fields.** Empty fields are wasted discoverability.
- **Separate concepts with spaces, not commas.** "diabetes guide" not "diabetes, guide"

### What Makes a Good Backend Keyword
- **Real search term**: actual phrase people type into Amazon
- **Relevant to your book**: the reader searching this would genuinely want your book
- **Not already in your title/subtitle**: no duplication
- **Moderate competition**: some search volume but not dominated by massive brands
- **Specific over generic**: "insulin resistance diet plan" beats "health book"

## Keyword Research Process

### Step 1: Seed List (15-20 candidates)
Generate initial keywords from:

**A. Amazon Autocomplete**
- Go to Amazon.com > Books
- Type the beginning of a relevant phrase and see what Amazon suggests
- Each suggestion is a real search term with actual volume
- Example: typing "blood sugar" might show "blood sugar solution", "blood sugar diet", "blood sugar monitor", "blood sugar cookbook"
- Capture ALL relevant suggestions — these are gold

**B. Competitor Analysis**
- Find the top 5-10 books in your niche
- Look at their titles, subtitles, and descriptions
- Note which words and phrases keep appearing
- Check their categories (these suggest related keywords)

**C. Reader Language Mining**
- Read 1-star and 3-star reviews of competitor books
- Note the exact words readers use to describe what they wanted but didn't get
- These are unmet search terms — your book can rank for them
- Example: readers complaining "no practical meal plan" suggests the keyword "practical meal plan for diabetics"

**D. Related Searches**
- After searching Amazon for your main keyword, scroll to "Related searches"
- These are algorithmically connected search terms with real volume
- Capture relevant ones for your seed list

### Step 2: Validation
For each candidate keyword, validate:

1. **Does it return book results?** Search Amazon > Books for the phrase. If few or no book results appear, readers aren't searching this in the book context.

2. **Are the results relevant to YOUR book?** If the results are all cookbooks and your book is about medication management, the keyword attracts the wrong readers.

3. **What's the competition level?** Check the top 3 results:
   - BSR under 10,000 = very competitive (established books dominate)
   - BSR 10,000-50,000 = moderately competitive (achievable with good launch)
   - BSR 50,000-200,000 = low competition (easier to rank but lower volume)
   - BSR over 200,000 = very low search volume (may not be worth a keyword slot)

4. **Is the intent right?** "Diabetes" alone is too vague. "Diabetes management plan" shows clear purchase intent.

### Step 3: Selection (Final 7)
From validated candidates, select 7 keywords that:

1. **Cover different search intents** — don't cluster all keywords around one phrase
2. **Include both broad and long-tail** — mix "heart health" (broader) with "{{AUTHOR_CREDENTIALS}} guide to lowering A1c naturally" (long-tail)
3. **Don't duplicate title/subtitle words** — every word must add NEW discoverability
4. **Reflect reader language** — use words readers use, not clinical jargon (unless your audience is clinical)
5. **Target achievable rankings** — include keywords where you can realistically reach page 1

### Step 4: Document
Produce a keyword research deliverable (see template below).

## Title & Subtitle Keyword Strategy

### Title
- The title should be compelling and memorable FIRST, keyword-aware SECOND
- Never stuff keywords into a title at the expense of it sounding like a real book
- **Good**: "The [Topic] Reset" (contains primary keyword naturally)
- **Bad**: "[Keyword 1] [Keyword 2] [Keyword 3] [Keyword 4] Book" (keyword stuffed, sounds like spam)

### Subtitle
- The subtitle is where you can be more keyword-intentional
- Include your highest-value keyword phrase naturally in the subtitle
- **Good**: "A {{AUTHOR_CREDENTIALS}}'s [Timeframe] Plan to [Benefit 1], [Benefit 2], and [Benefit 3]"
- This naturally includes credential keywords plus benefit-oriented search terms
- All of those become indexed search terms

### Combined Strategy
- Title + subtitle together should cover your 3-5 most important keyword concepts
- Backend keywords then cover the remaining search terms not in title/subtitle
- Zero overlap between title/subtitle words and backend keywords

## Book Description Keyword Integration

Amazon indexes book description text, but at a weaker signal than title or backend keywords. Still important.

### Rules
- Weave relevant keywords naturally into the description
- Front-load the most important keyword in the first sentence or two
- Use keywords in bullet points (high visual weight and possibly indexed more strongly)
- Never keyword-stuff the description — it must read as compelling sales copy first
- The description sells to humans, not algorithms — if the keyword doesn't fit naturally, leave it out

### Example
```html
<b>Your [pain point] is getting worse. Your energy is crashing. And [serious consequence] is starting to feel less theoretical.</b>

In <i>[Book Title]</i>, {{AUTHOR_NAME}} delivers a practical <b>[keyword phrase 1]</b> designed for adults who want to <b>[keyword phrase 2]</b>, reduce [risk], and rebuild <b>[keyword phrase 3]</b> — without extreme diets or confusing medical jargon.
```
Keywords are integrated naturally into compelling sales copy.

## Competitive Keyword Analysis

For every book project, analyze the top 5 competitors' keyword strategies:

### Process
1. **Find 5 top-ranking books** in your target category/niche
2. **For each book, record:**
   - Title and subtitle (extract keyword themes)
   - Categories they appear in
   - BSR rank in each category
   - Number of reviews and average rating
   - Publication date (recent books ranking well = active niche)
   - Price (ebook and paperback)

3. **Extract keyword patterns:**
   - What words appear in 3+ competitor titles/subtitles?
   - What categories do they share?
   - What phrases appear in their descriptions?

4. **Find the gaps:**
   - What keywords do readers search that NO competitor title contains?
   - What categories have low competition but relevant readership?
   - What reader complaints (from reviews) suggest unmet search demand?

## Common Keyword Mistakes

1. **Using single words instead of phrases.** "Diabetes" wastes a keyword slot. "Insulin resistance meal plan" targets a real search.

2. **Repeating title/subtitle words.** Amazon already indexes your title. Repeating those words in backend keywords adds zero value.

3. **Brainstorming instead of researching.** Keywords you think readers search are often wrong. Amazon autocomplete shows what they actually search.

4. **Choosing only competitive keywords.** If every keyword has BSR < 5,000 competitors, you'll never rank. Include winnable long-tail keywords.

5. **Ignoring reader language.** Clinical terms ("glycemic variability") may be accurate but readers search "blood sugar spikes after eating." Use their words.

6. **Not updating keywords after launch.** Keywords can be changed anytime in KDP. Review and optimize at 30, 60, and 90 days post-launch based on actual search performance.

7. **Stuffing the description.** If the description reads like an SEO article instead of compelling sales copy, readers bounce. Humans buy books, not algorithms.

8. **Skipping category research.** The right subcategory can make a book a #1 New Release with 50 sales. The wrong one requires 5,000 for the same badge.
