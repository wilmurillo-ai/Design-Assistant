# Humanization Rules — Mandatory for All Book Writing

These rules apply to EVERY word produced in any book chapter, manuscript, or book-related text. No exceptions. No shortcuts. A book that reads like AI is a book that doesn't get published.

---

## SECTION 1: FORBIDDEN LANGUAGE

### Category A: Banned Words & Phrases (NEVER USE)

**Pretentious Abstractions:**
delve, delved, tapestry, realm, testament to, beacon of, kaleidoscope, symphony of, labyrinth, juxtaposition, paradigm shift, ever-evolving, multifaceted, myriad, plethora, interplay, dichotomy, synergy, holistic (unless clinical context), embark, endeavor, uncharted, unveil, groundbreaking, transformative

**Filler Phrases:**
"it's worth noting," "it's important to note," "on a daily basis," "at the end of the day," "in today's world," "in this day and age," "when it comes to," "the fact of the matter is," "needless to say," "in conclusion" (at chapter ends), "as we all know," "without further ado," "let's dive in," "buckle up"

**Fake Profundity:**
"shed light on," "pivotal moment," "game-changer," "transformative journey," "the key takeaway," "food for thought," "the bottom line is," "at its core," "when all is said and done," "stands as a reminder"

**Overused Intensifiers:**
"very unique," "extremely important," "absolutely essential," "incredibly powerful," "truly remarkable," "quite literally," "fundamentally changed"

**AI Connector Words (overused):**
"moreover," "furthermore," "additionally," "consequently," "nevertheless," "notwithstanding," "henceforth," "thereby," "wherein," "thereof"
- Use ONLY if writing would be unclear without them, maximum once per 2,000 words total

**AI Descriptors:**
meticulous, robust, crucial, dynamic, landscape (non-literal), navigate/navigating, leverage, utilize (use "use"), facilitate, optimize, streamline, foster, cultivate, spearhead, champion (as verb), bolster, underscore, underpin, epitomize

### Category B: Structural AI Tells

- **Em-dash abuse:** Maximum ONE em-dash per 1,000 words
- **Colon-list pattern:** Do not start paragraphs with a statement followed by a colon and list. Vary structure.
- **Triple adjective stacks:** Never stack 3+ adjectives before a noun. "The warm, golden, inviting sunlight" = AI. "Warm sunlight" = human.
- **Repetitive openings:** Never start 2+ consecutive paragraphs with the same word or structure
- **Section summary pattern:** Do not end sections with "In summary..." or recap what was just said
- **The AI conclusion:** Never end a chapter with a neat moral lesson wrapped in a bow. End with forward momentum, unresolved tension, or a quiet moment.

## SECTION 2: WHAT HUMAN WRITING SOUNDS LIKE

### Rhythm & Pacing
- **Vary sentence length constantly.** Short. Then medium ones. Then longer sentences that build momentum and pull the reader forward, because that's how real writers create musicality on the page.
- **Use fragments.** For emphasis. For pacing. For voice.
- **One-sentence paragraphs are powerful.** Use them.
- **Dialogue paragraphs can be one line.** "Yeah, I know."

### Voice & Authenticity
- **Contractions are mandatory in dialogue** and natural in narrative. "It's" not "It is." "Don't" not "Do not."
- **Imperfection is authenticity.** Real writers occasionally start sentences with "And" or "But." They use "got" and "thing" and "stuff."
- **Specificity beats abstraction.** Not "a bird" — "a crow." Not "he drank something" — "he drank lukewarm Folgers from a styrofoam cup."
- **Opinion and personality.** Real authors have voices. They editorialize. They're funny sometimes. They're blunt sometimes. AI writes in a careful, hedged, neutral tone. Don't.

### Sensory Writing
- **Filter through character/narrator emotion.** Not "the room was cold" but "the cold hit her the second she opened the door."
- **Unexpected senses.** Smell, texture, temperature, sound — not just visual
- **Connect sensation to memory.** "The hand sanitizer smell reminded him of twelve-hour shifts at the hospital."
- **Active, not static.** Characters interact with their environment. They don't stand in rooms being described at.

### Scene Construction
- **Enter late, leave early.** Don't set up scenes — drop readers in.
- **Action carries description.** "She swept the pills into the counting tray" tells more than a paragraph describing the pharmacy counter.
- **Subtext over statement.** What characters DON'T say often matters more.

## SECTION 3: THE HUMANIZATION PASS

After every chapter draft, run this check before submitting for review:

### Automated Scan
1. Search for every word in the FORBIDDEN list. Flag and rewrite any found.
2. Count em-dashes. If >1 per 1,000 words, reduce.
3. Check first word of every paragraph. If any word appears 3+ times as an opener, restructure.
4. Check for triple-adjective stacks. Reduce to 1-2 max.
5. Check for "In summary," "In conclusion," "As we've seen" — remove all.
6. Read the last paragraph. Does it sound like a TED talk ending? Rewrite.
7. Check dialogue. Are contractions used naturally? Does each character sound distinct?
8. Check AI connector density. Count "moreover/furthermore/additionally/consequently" — if total exceeds 1 per 2,000 words, replace most with simpler transitions.

### Human-Feel Checklist
- [ ] Would a real person write this sentence this way?
- [ ] Does this paragraph have varied rhythm?
- [ ] Are the details specific enough that they feel observed, not generated?
- [ ] Does the writing have personality, or could any AI have written it?
- [ ] Is there at least one moment per chapter that surprises or delights?
- [ ] Would this be enjoyable to read aloud?
- [ ] Does this chapter make me want to read the next one?

### The Nuclear Test
Read any random paragraph from the chapter. If your first reaction is "this sounds like ChatGPT," the entire chapter needs rewriting. Not editing. Rewriting.
