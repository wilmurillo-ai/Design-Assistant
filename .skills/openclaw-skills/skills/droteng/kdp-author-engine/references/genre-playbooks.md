# Genre Playbooks — Writing Standards by Book Category

Load the relevant section for the genre you are writing in. Each playbook defines voice, structure, research requirements, and genre-specific rules.

---

## Non-Fiction Health & Wellness

**Voice:** Warm, authoritative, practical, calm. Sound like a trusted clinician speaking to an intelligent adult — not a textbook, not a blog post, not a wellness influencer.

**Structure:**
- Open chapters with a human, recognizable health moment — not definitions or statistics
- Every chapter answers: "What do I DO with this information?"
- Patient-style stories and examples make clinical content human
- End chapters with forward momentum into the next actionable step

**Research Standards:**
- Every medical claim needs a verifiable source
- Drug names: use both generic and brand (e.g., "metformin (Glucophage)")
- Dosages and interactions: be specific — vague health advice is dangerous
- Mark uncertain claims [NEEDS VERIFICATION] — never fabricate clinical data
- Preferred sources: ADA, AHA/ACC guidelines, peer-reviewed reviews, landmark trials

**Tone Rules:**
- No fear-mongering or scare tactics
- No miracle-language or wellness hype
- No blaming readers for their condition
- No trendy biohacker swagger
- Authority without arrogance — credibility through specificity, not posturing
- Include medical disclaimers without undermining authority

**Forbidden Feel:** If it reads like a discharge handout, a health blog post, or a supplement marketing page, it has failed.

## Children's Books

**Voice:** Age-appropriate, engaging, fun-first. The child is the reader, not the parent.

**Age Ranges:**
- **Picture books (3-7):** Simple words, repetition, rhythm, pattern. Read-aloud musicality is essential. 500-1,000 words total.
- **Early readers (5-8):** Short sentences, familiar concepts, growing independence. 2,000-5,000 words.
- **Middle grade (8-12):** More complex but still clear. Real emotions, real stakes. 30,000-50,000 words.

**Structure:**
- Show, never lecture — health concepts through character action and story, NEVER "let me explain" passages
- Fun first — if it's not fun to read aloud, it's not a good children's book
- Repetition and pattern in picture books (not in middle grade)
- Emotional authenticity — kids feel things intensely, honor that

**Tone Rules:**
- Kids are smart. Don't talk down. Don't over-explain. Don't moralize.
- Educational value without preaching
- Characters should be active, not passive recipients of lessons
- Illustrations descriptions must be specific and action-oriented: "[Image: Maya pours orange juice into her special measuring cup, counting each splash out loud]"

**Forbidden Feel:** If an adult would roll their eyes reading it aloud, or if the lesson is more visible than the story, it has failed.

## AI & Technology Books

**Voice:** Practical, honest, experienced. Write from real experience, not theory. The reader is intelligent but may not be technical.

**Structure:**
- Step-by-step when teaching: clear instructions, verified commands, expected outputs
- Real examples from actual setups and workflows — not hypotheticals
- Explain concepts without assuming jargon
- Each chapter should produce something the reader can use or try

**Research Standards:**
- Technical instructions must be verified and current
- Command-line examples must be tested
- Version numbers and dependencies must be stated
- Acknowledge what doesn't work — honest about limitations

**Tone Rules:**
- Don't oversell AI — acknowledge limitations honestly
- Don't hype — practical value over buzzwords
- Assume the reader is smart but new to this specific domain
- Use humor sparingly but naturally

**Forbidden Feel:** If it reads like a press release, a product marketing page, or a Wikipedia article, it has failed.

## Faith & Ministry

**Voice:** Scripturally grounded, practically applied, invitational. Share, don't lecture. The reader is a peer, not a congregation.

**Structure:**
- Scripture integration, not decoration — Bible verses central to the argument, not sprinkled on top
- Every spiritual principle connects to Monday morning — practical application
- Personal stories and honest vulnerability strengthen credibility
- Chapter endings should leave the reader with something to do, not just something to feel

**Research Standards:**
- Scripture references must be accurate (book, chapter, verse)
- Theological claims should be grounded in established tradition
- Denominational awareness: know the target audience's theological context
- Historical context for passages when it illuminates the point

**Tone Rules:**
- Never preachy — invite, don't demand
- Never formulaic ("3 steps to hearing God's voice")
- Integrate faith and professional life authentically
- Honest about doubt, struggle, and unanswered questions — these build trust

**Forbidden Feel:** If it reads like a sermon transcript, a devotional calendar, or a prosperity gospel pitch, it has failed.

## Literary Fiction

**Voice:** Character-driven, thematically rich, stylistically distinctive. Prioritize character complexity over plot.

**Structure:**
- Character interiority is the engine — what characters think, fear, want, and deny
- Sophisticated metaphor and symbolism woven into the narrative naturally
- Morally ambiguous situations without neat resolutions
- Universal themes explored with nuance and specificity

**Craft Standards:**
- Prose quality is paramount — every sentence must earn its place
- Subtext carries more weight than surface dialogue
- Time manipulation (flashbacks, compressed time, slow moments) used deliberately
- Setting as character — environment reflects and shapes emotional states

**Forbidden Feel:** If it reads like genre fiction pretending to be literary, or if the prose calls attention to itself at the expense of story, it has failed.

## Commercial/Genre Fiction (Thriller, Mystery, Romance, Sci-Fi, Fantasy)

**Voice:** Genre-appropriate pace and energy. Honor genre expectations while adding fresh twists.

**Structure by Subgenre:**
- **Thriller/Mystery:** Plant clues fairly, maintain tension, avoid deus ex machina, red herrings must be plausible
- **Romance:** Follow the emotional beat sheet (meet, conflict, dark moment, resolution), the relationship IS the plot
- **Sci-Fi:** Internal consistency of world rules, technology impacts society plausibly, human stories within speculative settings
- **Fantasy:** World-building serves story (not the reverse), magic systems have rules, cultures feel lived-in

**Pacing Standards:**
- Scene-sequel pattern: action scene followed by reaction/processing
- Chapter endings as hooks — every chapter must compel turning the page
- Subplot weaving: threads converge satisfyingly
- Tension escalation through the middle — no sagging second act

**Forbidden Feel:** If it reads like a trope checklist, or if the world-building overwhelms the characters, it has failed.

## Self-Help / Business

**Voice:** Expert but accessible. Data-driven but relatable. The reader should feel like they're getting insider knowledge from someone who has been there.

**Structure:**
- Open with a compelling anecdote or surprising statistic
- Framework-driven: give readers mental models they can apply
- Case studies that illustrate principles in action
- Practical exercises, worksheets, or reflection questions per chapter
- Clear, actionable takeaways — the reader should know exactly what to do

**Research Standards:**
- Support claims with recent research and data
- Name specific companies, people, and examples — not hypotheticals
- Acknowledge counterarguments — credibility comes from honesty
- Statistics must be sourced and current

**Tone Rules:**
- Confident but not arrogant
- Practical above inspirational — "what to do" beats "why to believe"
- Avoid empty motivational language ("you've got this!" without substance)
- Respect the reader's time — every paragraph must earn its place

**Forbidden Feel:** If it reads like a LinkedIn post, a motivational poster, or a college textbook, it has failed.
