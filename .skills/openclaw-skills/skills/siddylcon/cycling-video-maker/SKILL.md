# Cycling Video Maker — Cycling Training and Ride Videos

The rider just spent $4,000 on a carbon frame to save 800 grams but hasn't done a bike fit, which means their saddle is 2cm too high, their handlebars are 3cm too far forward, and the resulting knee pain costs them more speed than an extra kilogram ever would. Cycling content thrives because the sport is uniquely data-rich — power meters, heart-rate zones, cadence sensors, GPS elevation profiles, and aerodynamic drag coefficients create a depth of analysis that makes every ride a potential video and every training block a documentable experiment. This tool transforms ride footage, training data, and cycling knowledge into polished videos — POV ride footage with real-time power and heart-rate overlays, animated route maps with elevation profiles and segment highlights, bike-fit analysis with joint-angle overlays, training-plan visualizations showing periodization blocks, nutrition-timing diagrams synced to effort levels, and the data-rich storytelling that makes cycling content both educational and aspirational. Built for cycling coaches building training-plan libraries, bike-fit specialists creating educational content, cycling YouTubers documenting rides and races, cycling brands producing product-review videos, triathlon coaches covering the bike leg, and anyone who has a Garmin full of data and wants to turn it into a story.

## Example Prompts

### 1. Training — FTP Test and Zone Explanation
"Create a 6-minute video explaining FTP testing and power-based training zones. Opening (0-15 sec): a rider on a trainer, suffering — sweat dripping, power numbers climbing on screen. 'This is the worst 20 minutes in cycling. It's also the most important. Your Functional Threshold Power determines every training zone, every interval target, and whether your training is actually making you faster or just making you tired.' What is FTP (15-60 sec): animated — 'FTP is the maximum average power you can sustain for approximately one hour. It's measured in watts and it's the anchor for all structured training.' Show: a power curve — short efforts (5 sec) produce high watts, FTP is the sustainable plateau. 'A recreational cyclist might have an FTP of 180W. A Cat 3 racer: 260W. A professional: 380-420W.' The test (60-160 sec): the 20-minute test protocol. Warm-up: 10 minutes easy spinning. 3 × 1-minute high-cadence builds. 5 minutes easy. Then: 20 minutes as hard as you can sustain. 'Not as hard as you can go for the first 5 minutes — as hard as you can SUSTAIN for all 20.' Show the pacing strategy: 'Start at what feels like 8/10 effort. If you're dying at minute 10, you started too hard. If you have energy at minute 18, you started too easy.' After the test: average power for the 20 minutes × 0.95 = estimated FTP. 'The 5% reduction accounts for the fact that a 20-minute effort is slightly above hour-sustainable power.' Training zones (160-340 sec): animated zone chart building upward. Zone 1 — Active Recovery (< 55% FTP): 'Easy spinning. Promotes blood flow without stress. This is "I can have a full conversation" effort.' Zone 2 — Endurance (56-75%): 'The base-building zone. Long rides at conversational pace. This is where aerobic fitness develops. Most of your training should be here.' Zone 3 — Tempo (76-90%): 'Comfortably hard. You can talk in short sentences. Useful but risky — hard enough to accumulate fatigue, not hard enough to build top-end fitness. The "junk miles" zone if overused.' Zone 4 — Threshold (91-105%): 'FTP territory. You can sustain this for 20-60 minutes. This is where structured intervals live: 2×20 minutes at FTP is a classic session.' Zone 5 — VO2max (106-120%): 'Hard. 3-8 minute intervals. This builds your oxygen-processing ceiling.' Zone 6/7 — Anaerobic/Sprint (>120%): 'Short, explosive efforts. 30 seconds to 2 minutes. Builds top-end power for attacks and sprints.' Show each zone with a color band, power range, heart-rate equivalent, and perceived exertion description. The polarized approach (340-380 sec): 'Research shows the most effective training distribution is 80% easy (Zone 1-2) and 20% hard (Zone 4-5). Most recreational cyclists ride too hard on easy days and too easy on hard days — they live in Zone 3, getting mediocre results from maximum fatigue.' Show the distribution: wrong (bell curve centered on Zone 3) vs right (U-shape with peaks at Zone 2 and Zone 4-5). Closing: the rider finishing the FTP test — gasping, numbers on screen. 'Twenty minutes of pain. Twelve months of structured training built on those numbers. Test every 6-8 weeks. Watch the number rise.'"

### 2. Ride Video — Epic Climb with Data Overlay
"Build a 5-minute ride video of a famous climb with real-time data. Opening: drone shot of the mountain road switchbacking upward — 'Col du Galibier. 2,642 meters. 23 kilometers at 5.1% average gradient. The Tour de France has climbed it 62 times. Today it's my turn.' The start (0-60 sec): GoPro POV — rider at the base. Data overlay: elevation 700m, gradient 4%, power 220W, HR 135bpm, speed 28km/h, distance remaining 23km. 'The bottom is deceptive — it's gentle enough to feel easy. The trap is going too hard here and paying for it in the final 8km.' Animated route map showing position on the climb. Lower slopes (60-150 sec): POV footage through alpine meadows. Gradient increases to 6%. Power: 240W, HR: 155, speed drops to 18km/h. 'This is where you find your rhythm. The gradient is steady, the scenery is distracting enough to forget the effort.' Show the animated elevation profile with current position marker progressing. Switchbacks: count them on screen — switchback 3, 4, 5 — each one revealing more of the valley below. Mid-mountain (150-240 sec): the road narrows, the gradient kicks to 8%. Power: 260W, HR: 168, speed: 14km/h. 'The air is thinner at 1,800 meters. Your power drops 3% per 1,000 meters of elevation. The body notices before the numbers do.' Show the landscape changing — treeline disappearing, snow appearing on peaks. The psychological section: 'You can see the road above you. It looks impossibly far. Don't look at it. Look at the next switchback. One at a time.' The final kilometers (240-280 sec): gradient 9%, then a brief descent (false hope), then the final ramp at 10%. Power: 230W (fading), HR: 178 (maxed), speed: 10km/h. 'This is where the climb earns its reputation. The legs are empty. The altitude is real. The summit is visible but refuses to get closer.' POV: other riders stopped on the side of the road. 'The mountain doesn't care about your training plan.' Summit (280-320 sec): the summit sign — Col du Galibier, 2,642m. Stop, straddle the bike, data overlay: total time 1:22:15, average power 238W, average HR 162, 1,245 calories burned. Drone shot circling the rider at the summit with 360-degree mountain views. 'Twenty-three kilometers. Ninety-two minutes. One of the greatest roads in cycling.' Closing: the descent — GoPro at 65km/h through the switchbacks. 'The climb took 92 minutes. The descent takes 18. That's the deal with mountain cycling — you earn every meter on the way up and spend it in minutes on the way down.'"

### 3. Bike Fit — Setting Up Your Road Bike
"Produce a 5-minute basic bike fit video for recreational riders. Opening: a rider with obvious fit problems — knees flaring out, rocking hips, hunched shoulders. 'This rider's fitness isn't the problem. Their fit is. A proper bike fit eliminates knee pain, back pain, hand numbness, and neck strain — and it's free to adjust yourself if you know the principles.' Saddle height (0-80 sec): the most critical measurement. Method: 'Sit on the bike, clip in, put one heel on the pedal at the 6 o'clock position. Your leg should be completely straight.' Show: correct (straight leg with heel) vs too high (hips rocking to reach) vs too low (significant knee bend). 'When you move your foot to the ball-of-foot position (normal pedaling), you should have a 25-30 degree knee bend.' Show the goniometer or phone-app angle measurement. 'Too high: knee pain behind the kneecap, hip rocking, saddle sores from reaching. Too low: knee pain in front of the kneecap, wasted energy from insufficient leg extension.' Saddle fore-aft (80-140 sec): 'Sit on the bike with cranks horizontal (3 and 9 o'clock). Drop a plumb line from the front of your forward kneecap. It should fall over the pedal axle.' Show the plumb line technique. 'Forward of the axle: more quad-dominant, knee strain. Behind the axle: more glute-dominant, less power transfer. The axle is the default starting point.' Handlebar reach and drop (140-220 sec): 'Reach: the distance from saddle to handlebars. Too far: lower back strain, hand numbness, neck pain from looking up. Too close: cramped position, breathing restriction.' Test: 'Sit in riding position with hands on the hoods. Your elbows should have a slight bend (15-20 degrees). If your arms are locked straight, the bars are too far.' Drop: 'The height difference between saddle and handlebars. Recreational: bars level with or slightly above the saddle. Racing: bars 5-10cm below the saddle.' Show three positions: upright (comfort, beginner), moderate (fitness riding), aggressive (racing). Cleat position (220-280 sec): 'If you use clipless pedals, cleat position determines knee tracking.' Show the ball of the foot over the pedal axle. 'The cleat should position the widest part of your foot (the ball) directly over the center of the pedal axle.' Angular alignment: 'Look at your natural foot angle when walking. Some people point toes slightly out. Set the cleat to match your natural angle — forcing a parallel foot position on someone with natural toe-out causes knee pain.' Closing: the same rider, now with corrected fit — smooth pedal stroke, stable hips, relaxed shoulders. 'Same bike. Same rider. Different fit. The knee pain will be gone by next week. The extra 15 watts from efficient pedaling will show up on the power meter immediately.'"

## Tips for Best Results

1. **Show real-time data overlays on ride footage** — Power, heart rate, gradient, and speed updating live makes rides analytical and engaging. The AI syncs data HUDs to footage when data_overlay is enabled.
2. **Animate the route map alongside POV footage** — A moving dot on the elevation profile while the rider climbs gives spatial context. The AI generates route animations when route_map is enabled.
3. **Use joint-angle overlays for bike fit** — 25-30 degree knee bend at the 6 o'clock position is invisible without a goniometer overlay. The AI renders angle measurements on fit footage.
4. **Color-code training zones** — Zone 2 in blue, Zone 4 in orange, Zone 5 in red creates instant visual association. The AI generates color-banded zone charts from your described power ranges.
5. **Include the elevation profile with position marker** — Seeing where you are on the climb and what's ahead adds narrative tension. The AI renders scrolling elevation profiles synced to footage progression.
