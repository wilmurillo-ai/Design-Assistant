# Brand Aesthetic — Product Render

## Palette

Cool neutrals as the base — near-white, light grey, a structural mid-grey.
A single strong accent color per image (often a desaturated blue, terracotta,
or deep green), used sparingly. High contrast, but not harsh. Plenty of
breathing room in the lighter end of the range. No black-black; no white-white.

## Composition

Architectural and precise. Symmetric or grid-aligned framing. Isometric,
three-quarter, or straight-on product-photography angles. Subjects sit
deliberately in the frame, often centered. Shadows are controlled and
intentional. Studio-lit sensibility applied even to abstract subjects.

## Rendering

3D render quality — Octane, Redshift, or Blender Cycles as reference engines.
Clean surfaces with subtle material variation (brushed metal, matte plastic,
soft-touch rubber). Soft key light, gentle rim, low fill. Depth of field used
sparingly. Not photorealistic in the "fooling the eye" sense — rendered
enough to read as made, not captured.

## Tone

Confident. Engineered. Intentional. Not cold, not sterile — the precision is
warm because someone cared about the details. Quiet competence rather than
spectacle.

## Constraints

No hand-drawn or painterly surfaces. No photographic subjects with faces. No
sci-fi or cyberpunk aesthetics. No neon, no holographic gradients, no chrome
reflections. No AI-art hype signals: purple-blue gradient washes, neural
network illustrations, chain iconography, "futuristic" interfaces floating
in void. No generic stock-render tropes (hands typing on laptop, lightbulb
moments, handshakes).

## Anchors

Like a Linear or Vercel marketing render. Like Framer Studio visuals. Like
the better corners of Dribbble — restrained, technical, Swiss-adjacent. Like
Apple product photography translated to abstract subjects.
