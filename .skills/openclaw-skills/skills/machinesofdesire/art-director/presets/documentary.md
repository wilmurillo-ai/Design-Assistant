# Brand Aesthetic — Documentary

## Palette

Desaturated with cool undertones. Blue-grey shadows, muted ochres and warm
neutrals in the midtones, never fully saturated. High contrast in the shadow
range, softer in the highlights. The overall feel is closer to print than
screen — think magazine paper, not backlit display.

## Composition

Single subject, often off-center, with negative space that breathes. Natural
perspective, mid-range focal length, no wide-angle distortion. Framing feels
observed rather than arranged — moments caught, not portraits posed. Handheld
camera sensibility even when the subject is architectural or still.

## Rendering

Color photography, editorial grade. Fine grain visible. Kodak Portra 400 or
Fuji Pro 400H as reference stocks. Available light, no obvious flash. Not
HDR, not overprocessed, not the oversharpened look of stock photography.
Closer to Alec Soth, Rineke Dijkstra, or a Harper's feature than to Getty.

## Tone

Serious. Observed. Unvarnished. Images feel documented rather than
produced. Quiet authority rather than spectacle.

## Constraints

No text in image. No identifiable real people (use the backs of heads,
distant figures, partial framings). No logos, brands, or product shots. No
staged compositions that feel art-directed. No AI tells: over-smooth skin,
impossible light sources, faces too symmetric, hands with wrong digit
counts, text-like artifacts. No HDR halos. No bokeh balls. No color grading
pushed into teal-and-orange.

## Anchors

Like a feature photo in Harper's or The Atlantic. Like a New York Times
Magazine double-page spread. Like a documentary photographer working on
assignment with film and available light.
