# Brand Aesthetic — Schematic

## Palette

Near-white paper ground (warm cream or cool stone-white, not true white).
Linework in deep ink — black, dark navy, or blueprint-indigo. One accent
color at most, often a faded vermilion or ochre, used to highlight the
operative element. The palette reads as printed ink on physical paper,
not vector-on-screen.

## Composition

Orthographic, isometric, or exploded-view cross-section. The subject
occupies the center or is broken into numbered components arranged across
the frame. Generous margins. Fine gridlines or callout leaders permitted
but restrained. The composition is diagrammatic — legible at a glance,
rewarding to study.

## Rendering

Precise ink linework over flat paper stock. Line weights vary deliberately
(heavy for outer silhouette, medium for primary form, light for internal
detail and hatching). Hatching or stippling for shadow and material
indication; no rendered gradients. Texture is the paper itself — subtle
fiber, slight age, faint printer-impression. Not CAD-perfect; not
hand-drawn rough either. Think plate drawing from a reference manual.

## Tone

Instructive, confident, quietly specialized. The image is teaching — not
decorating. The viewer should feel invited to understand how the thing
works, as if reading a well-designed manual or a patent drawing from an
earlier era when patents were also beautiful.

## Constraints

No photorealism. No CGI. No color photography or rendered material
simulation. No dramatic lighting — this is a diagram, not a scene. No
people. No depicted environments — the object exists against the paper
ground. No cartoon-style linework; this is technical draftsmanship.

## Anchors

Like Dorling Kindersley cross-section books. Like O'Reilly animal-cover
interior illustrations. Like Monocle infographics. Like vintage Popular
Mechanics cutaways. Like USPTO patent drawings from the early 1900s.
Like the diagrams in Japanese camera manuals.
