# Brand Aesthetic — Conceptual Illustration

## Palette

Limited palette, three or four dominant colors per image. Richer and moodier
than photography would allow — saturation is a tool, used deliberately. One
color usually dominates; others accent. Flat areas of color sit against
textured areas. Avoid the full rainbow; avoid pastel-soft; avoid corporate
bright.

## Composition

Metaphor-first. The subject is rarely literal — it's an object that stands
for an idea. Centered or strong rule-of-thirds framing, clearly composed, not
candid. Scale distortions are welcome (a tiny figure inside a giant object,
a hand holding something impossible). Negative space is active, not empty.

## Rendering

Digital painting with the texture of traditional media. Visible brushwork
or stippling. Gouache, oil, or ink with wash as references. Not flat vector
illustration. Not photorealistic. Not the 3D-rendered look of app store
illustrations. Surfaces have grain, edges have intention, light is painted
rather than simulated.

## Tone

Contemplative. Essayistic. Slightly strange. Images should reward a second
look — the first glance reads as "a picture of X," the second glance finds
the idea underneath. Invites interpretation rather than explaining.

## Constraints

No photorealism. No generic stock-illustration flatness. No corporate
clip-art cleanliness (rounded edges, mascot figures, thumbs-up gestures).
No obvious AI tells: warped text, melting hands, symmetrical faces where
asymmetry would be natural. No tech-bro gradient backgrounds. No purple-blue
AI washes. No neural-network illustrations.

## Anchors

Like a New Yorker essay illustration. Like Noma Bar's negative-space work.
Like Malika Favre at her most restrained. Like a mid-century editorial
illustration updated with contemporary tools but not contemporary clichés.
