# Unreasonable Hospitality — Core Principles Reference

> Source: "Unreasonable Hospitality" by Will Guidara (2022)
> Eleven Madison Park: from struggling brasserie → #1 restaurant in the world (2017)

## The 12 Principles

### 1. Service is Black & White; Hospitality is Color
- **Service** = delivering what's expected, competently and efficiently (table, chair, task done)
- **Hospitality** = how you make people *feel* while delivering it
- Product teams build features (service). Great teams make users feel understood (hospitality).
- Ask: "Did we complete the task?" (service) vs "Did we make them feel something?" (hospitality)

### 2. The 95/5 Rule
- Manage 95% of your business down to the penny
- Spend the remaining 5% "foolishly" — on gestures that create outsized emotional impact
- That 5% isn't waste; it's the highest-ROI investment you'll make
- In product: 95% solid engineering, 5% delightful surprises users never asked for

### 3. One Size Fits One
- Mass personalization is the enemy of true hospitality
- Every interaction should feel custom-made for *this* person, *this* moment
- Read signals: What do they actually need vs what they say they need?
- The hot dog story: tourists who'd never been to NYC got a NYC street hot dog course at EMP

### 4. Intention Over Mechanics
- Every gesture must come from genuine care, not scripted obligation
- Users detect fake personalization instantly — it's worse than no personalization
- "The extraordinary power of intention" — small acts done with real thought outperform expensive gestures done by rote

### 5. Read and React (Improvisational Hospitality)
- Listen → observe → interpret → create → deliver
- Don't wait for feedback forms. Read real-time signals.
- Build systems that allow team members to act on observations without asking permission
- At EMP: servers overheard conversations and created spontaneous personalized moments

### 6. Earned Informality
- You must first master formality before you can break the rules
- Breaking rules only works after trust is established
- In product: nail the basics first, then surprise with unconventional choices
- Informality without mastery = sloppiness. Informality with mastery = magic.

### 7. Praise is Affirmation; Criticism is Investment
- Public praise, private criticism — always
- Feedback should be specific, immediate, and caring
- "I'm telling you this because I believe you can be better" — frame criticism as belief
- For AI: frame suggestions as investment in the user's success, not as corrections

### 8. Ownership at Every Level
- Everyone should "own" something — a domain, a detail, a part of the experience
- Ownership creates engagement, pride, and innovation
- At EMP: servers owned specific aspects (music, flowers, glassware)
- In product: let every team member own a piece of the user experience

### 9. Excellence is Thousands of Details Executed Perfectly
- No single thing makes the experience. Everything together does.
- Obsess over micro-details that most people won't consciously notice
- But they *feel* it. The aggregate creates an invisible quality signal.
- "The last 10% is what separates good from world-class"

### 10. The Audacious Goal
- Set a goal so ambitious it seems unreasonable
- Don't worry about not knowing how to achieve it yet
- The goal itself inspires creative problem-solving
- EMP: "Become #1 in the world" — they didn't know how, but the clarity drove every decision

### 11. Slow Down to Speed Up
- When you're pushing too hard, pause and reset
- The deep breath principle: in high-pressure moments, breathe first
- Sustainable excellence > burnout-driven sprints
- Quality deteriorates when the team is exhausted, no matter how skilled they are

### 12. Human Connection Over Transaction
- "We were no longer in the business of running a restaurant; we were in the business of human connection"
- Every product, service, or interaction is ultimately about the human on the other end
- Technology should amplify human connection, never replace it
- The goal: make people feel seen, heard, and valued

### 13. The Closed Loop — Hospitality That Sustains Itself
- One-time gestures fade. Systems persist.
- Plan the experience → Execute → Measure feedback → Improve → Repeat
- Every insight from a user must complete the loop: observed → captured → acted on → verified
- Knowledge capture is non-negotiable: if you learned it but didn't write it down, you'll lose it
- The PDCA cycle is the operational backbone; hospitality is the soul that rides on it
- "A feedback loop done well means you never make the same mistake twice — and every success gets replicated"

### 14. Accountability Without Bureaucracy
- Every part of the experience needs an owner — but ownership ≠ paperwork
- RACI light: who's Responsible, who's Consulted, who's Informed — that's it
- Remove approval layers that slow down hospitality moments
- Trust your team to act, then review together — don't pre-approve everything
- The goal: every person can make a hospitality decision in real-time without asking permission

## The Hospitality Mindset Equation

```
Unreasonable Hospitality = Technical Excellence × Emotional Intelligence × Intentional Generosity
```

- Missing technical excellence → you're just being nice (not useful)
- Missing emotional intelligence → you're technically perfect but cold
- Missing intentional generosity → you're good but forgettable

All three must coexist.
