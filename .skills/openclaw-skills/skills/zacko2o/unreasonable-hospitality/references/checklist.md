# Unreasonable Hospitality — Design & Development Checklist

## Pre-Design: Understanding Your "Guest"

- [ ] Who is the user? What's their emotional state when they arrive?
- [ ] What's their actual goal? (Not what they typed, but what they *need*)
- [ ] What signals can we read? (context, history, behavior, time of day)
- [ ] What would make them feel *seen* — not just served?
- [ ] What's their "hot dog moment"? (an unexpected delight only for them)

## Foundation: Technical Excellence (The 95%)

- [ ] Core functionality works flawlessly — no excuses
- [ ] Performance is fast — speed is respect for the user's time
- [ ] Error handling is graceful — failures should feel human, not robotic
- [ ] Consistency — the experience is reliable every single time
- [ ] Accessibility — hospitality means everyone is welcome
- [ ] Documentation — clear, helpful, not an afterthought

## The Magic: Hospitality Layer (The 5%)

### Micro-Moments of Delight
- [ ] First-use experience: Does it feel like a warm welcome or a cold form?
- [ ] Loading states: Is waiting time made pleasant or just tolerated?
- [ ] Success states: Do we celebrate with the user or just move on?
- [ ] Empty states: Do we guide warmly or show a blank void?
- [ ] Error messages: Do they help and empathize, or just blame?

### Personalization (One Size Fits One)
- [ ] Does the product adapt to *this* user's patterns?
- [ ] Are defaults smart — based on context, not assumptions?
- [ ] Does it remember what matters to each user?
- [ ] Is personalization genuine or performative? (Users detect fake instantly)

### Anticipation (Read and React)
- [ ] Can the system predict what the user needs next?
- [ ] Does it offer help before being asked?
- [ ] Does it get out of the way when the user is in flow?
- [ ] Does it notice when something is wrong and respond?

### Earned Informality
- [ ] Are the basics mastered before adding playful elements?
- [ ] Is the tone matched to the user's emotional context?
- [ ] Are unconventional choices earned through demonstrated competence?

## Communication: How We Make People Feel

### Feedback as Investment
- [ ] Suggestions feel like gifts, not corrections
- [ ] Errors are explained with empathy and clear next steps
- [ ] Progress is celebrated — even small wins
- [ ] Criticism is private, specific, and caring

### Language Principles
- [ ] Warm but not sycophantic
- [ ] Concise but not cold
- [ ] Helpful but not condescending
- [ ] Professional but not corporate
- [ ] Human but not unprofessional

## Team/Product Culture

- [ ] Everyone owns a piece of the experience
- [ ] Daily alignment on what "hospitality" means today
- [ ] Permission to break rules when it serves the user
- [ ] Sustainable pace — exhausted teams can't deliver magic
- [ ] Celebrate hospitality wins, not just metrics wins

## Closed-Loop Feedback (Sustaining Hospitality)

- [ ] Every feature/interaction has a defined "experience standard" (what should the user feel?)
- [ ] Feedback channels exist and are actively monitored (<48h response)
- [ ] Feedback is bilateral: we ask users AND we share back what we learned
- [ ] Insights are assigned to owners with deadlines (not just logged)
- [ ] Weekly/sprint retrospective includes hospitality quality, not just velocity
- [ ] Knowledge is captured: what we learned → written down → accessible to all
- [ ] Processes are simplified each cycle, not accumulated
- [ ] Metrics tracked: completion rate, feedback timeliness, issue resolution, rework rate
- [ ] "Pseudo-loop" check: is anyone actually reading and acting on the feedback?
- [ ] New team members are onboarded into the hospitality standard, not just the codebase

## The Ultimate Test

Ask after every interaction/release/feature:
1. ✅ Did it work? (Service)
2. ❤️ Did it feel good? (Hospitality)
3. 🌟 Will they remember it? (Unreasonable Hospitality)

If only #1 → You're competent
If #1 + #2 → You're good
If #1 + #2 + #3 → You're world-class
