## Income Tax by State

### States with No Income Tax
Texas, Florida, Washington, Nevada, Wyoming, South Dakota, Alaska (no income or sales tax)
- **Washington exception:** Has a 7% capital gains tax on gains over $262,000 (2024) — controversial, but upheld by WA Supreme Court
- **Tennessee & New Hampshire:** No tax on wages, but had (or still have for NH) taxes on investment income

## Retirement Income — State Tax Treatment

This is one of the most impactful state divergences for retirees.

### States That Don't Tax Any Retirement Income
Florida, Texas, Nevada, Washington, Wyoming, South Dakota, Alaska (no income tax)
Illinois, Mississippi, Pennsylvania — no income tax but **also specifically exempt** pension/retirement income

### States That Partially Exempt Retirement Income
- **New York:** Social Security exempt; pensions from NY state/local government fully exempt; up to $20,000 of private pension income exempt
- **California:** Does NOT follow federal rules — all retirement income taxable (except Social Security which is fully exempt at federal level but CA also doesn't tax SS)
- **Colorado:** Up to $24,000 of pension/retirement income excluded (age 65+)
- **Georgia:** Up to $65,000 per person exempt (age 65+)
- **Michigan:** Complex rules based on birth year for pension exemption

### Social Security: State Taxation
Federal: Up to 85% of SS benefits taxable depending on "combined income"
- **11 states still tax Social Security** (to some degree): Colorado, Connecticut, Kansas, Minnesota, Missouri, Montana, Nebraska, New Mexico, Rhode Island, Utah, Vermont
- Most others: fully exempt SS from state income tax

## Capital Gains — Notable State Differences

- **Federal:** Long-term capital gains taxed at 0/15/20% — separate from ordinary income
- **Most states:** Treat capital gains as ordinary income (taxed at the same rate as wages)
- **California:** Taxes all capital gains as ordinary income — no preferential rate. Top combined federal + CA rate can exceed 33%
- **Washington:** 7% on long-term gains over $262,000 — unique in having a separate capital gains tax
- **Hawaii:** Also taxes capital gains as ordinary income
- **Pennsylvania:** Flat 3.07% on all gains — no distinction between short and long-term
- **New Hampshire & Tennessee:** Historically taxed investment income separately; NH still has limited investment income tax through 2024

## Deductions — State-Level Variations

### SALT (State and Local Tax Deduction)
- Federal: Capped at $10,000 (through 2025 under TCJA)
- Many states have enacted **SALT workaround pass-through entity (PTE) taxes** — partnerships and S-corps can elect to pay state tax at the entity level (deductible federally), then give owners a state credit. This is widely available and worth noting for small business owners in high-tax states. Currently available in: CA, NY, NJ, IL, MA, and 30+ other states.

### Mortgage Interest Deduction
- Most states conform to federal (deductible on Schedule A if itemizing)
- **California:** Follows federal rules but with its own AGI-based limitations
- Some states have their own standard deductions that make itemizing irrelevant

### Standard Deduction — States Vary Widely
Some states have no standard deduction (forcing itemization or a different mechanism). Others have their own amounts completely disconnected from federal.

## Filing Status — State Differences

- Most states use the same filing status as federal
- **California** uses the same federal filing statuses
- Some community property states (AZ, CA, ID, LA, NM, NV, TX, WA, WI) have specific rules about how married couples report income — especially relevant when filing MFS federally

## Business / Self-Employment

### Franchise Tax
Some states charge a **franchise tax** (essentially a business privilege tax) even for small businesses:
- **California:** $800 minimum franchise tax for all LLCs, S-corps, and C-corps, regardless of income
- **Texas:** Franchise ("margin") tax — based on revenue, not income; applies to most businesses
- **Delaware:** Popular state for incorporation; charges annual franchise tax based on shares authorized

### Gross Receipts Tax
A few states tax business revenue directly (not profit):
- **Washington:** B&O (Business & Occupation) tax on gross revenue
- **Ohio:** Commercial Activity Tax (CAT)
- **Nevada, Texas:** Similar gross receipts mechanisms

## Homeownership & Property Tax

Property tax is entirely state/local — no federal property tax. Rates vary enormously:
- **New Jersey:** Highest effective property tax rates (~2.2%)
- **Illinois:** Also very high (~2.1%)
- **Hawaii:** Lowest effective rates (~0.3%) despite high property values
- **California Prop 13:** Assessed value locked to purchase price + 2% annual max increase. Long-time homeowners may pay tiny taxes on very valuable property
- **Homestead exemptions:** Most states offer some reduction for primary residence — amount varies hugely

**Home sale exclusion ($250K/$500K):** Most states conform to federal exclusion, but a few have nuances. California conforms fully.

## Reciprocity Agreements (For People Working in a Different State)

Some neighboring states have agreements so you only pay income tax to your state of residence (not the state where you work):
- **Examples:** NJ/PA, MD/VA/DC, IL/WI, KY and several neighbors
- No such agreement: **NY** taxes non-residents on NY-sourced income aggressively — many NJ/CT commuters must file both states
- **Remote work twist:** If you work remotely from State A for a company in State B, you may owe taxes in both — especially in NY, which has a "convenience of the employer" rule

## Crypto — State Treatment

- Most states follow federal treatment (property, not currency)
- **California:** No specific crypto guidance separate from federal; taxes as ordinary income at all stages
- **Wyoming:** More crypto-friendly regulations, but doesn't change income tax treatment (no income tax anyway)
- **New York:** BitLicense required for crypto businesses; personal tax treatment follows federal
