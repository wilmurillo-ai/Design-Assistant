# Escalation Guide — When and How to Flag Professional Help

> This reference supports the 🟢/🟡/🔴 flagging system.
> Use it to calibrate the bottom-line flag on each response.
> The goal: be honest about complexity without being so cautious that the skill becomes useless.

---

## The Core Principle

The escalation flag exists to protect the user — not to protect the skill from liability. Most people asking tax questions have never spoken to a CPA and are trying to figure out if they need to. Give them a real answer to that question.

**Over-flagging 🔴 on simple questions destroys trust.** If everything is "get professional help," the skill is useless.
**Under-flagging 🔴 on genuinely complex situations causes real harm.** A missed S-corp election or botched cost basis can cost thousands.

Calibrate like a knowledgeable friend who will tell you honestly: "You can handle this yourself" or "I'd spend the $300 on a CPA if I were you."

---

## 🟢 Handle It Yourself

**Default for:** Single-income W-2 employees, straightforward freelancers with clean income and common deductions, standard retirement account contributions, common credits (child tax, EITC, education).

**Signals it's 🟢:**
- Question has a clear yes/no answer with no unusual variables
- TurboTax, H&R Block, FreeTaxUSA, or Cash App Taxes can handle it
- The user's situation matches the textbook example
- Mistake risk is low (e.g., worst case they get a corrected return)
- Amount at stake is modest (under ~$1,000 in potential tax impact)

**Example 🟢 situations:**
- "Is my gym membership deductible?" (No — straightforward)
- "I got a 1099-NEC for $2,000 from a side project. Do I report it?" (Yes — simple SE income)
- "What's the 2024 standard deduction?" (Look up and answer)
- "Can I contribute to a Roth IRA?" (Check income against phase-out table)
- "I sold stock I held for 2 years. What rate am I taxed at?" (Long-term capital gains — look up rate)

---

## 🟡 Worth a Quick CPA Check

**Default for:** Self-employed with significant income or complex expense mix, first year of major life change (marriage, divorce, home purchase, business launch, major inheritance), rental property owners, high-income earners near phase-out thresholds.

**Signals it's 🟡:**
- The answer has a "but" — a scenario that changes things meaningfully
- A one-time question from a CPA would likely pay for itself in tax savings or penalty avoidance
- Tax software can technically handle it, but requires careful data entry the user may not know how to do
- A mistake wouldn't be catastrophic but could be costly
- There's a planning opportunity (e.g., "should you convert to Roth this year?") that requires knowing their full financial picture

**Example 🟡 situations:**
- Freelancer making $60,000+ who has never claimed home office, retirement contributions, or health insurance deduction
- First year owning rental property (depreciation schedule setup matters)
- Someone who got married or divorced mid-year (especially with very different incomes)
- W-2 employee with significant RSUs or ESPP activity
- Starting a business and wondering about entity structure for a <$100k income business
- Self-employed with employees for the first time
- Received a large inheritance (especially if from out of state or includes real estate)

**Recommended framing for 🟡:**
"This is manageable with tax software, but a quick session with a CPA (typically $150–$400 for a consult) could [specific benefit — e.g., confirm you're claiming the home office correctly / identify if an S-corp election makes sense at your income level]."

## 🔴 Get Professional Help

**Default for:** These categories always get 🔴 — they're not judgment calls.

### Always 🔴
1. **Crypto with complexity:** Multiple exchanges, DeFi/DEX trades, NFTs, staking rewards, yield farming, wrapped tokens. Cost basis errors are common and cumulative. Direct to crypto tax software (Koinly, CoinTracker, TaxBit) AND CPA review.

2. **International situations:**
   - Foreign bank accounts over $10,000 at any point in the year → FBAR required (FinCEN 114), separate from tax return, $10,000/day penalty for willful failure
   - Foreign financial assets → FATCA Form 8938
   - Foreign earned income → Form 2555, foreign tax credits (Form 1116)
   - Dual-status returns (part-year resident/non-resident alien)
   - Income from foreign investments or foreign trusts

3. **IRS notices and back taxes:**
   - Any CP or LTR notice from IRS — especially CP2000 (proposed changes), LT11 (intent to levy), Notice of Deficiency
   - Years of unfiled returns
   - Offer in Compromise discussions
   - Installment agreements on large balances

4. **Business structure decisions:**
   - S-corp election (Form 2553) — timing, reasonable salary requirement, payroll setup
   - C-corp vs. S-corp vs. LLC decision for a growing business
   - Partnership agreement and K-1 implications

5. **Estate and trust situations:**
   - Administering an estate (Form 1041, estate income tax)
   - Inherited IRA with complex rules (SECURE Act 10-year rule, stretch IRA, eligible designated beneficiaries)
   - Gift tax returns (Form 709) — especially gifts over $18,000 or with basis allocation issues
   - Generation-skipping transfers

6. **Large capital events:**
   - Sale of a business (Section 1202 exclusion, installment sales, asset vs. stock sale implications)
   - Stock options: ISO exercise with AMT exposure, NSO exercise timing, QSBS
   - Real estate with significant depreciation recapture (Section 1250)
   - Like-kind exchange (1031 exchange) — strict identification and timeline rules

7. **Self-employed with employees:**
   - Payroll tax compliance (Form 941, FUTA, state unemployment)
   - Trust Fund Recovery Penalty exposure (personal liability for unpaid payroll taxes)
   - Benefits plan setup

### Situationally 🔴 (higher income + complexity)
- AMT exposure (income over ~$200,000 with significant ISOs, large SALT deductions, or depreciation)
- Passive activity losses from real estate that have been accumulating for years
- Net operating losses (NOL) carryback/carryforward
- Qualified opportunity zone investments

## How to Write the Escalation Sentence

The sentence after the flag must be **specific**, not generic.

❌ Generic (useless): "This situation is complex and you should consult a tax professional."

✅ Specific (useful):
- 🟢 "This is a straightforward deduction — TurboTax or FreeTaxUSA will handle it with no issues."
- 🟡 "At your freelance income level, a CPA could likely identify enough deductions (retirement contributions, home office, health insurance) to more than cover their fee."
- 🔴 "Cost basis tracking across DeFi transactions is the most error-prone area in crypto tax right now — a single mistake can compound across years. Use Koinly or CoinTracker to generate the reports, then have a crypto-literate CPA review before you file."
- 🔴 "FBAR is a separate filing from your tax return with its own deadline, and the penalties for non-compliance are disproportionately severe — this is not an area to navigate alone."

## Finding Professional Help — What to Tell Users

When flagging 🔴 or 🟡, give actionable next steps:

**Finding a CPA or EA:**
- IRS "Find a Tax Professional" directory: https://irs.treasury.gov/rpo/rpo.jsf — shows credentialed professionals (CPAs, EAs, attorneys)
- AICPA CPA Locator: https://www.aicpa.org/forthepublic/findacpa
- NAEA (Enrolled Agent directory): https://www.naea.org/find-an-enrolled-agent/

**Credential types — explain when relevant:**
- **CPA (Certified Public Accountant):** Licensed by state, broad expertise, can represent before IRS
- **EA (Enrolled Agent):** Federally licensed, tax-only specialists, can represent before IRS, often more affordable than CPAs for pure tax work
- **Tax attorney:** For legal disputes, tax court, criminal matters, complex estate work
- **VITA volunteers:** Free tax prep for income under ~$67,000 — https://irs.gov/vita

**For crypto specifically:** Look for CPAs who list crypto/digital assets as a specialty. General CPAs often don't know the nuances.

**For international:** Look for CPAs with "international tax" in their specialty list, or firms that explicitly serve expats.

---

## What NOT to Say

- Don't say "I'm not a tax professional and this isn't tax advice" as an opener — it undermines every answer before it's given. Trust is built by being right and being clear, not by disclaiming everything upfront.
- Don't refuse to explain a concept just because the situation is complex — explaining how something works and recommending professional help are not mutually exclusive.
- Don't say "it depends" without immediately saying what it depends on.
- Don't add a generic "consult a professional" to every 🟢 situation — it signals you don't actually mean it when it matters.
