# Basic Usage Examples

This document demonstrates the continuous progressive questioning flow of the `idea-to-post` skill.

**Core characteristic: Not asking everything in one round, but continuously questioning until information is complete.**

---

## Example 1: Viewpoint-Type Topic (PREP Framework)

### User Input

```
Expand idea: Remote work is the future
```

### Continuous Questioning Dialogue

**【Round 1: Core Viewpoint】**

```
Skill: Is your core viewpoint that "remote work will inevitably become mainstream",
        or that "remote work is only suitable for certain industries"?

User: Only suitable for certain industries, not all industries
```

**【Round 2: Reason Inquiry】**

```
Skill: Why do you think so? What led you to this conclusion?

User: Our company tried fully remote, and collaboration efficiency decreased
```

**【Round 3: Case Deep Dive】**

```
Skill: Can you tell me more specifically? Which link's efficiency decreased?
        Any data comparison?

User: Meeting communication efficiency decreased by about 30%, number of meetings increased instead
```

**【Round 4: Counter-View Thinking】**

```
Skill: Any cases where remote work worked well?

User: Independent development tasks actually had improved efficiency
```

**【Round 5: Viewpoint Refinement】**

```
Skill: So what you want to express is:
        "Remote work suits independent tasks, not high-frequency collaboration"?

User: Yes, that's it
```

**【Round 6: Target Audience】**

```
Skill: Who is this article written for?
        A. People currently working remotely
        B. Company decision makers considering remote work
        C. People against remote work
        D. Other

User: B - Company decision makers
```

**【Round 7: Emotional Resonance】**

```
Skill: What feeling do you want readers to have after reading?
        Is it the reflection of "I should rethink our remote strategy",
        or the resonance of "my judgment was right"?

User: Resonance, let them know not all industries are suitable for remote
```

**【Round 8: Call to Action】**

```
Skill: What do you want readers to do after reading?
        A. Share their remote work experience
        B. Reevaluate their company's remote strategy
        C. Forward to people who need it
        D. Other

User: B - Reevaluate their company's remote strategy
```

**【Round 9: Publishing Platform】**

```
Skill: Mainly which platform to publish?
        A. WeChat Official Account
        B. Juejin/Zhihu
        C. Weibo/Twitter
        D. LinkedIn

User: B - Juejin/Zhihu
```

**【Skill Judgment: Information Complete】**

✅ Core viewpoint: Remote work only suits certain industries
✅ Supporting cases: Company experience + specific data
✅ Target audience: Company decision makers
✅ Emotional resonance: Not all industries are suitable for remote
✅ Call to action: Reevaluate strategy
✅ Publishing platform: Juejin/Zhihu

→ Start generating content

---

### Generated Result

#### Juejin/Zhihu Version

```markdown
# Remote Work Is Not a Silver Bullet: Our Fully Remote Experiment Failed

## Preface

Two years ago, our company decided to go fully remote.

At that time remote work was hot, many big companies were promoting its benefits. We followed the trend, thinking this was the future.

**One year later, we cancelled fully remote, switched to hybrid work.**

---

## The Failed Experience

### Problem: Collaboration Efficiency Decreased

After going fully remote, we discovered:
- Number of meetings increased 50%
- Meeting communication efficiency decreased by about 30%
- Many simple communications became complicated

What used to be solved by walking to a desk now requires scheduling meetings, waiting for times.

### Reason Analysis

Why did this problem occur?

**Because our industry (we do product design requiring frequent collaboration)
is not very suitable for fully remote.**

---

## The Successful Part

But it's not that remote work is completely useless.

**We found: Independent development tasks actually had improved efficiency.**

Developer colleagues reported:
- No office interruptions, longer focused time
- Flexible work schedule
- Saved commute time

---

## Conclusion

Remote work is not a "right or wrong" question, but a "suitable or not" question.

**Industries/jobs suitable for fully remote:**
- Tasks with strong independence
- Individual creation like copywriting, design
- Standardized processes like customer service

**Industries/jobs not suitable for fully remote:**
- Teams needing frequent creative collision
- Projects requiring real-time collaboration
- Customer-facing service industries

---

## Advice for Decision Makers

If you're considering going fully remote, ask yourself three questions first:

1. Is your work nature suitable for remote?
2. Does your team have enough self-discipline?
3. Are your collaboration tools complete enough?

**Don't follow the trend, make decisions based on your actual situation.**
```

```markdown
# Small Team's Breakthrough Path: We Found Light in Big Companies' Shadow

## S-Situation: The Starting Point

Three years ago, we were a 5-person small team.

Wanted to build a developer tool, but found big companies were all making similar products.

**We felt despair: How to compete with big companies?**

---

## C-Conflict: The Dilemma

The product was made, but few people used it.

Three months after launch, daily active users were still only a few hundred.

Team morale was low, someone started to doubt:
"Should we give up?"

---

## Q-Question: The Breakout

That was a late night, we few founders sat in the conference room.

We asked ourselves: **What don't big companies do?**

Big companies:
- Pursue big and comprehensive
- Serve mass users
- Make universal functions

So what about us?

---

## A-Answer: The Focus

We discovered the opportunity: **Niche but high-frequency scenarios.**

Big companies don't do it because the market isn't big enough.
But for us, this "small market" is enough to feed the team.

We cut 80% of features, focused on one scenario:
**Developer's local debugging tool.**

---

## The Turning Point: Another Dilemma

After focusing, users were still few.

We were crushed: Was the direction right?

Later we discovered the problem wasn't the direction, but guidance:
**Users didn't know how to use.**

We added product guidance, video tutorials, example code.

**Result: Retention rate increased 3 times.**

---

## Now

Three years later.

In this niche field, we're number one.

Although user count isn't as much as big companies' products, but:
- Users are more precise
- Stronger willingness to pay
- We survived

---

## Insight

Small teams shouldn't compete head-on with big companies.

**Find what big companies disdain to do, do it to the extreme.**

Small market doesn't matter, as long as it's enough for you to live well.
```

## Key Takeaways Summary

### Characteristics of Continuous Questioning

1. **Not asking all in one round** - Multi-round dialogue, gradually deepening
2. **Based on topic questions** - Each question is tightly tied to user input topic
3. **Based on framework design** - Use thinking framework logic to organize questions
4. **Dynamic adjustment** - Adjust next round questions based on user answers
5. **Complete information before stopping** - Core dimensions all present before generating

### Questioning Levels

```
Round 1: Big direction (goal, audience, platform)
    ↓
Round 2: Core elements (based on framework)
    ↓
Round 3: Detail supplement (cases, data, turning points)
    ↓
Round 4: Polish design (emotional points, call to action)
    ↓
Round 5: Confirm and revise
```

### Information Completeness Standards

| Dimension | Check |
|----------|-------|
| Core viewpoint | ✓ Clear |
| Target audience | ✓ Clear |
| Supporting material | ✓ Sufficient |
| Emotional value | ✓ Has resonance points |
| Call to action | ✓ Specific and clear |
| Platform adaptation | ✓ Fits characteristics |
| External validation | ✓ Has search materials |
