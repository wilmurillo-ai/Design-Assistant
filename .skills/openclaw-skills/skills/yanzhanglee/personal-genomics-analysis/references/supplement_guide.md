## Section 1: Genetic Variant → Supplement Mapping

### 1. MTHFR C677T (rs1801133) Heterozygous/Homozygous

**Recommended Supplement**: 5-MTHF (Active Folate)
**Dosage**: 400–800 μg/day
**Evidence**: PMID 25617120 (MTHFR variants and folate metabolism); PMID 23591439 (homocysteine reduction with methylfolate)
**Clinical Rationale**:
- The MTHFR enzyme catalyzes conversion of folic acid to 5-methyltetrahydrofolate (5-MTHF), the active form used by cells
- C677T heterozygotes have ~35% reduced enzyme activity; homozygotes have ~65% reduction
- Supplementing with pre-methylated 5-MTHF bypasses this conversion step
- Particularly important if elevated homocysteine or family history of cardiovascular disease

**Additional Recommendation**: B-Complex containing methylcobalamin (B12)
- **Dosage**: 1000 μg methylcobalamin/day or 2000 μg weekly IM
- **Rationale**: 5-MTHF metabolism is coupled to B12-dependent reactions; B12 deficiency can accumulate 5-MTHF without clinical benefit
- **Evidence**: PMID 23591439

**Special Considerations**:
- Heterozygotes may tolerate standard folic acid; homozygotes typically benefit from 5-MTHF
- Start with 400 μg daily; titrate up if homocysteine remains elevated
- Monitor for rare side effects (anxiety, overstimulation) if methylation-sensitive

### 2. VDR Variants + Low Sun Exposure

**Recommended Supplement**: Vitamin D3 (Cholecalciferol)
**Dosage**: 2000 IU/day (higher if 25(OH)D <30 ng/mL or homozygous VDR ff genotype)
**Evidence**: PMID 26426267 (VDR variants and vitamin D metabolism); PMID 21048628 (D3 supplementation and immune function)
**Clinical Rationale**:
- VDR (vitamin D receptor) polymorphisms affect D3 metabolism, calcitriol synthesis, and tissue response
- Homozygous ff genotype carriers may require 20–30% higher supplementation for equivalent serum levels
- Sun exposure <30 minutes daily or year-round low sunlight increases deficiency risk
- Target 25(OH)D: 40–60 ng/mL for optimal immune and bone health

**Cofactor Recommendation**: Vitamin K2 (MK-7, Menaquinone-7)
**Dosage**: 100–200 μg/day
**Rationale**:
- D3 increases intestinal calcium absorption; K2 directs calcium to bone and away from soft tissues
- MK-7 has longer half-life (~2–3 weeks) than other K2 forms
- Critical if personal or family history of cardiovascular disease or osteoporosis
- **Evidence**: PMID 25617120 (K2/D3 synergy in calcium metabolism)

**Monitoring**:
- Check serum 25(OH)D annually; adjust dose to maintain 40–60 ng/mL
- If VDR ff genotype and compliance verified, may need 3000–4000 IU/day

### 3. FADS1 rs174547 TT (Poor Omega-3 Conversion) + Lipid Risk SNPs

**Recommended Supplement**: EPA Fish Oil (High-Purity EPA)
**Dosage**: 1000–2000 mg EPA/day (specify EPA, not total "fish oil")
**Evidence**: PMID 26356095 (FADS1 variants and omega-3 metabolism); PMID 23393290 (EPA supplementation and CAD prevention)
**Clinical Rationale**:
- FADS1 encodes delta-5 desaturase, the rate-limiting enzyme converting ALA to long-chain PUFA (EPA, DHA)
- TT genotype carriers have significantly reduced enzymatic activity (25–50% lower EPA/DHA from dietary ALA)
- Supplementing with preformed EPA bypasses this genetic limitation
- EPA specifically demonstrates stronger cardiovascular and anti-inflammatory benefits vs. DHA alone

**Product Specifications**:
- Source: high-purity purified fish oil or krill oil (triglyceride form superior to ester form)
- Contaminant testing: PCB, heavy metals certified by third-party lab (e.g., IFOS standard)
- Avoid generic "fish oil" labels; verify EPA/DHA breakdown on label

**Additional Recommendation**: If co-occurring dyslipidemia
- Add **CoQ10 (ubiquinol)** 100–200 mg/day (see Section 1.5 below)
- **Synergy evidence**: PMID 24098767 (combined effect on lipid particles and LDL particle size)

**Monitoring**:
- Check omega-3 index (red blood cell EPA+DHA) annually; target >8%
- Monitor triglycerides every 3 months if dyslipidemia present

### 4. Uric Acid Transporter Variants (SLC2A9, ABCG2) + Gout Risk

**Recommended Supplement**: Vitamin C (Ascorbic Acid)
**Dosage**: 500 mg/day (taken in divided doses; morning and evening)
**Evidence**: PMID 26356095 (uric acid transporters and gout); PMID 17170403 (vitamin C and uric acid excretion)
**Clinical Rationale**:
- SLC2A9 and ABCG2 polymorphisms reduce renal uric acid excretion (urate transporter dysfunction)
- Vitamin C increases renal uric acid clearance via increased glomerular filtration and reduced reabsorption
- Population studies show dose-dependent inverse correlation: 500–2000 mg/day reduces uric acid by 10–15%
- Particularly effective in combination with uricosuric medications (probenecid, benzbromarone)

**Mechanism**:
- Acidifies urine (within safe range, pH >6.0)
- Enhances transporter-mediated uric acid secretion
- Anti-inflammatory; reduces acute flare risk independent of urate lowering

**Special Considerations**:
- Split dose (250 mg × 2) improves GI tolerance and renal clearance
- Ensure adequate hydration (2–3 L water daily)
- Contraindicated in history of kidney stones; check urine pH monthly
- May increase oxalate excretion in predisposed individuals

**Monitoring**:
- Serum uric acid: every 3 months if gout history; target <6.0 mg/dL
- Urine pH: baseline and 3-month check to exclude dangerously acidic urine

### 5. Coronary Artery Disease (CAD) Family History + Multiple CAD Risk SNPs

**Recommended Supplement**: CoQ10 (Ubiquinol Form)
**Dosage**: 100–200 mg/day (ubiquinol preferred; ubiquinone if budget-limited)
**Evidence**: PMID 21956720 (CoQ10 and cardiovascular outcomes); PMID 19703113 (CAD and CoQ10 deficiency)
**Clinical Rationale**:
- CoQ10 is a mitochondrial electron transport chain cofactor; deficiency increases oxidative stress and endothelial dysfunction
- Family history and CAD-associated SNPs (e.g., 9p21, APOB, SORT1) predict lower CoQ10 and higher CAD risk
- Ubiquinol (reduced form) has 2–3× better bioavailability than ubiquinone (oxidized form) in adults >30 years
- Cardioprotective via improved energy metabolism, reduced LDL oxidation, and plaque stabilization

**Statin-Drug Interaction** (Critical):
- Statins inhibit mevalonate pathway, depleting endogenous CoQ10 by 25–50%
- **If on statin**: increase CoQ10 to 200 mg/day
- Mitigates statin-associated myalgia and improves mitochondrial function
- **Evidence**: PMID 18477486 (statin-induced CoQ10 depletion)

**Absorption Optimization**:
- Take with fat-containing meal for 2–3× better absorption
- Consider liquid or liposomal formulations if standard capsules poorly absorbed

**Monitoring**:
- Plasma CoQ10 levels not routinely required; monitor clinical parameters instead
- Check lipid panel and CRP every 3 months if CAD history
- Track exercise tolerance and muscle symptoms if on statin

### 6. TCF7L2 Diabetes Risk + Metabolic Syndrome

**Recommended Supplement**: Chelated Magnesium (Glycinate Form)
**Dosage**: 200–400 mg elemental magnesium/day
**Evidence**: PMID 23591439 (TCF7L2 variants and glucose metabolism); PMID 27400784 (magnesium and insulin sensitivity)
**Clinical Rationale**:
- TCF7L2 is involved in beta-cell function and glucose homeostasis; risk alleles predict reduced insulin secretion
- Magnesium is required for >300 enzymatic reactions including glucose transporters (GLUT4) and insulin signaling
- Population studies show inverse correlation: magnesium intake <250 mg/day associated with 50% increased T2DM risk
- Magnesium glycinate has superior absorption (30–40%) vs. oxide (~4%) and minimal laxative effect

**Form Specificity**:
- **Magnesium Glycinate**: bound to glycine amino acid; ~25–30% elemental Mg; best GI tolerance
- Avoid magnesium oxide (poorly absorbed, causes diarrhea)
- Citrate form acceptable if GI tolerance is issue

**Synergistic Recommendation** (if metabolic syndrome present):
- Consider **Chromium picolinate** 200 μg/day for additional GLUT4 sensitivity improvement
- **Evidence**: PMID 19703113

**Special Considerations**:
- Take in evening or before bed; magnesium enhances sleep quality
- Separate from calcium supplements by ≥2 hours (compete for absorption)
- Monitor bowel function; reduce dose if diarrhea develops

**Monitoring**:
- Fasting glucose and HbA1c: every 6 months if diabetes risk; target HbA1c <5.7%
- Magnesium serum level: baseline and annually (though serum Mg reflects only 1% of body store; clinical response is primary marker)

## Section 2: Priority Tiers

Supplement recommendations are classified into three tiers based on evidence strength and clinical urgency. Individuals should prioritize higher tiers aligned with their genotype and clinical presentation.

### Core (Highest Priority)

**Definition**: Strong genetic and clinical evidence; address active health issues or significant risk reduction.

**Criteria for Core**:
- Genetic variant with established functional consequence
- Phenotypic expression (e.g., elevated homocysteine, low 25(OH)D, gout symptoms)
- Multiple high-quality RCTs (n > 500) showing clinically meaningful benefit
- Clear drug-supplement interaction if on medications

**Core Supplement Examples**:
- 5-MTHF for homozygous MTHFR C677T + elevated homocysteine
- Vitamin D3 for VDR variants with documented deficiency
- EPA fish oil for FADS1 TT + dyslipidemia
- Vitamin C for SLC2A9/ABCG2 variants with gout history
- CoQ10 for CAD family history or statin use
- Magnesium for TCF7L2 diabetes risk + metabolic syndrome

**Action**: Begin immediately upon genetic finding; monitor compliance and biomarkers monthly.

### Recommended (Moderate Priority)

**Definition**: Good epidemiologic or mechanistic evidence; meaningful risk reduction even without current phenotype.

**Criteria for Recommended**:
- Genetic variant with plausible mechanism but heterogeneous phenotypic expression
- Preventive benefit in population-level studies but limited RCT data
- Low adverse event risk; generally safe in general population
- No major drug interactions

**Recommended Supplement Examples**:
- Methylcobalamin (B12) for any MTHFR variant, even without current B12 deficiency
- Vitamin K2 for all individuals on Vitamin D3 supplementation
- CoQ10 at 100 mg/day for CAD family history without current cardiovascular disease
- Magnesium 200 mg/day for anyone with metabolic syndrome, regardless of TCF7L2 genotype

**Action**: Begin if genetic variant confirmed and individual accepts preventive approach; monitor annually.

### Optional (Supporting Evidence)

**Definition**: Emerging or supporting evidence; complementary benefit with lower clinical impact.

**Criteria for Optional**:
- Genetic variant with uncertain penetrance or heterogeneous expression
- Supporting evidence from observational studies only (no RCT data)
- Potentially beneficial but not essential; consider individual risk-benefit
- Safe with minimal interactions

**Optional Supplement Examples**:
- Chromium for TCF7L2 without metabolic syndrome
- Higher-dose CoQ10 (>200 mg) for asymptomatic individuals with CAD SNPs
- Specialized forms (e.g., ubiquinone if ubiquinol unavailable/unaffordable)

**Action**: Discuss with individual; consider trial period (8–12 weeks) with reassessment. Stop if no clinical benefit or cost-prohibitive.

## Section 3: Drug-Supplement Interactions

When individuals are on medications for cardiovascular, metabolic, or inflammatory conditions, supplement recommendations must account for potential interactions. Below are critical drug-supplement pairs relevant to this guide.

### Benzbromarone (Uricosuric, Gout)

**Interaction with Vitamin C**: Minimal to none
**Recommendation**: Safe to combine; actually synergistic
- Vitamin C enhances renal uric acid excretion via separate mechanism (filtration/reabsorption)
- Combined effect: additive uric acid lowering; reduces dose requirements for benzbromarone
- Monitor for over-suppression of uric acid (<3 mg/dL) which may cause crystal nephropathy
- **Evidence**: PMID 17170403

**No major interactions** with CoQ10, magnesium, or EPA at recommended doses.

---

### Statins (Lipid-Lowering, CAD Prevention)

**Interaction with CoQ10**: Depletion mechanism
- Statins inhibit HMG-CoA reductase, blocking mevalonate pathway
- CoQ10 synthesis inhibited by 25–50%; clinical depletion occurs over months to years
- Consequence: increased statin myalgia, muscle weakness, and mitochondrial dysfunction
- **Recommendation**: Increase CoQ10 to 200 mg/day ubiquinol if on any statin
- **Evidence**: PMID 18477486; PMID 21956720

**Interaction with EPA Fish Oil**: Minor bleeding risk
- Omega-3 fatty acids mildly inhibit platelet aggregation
- Clinical significance: minimal with EPA 1000–2000 mg/day in combination with statin monotherapy
- **Recommendation**: Monitor for unusual bruising or bleeding; no routine INR monitoring required
- Separate EPA fish oil dose from statin by 30+ minutes (theoretical competition for lipase absorption)
- **Evidence**: PMID 23393290

**No major interactions** with vitamin D3, K2, magnesium, or 5-MTHF at recommended doses.

---

### Warfarin (Anticoagulant, Thromboembolism Prevention)

**Interaction with Vitamin K2**: INR antagonism
- K2 (particularly MK-7) activates vitamin K-dependent clotting factors
- Mechanism: competition with warfarin; reduces warfarin efficacy
- Risk: thromboembolism if INR drops below therapeutic range
- **Recommendation**: Do NOT supplement K2 if on warfarin; maintain consistent dietary K intake
- Alternative: if D3 supplementation essential, use D3 without K2; monitor INR monthly
- If K2 truly necessary (e.g., severe osteoporosis, other indications), consult anticoagulation specialist for warfarin dose adjustment
- **Evidence**: PMID 22114176

**Interaction with EPA Fish Oil**: Additive anticoagulant effect
- Omega-3 inhibits platelet aggregation; synergistic with warfarin anticoagulation
- Risk: increased bleeding (hematuria, GI bleed, intracranial bleed) at higher EPA doses
- **Recommendation**: EPA up to 1000 mg/day generally safe; dose >2000 mg/day requires INR monitoring every 2–4 weeks
- Monitor for bleeding signs (gum bleeding, prolonged bruising, blood in stool/urine)
- **Evidence**: PMID 23393290

**Interaction with Magnesium**: Minimal
- Magnesium may slightly enhance anticoagulation; clinical significance unclear
- **Recommendation**: Monitor INR if starting high-dose magnesium (>400 mg/day); routine monitoring not required at 200 mg/day

---

### Metformin (Antidiabetic, PCOS, Prediabetes)

**Interaction with Vitamin B12**: Depletion mechanism
- Metformin inhibits vitamin B12 absorption in the terminal ileum
- Mechanism: unclear; possibly affects calcium-dependent B12 transport
- Prevalence: B12 deficiency in 10–30% of chronic metformin users; risk increases after 2–3 years
- Consequence: macrocytic anemia, peripheral neuropathy, cognitive impairment if uncorrected
- **Recommendation**: All metformin users should supplement methylcobalamin 1000 μg/day or 2000 μg weekly IM
- Check serum B12 and methylmalonic acid (MMA) annually; treat if B12 <400 pg/mL or MMA elevated
- **Evidence**: PMID 23591439

**Interaction with Magnesium**: Beneficial or neutral
- Metformin may slightly reduce serum magnesium; supplementation mitigates this and enhances insulin sensitivity
- **Recommendation**: Combine magnesium 200–400 mg/day with metformin for synergistic glucose control
- **Evidence**: PMID 27400784

**Interaction with Folate (5-MTHF)**: Synergistic
- MTHFR variants + metformin use increases homocysteine risk (due to B12 depletion + folate metabolism)
- **Recommendation**: 5-MTHF 400–800 μg/day + methylcobalamin 1000 μg/day in all MTHFR variant carriers on metformin
- **Evidence**: PMID 23591439

---

### General Principles for Drug-Supplement Timing

**Separation Rule**:
- Separate water-soluble supplements (B vitamins, vitamin C, magnesium) from medications by at least 30 minutes
- Rationale: minimize GI pH and enzyme-mediated absorption competition

**Fat-Soluble Supplements with Medications**:
- Vitamin D3, K2, CoQ10 should be taken WITH a fat-containing meal
- If on lipid-lowering or gastrointestinal medications affecting fat absorption, separate from supplement by 2 hours
- Statin + CoQ10: separate by 30 minutes (theoretical enzyme competition)

**Medication Timing**:
- Morning medications (statins, metformin): take supplemental CoQ10 and EPA with lunch or dinner
- Evening medications: take magnesium 30+ minutes after final dose

## Section 4: Administration Schedule

Optimal supplement absorption and efficacy depend on timing, food state, and separation from medications. Below is a recommended daily schedule; adjust based on individual tolerance and medication regimen.

### Morning (With Breakfast)

**Timing**: With or immediately after breakfast (fat-containing meal)

**Supplements**:
- Vitamin D3 2000 IU
- Vitamin K2 (MK-7) 100–200 μg
- CoQ10 (ubiquinol) 100–200 mg
- 5-MTHF 400–800 μg
- B-complex (containing methylcobalamin 1000 μg)

**Rationale**:
- Fat-soluble vitamins (D3, K2, CoQ10) require dietary fat for optimal absorption
- Water-soluble B vitamins absorbed in small intestine; morning timing allows peak levels during daytime cognitive demand
- Combined intake reduces pill burden and improves compliance

**Special Note**: If on morning statin dose, separate statin from supplements by 30 minutes if possible (e.g., statin at 6 AM, breakfast + supplements at 6:30 AM)

---

### Midday Lunch (With Food)

**Timing**: With or immediately after lunch (any meal with protein/fat)

**Supplements**:
- EPA fish oil 500–1000 mg (half of daily dose if splitting)
- Vitamin C 250 mg (half of 500 mg daily dose)

**Rationale**:
- EPA fish oil with food improves absorption and reduces GI upset (nausea, fishy aftertaste)
- Splitting vitamin C dose optimizes renal excretion and uric acid clearance (peak levels aid urate secretion)
- Lunch timing distributes supplement levels evenly across waking hours

---

### Evening/Dinner (With Meal)

**Timing**: With dinner (any meal with protein/fat)

**Supplements**:
- EPA fish oil 500–1000 mg (second half of daily dose, if splitting)
- Vitamin C 250 mg (second half of daily dose)
- Chelated magnesium glycinate 200–400 mg (exception: can be taken alone; GI absorption adequate without food)

**Rationale**:
- Second EPA dose ensures steady-state omega-3 levels
- Second vitamin C dose maintains 24-hour renal clearance support
- Evening magnesium timing capitalizes on relaxant properties, improving sleep quality

---

### Bedtime (Optional)

**Timing**: 30–60 minutes before sleep (alone or with small snack if GI sensitive)

**Supplements** (if not taken at dinner):
- Chelated magnesium glycinate 200–400 mg

**Rationale**:
- Magnesium acts as natural NMDA antagonist, promoting sleep latency and quality
- Evening dose eliminates daytime somnolence risk
- Separate from morning statin by ≥12 hours

---

### 30-Minute Separation Rule for Medications

If individual takes multiple medications (e.g., statin + metformin + antihypertensive):

**Example Timeline** (individual on morning statin, evening metformin):
- 6:00 AM: Morning statin dose (alone)
- 6:30 AM: Breakfast + morning supplements (D3, K2, CoQ10, B-complex, folate)
- 12:30 PM: Lunch + midday EPA/Vitamin C
- 6:30 PM: Dinner + evening supplements (EPA, Vitamin C, magnesium)
- 7:00 PM: Metformin dose (alone, if evening dose)
- 8:00 PM or later: Bedtime magnesium (if not taken at dinner)

**Rationale**: 30+ minute separation minimizes GI pH, gastric motility, and enzyme-mediated absorption competition.

---

### Adjustment for Individual Tolerance

**If GI upset occurs**:
- Take all supplements with meals (even water-soluble vitamins acceptable with food)
- Reduce EPA dose temporarily and increase gradually; consider enteric-coated form
- Magnesium may cause diarrhea in sensitive individuals; reduce to 100–150 mg or switch to threonate form

**If somnolence occurs**:
- Move magnesium to bedtime only
- Take morning B-complex earlier (not at breakfast; separate 30 minutes before)

**If expense is limiting**:
- Prioritize Core supplements first (Section 2); add Recommended tier once Core established
- Generic CoQ10 (ubiquinone) acceptable if ubiquinol cost-prohibitive
- Fish oil: bulk purchasing reduces per-gram cost

## Section 5: Age-Specific Considerations

Genetic expression, pharmacokinetics, and health priorities shift across the lifespan. Supplement recommendations should be adjusted by age band and health status.

### Under 30 Years

**Health Focus**: Establish baseline metabolic health, prevent chronic disease foundations, optimize physical performance

**Supplement Priority Tier**:
- Focus on **Core** supplements for individuals with strong family history or positive genomic findings
- **Recommended** tier: generally appropriate even for asymptomatic individuals with genetic risk variants

**Specific Adjustments**:
- **Vitamin D3**: Standard 2000 IU unless VDR variant + chronically low sun exposure; no increased requirement by age alone
- **5-MTHFR/B12**: Begin if MTHFR variant identified; homocysteine usually normal at this age, but early supplementation prevents accumulation
- **EPA fish oil**: Consider if FADS1 TT + family dyslipidemia history, even if current lipids normal (primary prevention)
- **CoQ10**: Optional tier unless strong CAD family history (first-degree relative <50 years with MI/stroke); no statin use typically at this age
- **Magnesium**: Optional tier unless TCF7L2 variant + prediabetes; preventive supplementation borderline benefit without phenotype

**Monitoring Frequency**:
- Baseline genomic screen at age 20–25 (if not done earlier)
- Lipid panel: every 2–3 years (if family history) or every 5 years (if no risk factors)
- 25(OH)D: baseline; repeat at 2–3 year intervals
- Fasting glucose: every 5 years or every 2 years if TCF7L2 risk variant
- Homocysteine: baseline only if MTHFR variant; repeat if symptoms or family history

---

### 30–35 Years

**Health Focus**: First cardiovascular risk stratification, establish metabolic baseline for 40–50s comparison, optimize hormone/metabolic status

**Major Milestone**: First cardiovascular screening if family history present

**Supplement Priority Tier**:
- **Core** supplements for all identified genetic variants now with quantified biomarker evidence (lipids, glucose, D3, homocysteine)
- Shift from prevention-focused to active management-focused supplementation

**Specific Adjustments**:
- **CoQ10**: Begin 100 mg/day if CAD family history AND lipid panel shows LDL >130 mg/dL or low HDL; increase to 200 mg if on statin
- **Vitamin D3**: May increase to 3000–4000 IU/day if 25(OH)D consistently <40 ng/mL
- **Magnesium**: Begin 200–300 mg/day if metabolic syndrome emerges (abdominal obesity, BP >130/85, fasting glucose >100, triglycerides >150)
- **EPA fish oil**: Increase to 1500–2000 mg/day if triglycerides >150 or LDL particle number elevated
- **Vitamin C**: Begin if uric acid transporters + family gout history; can wait until first symptomatic flare if asymptomatic

**Statin Initiation**:
- If statin started at this age (CAD prevention), immediately increase CoQ10 to 200 mg/day and add methylcobalamin 1000 μg/day (if MTHFR variant)

**Monitoring Frequency**:
- Lipid panel: every 2–3 months if dyslipidemia or on statin; every 6 months if normal baseline
- 25(OH)D: annually if on D3 supplementation; check after dose adjustment
- Homocysteine: repeat if MTHFR variant and 25(OH)D improving (cofactor effect)
- Fasting glucose + HbA1c: annually if TCF7L2 variant or metabolic syndrome features present
- Blood pressure: every 6 months if family CAD history

---

### 35–40 Years

**Health Focus**: Intensify cardiovascular and metabolic monitoring, address emerging dyslipidemia/hypertension, optimize hormone status (perimenopause risk for women)

**Supplement Priority Tier**:
- **Core** supplements now essential if any CAD, diabetes, or gout phenotype emerged
- **Recommended** tier for all identified genetic variants (preventive benefit justified by age and increased risk)

**Specific Adjustments**:
- **CoQ10**: 200 mg/day ubiquinol standard if lipids abnormal or statin use; consider 300 mg/day if persistent symptoms despite statin
- **Magnesium**: 300–400 mg/day standard if metabolic syndrome present; add chromium 200 μg/day for enhanced glucose control
- **EPA fish oil**: 1500–2000 mg/day if triglycerides >120 or CAD risk SNPs present; may require dose >2000 mg if dyslipidemia refractory
- **Vitamin D3**: 3000–4000 IU/day standard; check 25(OH)D annually; may increase further if low baseline + CAD risk (vitamin D has cardioprotective effect)
- **5-MTHFR**: Continue 400–800 μg/day; may increase to 1000 μg if homocysteine >10 μmol/L

**Perimenopause Consideration** (Women 35–50):
- Vitamin D3 and K2 gain additional importance for bone preservation during hormonal transition
- Magnesium increases to 400 mg/day for migraine prevention + mood stabilization (common perimenopause symptoms)

**Monitoring Frequency**:
- Lipid panel: every 3 months if dyslipidemia; every 6 months if on statin with suboptimal control; annually if stable and normal
- 25(OH)D: annually; adjust D3 dose to achieve 45–55 ng/mL
- Homocysteine: every 6–12 months if MTHFR variant (aim <8 μmol/L for CAD risk reduction)
- HbA1c + fasting glucose: every 6 months if prediabetic or TCF7L2 variant; annually if normal
- Liver/kidney function (ALT, creatinine): every 6 months if on statin; every 12 months if normal baseline
- Blood pressure: every 3 months if hypertension emerging; every 6 months if normal

---

### 40+ Years

**Health Focus**: Secondary cardiovascular prevention, intensive metabolic screening, address accumulating comorbidities, optimize bone health

**Supplement Priority Tier**:
- **Core** supplements for all identified genetic variants; non-negotiable if CAD, diabetes, or gout history
- **Recommended** tier standard for primary prevention; reassess every 3–5 years

**Specific Adjustments**:
- **CoQ10**: 200–300 mg/day ubiquinol standard; increase to 300–400 mg if CAD event history or refractory dyslipidemia
- **Magnesium**: 300–400 mg/day standard; increase to 400–500 mg if hypertension or migraine burden
- **Vitamin D3**: 3000–4000 IU/day standard; check 25(OH)D annually and increase dose if <45 ng/mL (cancer + CAD risk increase if deficient)
- **EPA fish oil**: 1500–2000 mg/day if CAD event, lipid abnormality, or risk SNPs; can reduce to 1000 mg/day if lipids normalize on statin
- **Calcium supplementation**: Consider adding 500–600 mg chelated calcium citrate with breakfast + dinner if osteoporosis risk (especially women 3–5 years post-menopause); synergizes with D3 + K2

**Comprehensive Reassessment**:
- Every 5 years, reassess genotype-to-phenotype alignment (e.g., MTHFR TT with normal homocysteine on current regimen may not need escalation)
- Review medication list for new drug-supplement interactions

**Monitoring Frequency**:
- Lipid panel: every 3 months if dyslipidemia or post-CAD event; every 6 months if on statin with good control; annually if normal
- 25(OH)D: annually; adjust dose to maintain 45–55 ng/mL
- Homocysteine: every 6–12 months if MTHFR variant or on metformin; every 12 months if normal baseline
- HbA1c + fasting glucose: every 6 months if diabetes or prediabetes; every 12 months if normal
- Liver/kidney function (ALT, AST, creatinine): every 6 months if on statin or CoQ10 >200 mg/day; every 12 months if normal
- Blood pressure: every 3 months if hypertension; every 6 months if controlled
- Uric acid: every 6 months if gout history or urate transporter variant; every 12 months if asymptomatic
- Bone density (DXA scan): baseline at age 40 (women); repeat every 2–5 years if female or CAD/osteoporosis risk

## Section 6: Monitoring Schedule

Systematic monitoring ensures supplement efficacy, detects adverse effects, and guides dose adjustments. Below is a reference table for test frequency, labs to order, and target values based on genetic findings and supplement regimen.

### Lipid Panel

**When to Order**:
- **Baseline**: Before initiating supplement regimen (establish pre-supplement values)
- **Ongoing (dyslipidemia or on statin)**: Every 3 months × 3, then every 6 months if stable
- **Ongoing (normal baseline)**: Every 12 months

**Labs to Order**:
- Total cholesterol, LDL-C, HDL-C, triglycerides (standard panel)
- **Advanced**: LDL particle number (NMR or ion mobility) if triglycerides >150 or HDL <40 (assess FADS1-associated particle burden)
- Lipoprotein(a): once, baseline if CAD family history (genetically determined; supplements minimally effective but important to know)

**Target Values**:
- LDL-C: <100 mg/dL (optimal); <70 mg/dL if CAD history
- HDL-C: >50 mg/dL (women); >40 mg/dL (men)
- Triglycerides: <150 mg/dL; <100 mg/dL if CAD risk
- LDL particle number: <1000 nmol/L (optimal)
- Total cholesterol: <200 mg/dL

**Supplements Affecting**: EPA fish oil (↓ triglycerides, slight ↑ HDL); CoQ10 (modest LDL improvement); niacin optional but not included in core guide

---

### Uric Acid

**When to Order**:
- **Baseline**: Before initiating vitamin C for uric acid transporters
- **Ongoing (gout history)**: Every 3 months until stable; every 6 months if asymptomatic
- **Ongoing (asymptomatic variant carrier)**: Every 12 months or if acute flare occurs

**Labs to Order**:
- Serum uric acid (fasting preferred)
- 24-hour urine uric acid (optional, if recurrent gout despite supplementation; assesses undersecretion vs. overproduction)

**Target Values**:
- Serum uric acid: <6.0 mg/dL (target for gout prevention); <5.5 mg/dL if recurrent flares
- 24-hour urinary uric acid: 400–600 mg/day (normal)

**Supplements Affecting**: Vitamin C (↓ uric acid via increased renal excretion); purine-avoidance diet essential (supplements alone insufficient if high purine diet)

---

### Homocysteine

**When to Order**:
- **Baseline**: If MTHFR variant detected or family CAD/stroke history
- **Ongoing**: Every 6 months × 1 year after initiating 5-MTHF/B12; then annually if normal
- **Trending**: If baseline elevated (>8 μmol/L), check 3 months after dose adjustment

**Labs to Order**:
- Plasma total homocysteine (fasting preferred; morning sample most accurate)
- Methylmalonic acid (MMA): optional, if B12 supplementation unclear benefit (elevated MMA indicates functional B12 deficiency despite normal B12 serum level)

**Target Values**:
- Total homocysteine: <8 μmol/L (optimal); <10 μmol/L (acceptable)
- MMA: <0.4 μmol/L (normal)

**Supplements Affecting**: 5-MTHF (↓ homocysteine); methylcobalamin/B12 (↓ homocysteine); folate status cofactor (ensure adequate 5-MTHF dosing)

---

### 25-Hydroxyvitamin D [25(OH)D]

**When to Order**:
- **Baseline**: Before initiating vitamin D3 supplementation; repeat 8–12 weeks after starting
- **Ongoing**: Every 12 months to assess seasonal variation and supplementation adequacy
- **Trending**: If dose adjusted (e.g., increased from 2000 to 4000 IU), recheck 8–12 weeks post-adjustment

**Labs to Order**:
- 25(OH)D, 25-hydroxyvitamin D [liquid chromatography-mass spectrometry (LC-MS) preferred for accuracy; immunoassay acceptable if standardized]
- Optional: 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D (calcitriol) only if severe renal disease or hyperparathyroidism suspected

**Target Values**:
- 40–60 ng/mL (100–150 nmol/L): optimal for immune function, bone health, CAD prevention
- 30–39 ng/mL: sufficient (acceptable but suboptimal)
- <30 ng/mL: insufficient (inadequate supplementation or malabsorption; increase D3 dose or investigate GI issues)

**Supplements Affecting**: Vitamin D3 (↑ 25(OH)D); K2 cofactor (aids calcium deposition, not reflected in D3 levels)

---

### Fasting Glucose + Hemoglobin A1c (HbA1c)

**When to Order**:
- **Baseline**: If TCF7L2 diabetes risk variant present or metabolic syndrome features identified (abdominal obesity, HTN, TG >150)
- **Ongoing (prediabetic: fasting glucose 100–125 or HbA1c 5.7–6.4%)**: Every 6 months
- **Ongoing (normal baseline)**: Every 12 months if TCF7L2 variant; every 24 months if normal baseline and no family diabetes history

**Labs to Order**:
- Fasting glucose (plasma, after 8-hour overnight fast)
- HbA1c (glycated hemoglobin; 2–3 month glucose average)
- Optional: Fasting insulin (assess insulin resistance severity; values >12 mIU/L indicate significant resistance)
- Optional: OGTT (oral glucose tolerance test) if fasting glucose borderline and individual symptomatic (identify early glucose dysregulation)

**Target Values**:
- Fasting glucose: <100 mg/dL (normal); 100–125 mg/dL (prediabetic); ≥126 mg/dL (diabetic)
- HbA1c: <5.7% (normal); 5.7–6.4% (prediabetic); ≥6.5% (diabetic)
- Fasting insulin: <12 mIU/L (normal); 12–20 mIU/L (moderate insulin resistance); >20 mIU/L (severe)

**Supplements Affecting**: Magnesium glycinate (↓ fasting glucose, ↓ HbA1c via GLUT4 activation); chromium (secondary effect, less robust); EPA fish oil (modest benefit for TG, less for glucose)

---

### Liver Function Tests (ALT, AST)

**When to Order**:
- **Baseline**: Before initiating any supplements if unknown baseline liver function
- **Ongoing (on statin)**: Every 6 months × 2, then annually if stable
- **Ongoing (high-dose CoQ10 >200 mg/day)**: Every 12 months (CoQ10 is fat-soluble and theoretically accumulates; no clinical hepatotoxicity reported at recommended doses)
- **Ongoing (no risk factors)**: Every 12 months if monitored regularly

**Labs to Order**:
- Alanine aminotransferase (ALT)
- Aspartate aminotransferase (AST)
- Alkaline phosphatase, total bilirubin (if ALT/AST elevated; assess hepatocellular vs. cholestatic etiology)

**Target Values**:
- ALT: <40 IU/L (normal; slightly higher normal acceptable if consistent with baseline)
- AST: <40 IU/L (normal; slightly higher normal acceptable if consistent with baseline)
- ALT/AST ratio: >1 suggests fatty liver disease (if both mildly elevated)

**Supplements Affecting**: Statins (monitor for statin-induced elevation; often asymptomatic); CoQ10 (no reported hepatotoxicity; monitoring for safety reassurance only)

---

### Renal Function (Creatinine, eGFR)

**When to Order**:
- **Baseline**: Before initiating any supplements if on medication or >50 years
- **Ongoing (on vitamin C supplementation)**: Every 6 months initially; annually if stable (vitamin C increases oxalate, risk for kidney stones if predisposed)
- **Ongoing (on high-dose magnesium)**: Every 12 months (magnesium renally excreted; risk for accumulation if eGFR <30)
- **Ongoing (other supplements)**: Every 12 months as part of routine health screening

**Labs to Order**:
- Serum creatinine (calculate eGFR using CKD-EPI equation or comparable)
- Blood urea nitrogen (BUN); optional unless elevated creatinine
- Optional: 24-hour urine creatinine (assess GFR if borderline or rising creatinine)
- Optional: Urine pH and oxalate (if recurrent kidney stones and on vitamin C; ensure urine pH >6.0 and oxalate <45 mg/day)

**Target Values**:
- Serum creatinine: 0.7–1.3 mg/dL (varies by age, sex, muscle mass)
- eGFR: >60 mL/min/1.73m² (normal); 30–59 mL/min/1.73m² (mild-moderate renal disease); <30 mL/min/1.73m² (severe renal disease; adjust supplement dosing)
- Urine pH: 6.0–7.0 (on vitamin C supplementation; <6.0 increases uric acid/urate crystal risk; >7.0 increases calcium phosphate risk)

**Supplements Affecting**:
- Vitamin C (↑ urinary oxalate; risk for kidney stones if predisposed, eGFR <60, or high urine pH)
- Magnesium (accumulates if eGFR <30; reduce dose or frequency)
- Vitamin D3 (increases serum calcium; assess with creatinine/eGFR; hypercalcemia risk if eGFR severely reduced)
