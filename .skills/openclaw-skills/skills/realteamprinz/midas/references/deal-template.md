# Deal Analysis Template

A structured framework for deep-diving on any specific transaction or business opportunity.

---

## When to Use This Template

Use this template when:
- You encounter a specific deal or transaction
- Someone proposes a business arrangement
- You're evaluating an investment opportunity
- You're structuring your own deal
- You want to understand "how money moves" in a specific context

---

## The 7-Part Deal Analysis

### Part 1: Parties Identification

**Who is involved in this deal?**

| Party | Role | What They Bring | What They Want |
|-------|------|----------------|----------------|
| Buyer | [Role] | [Capital/Resources/Access] | [Outcome] |
| Seller | [Role] | [Asset/Service/Expertise] | [Consideration] |
| Broker/Intermediary | [Role] | [Access/Relationships/Deal flow] | [Fee/Commission] |
| Investor | [Role] | [Capital] | [Return/Equity] |
| Other | [Role] | [Specific value-add] | [Specific return] |

**Key Questions:**
- Who holds the asymmetric information?
- Who has the most optionality?
- Who can walk away easiest?
- Who is most motivated to close?

---

### Part 2: Structure Analysis

**How is this deal structured?**

| Element | Details |
|---------|---------|
| **Transaction type** | Cash / Equity / Debt / Hybrid / Barter |
| **Payment timing** | Upfront / Milestones / Earnout / Retrospective |
| **Legal structure** | Asset purchase / Equity sale / Partnership / License |
| **Governing terms** | Exclusivity / Non-compete / Non-solicit / Leases assumed |

**Structure Red Flags:**
- Hidden liabilities being assumed
- Earnout metrics that are impossible to achieve
- Equity with no real control provisions
- Debt that doesn't appear on the balance sheet

## Deal Analysis Examples

### Example 1: SaaS Acquisition

**Scenario:** You're considering acquiring a small SaaS company for $2M.

```
Parties:
- Buyer (you): Capital + operational expertise
- Seller: Founder exiting after 5 years
- Broker: M&A advisor taking 5% fee

Structure:
- 70% cash at close
- 30% earnout over 2 years tied to MRR growth
- Seller has 1-year non-compete

Source of Funds:
- $1.2M from savings (opportunity cost: 10% annual)
- $500K from bank loan at 7%
- $300K earnout (no cost unless achieved)

Risk Allocation:
- Market risk: Shared (earnout alignment)
- Execution risk: Mostly buyer (buyer controls operations)
- Key person risk: High (seller is the product)

Exit Path:
- Resale in 3-5 years at 4-6x revenue
- Potential strategic buyer premium
- Could also run as cash-flow business indefinitely

Hidden Leverage:
- Existing customer relationships (need to validate)
- Proprietary tech (need IP audit)
- Founder knowledge (non-compete limits competition)

VERDICT:
This is a reasonable deal IF:
1. Customer churn is < 10% annually
2. Seller's non-compete covers your target market
3. You can grow MRR 20%+ annually
4. You have operational expertise to improve margins

Red flags:
- Seller wants liquid at close but earnout is not guaranteed
- 30% of price in earnout is significant exposure
- 5-year-old SaaS without significant growth = warning sign

IMMEDIATE NEXT ACTION: Request 3-year monthly cohort retention data before proceeding
```

### Example 2: Real Estate Deal

**Scenario:** Friend offers you 20% equity in their restaurant for $50K.

```
Parties:
- Buyer (you): $50K capital
- Operator (friend): Operational expertise + $150K personal capital
- Landlord: Commercial lease holder
- Chef: Key employee with leverage

Structure:
- LLC membership with 20% economic interest
- No management role specified
- distributions quarterly if profitable

Source of Funds:
- Your $50K = 25% of total capitalization
- Friend's $150K = 75% of capitalization
- You're getting 20% for 25% of capital = overvalued

Risk Allocation:
- Market risk: Shared equally
- Execution risk: Friend bears (they run it)
- Liquidity risk: High (no exit mechanism)
- Key person risk: Very High (chef could leave)

Exit Path:
- No clear exit mechanism defined
- Could sell membership interest but finding buyer is hard
- Business sale would require majority consent
- Could run for years with no liquidity event

Hidden Leverage:
- Friend's location knowledge (but that's their advantage)
- Existing customer base (need to validate)
- Lease terms (need to review)

VERDICT:
This is NOT a good deal for you because:
1. You're paying premium for minority position
2. No management rights = no control
3. No clear exit = trapped capital
4. Restaurant industry = high failure rate
5. Friend relationship creates awkwardness if things go wrong

Alternative structure to propose:
- Convert to convertible note: $50K loan at 8% interest
- Converts to 20% equity only if certain revenue thresholds met
- Gives you downside protection + upside potential

IMMEDIATE NEXT ACTION: Decline the current structure, propose convertible note terms
```

## Quick Deal Scoring

When you need a fast assessment:

| Factor | Good (+2) | Neutral (0) | Bad (-2) |
|--------|-----------|-------------|----------|
| Risk allocation | Risk to party who can bear it | Shared equally | Risk on party least able to bear |
| Exit path | Clear, realistic, timely | Vague or long | None or impossible |
| Information | You have more info | Balanced | Other side has more info |
| Leverage | You have leverage | Neutral | Other side has leverage |
| Motivation | Other side is motivated | Neutral | You are more motivated |
| Structure | Clean, simple, favorable | Standard terms | Complex, unfavorable |
| Relationship | Deal creates new relationship | No change | Deal strains existing relationship |

**Score Range:**
- +10 to +14: Very good deal — act quickly
- +5 to +9: Good deal — negotiate improvements
- -4 to +4: Neutral — pass or restructure
- -5 to -9: Bad deal — walk away
- -10 to -14: Very bad deal — run away

## Honest Boundaries for Deal Analysis

**Midas CAN:**
- Provide structured analysis framework
- Identify common deal pitfalls
- Surface hidden leverage and risk allocation
- Compare deals against common structures

**Midas CANNOT:**
- Predict if a specific deal will succeed
- Replace legal, financial, or tax advice
- Guarantee any investment returns
- Access private company financials without you providing them

**Your responsibility:**
- Verify all claims made by counterparties
- Consult qualified professionals for legal/tax/financial matters
- Make your own judgment on risk tolerance
- Execute deals at your own risk
