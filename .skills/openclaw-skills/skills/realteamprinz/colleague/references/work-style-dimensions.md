# Work Style Dimensions Reference Guide

This reference explains each dimension in the colleague profile — what it means, why it matters, and examples of different types.

---

## 1. Work Identity

### Role & Team
**What it means**: The colleague's formal position and organizational placement.

**Why it matters**: Context shapes expectations and influence. A senior IC has different dynamics than a manager; someone in sales interacts differently than engineering.

**Examples**:
- Senior Software Engineer, Platform Team
- Product Manager, Growth
- Engineering Manager, Infrastructure

### Tenure
**What it means**: How long they've been at the organization and how long you've worked together.

**Why it matters**: Longer tenure means more institutional knowledge, relationships, and political capital. Also indicates stability and organizational fit.

**Examples**:
- 3 months (new)
- 2 years (established)
- 7 years (veteran)

### Expertise Areas
**What it means**: Specific domains where they have deep knowledge or skills.

**Why it matters**: Helps route problems to the right person and understand their perspective on issues within their domain.

**Examples**:
- Distributed systems architecture
- Client relationship management
- Regulatory compliance
- Frontend performance optimization

## 2. Communication Style

### Preferred Channel
**What it means**: The communication medium they naturally gravitate toward.

**Why it matters**: People have preferences. Respecting them improves response time and engagement quality.

| Channel | Characteristics |
|---------|----------------|
| Email | Formal, asynchronous, good for documentation |
| Slack/Teams | Real-time, casual, fast iterations |
| Face-to-face | Richest context, builds relationships |
| Voice notes | Expressive, efficient for complex thoughts |
| Video calls | Balance of synchronous and visual |

### Response Pattern
**What it means**: How and when they respond to communications.

**Why it matters**: Sets expectations and helps you know when to follow up or escalate.

**Examples**:
- **Instant replier**: Responds within minutes during work hours
- **Batch processor**: Checks and responds at set times (end of day, morning)
- **Slow but thorough**: Takes time but gives comprehensive responses
- **Selective responder**: Only responds to high-priority items

### Writing Style
**What it means**: How they express themselves in written communication.

**Why it matters**: Matching style improves comprehension and rapport.

**Examples**:
- **Bullet points**: Structured, scannable, efficient
- **Long paragraphs**: Narrative, context-rich, thorough
- **Voice messages**: Expressive, conversational, detailed
- **Diagrams**: Visual, technical, shows thinking

### Meeting Behavior
**What it means**: How they act in group discussions.

**Why it matters**: Affects meeting dynamics and outcomes.

**Types**:
- **Dominates**: Talks a lot, leads discussion
- **Listens**: Observes, speaks when has input
- **Deep-diver**: Goes deep on technical details
- **On-track**: Keeps conversations productive
- **Derails**: Goes off on tangents
- **Silent**: Participates minimally

## 3. Decision-Making Pattern

### Approach
**What it means**: The primary lens through which they evaluate decisions.

**Why it matters**: Understanding this helps you present information in a persuasive way.

| Approach | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| Data-driven | Needs metrics, statistics, evidence |
| Gut feeling | Trusts intuition and experience |
| Consensus builder | Needs team agreement before proceeding |
| Authority-based | Respects hierarchy and expertise |
| Precedent-based | Looks for similar past situations |

### What Convinces Them
**What it means**: The type of evidence or argument that tips the scales.

**Why it matters**: Tailoring your pitch to their convincing factor is more persuasive.

**Examples**:
- Hard numbers and statistics
- Stories and case studies
- Authority figures' opinions
- Successful precedents
- Team consensus
- Personal relationship/trust

### Decision Speed
**What it means**: How quickly they reach conclusions.

**Why it matters**: Impacts project timelines and meeting pacing.

**Types**:
- **Fast with adjustments**: Decides quickly, course-corrects as needed
- **Slow and deliberate**: Takes time to evaluate all angles
- **Avoids decisions**: Delays or deflects commitment
- **Context-dependent**: Varies based on stakes

### Risk Tolerance
**What it means**: How much uncertainty they're comfortable with.

**Why it matters**: Affects project planning and strategic direction.

**Spectrum**:
- **Cautious**: Minimizes risk, thorough vetting required
- **Calculated**: Accepts risk with analysis and mitigation
- **Bold**: Comfortable with significant uncertainty
- **Risk-averse**: Will not proceed without guarantees

## 4. Problem-Solving Style

### Thinking Approach
**What it means**: Whether they start with the answer or the data.

**Why it matters**: Affects how they process information and solve problems.

| Approach | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| Top-down | Starts with hypothesis/answer, finds supporting evidence |
| Bottom-up | Gathers data first, builds conclusion from evidence |

### Processing Style
**What it means**: How they mentally work through problems.

**Why it matters**: Helps you support them effectively and know when to give space.

**Types**:
- **Visual**: Thinks in images, diagrams, spatial relationships
- **Verbal**: Processes through reading and writing
- **Kinesthetic**: Needs to work through physically
- **Auditory**: Processes through discussion/explanation

### Crisis Response
**What it means**: Their first reaction when things go wrong.

**Why it matters**: Predicts behavior during high-pressure situations.

**Types**:
- **Stays calm**: Maintains composure, thinks clearly
- **Action mode**: Immediately starts doing something
- **Processes**: Needs to understand before acting
- **Delegates**: Assigns tasks to others
- **Takes control**: Asserts authority over situation

## 5. Collaboration Manual

### Best Way to Pitch Ideas
**What it means**: How to most effectively present new concepts.

**Why it matters**: The same idea pitched wrong can be rejected; pitched right, embraced.

**Approaches**:
- **Data first**: Show metrics and evidence before the ask
- **Story first**: Build narrative and context, then reveal conclusion
- **Direct ask**: Get to the point immediately
- **Problem-solution**: Present the problem, then your solution
- **Vision first**: Paint the picture of success, then show path

### Meeting Triggers
**What it means**: What frustrates them in meetings.

**Why it matters**: Avoiding triggers improves meeting effectiveness.

**Common Triggers**:
- Being interrupted mid-thought
- Meetings without clear agenda
- Lack of preparation by attendees
- Wasted time on topics that could be async
- Unclear action items
- People who haven't done homework

### Disagreement Style
**What it means**: How they handle conflicts or differences of opinion.

**Why it matters**: Predicts how conflicts will play out.

| Style | Behavior |
|-------|----------|
| Avoids | Sidesteps conflict, changes subject |
| Confronts | Addresses directly and openly |
| Passive-aggressive | Indirect resistance, backhanded comments |
| Finds compromise | Works toward middle ground |
| Escalates | Brings in others to resolve |

### Feedback Style
**What it means**: How they give and receive feedback.

**Why it matters**: Feedback is crucial for growth; understanding style helps both give and receive better.

| Style | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Direct and blunt | Says exactly what they think, no sugarcoating |
| Sandwich method | Positive-negative-positive structure |
| Hints and hopes | Subtly implies, expects you to infer |
| Written only | Prefers written feedback over verbal |
| Private only | Gives feedback one-on-one, never public |

### Trust Building
**What it means**: What earns their trust and respect.

**Why it matters**: Knowing this helps build stronger relationships faster.

**Factors**:
- Delivering results consistently
- Being transparent about problems
- Showing loyalty to team and company
- Demonstrating expertise
- Being consistent in words and actions
- Admitting mistakes openly

## 6. Recurring Patterns

### Catchphrases
**What it means**: Repeated phrases they use.

**Why it matters**: Can be useful, harmless, or signal something. Understanding usage context matters.

**Examples**:
- "Let's circle back on that"
- "What does the data say?"
- "At the end of the day..."
- "Ship it and iterate"
- "Can we table this?"

### Approval/Disapproval Signals
**What it means**: How they show agreement or disagreement.

**Why it matters**: Reading these signals helps gauge reception in real-time.

**Approval Signals**:
- Enthusiastic agreement
- "Let's do it"
- Immediately positive body language
- Building on the idea

**Disapproval Signals**:
- "Let's discuss more"
- Raising concerns
- Going quiet
- Deflecting to other topics
- Qualified responses

### Stress Signals
**What it means**: How they behave differently under stress.

**Why it matters**: Recognizing stress helps support colleagues and adjust interactions.

**Common Signals**:
- Going quiet
- Snappy or short responses
- Working late/overtime
- Canceling meetings
- Avoiding one-on-ones
- Increased formality
- More email than usual

## 7. Institutional Knowledge

### What Only They Know
**What it means**: Unique knowledge or context they hold.

**Why it matters**: Critical for succession planning and continuity.

**Examples**:
- Specific client relationship history
- Legacy system details
- Past decision rationale
- Vendor relationships

### Processes They Own
**What it means**: Workflows they've built or maintain.

**Why it matters**: If they leave, someone needs to take over.

**Examples**:
- Deployment process
- Onboarding workflow
- Incident response
- Code review practices

### Historical Context
**What it means**: Background knowledge about past events.

**Why it matters**: Provides crucial context for decisions and understanding current state.

**Examples**:
- "We chose X because Y failed in 2019"
- "The reason for this structure is..."
- "Nobody remembers but we tried Z before"

## Capturing Confidence

For each dimension, note confidence level based on:

| Level | Basis |
|-------|-------|
| **High** | Directly observed multiple times across contexts |
| **Medium** | Observed once or twice, or second-hand information |
| **Low** | Single mention, speculation, or self-reported only |

**Remember**: Profiles are living documents. Update confidence as you observe more.
