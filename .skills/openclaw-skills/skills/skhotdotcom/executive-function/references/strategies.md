# Strategy Reference

A library of specific techniques organized by the challenge they address. These aren't
prescriptions — they're options. Not every strategy works for every person or every day.
Offer them as suggestions, note what seems to work, and adapt.

---

## Initiation Resistance

When the person can see what needs doing but can't make themselves start.

**The 5-minute contract:** "Just do it for 5 minutes. Set a timer. When it goes off, you
have full permission to stop." The trick is that starting is the bottleneck — once
someone is 5 minutes in, they often continue. And if they don't, they still did 5
minutes. Either outcome is fine.

**Lowest-possible-bar first step:** Find the step that requires zero thought or
creativity. Open the file. Write your name at the top. Copy the template. Create the
folder. The action should be so trivially easy that resistance feels silly. This is not
condescending — it's exploiting the fact that initiation difficulty is not proportional
to task difficulty.

**Temptation bundling:** Pair the hard task with something enjoyable. "Work on the
report while listening to that podcast you like." "Do the expense reports with fancy
coffee." This adds a small dopamine source to an otherwise unrewarding task.

**Artificial urgency:** "Can you get the first three bullet points done in the next 10
minutes?" A mild time constraint can create just enough urgency to activate an
ADHD brain that doesn't respond to "this is important but not due for two weeks."

**Change the environment:** Sometimes a different room, a different device, or a
different app is enough novelty to break the initiation barrier. "What if you drafted it
on your phone instead of the laptop?" Weird, but sometimes effective.

**Implementation intentions:** Instead of "I'll work on the report," use "When I sit
down after lunch, I will open report.docx and write the introduction." The when-then
format binds the intention to a specific trigger, which dramatically increases follow-
through. Help the person formulate these.

## Overwhelm Recovery

When everything feels like too much and the person has shut down or is close to it.

**Permission to stop.** Sometimes the most helpful thing you can say is "you don't have
to do anything right now." Executive function collapse often comes with guilt about the
collapse itself, which makes it worse. Breaking the guilt cycle is the first step.

**One-item focus.** Everything is off the table except one thing. Not "the most
important thing" — just one thing. Any thing. Completing even one small task during a
shutdown state builds a tiny foothold of agency.

**Brain dump (see modes.md).** Get everything out of the head and onto a list. The
relief of seeing that it's a finite, countable number of items — not the infinite fog
it felt like — is often enough to start recovering.

**Worst-first triage.** Sometimes what's driving the overwhelm isn't volume but one
specific dreaded item that's contaminating everything. Ask: "Is there one thing on this
list that feels the worst? Like a weight on your chest?" If yes, consider addressing
that one directly — the relief can be disproportionate to the effort.

**Declare triage mode.** "Today is a triage day, not a productivity day. The goal is
to figure out what genuinely needs to happen this week and what can slide. Nothing else."
Reframing the entire day's purpose removes the pressure to be productive.

## Focus Maintenance

When the person can start but keeps drifting or getting pulled away.

**Work sprints.** The Pomodoro technique (25 minutes work / 5 minutes break) is the
classic, but the intervals should match the person. Some people do better with 15/5,
others with 45/10. Start with shorter intervals and extend if they're sustaining focus.

**Distraction parking lot.** Keep a running list of "things that popped into my head
while working." When they think "oh I should check if the package shipped," they write
it down instead of switching contexts. The list removes the anxiety that they'll forget
while letting them stay on task.

**Environmental design.** Close unnecessary tabs. Put the phone in another room (not
just face-down — actually out of reach). Use full-screen mode. These are not willpower
strategies; they're environment modifications that reduce the number of temptations the
person needs to resist.

**Narrate the work.** Some people maintain focus better when they describe what they're
doing as they do it: "OK, I'm writing the methodology section. I need to cover our
sample size and the analysis approach." This can feel strange but it keeps the executive
system engaged.

**Accountability anchors.** "I'm going to check in at 2:30. What do you think you'll
have done by then?" This is gentle accountability, not surveillance. The person sets
their own expectation; you just provide the timestamp.

## Transition Difficulty

When switching from one task or context to another is disproportionately hard.

**Save state explicitly.** Before switching, write down exactly where you are and what
the next step is. "I'm on slide 7, need to add the chart, the data is in the shared
drive." This makes coming back effortless because the reloading work is already done.

**Buffer rituals.** Build in a tiny ritual between tasks — make tea, walk to the
mailbox, do a 2-minute stretch. This creates a sensory boundary between contexts.
The ritual doesn't need to be long; it needs to be *different* from both the previous
and next activity.

**Warm-up steps.** When starting the new task after a transition, begin with the easiest
possible subtask — even if it's not logically first. The goal is re-engagement, not
optimal sequencing. Once you're back in the flow, you can hop to wherever you actually
need to be.

**Hyperfocus exit ramp.** If the person is hyperfocusing and needs to stop, don't demand
a hard cut. "Find a stopping point in the next 10 minutes" respects the momentum while
setting a boundary. If they still can't stop: "What would you need to feel OK about
pausing this?" Sometimes they just need to finish one more small piece.

## Time Awareness

For building a more accurate sense of how long things take.

**Predict-then-check.** Before starting a task, ask "how long do you think this will
take?" After, note the actual time. Don't frame this as a test — frame it as data
collection. "Not a quiz, just curious — I'm tracking these so we can calibrate over
time."

**Reference class forecasting.** When estimating, encourage the person to think about
the *last time* they did something similar rather than how long it *should* take. "How
long did the last expense report take you?" is more useful than "how long do expense
reports take?"

**Time anchoring.** Use specific clock times instead of durations. "It's 2:00 now. If
this takes 30 minutes, you'll be done at 2:30 — right before your meeting." This creates
an external frame the person can check against reality.

**Visible timers.** When tools allow it, suggest a visible countdown timer — not as
pressure, but as a way to make invisible time visible. Some people find this grounding;
others find it anxiety-inducing. Check which one they are.

## Working Memory Support

For holding information and context when the brain keeps dropping it.

**Externalize immediately.** If something is mentioned — a decision, a detail, a stray
thought — write it down. Don't trust "I'll remember that." The cost of writing it down
is tiny; the cost of losing it is not.

**Summarize before adding.** Before introducing new information, briefly summarize what's
already been established. "OK so we decided you're doing X first, then Y. Now, for Z..."
This re-anchors the person before adding load.

**One thread at a time.** Resist the temptation to bring up related topics while
discussing something. If a connection occurs to you, note it for later. Keep the current
conversation channel clean.

**Checklists for multi-step tasks.** If a task has more than 3 steps, write them out as
a checklist even if they seem obvious. Holding "gather documents, fill in form, scan,
email to HR" costs working memory even though each step is simple.

**End-of-session state save.** At the end of any work session, capture: what was done,
what's in progress, what the next step is, and any important context. This is the
handoff to future-them, who won't remember today's context as well as present-them
thinks they will.
