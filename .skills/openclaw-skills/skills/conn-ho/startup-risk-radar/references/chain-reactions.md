# Antipattern Chain Reactions

Antipatterns are not isolated — they form a system. One failure mode triggers another.

## The 3 Deadly Cascades

### 1. The Hallucination Spiral
🔮 Hallucination → 📉 Math Problem → 🔥 Bonfire

You built something nobody wants. To manufacture demand, you pour money into customer acquisition. CAC climbs while LTV stays flat. You're bleeding cash to acquire customers who don't really want what you're selling. The cascade ends when capital and narrative credibility are exhausted simultaneously.

**Example arc**: Build product → polite interest but no urgency → spend heavily on sales/marketing to create demand → unit economics worsen → burn through cash → die.

### 2. The Siege
🏴 Crushed → 📉 Math Problem → 🔥 Bonfire

You launched into a market that looked open but was a killing field. To survive competition, you drop prices, offer custom features, spend heavily on marketing. Competitors do the same — race to the bottom. Gross margins compress, sales cycles lengthen. You're losing the competitive battle while burning cash to stay in the fight.

**Example arc**: Enter market → underestimate competition → price war → unit economics destroyed → cash depleted → die.

### 3. The Slow Bleed
⚖️ Outlaw → 🔥 Bonfire → ⚔️ Civil War

You built in a regulated industry. The 7-year median lifespan means years of grinding regulatory war. Legal fees and compliance infrastructure consume cash instead of product and growth. Burn rate becomes unsustainable. In the final stage, founders fracture over whether to pivot, fight, or shut down.

**Example arc**: Regulatory ambiguity → grow → regulatory pressure → legal costs drain cash → co-founders disagree on response → team fractures → die.

## Connection Map (All Pairwise Relationships)

### 🔮 Hallucination triggers:
- → 🔥 Bonfire: No market need is a slow-burning fuse to cash depletion. Without genuine demand, you burn reserves manufacturing traction that never materializes organically.
- → 📉 Math Problem: When solving a problem people don't urgently need, CAC skyrockets while LTV craters. You end up paying to convince people they have a problem.
- → 🏴 Crushed: Building for a theoretical customer while competitors iterate with real users. By the time you recognize the real problem, they own the solution space.
- → 🍋 Lemon: Without real market feedback, product development becomes unmoored. Features built on assumptions rather than usage data.

### 🔥 Bonfire triggers:
- → 🔮 Hallucination: Aggressive spending masks lack of product-market fit. When spending millions on sales/marketing, you can't tell if customers buy because you solved a real problem or because you made it cheap enough to say yes.
- → 📉 Math Problem: You know unit economics are broken but convince yourself scale will fix it. Cash runs out before the math starts working.
- → 🏴 Crushed: You see competitors raising large rounds, so you match their burn rate. Mutual assured destruction where everyone spends unsustainably.
- → ⚔️ Civil War: When runway gets short, founders split between cutting burn vs. spending aggressively. Financial pressure reveals fundamental strategic disagreements.

### ⚔️ Civil War triggers:
- → 🔥 Bonfire: Duplicated efforts, contradictory initiatives, executive paralysis. Runway disappears while you fight internally.
- → 🔮 Hallucination: Internal warfare blinds you to market signals. Optimizing for winning internal arguments rather than serving customers.
- → 🏴 Crushed: While consumed by internal conflict, competitors execute with unified focus. Best talent leaves for sane work environments.
- → 🍋 Lemon: Competing technical visions lead to architectural inconsistency. Engineering receives contradictory priorities.

### 🏴 Crushed triggers:
- → 🔥 Bonfire: Competition forces escalating spending on customer acquisition, feature parity, and market defense.
- → 📉 Math Problem: Competitive pressure compresses prices, increases CAC, prevents operational leverage. Competition ensures you never reach profitable scale.
- → 🔮 Hallucination: What appears as competition failure often masks market need failure. When customers easily switch, the underlying issue may be insufficient urgency.
- → 🍋 Lemon: Must simultaneously match competitors, maintain quality, and innovate — with limited resources. Forces technical debt and quality compromises.

### 🍋 Lemon triggers:
- → 🔮 Hallucination: When tech can't deliver, you reframe as "customers don't understand the value." Blame market education rather than admitting technical impossibility.
- → 🔥 Bonfire: Each failed attempt to fix core product requires another engineering cycle, another rebuild, another 6 months of runway.
- → ⚔️ Civil War: Team splits between those who see technical reality and those who can't admit it. Engineers want to pivot; founders want one more attempt.
- → 📉 Math Problem: Workarounds to make broken product function (manual processes, custom solutions per customer) destroy cost structure.

### ⚖️ Outlaw triggers:
- → 🔥 Bonfire: Legal fees, compliance costs, and frozen operations drain capital. Impossible to raise new funding once investigations become public.
- → 📉 Math Problem: Unit economics that only work through regulatory arbitrage. Compliant version reveals the business was always The Math Problem in disguise.
- → 🏴 Crushed: Incumbents lobby regulators to shut down threatening startups. Compliance weaponized as competitive moat.
- → ⚔️ Civil War: Founders fracture over whether to pivot to compliance, fight regulators, or shut down. Existential stress of potential criminal charges.

### 📉 Math Problem triggers:
- → 🔥 Bonfire: Broken unit economics makes cash burn terminal. Without sound economics, you must raise perpetually until capital markets close.
- → 🔮 Hallucination: Negative unit economics often signals customers don't value your solution enough to pay what it costs to deliver.
- → 🏴 Crushed: When competitors also have broken economics, sector enters subsidy war. Company with most capital wins temporarily but nobody achieves sustainable profitability.
- → ⚔️ Civil War: As math problem becomes undeniable, founders fracture between those who want radical restructuring and those who believe one more quarter of optimization will work.
