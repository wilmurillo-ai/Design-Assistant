# Customer — Real Usage Examples

*Copy these prompts directly into your AI agent to get started.*

---

## Quick Start Prompts

### At-Risk Customer

"A customer who is up for renewal in 60 days has gone dark. Their usage dropped 40% last month and my last two emails went unanswered. What do I do?"

### Onboarding Plan

"I just closed a new enterprise customer. 200 seats, 3 departments, go-live in 6 weeks. Help me build an onboarding plan."

### Expansion Conversation

"A customer has been using our core product for 8 months with great results. I think they are ready for our advanced tier. How do I have that conversation?"

### Churn Analysis

"We lost 6 customers last quarter. Here is what I know about each one: [summaries]. Help me find the pattern."

## Conversation Examples

### Example 1: Saving an At-Risk Renewal

User: My biggest customer — $180k ARR — has a renewal in 90 days. Their champion just left the company. The new contact has not responded to my last three outreach attempts. I am nervous.

Agent: This is a genuine renewal risk and the right time to act is now, not at 30 days. Three things need to happen in parallel. First, go around the unresponsive contact — use LinkedIn or your internal network to identify who the new decision-maker for this type of tool is likely to be, and ask your executive sponsor to make a warm introduction at the executive level. An unresponsive middle manager is often a signal that they do not feel empowered to make the decision, not that the company has decided to leave. Second, prepare a business review document that quantifies the value delivered in the first contract period in terms the new stakeholder will care about — not features used but business outcomes achieved. Third, get your own executive involved. A CEO or VP to VP call signals that you value the relationship and creates a different conversation than a CSM to mid-level contact dynamic. What do you know about the new contact and who in their organization was involved in the original purchase?

### Example 2: Building an Onboarding Plan

User: We just closed a 150-seat deal with a logistics company. Three departments will use the product differently. Implementation is supposed to take 8 weeks. I have never done an onboarding this complex. Help me structure it.

Agent: For a 150-seat, multi-department implementation, the onboarding needs its own project structure. Here is how I would frame the 8 weeks. Weeks 1-2: Foundation. Single kickoff with all three department leads and your executive sponsor. Establish one success metric per department — specific, measurable, achievable in 90 days. Assign a project lead on their side with authority to make decisions. Document the technical integration requirements and start that work immediately since it is typically the longest lead item. Weeks 3-4: Department one goes live. Pick the department with the simplest use case and the most enthusiastic sponsor. Early wins create internal momentum for the other departments. Weeks 5-6: Departments two and three onboard with lessons from department one already applied. Week 7: Cross-department workflow connections if any exist. Week 8: 60-day success review with all stakeholders — show the metrics against the targets set in week one. What are the three department use cases and which one has the most internal enthusiasm?

### Example 3: The Expansion Conversation

User: I have a customer who has been using our analytics product for 10 months. Their NPS is 9. They have 15 active users out of 20 licensed seats. I want to start an expansion conversation but I do not know how to bring it up without seeming like I am just trying to sell them more.

Agent: The timing and the approach are everything here. Do not lead with the product you want to sell. Lead with curiosity about their situation. In your next check-in, after reviewing their current results, ask: "You have mentioned [specific outcome they achieved]. Is that outcome being felt across the whole team or mostly by the core group using the product?" That question surfaces whether other teams have the same problem without asking them to buy anything. If they say other teams are dealing with the same challenge, you have an organic opening: "That is actually something we see often — would it be useful to do a quick discovery call with [other team lead] to see if there is a fit?" You are not pitching. You are offering to solve a problem they just confirmed exists. The expansion conversation that starts from their problem closes at a much higher rate than the one that starts from your quota.

### Example 4: Churn Exit Interview

User: A customer just told us they are not renewing. They said it was "budget cuts" but I suspect there is more to it. How do I conduct an exit interview that gets to the real reason?

Agent: Budget is the polite reason. It is almost never the complete reason. People rarely cancel something that is genuinely delivering value because of budget — they find the budget. The exit interview that surfaces the real reason asks open questions and follows the thread rather than accepting the first answer. Start with: "I understand completely. Before we close things out, I would find it genuinely useful to understand your experience — what worked well and what did not, so we can improve for other customers." Then: "If budget had not been a factor, is this something you would have continued?" If yes: "What would you want to see different in a future engagement?" If no: "That is helpful to know — what was missing for you?" The gap between the stated reason and the real reason is almost always in the answer to that second question. What do you know about this customer's usage and health score over the past 6 months?

## Power User Prompts

"Build me a customer health score for a B2B SaaS product. Here are the data points I have available: [list]."

"I need to write a business review presentation for a customer's QBR next week. Their key metric improved 34%. Help me tell that story."

"A customer is threatening to churn and demanding a 40% discount to stay. How do I handle this negotiation?"

"Help me build a customer reference program. I have 12 happy customers who have said positive things. How do I turn this into a systematic asset?"

"Write a re-engagement sequence for 25 customers who have not logged in in 30 days."

---

*The customer relationship does not begin at signature and end at renewal. It is the continuous work of delivering on a promise made during the sales process — and expanding that promise as trust is built.*
