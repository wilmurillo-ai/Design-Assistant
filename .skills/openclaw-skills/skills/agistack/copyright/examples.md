## Conversation Examples

### Example 1: Creator Discovering Infringement

User: I am a photographer and I found one of my images being used in a major brand's advertising campaign on Instagram. They never contacted me. What can I do?

Agent: This is a strong infringement case if you own the copyright and the image is registered. First, document everything — screenshot the posts with timestamps before anything is removed. Second, check whether you have a registration for this image and when it was registered relative to when the infringement began. If registered before the infringement or within three months of publication, you may be eligible for statutory damages up to $150,000 for willful infringement plus attorney fees — numbers that make this worth pursuing seriously. Third, consider your approach: a direct licensing negotiation often resolves commercial infringement cases faster than litigation and can produce a fair payment without the cost of a lawsuit. A cease and desist or a licensing demand letter to the brand's legal department is a reasonable first step. Tell me whether the image is registered and I will help you think through the specific options.

### Example 2: Fair Use Question

User: I run an educational YouTube channel. I want to use clips from movies to illustrate points about cinematography. Is this fair use?

Agent: Educational commentary on cinematography using film clips is one of the stronger fair use cases — it hits the transformative use factor strongly and the educational purpose factor. But fair use is evaluated case by case, not categorically, so let me walk through the four factors for your specific use. Purpose: educational commentary that transforms the clip by adding analysis — favors fair use. Nature of the original: creative films receive strong copyright protection — slightly against. Amount used: short clips used to illustrate specific points — favors fair use, but the most iconic scenes carry more risk even in short excerpts. Market effect: commentary channel that does not substitute for watching the film — favors fair use. Your risk level depends on clip length, which films you use, and whether you are monetizing the channel. Short clips for genuine critical analysis of technique from non-litigious studios is lower risk. Long clips from studios with aggressive enforcement is higher risk. What specifically are you planning to use?

### Example 3: DMCA Counter-Notice

User: I received a DMCA takedown on my YouTube video. The video uses a song but I licensed it through a music licensing platform. What do I do?

Agent: If you have a valid license for the music, you have grounds to file a counter-notice. The counter-notice tells YouTube that you have the right to use the content and that the takedown was improper. YouTube is then required to notify the claimant, who has 10 to 14 business days to file a lawsuit — if they do not, your video is restored. Before filing, confirm that your license from the music platform covers YouTube use specifically — some sync licenses are limited by platform or territory. Find your license agreement and verify it covers this use. If it does, the counter-notice is appropriate and the burden shifts to the claimant to either file suit or let the video be restored. I can walk you through the exact counter-notice format and what it needs to contain.
