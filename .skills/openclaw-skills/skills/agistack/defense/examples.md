## Conversation Examples

### Example 1: First Contact With Law Enforcement

User: Police showed up at my door this morning asking questions about my former business partner. They said I am not under arrest and they just want to talk. Should I talk to them?

Agent: Do not talk to them without an attorney present. This is true regardless of whether you did anything wrong. Voluntary interviews with law enforcement without counsel present are almost never in the interviewee's interest. Investigators are trained to ask questions in sequences designed to produce information that is useful to their investigation, and innocent people regularly make statements that are later used against them because they misunderstood a question, misspoke, or provided context that implicated them in something they did not intend to discuss. The right response is polite and brief: you want to cooperate but you would like to have an attorney present. Give them nothing else. Then call a criminal defense attorney today — not tomorrow — and describe exactly what happened and what they asked.

### Example 2: Being Sued for the First Time

User: I am a small business owner and a former customer is suing me for $45,000 claiming my service caused them business losses. I just got the complaint. What do I do first?

Agent: First, note the date you were served — your response deadline runs from that date, typically 20 to 30 days depending on your state. Missing this deadline means the plaintiff can request a default judgment against you for the full amount without you having any say. Second, read the complaint carefully and identify what specifically they are alleging — what contract or duty they claim existed, how they claim you breached it, and how they are calculating the $45,000. Third, check whether you have any insurance that might cover this claim — general liability, professional liability, or errors and omissions policies sometimes cover business disputes and your insurer may be required to defend you. Fourth, contact a business litigation attorney this week. Tell me your state and what type of service your business provides and I will help you think through the insurance question and what kind of attorney to look for.

### Example 3: Evaluating a Plea Offer

User: I was charged with a DUI. My attorney says I should take a plea to a reduced charge of reckless driving with a $1,500 fine and no jail time. My BAC was 0.09 and I do not have a prior record. Is this a good deal?

Agent: A reckless driving plea to a first-offense DUI with a BAC just above the legal limit and no prior record is generally considered a favorable outcome in most jurisdictions. Here is why: a DUI conviction carries consequences beyond the sentence — it typically appears on your driving record for years, can affect insurance rates significantly, creates complications for professional licenses in some fields, and in some states cannot be expunged. Reckless driving is a lesser offense with fewer of these collateral consequences. The questions worth asking your attorney: whether reckless driving is expungeable in your state, how this affects your insurance, whether the plea affects any professional license you hold, and whether there are any grounds to challenge the stop or the BAC test that would make a better outcome at trial realistic. If your attorney has evaluated the evidence and believes this is the best available outcome, and the collateral consequences are acceptable, this is likely sound advice.
