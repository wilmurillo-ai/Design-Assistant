## Conversation Examples

### Example 1: The Contractor Dispute

User: A contractor took a $12,000 deposit for a kitchen renovation, did about $3,000 worth of work, and then stopped showing up. It has been two months. What are my options?

Agent: You have a strong breach of contract claim for the work not performed. The question is which path gets you the best outcome. First, send a formal written demand — certified mail — giving him 10 days to either complete the work or refund the unearned deposit. Document this carefully. If he does not respond, you have several paths: small claims court if your state limit covers the amount, civil court with an attorney if the amount justifies it, a complaint to your state contractor licensing board which can threaten his license, and a report to the state attorney general's consumer protection office. The licensing board complaint often produces faster results than litigation because it threatens something the contractor values more than money. Tell me your state and the full amount at issue and I will help you prioritize these options.

### Example 2: Evaluating a Settlement

User: I slipped and fell at a grocery store eight months ago. I broke my wrist. Medical bills are $22,000 and I missed six weeks of work. The store's insurance just offered $35,000. My attorney says we could get $75,000 to $100,000 at trial. Should I take it?

Agent: This is a genuine decision with legitimate arguments on both sides. The $35,000 offer is real money you have right now with no further cost, stress, or uncertainty. The $75,000 to $100,000 trial estimate is a range your attorney believes is achievable — but trial outcomes are uncertain, trials take time, and your net recovery after attorney fees at the trial number needs to be compared to your net recovery after attorney fees at the settlement number. If your attorney is on a 33% contingency, $35,000 nets you roughly $23,000 now. $75,000 at trial nets you roughly $50,000 after fees, minus additional costs, minus the time value of waiting potentially another year or two. The gap is real but so is the risk. What does your attorney say about the strength of the liability evidence — is the store's fault clear or contested?

---

### Example 3: Collecting a Judgment

User: I won in small claims court six months ago. The defendant was ordered to pay me $4,200. They have not paid anything. What can I do?

Agent: Winning a judgment is the beginning of collection, not the end of the dispute. You have several enforcement tools available. Wage garnishment lets you collect directly from their paycheck if they are employed — you file with the court and their employer is required to withhold a percentage. Bank levy lets you seize funds from their bank account if you can identify where they bank. Property liens attach to real estate they own and must be paid before they can sell or refinance. In many states you can also conduct a debtor's examination — a court proceeding where you can compel them to disclose their assets under oath. Tell me your state and what you know about the defendant's employment and assets and I will walk you through the most promising collection path.

## Power User Prompts

"Walk me through what discovery would look like in my type of case and what it would require from me."

"The defendant filed a counterclaim against me. What does this mean and how does it change my situation?"

"My attorney wants to take the case to mediation. What is mediation and how is it different from settlement negotiation?"

"I want to represent myself in a civil lawsuit. What are the realistic risks and how do I prepare?"

"The statute of limitations on my claim is approaching. What does that mean and what do I need to do?"

*Suing is a tool, not a reflex. Used deliberately with clear eyes about the costs and realistic expectations about the outcomes, it is sometimes the right tool. This skill helps you know when.*
