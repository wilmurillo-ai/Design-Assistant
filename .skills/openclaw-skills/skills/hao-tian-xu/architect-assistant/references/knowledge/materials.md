## Wood & Natural Fibers

### Solid Timber (Oak, Walnut, Ash)
- **Best for:** Feature walls, custom furniture, flooring in controlled environments
- **Watch out:** Expands/contracts with humidity — in high-humidity environments (80%+ RH), causes warping and joint gaps in untreated or poorly sealed timber. Needs acclimatization (2+ weeks on site) before installation. Not suitable for outdoor or semi-outdoor without heavy treatment.
- **Cost tier:** Medium–High (species dependent; walnut ≈ 2x oak typically)
- **Lifespan:** 15–30 years indoors with maintenance; 3–5 years outdoor even treated
- **Pairs well with:** Steel (contrast), stone (grounding), brass hardware
- **Avoid combining with:** Direct contact with concrete (moisture wicking causes rot); unventilated cavities

### Plywood / Multi-layer Board
- **Best for:** Cabinetry, wall cladding, temporary/pop-up structures, CNC-cut elements
- **Watch out:** Edge exposure reveals layers — needs edge banding or deliberate design detail. Cheap plywood off-gasses formaldehyde (specify E0 or E1 grade for interiors). Moisture resistance varies wildly by grade.
- **Cost tier:** Low–Medium
- **Lifespan:** 5–15 years (depends on grade and environment)
- **Pairs well with:** Powder-coated steel frames, laminate surfaces
- **Avoid combining with:** High-humidity environments without marine-grade specification

### Bamboo
- **Best for:** Flooring, wall panels, structural elements in temporary installations
- **Watch out:** Harder than most hardwoods but brittle — cracks under point loads. Strand-woven bamboo is more durable than laminated. Color darkens significantly over time (which may or may not be desirable). Quality varies enormously between suppliers.
- **Cost tier:** Low–Medium
- **Lifespan:** 10–20 years (strand-woven); 5–10 years (laminated)
- **Pairs well with:** Concrete, white-painted surfaces, natural textiles
- **Avoid combining with:** Areas with standing water; direct sunlight exposure (fading)

### Rattan
- **Best for:** Display fixtures, decorative screens, pendant light shades, small-scale furniture
- **Watch out:** Warps and sags in humid environments (>70% RH) over 4–6 months. Not structural. Difficult to clean in commercial settings — dust and grease accumulate in weave. Fire rating is poor — check local codes for commercial use.
- **Cost tier:** Low–Medium (labor-intensive handwork can push cost up)
- **Lifespan:** 2–5 years in commercial settings; longer in residential
- **Pairs well with:** Powder-coated steel frames (structural support), linen, ceramic
- **Avoid combining with:** Humid mall atriums for long-term installations; high-traffic touchpoints; food service areas

## Metals

### Steel (Mild / Carbon)
- **Best for:** Structural frames, shelving systems, modular display systems, custom furniture
- **Watch out:** Rusts quickly without coating — even "indoor" steel needs primer + finish. Hot-dip galvanizing for outdoor; powder coat for interior. Welding quality varies — specify Grade II welds minimum for visible joints. Heavy — affects transport costs for modular/pop-up.
- **Cost tier:** Low–Medium (material cheap; fabrication labor is the cost driver)
- **Lifespan:** 20+ years (coated); 2–5 years bare in humid climates
- **Pairs well with:** Wood (warm/cool contrast), concrete, glass
- **Avoid combining with:** Direct contact with copper/brass (galvanic corrosion); salt spray environments without marine-grade coating

### Stainless Steel
- **Best for:** Kitchen/F&B fixtures, bathroom fittings, high-traffic handrails, exterior cladding
- **Watch out:** 304 grade is standard interior; 316 required for coastal/outdoor. Fingerprints show on brushed finish — specify PVD coating or bead-blast for high-touch surfaces. Welding discoloration needs grinding and passivation. Much more expensive to fabricate than mild steel.
- **Cost tier:** Medium–High
- **Lifespan:** 25+ years
- **Pairs well with:** Glass, stone, timber
- **Avoid combining with:** Chlorine-rich environments (pool areas) without 316 grade

### Aluminum
- **Best for:** Lightweight structural frames, curtain wall systems, signage, modular/transportable elements
- **Watch out:** Soft — scratches easily. Anodizing is more durable than paint for finish. Cannot be welded as easily as steel (TIG welding required, adds cost). Excellent for weight-sensitive applications (pop-ups, transportable installations).
- **Cost tier:** Medium
- **Lifespan:** 20+ years (anodized)
- **Pairs well with:** Glass, composite panels, fabric tensile elements
- **Avoid combining with:** Direct contact with concrete or alkaline materials (causes corrosion)

### Brass / Copper
- **Best for:** Hardware, accent trim, light fixtures, small feature elements
- **Watch out:** Patinas naturally — beautiful or unacceptable depending on client. Lacquer coating delays patina but eventually fails (1–3 years). PVD coating is more durable. Expensive at scale — use as accent, not primary surface. Fingerprints visible on polished finish.
- **Cost tier:** High–Premium
- **Lifespan:** Indefinite (patina is aging, not degradation)
- **Pairs well with:** Dark stone, dark timber, white surfaces
- **Avoid combining with:** Large exterior surfaces (maintenance nightmare); budget-tier projects (cost doesn't justify)

## Stone & Masite

### Marble
- **Best for:** Reception counters, feature walls, flooring in low-traffic luxury settings
- **Watch out:** Etches from acidic liquids (wine, citrus, coffee) — terrible for F&B countertops. Porous — stains without regular sealing. Heavy — structural support needed for wall applications. White marble yellows over time in some conditions. Locally-sourced marble varies in quality; premium imported stone is more consistent but significantly more expensive.
- **Cost tier:** Medium (local) to Premium (imported)
- **Lifespan:** 20+ years with maintenance
- **Pairs well with:** Brass, timber, plaster
- **Avoid combining with:** F&B surfaces; high-traffic flooring without honing; budget projects (maintenance cost is ongoing)

### Terrazzo
- **Best for:** Permanent flooring, countertops, feature walls
- **Watch out:** Traditional poured terrazzo is permanent — does not make sense for pop-ups or temporary installations. Terrazzo tiles exist but lose the seamless quality. For pop-ups: terrazzo-look vinyl tiles or epoxy finishes give similar visual at 1/5 cost and are reusable. Poured terrazzo needs 2+ weeks cure time — factor into schedule.
- **Cost tier:** High (poured); Medium (tiles)
- **Lifespan:** 30+ years (poured); 10–15 years (tiles)
- **Pairs well with:** Brass inlays, timber, plants
- **Avoid combining with:** Temporary installations (cost and permanence mismatch); tight construction schedules

### Concrete (Exposed / Fair-faced)
- **Best for:** Walls, floors, countertops, structural expression
- **Watch out:** Fair-faced concrete requires highly skilled formwork — poor execution looks terrible, not "industrial chic." Cracks are inevitable — design for them or seal. Absorbs stains and moisture without sealer. Cold and acoustically reflective — pair with warm/soft materials. Very heavy.
- **Cost tier:** Low (material) but Medium–High (skilled labor for fair-faced)
- **Lifespan:** 30+ years
- **Pairs well with:** Timber (warmth), steel, plants, soft textiles
- **Avoid combining with:** Rooms requiring acoustic comfort without additional treatment; spaces where "unfinished" reads as cheap rather than deliberate

## Composites & Synthetics

### HPL (High-Pressure Laminate)
- **Best for:** Cabinetry surfaces, wall cladding, toilet partitions, budget-friendly finishes
- **Watch out:** Edges are vulnerable — needs aluminum or PVC edging. Can delaminate in high-humidity environments if substrate is poor (use moisture-resistant MDF or plywood). Fire rating varies by product — check for B1 rating in commercial applications.
- **Cost tier:** Low
- **Lifespan:** 10–15 years
- **Pairs well with:** Any framing material (it's a surface finish)
- **Avoid combining with:** Premium interiors where laminate reads as cheap; curved surfaces (limited bend radius)

### Corian / Solid Surface
- **Best for:** Seamless countertops, reception desks, backlit panels, wet areas
- **Watch out:** Scratches easily (but can be sanded and refinished). Yellows under prolonged UV exposure — not for exterior or near windows. Thermoformable for curves but needs specialist fabricator. Looks "plasticky" in large flat expanses — better in shaped/detailed applications.
- **Cost tier:** Medium–High
- **Lifespan:** 15–20 years
- **Pairs well with:** Timber, metal accents, glass
- **Avoid combining with:** Exterior applications; near heat sources (warps above 150°C)

### GRC / GRP (Glass-fiber Reinforced Concrete / Plastic)
- **Best for:** Lightweight cladding mimicking stone/concrete, decorative columns, complex molded shapes
- **Watch out:** GRC is brittle in thin sections — needs careful handling and proper fixing. Color matching between batches is difficult. Seams are visible and hard to hide. Fire performance of GRP varies — some formulations are flammable.
- **Cost tier:** Medium
- **Lifespan:** 15–25 years (GRC); 10–15 years (GRP)
- **Pairs well with:** Real stone accents, steel structure, hidden lighting
- **Avoid combining with:** Areas where people touch/lean against (dents and chips)

## Glass

### Float / Clear Glass
- **Best for:** Partitions, shopfronts, display cases, balustrades
- **Watch out:** Must be tempered or laminated for safety in any public-facing application — this is code, not preference. Tempered glass cannot be cut or drilled after treatment (plan openings beforehand). Heavy at large sizes — check structural support. Thermal performance is poor without double-glazing.
- **Cost tier:** Low (standard); Medium (tempered/laminated)
- **Lifespan:** 25+ years
- **Pairs well with:** Steel or timber frames, minimal hardware
- **Avoid combining with:** Areas requiring privacy without film/treatment; ground-level without marking (collision risk)

### Textured / Patterned Glass (Fluted, Reeded, Frosted)
- **Best for:** Privacy screens, decorative partitions, light diffusion, cabinet fronts
- **Watch out:** Textured glass traps dust and grease on the patterned side — poor for kitchen/F&B adjacencies. Fluted glass is trendy (2023–2025) — may date quickly. Custom patterns have long lead times (4–6 weeks).
- **Cost tier:** Medium–High
- **Lifespan:** 25+ years
- **Pairs well with:** Brass or black steel frames, warm lighting behind
- **Avoid combining with:** Greasy environments; projects where "timeless" is a stated goal (trend risk)

## Textiles & Soft Materials

### Acoustic Panels (Felt, Fabric-wrapped)
- **Best for:** Meeting rooms, restaurants, open offices, ceilings
- **Watch out:** Fabric-wrapped panels attract dust — need regular vacuum cleaning. Felt (PET recycled felt) is more durable and easier to clean. Color fading under direct light — specify UV-stable dyes. Fire rating is critical in commercial — specify B1 minimum.
- **Cost tier:** Medium
- **Lifespan:** 5–10 years (fabric); 10–15 years (PET felt)
- **Pairs well with:** Timber slats, plaster, exposed structure
- **Avoid combining with:** Humid environments (mold risk in fabric); areas without maintenance budget

### Leather / Faux Leather
- **Best for:** Seating, wall panels, headboards, joinery pulls
- **Watch out:** Real leather patinas (desirable in premium settings). Faux leather cracks and peels within 2–3 years in commercial use — specify marine-grade PU for durability. Both are difficult to repair if damaged. Dark colors hide wear better.
- **Cost tier:** Medium (faux) to High (genuine)
- **Lifespan:** 3–5 years (faux in commercial); 10+ years (genuine with care)
- **Pairs well with:** Timber, brass, wool textiles
- **Avoid combining with:** High-traffic commercial seating with faux leather (it will crack); F&B spill zones

## Finishes & Coatings

### Powder Coat
- **Best for:** Metal furniture, steel frames, shelving, modular systems
- **Watch out:** Chips on impact — touch-up paint never matches perfectly. Limited to items that fit in an oven (max ~2m for most local fabricators). Color consistency is excellent batch-to-batch. Matt or satin finishes hide imperfections better than gloss.
- **Cost tier:** Low
- **Lifespan:** 10–15 years indoors; 5–8 years outdoor
- **Pairs well with:** Everything — it's a utilitarian finish
- **Avoid combining with:** Oversized elements that exceed oven dimensions

### Microcement / Micro-topping
- **Best for:** Seamless floors, walls, countertops — industrial/minimal aesthetic
- **Watch out:** Requires very skilled applicator — bad application cracks and looks patchy. Not truly seamless — will develop hairline cracks over time (design feature, not defect, if client is briefed). Needs sealing for wet areas. Slippery when wet unless textured.
- **Cost tier:** Medium–High (labor-intensive)
- **Lifespan:** 10–15 years with resealing
- **Pairs well with:** Timber, black steel, concrete, plants
- **Avoid combining with:** Clients who expect perfection (it's a handcrafted finish); wet areas without anti-slip specification

### Paint (Standard / Specialty)
- **Best for:** Walls, ceilings, joinery — the default finish
- **Watch out:** Dark colors show every imperfection in the wall — substrate prep is critical. Matt finishes scuff and are hard to clean (use eggshell for high-traffic). Specialty finishes (lime wash, Venetian plaster, suede effect) need specialist applicators and cost 5–10x standard paint.
- **Cost tier:** Low (standard); Medium–High (specialty)
- **Lifespan:** 3–5 years before recoat (high-traffic); 8–10 years (low-traffic)
- **Pairs well with:** Everything
- **Avoid combining with:** Dark matt in high-traffic corridors (scuff marks); specialty finishes in budget projects
