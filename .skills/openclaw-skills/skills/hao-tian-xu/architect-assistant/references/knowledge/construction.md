## Structural Systems

### Steel Frame
- **Best for:** Retail fit-outs, modular/demountable structures, mezzanines, long spans
- **Watch out:** Requires qualified welder for on-site modifications. Fire protection required in most commercial applications (intumescent paint or board encasement). Lead time for custom fabrication: 2–4 weeks typical (varies by region). Connection details (bolted vs welded) affect both aesthetics and assembly speed.
- **Speed:** Fast to erect once fabricated. Bolted connections: 1–2 days for small structures. Welded: add 50% for site welding + grinding + finishing.
- **Demountable?** Yes, if bolted connections are specified from the start. Welded frames are permanent.
- **Cost driver:** Fabrication labor > material cost. Complex details multiply cost non-linearly.

### Timber Frame
- **Best for:** Residential, small commercial, warm aesthetic, sustainability-conscious projects
- **Watch out:** Fire rating is the main constraint in commercial — treated timber can achieve required ratings but adds cost. Engineered timber (CLT, glulam) enables larger spans than solid timber. Moisture protection is critical during construction — exposed timber on site absorbs water fast.
- **Speed:** Moderate. Prefab timber panels are fast; site-built timber is slow (skilled carpentry).
- **Demountable?** Possible with mechanical connections but rare in practice.
- **Cost driver:** Skilled carpentry labor; engineered timber products are premium.

### Concrete (Cast-in-place)
- **Best for:** Permanent structures, basements, foundations, buildings with heavy loads
- **Watch out:** Slow — formwork, pour, cure (minimum 28 days for full strength, 7 days for stripping). Mistakes are extremely expensive to fix. Fair-faced concrete needs A-grade formwork and skilled crew. Weather-dependent — rain and extreme cold affect pour quality.
- **Speed:** Slowest structural system. 1 floor per 1–2 weeks typical.
- **Demountable?** No.
- **Cost driver:** Formwork (reusable vs single-use); concrete pump access; rebar complexity.

## Construction Approaches

### Prefab / Off-site Fabrication
- **Best for:** Repetitive elements, modular systems, tight site schedules, pop-ups
- **Advantages:** Quality control in factory > on site. Weather-independent fabrication. Faster on-site assembly. Can be designed for disassembly and reuse.
- **Watch out:** Transport dimensions limit panel/module size (check local truck/container limits). Site tolerances must be tight — prefab doesn't forgive sloppy groundwork. Design changes after fabrication starts = expensive rework or waste.
- **Lead time:** 3–6 weeks fabrication + 1–2 weeks install (small–medium project)
- **When to use:** When install window is short (mall fit-outs with overnight-only access); when the design will be replicated across locations; when quality needs to be tightly controlled.

### Site-built / Traditional
- **Best for:** One-off custom work, renovation where conditions are unpredictable, projects where design evolves during construction
- **Advantages:** Flexible — can adapt to site conditions discovered during construction. No transport constraints.
- **Watch out:** Quality depends entirely on the crew. Skilled tradespeople are increasingly scarce. Weather delays. Dust, noise, waste on site. Longer program.
- **When to use:** Complex renovations; projects where the budget doesn't support prefab tooling; genuinely one-off designs.

### Hybrid (Prefab structure + Site-built finishes)
- **Best for:** Most commercial interiors — this is the default approach
- **Structure:** Steel or aluminum frames fabricated off-site, bolted on-site
- **Finishes:** Painted, tiled, or clad on-site for tolerance absorption and quality
- **Watch out:** Coordination between off-site and on-site teams is the risk point. Dimensional survey of the site before fabrication is essential.

## Fit-out vs Shell & Core

### Shell & Core (Landlord delivers)
- Typically includes: structure, external envelope, core services (lifts, stairs, main MEP risers), fire exits, basic fire protection
- **Check:** What finish level? Some landlords deliver raw concrete floors and exposed ceiling; others provide basic floor screed and suspended ceiling grid.

### Tenant Fit-out (Your scope)
- Typically includes: internal partitions, finishes (floor, wall, ceiling), lighting, HVAC distribution, plumbing within tenancy, joinery, fixtures, signage
- **Watch out:** MEP connection points from landlord's systems need early coordination. Electrical capacity (kVA allocation) may limit equipment plans. HVAC tonnage allocated to tenancy — check before designing open kitchens or server rooms that generate heat.

## Common Failure Points

### Coordination Failures
- **MEP vs ceiling design:** Services don't fit in the ceiling void. Measure actual duct sizes, not just pipe diameters. Allow 150–200mm clearance above finished ceiling for distribution.
- **Floor build-up:** Raised floors, underfloor heating, waterproofing layers — these stack up. A "simple" bathroom can need 150–200mm floor build-up. Check floor-to-floor height before committing to any floor system.
- **Structural vs partition:** Load-bearing walls mistaken for partitions during renovation. Always verify with structural drawings or site investigation before demolishing anything.

### Schedule Risks
- **Material lead times:** Natural stone: 4–8 weeks. Custom glass: 3–6 weeks. Imported fixtures: 6–12 weeks. Bespoke metalwork: 3–5 weeks. Order early — lead times cause more delays than construction speed.
- **Approval delays:** Fire safety, landlord design approval, building management coordination — each can add 1–4 weeks.
- **Wet trade sequencing:** Concrete/screed/plaster need to dry before next trades can start. In humid seasons, drying times can double. Plan accordingly or use dehumidifiers.

### Quality Risks
- **Hidden conditions:** Renovation projects always uncover surprises — old waterproofing failures, non-standard wiring, structural defects. Budget 10–15% contingency for renovation; 5–10% for new fit-out.
- **Skill gaps:** Fair-faced concrete, Venetian plaster, complex metalwork, precision joinery — all require specialist crews. Verify fabricator capability with samples before committing to a design.
- **Site supervision:** Without daily supervision, quality drops. For premium finishes, supervise during application — fixes after the fact are always worse.

## Modular & Transportable Design

### Design for Disassembly Principles
1. **Bolted, not welded.** Every permanent connection is a disposal decision later.
2. **Standard dimensions.** Size panels/modules to fit standard shipping (container internal: 2.35m W x 2.39m H x 5.9/12m L).
3. **Layered assembly.** Structure → skin → fixtures → graphics. Each layer independent so graphics can change without dismantling structure.
4. **Numbered components.** Every piece gets a unique ID for assembly/disassembly instructions.
5. **Durable connections.** Bolts, clips, and cam-locks — not screws that strip after 3 cycles.

### Transport & Storage
- **Flat-pack:** Panels < 100mm thick stack efficiently. Target < 2 CBM per sqm of installed display.
- **Nested:** Design nesting into the module geometry — smaller elements fit inside larger ones.
- **Cases:** Reusable flight cases for premium/fragile elements; blanket wrapping for structural frames.
- **Storage:** Between deployments, modular systems need clean, dry storage. Budget for warehouse space if the client doesn't have it.
