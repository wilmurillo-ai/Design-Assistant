## Embodied Carbon Basics

### Why It Matters
- Embodied carbon = emissions from manufacturing, transporting, and installing materials (before the building even opens)
- For short-lifecycle projects (pop-ups, fit-outs replaced every 5–10 years), embodied carbon dominates over operational carbon
- Rule of thumb: a retail fit-out replaced every 5 years generates more embodied carbon than the HVAC running during that period

### Material Carbon Intensity (Approximate)

| Material | Embodied Carbon | Notes |
|----------|----------------|-------|
| Concrete | High (cement is ~8% of global CO₂) | Low-carbon concrete mixes available (GGBS, fly ash replacement). Specify if available locally |
| Steel | High (but highly recyclable) | Recycled steel content varies — specify minimum recycled content. Check local market for typical recycled content |
| Aluminum | Very High (smelting is energy-intensive) | Recycled aluminum uses 95% less energy. Always specify recycled content |
| Timber | Low (sequesters carbon) | Only if sustainably sourced (FSC/PEFC). Tropical hardwoods can be net-negative if from deforestation |
| Bamboo | Very Low | Fast-growing, available in many regions. Good carbon story if sourced responsibly |
| Glass | Medium | Float glass is energy-intensive. No meaningful recycled content available in architectural glass |
| Natural stone | Low–Medium | Extraction impact varies. Local stone < imported stone (transport dominates) |
| Plastics/composites | Medium–High | Poor end-of-life story. Avoid where alternatives exist |

### Practical Actions
- **Reuse before recycle.** Reusing existing elements (furniture, fixtures, structural frames) is always better than recycling materials into new products.
- **Specify recycled content.** Steel (≥30% recycled), aluminum (≥50% recycled), PET felt (≥60% recycled). Check local market availability for these specs.
- **Local sourcing.** Transport carbon is real. Check local fabrication capacity before importing — many regions can produce what you need at lower carbon cost.
- **Design for longevity or disassembly.** Either make it last (reducing replacement cycles) or make it demountable (enabling reuse). The worst outcome is permanent installation that gets demolished in 5 years.

## Certifications

_Check which certifications are relevant in your region and for your client. Below are the most common international systems._

### LEED (International)
- Most recognized globally. Common for multinational tenant fit-outs.
- LEED for Commercial Interiors (LEED CI) is the relevant scope for fit-outs.
- **Watch out:** Certification cost is USD 3,000–25,000+ depending on project size. Some credits are easy (transit access, regional materials); others are hard (energy modeling for small fit-outs is disproportionate effort).
- **Quick wins:** Low-VOC finishes, recycled content, regional materials, daylight access, bicycle storage.

### WELL (Health & Wellness)
- Focus on occupant health: air quality, water, light, thermal comfort, sound, materials.
- Growing demand in premium offices and wellness-oriented retail.
- **Watch out:** Ongoing performance testing required — not just design intent. Air quality monitoring, water testing, light measurement. This is an operational commitment, not just a design one.

### BREEAM
- Common in Europe and UK. Some international clients require it.

### Regional Certifications
- Many countries have national green building standards (e.g., Green Star in Australia, DGNB in Germany, Green Building Label in China). Check local requirements — some are mandatory for government or premium commercial projects.

## Low-Carbon Material Alternatives

| Standard Material | Lower-Carbon Alternative | Trade-off |
|-------------------|------------------------|-----------|
| Portland cement concrete | GGBS/fly ash concrete (30–50% cement replacement) | Slower early strength gain; longer formwork time |
| Virgin steel | High-recycled-content steel | Specify with fabricator; minimal cost impact |
| Aluminum composite panel | Timber cladding or fiber cement | Different aesthetic; fire rating check needed |
| Carpet (nylon, PVC-backed) | PET recycled felt tiles or wool carpet | PET felt is excellent; wool is premium cost |
| PVC flooring | Linoleum (natural, biodegradable) | Different feel/aesthetic; excellent durability |
| Acrylic solid surface | Bio-resin solid surface | Limited supplier base; similar performance |
| MDF (formaldehyde binder) | Straw board or E0 MDF | E0 MDF widely available; straw board availability varies by region |
| Spray foam insulation | Cork, wood fiber, mineral wool | Generally thicker for same R-value |

## Lifecycle Thinking for Different Project Types

### Pop-up (< 2 years)
- **Priority:** Design for reuse and disassembly. Every material choice should assume multiple deployment cycles.
- **Best strategy:** Modular steel/aluminum frames (infinite reuse) + changeable surface materials (fabric graphics, interchangeable panels). Structure is permanent investment; surfaces are consumable.
- **Avoid:** Single-use custom elements; materials that can't survive transport and reassembly.

### Medium-term Fit-out (3–10 years)
- **Priority:** Durable materials that maintain appearance without maintenance. Balance between investment and anticipated lease term.
- **Best strategy:** Invest in quality for high-traffic surfaces (flooring, counters, main walls). Use easily-replaced materials for trend-sensitive elements (feature walls, graphics, soft furnishings).
- **Avoid:** Premium permanent materials (stone, terrazzo) if lease is < 5 years; disposable materials for main surfaces.

### Long-term / Permanent (10+ years)
- **Priority:** Longevity, timelessness, repairability, graceful aging.
- **Best strategy:** Natural materials that age well (timber, stone, brass, concrete). Classic detailing that won't date. Design for maintenance access.
- **Avoid:** Trend-driven finishes; synthetic materials that degrade visibly; anything that can't be locally repaired.

## Indoor Environment Quality

### Air Quality
- **VOCs:** Specify low-VOC or zero-VOC for all paints, adhesives, sealants, and composite boards. E0 or E1 formaldehyde rating for all panel products. In climates where windows are often closed (AC or heating seasons), off-gassing builds up — ventilation design is critical.
- **Ventilation:** Fresh air rates per local building code for commercial. F&B needs enhanced rates. Verify HVAC design provides adequate fresh air, not just recirculated cooling.

### Thermal Comfort
- Check the project's climate/environment constraints. Design HVAC and envelope strategy for the specific local conditions.
- **Radiant surfaces:** Large glass facades create radiant heat gain in summer and cold downdrafts in winter. Mitigate with low-E glass, solar film, or perimeter heating.

### Acoustic Comfort
- **Retail:** Background music typically 55–65 dB. Hard surfaces amplify — if the space is all stone and glass, conversation becomes difficult above ~50% occupancy.
- **F&B:** Restaurant noise is the #1 complaint from diners. Target RT60 < 0.8s. Budget for ceiling and wall acoustic treatment from the start — retrofitting is ugly and expensive.
- **Office:** NC 35–40 for open plan; NC 30–35 for meeting rooms. Sound masking systems help but are often overlooked in fit-out budgets.
