## Budget Tiers by Project Type (Relative, fit-out only)

_Fit-out cost = everything inside the shell: finishes, MEP distribution, lighting, joinery, fixtures, signage. Excludes rent, FF&E (loose furniture), and design fees._

_Use these tiers as relative guidance. Actual costs vary significantly by region, market conditions, and labor rates. When discussing budgets with the user, ask about their local market context if not already captured in the project's Constraints._

### Retail

| Tier | What You Get |
|------|--------------|
| Budget | Paint, vinyl flooring, basic lighting, off-the-shelf fixtures, minimal joinery |
| Mid-range | Engineered timber or tile flooring, custom joinery, feature lighting, some stone/metal accents |
| Premium | Natural stone, custom metalwork, bespoke lighting, integrated AV, high-end finishes throughout |
| Luxury | Artisan finishes, imported materials, complex geometry, museum-grade lighting, brand flagship level |

### F&B

| Tier | Notes |
|------|-------|
| Fast-casual | Simple kitchen, standard ventilation, durable commercial finishes |
| Mid-range dining | Full kitchen fit-out, acoustic treatment, quality finishes, feature bar |
| Fine dining | Premium everything, wine storage, specialty equipment, bespoke everything |

_F&B costs are always higher than retail at equivalent quality because of kitchen MEP (ventilation, grease traps, fire suppression, gas, water, waste)._

### Office

| Tier | Notes |
|------|-------|
| Basic | Carpet tile, painted walls, grid ceiling, basic lighting |
| Mid-range | Glass partitions, feature reception, improved lighting, acoustic treatment |
| Premium | Bespoke joinery, premium finishes, integrated AV, high-end lighting throughout |

## Cost Drivers & Where Money Goes

### Typical Cost Breakdown (Retail Fit-out)

| Category | % of Total | Notes |
|----------|-----------|-------|
| Joinery & fixtures | 25–35% | Biggest single line item. Quality of materials and complexity of design drive this |
| MEP (mechanical, electrical, plumbing) | 20–30% | Higher for F&B. Often underestimated in early budgets |
| Finishes (floor, wall, ceiling) | 15–25% | Floor is usually the most expensive surface per sqm |
| Lighting | 8–15% | Good lighting transforms a space. Often the best ROI investment |
| Structure & partitions | 5–10% | Minimal for fit-out; higher for mezzanines or significant structural modifications |
| Signage & graphics | 3–8% | External signage can be surprisingly expensive (approvals, structural fixing, illumination) |
| Contingency | 5–15% | 10% for new fit-out; 15% for renovation. Non-negotiable |

### What Costs More Than People Expect
- **Fair-faced concrete:** Material is cheap; the skilled formwork crew is not. Budget 3–5x the cost of standard concealed concrete.
- **Curved elements:** Any curve (walls, glass, joinery) costs 2–5x its straight equivalent. Compound curves even more.
- **Integrated lighting:** Cove lighting, backlit panels, under-counter strips — each requires its own driver, wiring channel, and dimming circuit. It adds up fast.
- **Acoustic treatment:** Proper acoustic panels, laminated glass, seals — rarely budgeted adequately. Retrofit is uglier and more expensive.
- **Overseas sourcing:** Import duty, shipping, customs clearance, storage, and the risk of damage. Total landed cost is typically 1.5–2x the FOB price. Source locally when possible.

### What Costs Less Than People Expect
- **Paint (standard colors):** The cheapest way to transform a space. A bold color palette costs almost nothing extra vs. white.
- **Powder coating:** Very affordable finish for steel. Wide color range. Looks premium at budget cost.
- **Vinyl flooring (LVT):** Modern luxury vinyl is visually convincing, durable, and cheap. Excellent for pop-ups and mid-range retail.
- **Fabric graphics:** Large-format dye-sublimation fabric prints on frames. High impact, low cost, easily changed.

## Fee Structures

### Design Fee Models
- **Per sqm:** Standard in many markets for interior design. Clear, easy to benchmark. Rates vary significantly by region.
- **Percentage of construction cost:** 8–15% typical. Aligns designer with quality but creates perverse incentive on budget.
- **Fixed fee:** Best for clearly scoped projects. Risky if scope creeps.
- **Phased billing:** Typical split: 30% on signing, 30% at design completion, 30% at construction completion, 10% on handover.

## Schedule Management

### Critical Path Items (Usually)
1. **Landlord/authority approvals** — Start first, run parallel to design. 2–8 weeks.
2. **Long-lead materials** — Natural stone (4–8 weeks), custom glass (3–6 weeks), imported items (8–16 weeks). Order as soon as design is confirmed.
3. **MEP rough-in** — Must happen before wall/ceiling finishes close up. Coordinate early.
4. **Joinery fabrication** — 3–6 weeks off-site. Can run parallel to on-site wet trades.
5. **Signage** — Often forgotten until the end. Structural fixing, electrical connection, and approval needed. Start early.

### Schedule Compression Tactics
- **Overlap design and procurement.** Confirm key materials (flooring, stone, major joinery) while detail design continues.
- **Prefabricate off-site.** Joinery, steel frames, ceiling modules — anything that can be built in a factory while site work continues.
- **Night shifts (mall context).** Double the labor for the same wall-clock duration. Expensive but sometimes the only way to meet an opening date.
- **Reduce wet trades.** Every pour, screed, or plaster application adds drying time (3–14 days). Dry construction (steel + panel systems) is faster.
- **Simplify.** The fastest way to compress schedule is to reduce scope. Cut a feature wall, simplify joinery details, use standard sizes. A delivered project beats a perfect unfinished one.

### Regional Schedule Factors
_Check the user's region for local factors that affect construction timelines:_
- **Public holidays and seasonal shutdowns** — Many regions have extended holiday periods where construction stops. Factor these into any project timeline.
- **Rainy/humid seasons** — High humidity extends drying times for all wet trades (paint, plaster, screed, concrete). Add 30–50% to cure times during these periods.
- **Extreme weather** — Typhoons, monsoons, extreme cold — outdoor work and delivery disruptions are possible. Plan material deliveries around weather patterns.

## Budget Negotiation & Value Engineering

### Value Engineering Principles
1. **Protect the hero moment.** Every project has 1–2 design moves that define the experience. Protect these. Cut elsewhere.
2. **Simplify, don't cheapen.** A simpler version of a good material (e.g., honed limestone instead of polished marble) reads better than a cheap version of a premium material (e.g., marble-print laminate trying to pass as stone).
3. **Reduce area, not quality.** A smaller space done well is better than a larger space done cheaply. If budget is tight, consider whether the program actually needs all the allocated space.
4. **Standardize where nobody looks.** Back-of-house, storage, staff areas — standard finishes here save budget for customer-facing spaces.
5. **Question every import.** Can it be sourced or fabricated locally? Check local fabrication capacity before defaulting to imports.
