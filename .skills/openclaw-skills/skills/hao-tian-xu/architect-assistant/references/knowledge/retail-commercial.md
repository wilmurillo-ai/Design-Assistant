## Project Types & Their Constraints

### Pop-up Retail (< 2 years)
- **Priority:** Speed of installation, reusability, transport logistics, visual impact
- **Watch out:** Everything must be demountable. Avoid wet trades (concrete, terrazzo, plaster) — they're permanent and slow. Modular steel + panel systems are the backbone. Budget per sqm is lower than permanent, but cost-per-use can be optimized through reuse across locations.
- **Typical timeline:** Design 2–4 weeks; fabrication 3–6 weeks; install 3–7 days
- **Material palette:** Powder-coated steel, plywood, fabric, modular flooring (vinyl tiles, interlocking rubber), tension fabric graphics
- **Avoid:** Bespoke joinery (can't be reconfigured), heavy stone, anything requiring wet installation

### Permanent Retail (5+ years)
- **Priority:** Brand expression, durability, customer experience, operational efficiency
- **Watch out:** Mall landlords have fit-out guides — check before design (floor loading, ceiling height access, fire separation requirements, signage restrictions). Lease terms affect investment level — don't specify premium materials for a 3-year lease unless the brand demands it.
- **Typical timeline:** Design 4–8 weeks; construction 6–12 weeks
- **Material palette:** Full range available — stone, timber, metal, glass, custom joinery
- **Avoid:** Trendy materials/finishes that will date before the lease ends

### F&B (Restaurant, Café, Bar)
- **Priority:** Hygiene compliance, durability under daily abuse, atmosphere, acoustic comfort
- **Watch out:** Kitchen/BOH has strict code requirements (stainless surfaces, grease traps, ventilation rates, fire suppression). FOH materials must handle spills, heat, and constant cleaning. Marble etches from acidic foods. Timber needs heavy-duty sealant. Upholstery must be commercial-grade and cleanable.
- **Typical timeline:** Design 4–8 weeks; construction 8–16 weeks (MEP-heavy)
- **Material palette:** Tile, stainless steel, sealed timber, commercial-grade upholstery, resin or sealed concrete floors
- **Avoid:** Porous unsealed stone in food zones; delicate fabrics; materials that absorb odors

### Office / Co-working
- **Priority:** Acoustic privacy, flexibility, biophilic elements, brand identity
- **Watch out:** Open plan is cheap but destroys productivity without acoustic treatment. Glass partitions look great but transmit sound — specify laminated acoustic glass or add seals. Raised floors enable future reconfiguration but add 100–150mm to floor-to-floor height.
- **Typical timeline:** Design 4–8 weeks; construction 8–14 weeks
- **Material palette:** Carpet tile, acoustic panels, glass partitions, system furniture, timber/laminate joinery
- **Avoid:** Hard reflective surfaces without acoustic mitigation; fixed layouts in spaces that may need to adapt

## Brand-to-Design Translation

### The Process
1. **Extract brand values** — Not "we're premium" (everyone says that). Push deeper: What's the craft story? What's the origin? What sensory associations does the brand want?
2. **Map values to spatial qualities** — "Handcrafted" → visible material joints, natural textures, imperfection-as-feature. "Minimalist" → concealed storage, flush details, restrained palette.
3. **Filter through constraints** — A "handcrafted" aesthetic for a pop-up can't use artisan plaster (too slow, too permanent). Instead: handwoven textile panels on modular frames, hand-finished timber display units that travel.
4. **Test with a material board** — 3–5 key materials, not 15. Each material must justify itself against brand values AND project constraints.

### Common Brand Archetypes & Material Responses

| Brand Archetype | Spatial Quality | Material Direction | Watch Out |
|----------------|----------------|-------------------|-----------|
| Artisanal / Handcrafted | Texture, warmth, visible process | Natural fibers, timber, ceramic, handmade tiles | Handcrafted finishes are slow and expensive — budget accordingly or use machine-made materials with handcraft detailing |
| Minimal / Clean | Restraint, precision, negative space | Corian, glass, powder-coat, microcement | Minimalism demands perfect execution — every flaw shows. Budget must support precision fabrication |
| Heritage / Legacy | Weight, permanence, rich materials | Dark timber, brass, leather, stone, moldings | Heritage codes can look costume-like if overdone. Restraint is key — one or two heritage materials, rest neutral |
| Sustainable / Natural | Lightness, transparency, biophilia | Bamboo, recycled materials, timber, plants, cork | "Sustainable" materials can be more expensive. Green-washing risk if claims can't be substantiated. Verify certifications |
| Tech / Innovation | Precision, dynamism, interactive | Metal, glass, LED integration, digital surfaces | Tech elements date fastest — design for upgradeability. Screen-heavy interiors feel cold without material warmth |
| Luxury / Premium | Richness, exclusivity, sensory depth | Stone, metal leaf, bespoke finishes, art integration | Luxury ≠ expensive everything. Strategic splurge on hero moments, restraint elsewhere. Over-gilding looks tacky |

## Display & Fixture Systems

### Modular Display Systems
- **Best for:** Multi-location rollouts, pop-ups, brands that change seasonal displays
- **System:** Steel or aluminum frame + interchangeable panels (timber, fabric, perforated metal)
- **Watch out:** Modular ≠ generic. The frame system can be standard while panel finishes express the brand. Design the connection detail — visible bolts read industrial; concealed clips read refined.
- **Cost:** Higher upfront, lower per-use across multiple locations/seasons

### Fixed Joinery
- **Best for:** Permanent retail, flagship stores, premium positioning
- **Watch out:** Cannot be reconfigured. If the brand changes product lines or display needs, fixed joinery becomes obsolete. Spec adjustable shelving within fixed frames as a compromise.
- **Cost:** Medium–High per unit, but no reuse value

### Freestanding Furniture-as-Display
- **Best for:** Lifestyle brands, concept stores, pop-ups
- **Watch out:** Requires floor space — doesn't maximize product density. Looks great in renders, hard to merchandise effectively. Products need integrated anti-theft if high-value.
- **Cost:** Variable — can use off-the-shelf furniture modified with brand elements

## Lighting

### Retail Lighting Rules of Thumb
- **Ambient:** 300–500 lux for general retail; 150–200 lux for premium/mood retail
- **Accent:** 3:1 ratio minimum (accent to ambient) for product highlighting; 5:1 for drama
- **Color temperature:** 2700–3000K for warm retail (fashion, food, lifestyle); 3500–4000K for tech, pharmacy, optical
- **CRI:** Minimum 90 for any space where color accuracy matters (fashion, food, cosmetics, art)

### Watch Outs
- Track lighting is flexible but visually cluttered if overdone — use recessed where layout is fixed
- LED strips behind shelves/under counters: effective but cheap-looking if the LED source is visible. Always use channels/diffusers
- Mall ceiling heights limit fixture options — check landlord requirements before specifying pendants
- Dimmable drivers add 30–50% to lighting cost but are essential for F&B and experiential retail
- Natural light is free ambience but causes UV damage to products and materials — control with film or layout

## Circulation & Layout

### Retail Layout Principles
- **Decompression zone:** First 1.5–2m inside the entrance — customers don't look at anything here. Don't put key products in this zone.
- **Right-turn bias:** Most people turn right upon entering. Place hero/seasonal product on the right wall.
- **Sightlines:** The back wall is the most powerful visual magnet. Use it for brand statement or hero display.
- **Path width:** 1.2m minimum for comfortable single-person; 1.8m for two-person passing. ADA/accessibility requires wider.
- **Dwell time:** Deeper stores with curved paths increase dwell time. Grid layouts increase efficiency (grocery model).

### F&B Layout
- **Kitchen to table distance:** Shorter = hotter food, fewer staff needed. Long passes need heated runners.
- **Covers per sqm:** 1.0–1.5 covers/sqm is comfortable; 1.5–2.0 is dense (fast-casual); above 2.0 feels cramped.
- **Bar seating:** 600mm per person minimum; 750mm comfortable. Bar height: 1050–1100mm; counter height: 900mm.
- **Service circulation:** 900mm minimum behind seated diners for staff to pass.

## Mall & Landlord Constraints

### Typical Mall Fit-out Rules
- **Fire separation:** Tenants must maintain fire-rated boundaries at lease line. Glass frontage often required to be fire-rated.
- **Floor loading:** Typically 3.5–5 kN/sqm for retail; check before specifying stone flooring or heavy fixtures.
- **Ceiling access:** Mall MEP runs above tenant ceilings. Maintain access panels per landlord spec. Open ceiling designs expose ugly services — budget for painting/organizing exposed services.
- **Signage:** Size, illumination, projection from facade all regulated. Blade signs, hanging signs may be prohibited.
- **Operating hours:** Fit-out work usually restricted to overnight (22:00–08:00) once mall is operational. Factor into schedule.
- **Common area interface:** Flooring, ceiling, lighting must transition gracefully at lease line. Coordinate with landlord's design.

### Approvals Process
- Design submission to landlord for approval (2–4 weeks)
- Fire safety review (1–2 weeks, can be concurrent)
- Construction permit from mall management
- Multiple inspections during fit-out
- Final inspection before opening

**Watch out:** Mall approval processes can add 4–8 weeks to a project. Start submissions early. Changes after approval restart the clock.

## Plants & Biophilic Elements

### Live Plants
- **Best for:** Permanent installations with maintenance contracts
- **Watch out:** Need irrigation, drainage, light, and regular maintenance. Mall locations often have poor natural light. Irrigation can leak and damage finishes below. Seasonal die-off in temperate climates. Cost is ongoing, not one-time.
- **Alternative for pop-ups:** Preserved/stabilized plants (real plants treated to need zero maintenance, last 3–5 years indoors, no water/light needed). More expensive upfront but zero operational cost.

### Preserved Moss / Stabilized Greenery
- **Best for:** Green walls, ceiling installations, brand-nature association without maintenance
- **Watch out:** Cannot be in direct sunlight (fades). Cannot get wet (disintegrates). Dusty environments require occasional compressed-air cleaning. Has a distinct smell when new (dissipates in 1–2 weeks). Not suitable for outdoor.
- **Cost tier:** Medium–High (one-time)
- **Lifespan:** 3–7 years indoors

### Dried/Artificial Elements
- **Best for:** Budget installations, pop-ups, seasonal displays
- **Watch out:** Dried flowers collect dust and are fire hazards — check codes. Artificial plants look artificial up close — use in elevated/distant positions. Quality varies enormously.
- **Cost tier:** Low–Medium
- **Lifespan:** 6–18 months (dried); 3–5 years (quality artificial)
