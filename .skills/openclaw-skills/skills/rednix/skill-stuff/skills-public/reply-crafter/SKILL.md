# Reply Crafter

Most social media replies are garbage.
"Thanks for sharing!" "Great point!" "Totally agree!"

These replies exist to be seen replying, not to say anything.

This skill writes replies worth reading.

---

## On-demand only

`/reply [paste the comment/DM] [optional: your intended response]`
Or: "Help me reply to this" / "How should I respond to this?"

---

## Reply types

**The genuine engagement reply:**
Someone made a real point. You want to respond substantively.
Not to be seen engaging. Because you actually have something to add.

**The acknowledgment reply:**
Someone shared something personal or vulnerable.
The reply that makes them feel heard without being performative about it.

**The correction reply:**
Someone is wrong, or you disagree.
The reply that's honest without being aggressive.

**The boundary reply:**
A DM or comment that needs a clear, polite decline.
"Not taking on clients right now." "This isn't something I can help with."

**The fan/support reply:**
Someone said something kind. You want to respond without being sycophantic back.

**The question reply:**
Someone asked something. You want to answer it properly, not with a three-word reply.

## The approach

### Step 1 — What is this actually about

Read the original comment or DM.
What is the person actually saying or asking?
What do they probably want from a reply?

Not what's in the words. What's the intent.

### Step 2 — What does the user want to convey

If the user provided their intended response: use that as the direction.
If not: ask, or infer from context.

One question if needed: "What do you want them to take away from your reply?"

### Step 3 — Write the reply

Rules:
- Match the length and register of the original — don't reply with an essay to a two-line comment
- Say the thing you want to say, then stop
- Be specific to what they actually wrote — no generic responses
- Don't perform engagement — actually engage
- No "great question!" as an opener

For genuine engagement: add something. Extend the thought. Push back gently if there's something worth pushing back on. Agree specifically, not generically.

For acknowledgment: name what they shared. Don't analyse it. Don't add a lesson. Just acknowledge it.

For correction: one sentence on the disagreement. One sentence on why. Optional: what you'd add or what you think they might be missing.

For boundary: one sentence, warm. No over-explaining. No apologising for having a boundary.

---

## Tone calibration

Short. Direct. Human.
The reply should sound like a quick message from a real person.

If the user's public voice is established: match it.
If not: ask once or default to warm and conversational.

---

## DM replies

DMs are more personal than public comments.
They deserve a slightly longer, more personal reply when appropriate.

For cold outreach DMs: the boundary reply is usually right.
For genuine connection DMs: actually connect.
For collaboration/partnership DMs: one of two options — genuine interest (reply properly) or not the right fit (clear and kind decline).

---

## The one thing this skill won't do

Write a generic engagement reply designed to be seen rather than to say something.
"Thanks for sharing, this really resonated!" is not a reply — it's a notification.

If the user asks for this kind of reply: write a real one instead,
and note that the generic version isn't worth sending.

## What makes it good

Specificity to what was actually written.
The reply that references a specific word or phrase from the original message
feels like the person actually read it. Because the skill did.

The "match the length" rule.
A two-line comment gets a two-to-four-line reply.
A two-line comment getting a five-paragraph reply is weird.

The boundary reply done cleanly.
"Not taking on clients right now but thank you for reaching out" is better than
a three-sentence apology for having a boundary.
