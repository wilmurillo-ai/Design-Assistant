# Short Form Script

Short-form video is the most compressed form of communication that works.
30 seconds to make a point. No setup. No filler. No "smash that like button."

This skill writes scripts that respect the viewer's time.

---

## On-demand only

`/reel [idea or topic]`
`/tiktok [idea or topic]`
`/short [idea or topic]`
Or: "Write me a script for a Reel about X"

---

## Platform differences

**Instagram Reels:**
Slightly longer tolerated (up to 90s). Can be more polished.
Text overlays work well. Good for: tips, insights, demonstrations.

**TikTok:**
Fastest-paced. Trend-aware but not trend-dependent.
Raw/authentic works as well as polished. Good for: personality-driven, story, opinion.

**YouTube Shorts:**
Often the most substantive short-form. Viewers expect slightly more depth.
Good for: compressed versions of longer content, answers to specific questions.

The script structure is the same across all three. Pacing and energy differ.

## The structure

Every short-form script has three parts. The ratio matters.

**Hook (5-8 seconds / ~15-25 words)**
The reason to keep watching. Must be in the first 3 seconds.
Not a teaser. The actual interesting thing, stated immediately.

Good hooks:
- The claim: "Most people are doing X wrong."
- The specific moment: "Yesterday I watched someone [thing] and I had to share this."
- The question with a non-obvious answer: "Why does X happen? It's not what you think."
- The counter-intuitive: "The best thing I did for my productivity was stop using a to-do list."

Bad hooks:
- "Hey guys! Today I'm going to be talking about..."
- "So I've been thinking about X lately..."
- "In this video I'm going to share..."

**Point (15-60 seconds / the substance)**
The actual thing. Said clearly. No padding.
One point per video. Not three. One.
If there are naturally three, make three videos.

Each sentence should advance the point or it shouldn't be there.
Say the thing. Then say why it matters. Then give one example.

**Close (5-10 seconds)**
Land the plane. Don't meander.
Options:
- Restate the core insight in one new way
- Give one concrete action to take
- End on the question the viewer is now thinking about

No "if you found this helpful smash that like button."
No "follow for more."
End like the video is complete, not like you're begging.

---

## Script format

```
HOOK (0-5s):
[Exact words to say. Short. Direct.]

POINT (5-50s):
[The substance. Broken into beats if needed.]
Beat 1: [what to say]
Beat 2: [what to say]
Beat 3: [what to say — only if needed]

CLOSE (50-60s):
[Landing. One or two sentences.]

---
VISUAL NOTES (optional):
[Any specific visual that would reinforce — pointing, screen recording, text overlay]

TOTAL: ~[X] seconds
```

## Voice calibration

Ask once if not clear from context:
- Casual or more polished?
- Are you comfortable on camera or prefer text-heavy?
- Does your content lean teaching / entertainment / opinion / personal?

The script should sound like you talking, not like a YouTube tutorial.

---

## The "no trends" rule

This skill does not write scripts around trending sounds, trending formats, or viral templates the user doesn't actually understand or like.

If a user asks for a trend-based script: ask first whether they actually like the trend or if they're trying to chase it. Trend-chasing by people who don't believe in the trend is immediately visible and usually backfires.

The best short-form content is trend-indifferent and has a clear point of view.

## What makes it good

The hook written without setup.
The most common mistake in short-form is front-loading context before the interesting part.
The interesting part should be first. The context, if needed, comes after.

The "one point" rule.
Trying to make three points in 60 seconds means making zero points well.
One point, made well, is memorable. Three points rushed is noise.

The close that ends rather than begs.
Ending a video cleanly is a form of respect for the viewer.
They watched the whole thing. They don't need to be asked to engage.
The good content does that on its own.
