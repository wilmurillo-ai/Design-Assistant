# LinkedIn Voice

LinkedIn has a voice. It's not yours.

"Humbled and grateful." "Excited to share." "This resonated deeply."
"I've learned more in the last year than in the previous decade combined."

This skill writes LinkedIn posts that sound like a person wrote them.

`/linkedin [what you want to post about]`
Or: "Write a LinkedIn post about X" / "Help me share this on LinkedIn"

## The LinkedIn problem

Most LinkedIn content fails in one of three ways:

**The Performance Post:**
Performed vulnerability. Performed gratitude. Performed humility.
The lesson is always the same. The emotion is always disproportionate to the event.
"I almost quit. Then I didn't. Here's what I learned."

**The List Post:**
"5 things I wish I knew at 25:"
The list is generic. The items are ones everyone already knows.
The formatting does all the work the content isn't doing.

**The Announcement With Feelings:**
"Excited to announce I've joined [COMPANY] as [TITLE]!"
Who is this for? The company? The algorithm? Not the reader.

---

## What good LinkedIn posts do

They say something the reader didn't already know.
Or they say something the reader knew but hadn't seen said this clearly.
Or they make the reader feel less alone in something they'd been thinking.

They sound like a person talking to another person, not a professional talking at an audience.

They're specific. The specific detail is always more interesting than the general principle.

## The approach

### Step 1 — Find the actual thing

Ask if not clear from the user's input:
- What happened / what did you observe / what do you think?
- Why does it matter? To whom?
- What's the specific detail that makes this real?

The specific detail is the difference between a good post and a generic one.
"I watched a junior developer fix a bug in 10 minutes that the senior team had been stuck on for two days. Here's what he did differently." is a post.
"Sometimes the most important voice in the room is the quietest one." is not a post.

### Step 2 — Pick the format

**The observation:**
Something you noticed that's worth sharing. Specific. Grounded.
Works for: industry insight, professional experience, something you saw.

**The story:**
Something that happened. Short. Specific. With a point that emerges naturally.
Works for: career moments, lessons from failure, things that changed how you think.

**The opinion:**
A position on something. Not hedged. Not "there are many ways to think about this."
Works for: controversial-ish takes on professional topics, disagreements with conventional wisdom.

**The resource:**
Something you built, learned, or found that's genuinely useful.
Works for: sharing work, tools, frameworks — when the thing itself is the point.

**The question:**
A genuine question you don't know the answer to.
Works for: when you actually want responses, not when you're using a question as a rhetorical device.

### Step 3 — Write the post

**Opening line:**
Not a hook. A first sentence.
The difference: a hook manipulates. A first sentence starts the conversation.

Good first sentences:
- "We made a mistake last year that cost us three months."
- "The best piece of feedback I ever received was also the harshest."
- "Something changed in how I run meetings after reading one paragraph."

Bad first sentences:
- "I've been thinking about this for a while now."
- "Unpopular opinion:"
- "Let me tell you about the day I almost gave up."

**Body:**
Say the thing. Be specific. Don't over-explain.
Short paragraphs. White space.
No bullet points unless the content is genuinely a list.

**Ending:**
End when you're done. No call to action unless it's genuine.
"What do you think?" is only worth asking if you actually want to know.

## Things this skill never writes

- "Humbled and grateful"
- "Excited to announce"
- "I've learned more in [period] than [comparison]"
- "This resonated deeply"
- "Dropping this here in case it helps someone"
- Lists of lessons that read like fortune cookies
- Fake vulnerability with a tidy lesson at the end
- "The algorithm won't show this to many people but..."
- Ending with "Agree? Disagree? Let me know in the comments."

---

## Voice calibration

If the user has shared previous LinkedIn posts: match the voice.
Ask if unclear: "How do you want this to sound — professional and precise, conversational, or somewhere in between?"

The post should be indistinguishable from something the user wrote on a good day.

## Post variants

`/linkedin story [what happened]` — narrative format
`/linkedin opinion [position]` — take a position
`/linkedin share [work or resource]` — share something you made or found
`/linkedin short [thought]` — under 150 words

---

## Management commands

- `/linkedin [topic or idea]` — write a post
- `/linkedin shorter` — cut it down
- `/linkedin less [adjective]` — "less formal", "less earnest", "less long"
- `/linkedin hook` — try different opening lines
- `/linkedin no list` — rewrite without bullet points

---

## What makes it good

Specificity is the whole game.
"I watched a junior developer fix a bug" is a post.
"Sometimes unexpected people have unexpected insights" is nothing.

The fake-vulnerability ban is real.
LinkedIn has trained people to perform emotion in exchange for engagement.
Posts that don't do this stand out immediately.

The skill should ask "what's the specific detail?" before writing anything.
Without the specific detail, the post will be generic.
With the specific detail, it might actually be worth reading.
