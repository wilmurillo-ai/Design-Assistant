# Citation Defense Template: Contesting S&S (Significant & Substantial) Findings
## Applicable to Any 30 CFR Part 56 Citation

**Use this template when:** Any citation has been marked S&S and you believe the finding is not warranted. This template supplements the citation-specific defense — it focuses specifically on the S&S legal test.

**Why S&S matters:** An S&S finding significantly increases the penalty and counts toward a "pattern of violations" (POV) determination. Multiple S&S citations can trigger POV status, which doubles penalties and increases scrutiny. Contesting unjustified S&S findings is critical to long-term enforcement posture.

---

## The Mathies Test (What MSHA Must Prove)

Under *Mathies Coal Co.*, 6 FMSHRC 1, 3-4 (1984), a violation is S&S if MSHA proves ALL FOUR elements:

1. **An underlying violation of a mandatory safety standard**
2. **A discrete safety hazard — a measure of danger to safety — contributed to by the violation**
3. **A reasonable likelihood that the hazard contributed to will result in an injury**
4. **A reasonable likelihood that the injury in question will be of a reasonably serious nature**

**Your defense attacks elements 3 and/or 4.** Elements 1 and 2 are usually conceded (the violation exists and creates some hazard). The fight is over whether injury was *reasonably likely* and whether it would be *reasonably serious*.

---

## Element 3 Defense: Injury Was Not Reasonably Likely

MSHA must show more than "injury is possible." They must show it was *reasonably likely* given the actual conditions. Consider:

### Exposure Factors
- **How many people were exposed?** One person briefly is different from an entire crew for a full shift.
- **How long did the condition exist?** A condition that existed for 5 minutes during a shift is different from one that persisted for weeks.
- **How often were people in the area?** A rarely-accessed location vs. a high-traffic walkway.

### Mitigating Controls
- **Were other protective measures in place?** Even if the cited control was absent, other controls may have reduced risk.
- **Was the area barricaded, signed, or otherwise controlled?**
- **Were affected employees aware of the condition and taking precautions?**

### Operational Context
- **Was the equipment running at the time?** A guarding citation on idle equipment is less likely to result in injury.
- **Was the condition actively being corrected?**
- **Were environmental conditions relevant?** (Lighting, weather, visibility)

**Template language:**

> While [PLANT NAME] acknowledges the cited condition, we respectfully submit that injury was not reasonably likely under the actual conditions at the time of the inspection. [Describe specific mitigating factors: limited exposure, short duration, other controls in place, equipment status, employee awareness]. The S&S analysis must be based on the conditions as they actually existed, not on a worst-case hypothetical. *See Newtown Energy, Inc.*, 38 FMSHRC 2033 (2016).

---

## Element 4 Defense: Injury Would Not Be Reasonably Serious

If injury is conceded as possible, you can still argue it would not be "reasonably serious." Consider:

- **What type of injury is realistically expected?** A bruise or scrape from a housekeeping trip hazard is not the same as a crush injury from unguarded equipment.
- **What is the energy involved?** Low-speed, low-force conditions produce less serious injuries.
- **What PPE was being worn?** Hard hat, steel toes, safety glasses, etc. reduce injury severity.

**Template language:**

> Even assuming some likelihood of injury, the reasonably expected injury from the cited condition would be [describe — minor laceration, bruise, strain], not the serious injury MSHA's S&S finding implies. The cited [CONDITION] involved [low energy / low speed / limited force], and the affected employee was wearing [PPE]. Under the Mathies test, the expected injury must be "reasonably serious" — the actual conditions here do not support that conclusion.

---

## Common MSHA Overreach on S&S (Arguments to Make)

1. **"Worst-case scenario" analysis:** MSHA sometimes bases S&S on the absolute worst possible outcome rather than the reasonably likely outcome. Push back: "The S&S analysis requires reasonable likelihood, not theoretical possibility."

2. **Assumption of no PPE:** Inspector assumes no PPE when PPE was actually being worn. Document what PPE was in use.

3. **Assumption of maximum exposure:** Inspector assumes continuous exposure when the actual exposure was brief or intermittent.

4. **Ignoring other controls:** Inspector cites one missing control while ignoring multiple other controls that were in place and functioning.

---

## Evidence Checklist for S&S Contest

- [ ] Photographs of the actual conditions (not just the violation, but the entire context)
- [ ] Exposure analysis: how many people, how long, how often
- [ ] Documentation of other controls that were in place
- [ ] PPE records for affected employees
- [ ] Equipment operating status at time of citation
- [ ] Duration the condition existed (if short — prove it with records)
- [ ] Employee statements about their awareness of the condition
- [ ] Similar conditions at other operations that were NOT cited as S&S (if available)
