# Notice of Violation (NOV) Response Guide

## Purpose
Step-by-step guidance for responding to a Notice of Violation from EPA, state, or local environmental regulatory agency for a cement manufacturing facility.

## Immediate Actions (Day 0-3)

- [ ] Review the NOV carefully and identify all cited violations, regulatory citations, and deadlines
- [ ] Notify corporate legal counsel and corporate environmental department immediately
- [ ] Notify plant manager, environmental manager, and relevant department heads
- [ ] Preserve all records, data, and documents related to the cited violations (litigation hold)
- [ ] Do NOT destroy, alter, or overwrite any CEMS data, logbooks, or electronic records
- [ ] Identify the responding agency contact and case number

## Assessment Phase (Day 3-10)

### Verify the Factual Basis
- [ ] Compare cited emission values or conditions against actual plant records
- [ ] Review CEMS data for the cited time periods
- [ ] Check if cited emission limits are correctly stated (units, averaging period, O2 correction)
- [ ] Verify applicable regulation citations are correct
- [ ] Determine if the violation actually occurred as described

### Assess Severity and Exposure
- [ ] Determine if the violation is an emission exceedance, monitoring failure, reporting failure, or permit condition violation
- [ ] Assess duration of the violation
- [ ] Determine if the violation is ongoing or has been corrected
- [ ] Review facility compliance history for repeat violations of the same type
- [ ] Estimate potential penalty exposure using EPA BEN and ABEL models or penalty policy

### Evaluate Defenses
- [ ] Was the event caused by an emergency or malfunction beyond the facility's control?
- [ ] Were all reasonable steps taken to minimize emissions during the event?
- [ ] Was proper notification given within required timeframes?
- [ ] Is there a factual error in the NOV that can be documented?
- [ ] Are there mitigating circumstances (e.g., first-time violation, good compliance history, voluntary disclosure)?

## Response Preparation (Day 10-20)

### Draft Response Letter
- [ ] Acknowledge receipt of the NOV
- [ ] State whether the facility agrees, disagrees, or partially agrees with each cited violation
- [ ] For agreed violations: describe root cause, corrective actions taken and planned, and timeline
- [ ] For disputed violations: provide factual basis for disagreement with supporting documentation
- [ ] Describe any mitigating factors
- [ ] Express commitment to compliance
- [ ] Have response reviewed by legal counsel before submission

### Supporting Documentation
- [ ] CEMS data records for cited periods
- [ ] Calibration and QA/QC records
- [ ] Maintenance logs
- [ ] Operator logbook entries
- [ ] Any relevant correspondence with regulatory agency
- [ ] Compliance history demonstrating good faith efforts
- [ ] Corrective action plan with milestones and responsible parties

## Response Submission

- [ ] Submit response by the deadline stated in the NOV (typically 15-30 days)
- [ ] Send via certified mail or other method providing delivery confirmation
- [ ] Keep copies of everything submitted
- [ ] Request a meeting with the agency if complex issues need discussion

## Post-Response Actions

- [ ] Implement all committed corrective actions on schedule
- [ ] Document completion of each corrective action
- [ ] Monitor for recurrence of the cited violations
- [ ] Prepare for potential follow-up inspection by the agency
- [ ] If penalty is proposed, engage in settlement negotiations through legal counsel
- [ ] Update compliance management system with lessons learned
- [ ] Consider Supplemental Environmental Project (SEP) as part of penalty negotiation

## Key Principles

1. **Respond on time.** Late response worsens the situation significantly.
2. **Be truthful.** False statements to a federal agency are a criminal offense.
3. **Be cooperative but strategic.** Work with the agency but protect your rights.
4. **Fix the problem.** Demonstrating prompt corrective action is the strongest mitigating factor.
5. **Document everything.** Maintain a complete record of all actions and communications.
6. **Involve legal counsel.** Environmental enforcement can carry significant civil and criminal penalties.
