COVERAGE STRATEGY
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Start wide to lock geography (7-1, 7-2). The coffee shop must feel like a contained space —
the audience needs to understand the exits and the witnesses before we trap them in close-ups.

Build the dialogue coverage from both OTS positions (7-3, 7-4) first, then push into close-ups
(7-5, 7-6). The close-ups are where this scene lives — Anna's certainty and Marcus's cracking
composure are the performance. Get multiple takes.

The physical beat (Marcus standing 7-7, Anna's hand 7-8) needs careful staging — confirm blocking
with actors before rolling. The wrist grab insert (7-8) may require a separate camera position
lower than the main setup. Don't rush this — it's the dramatic hinge.

Save the background witness shots (7-12, 7-13) for after principal coverage is locked. Background
can be slower to work with, and you don't need them to tell the story.

B and C shots (7-11, 7-14, 7-15, 7-16, 7-17) are atmospheric. If running behind, cut C first,
then B. The scene can be cut entirely from A shots.

LIGHTING CONSIDERATIONS
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Day interior. Work with the coffee shop's practical lighting (overhead warm lights) as much as
possible — augment rather than replace. A soft key light on Anna from camera-left, matching the
window direction. Marcus can afford slightly harder lighting to emphasize his discomfort.

IMPORTANT: If the location has windows, the daylight will shift through the shoot day. Lock the
look early and check against it hourly. A hard noon sun coming through a south-facing window will
blow out your exposure by 11am.

Recommend: shoot the tight close-ups (7-5, 7-6, 7-9, 7-10) in the middle of the day when outdoor
light is most stable and controllable.
