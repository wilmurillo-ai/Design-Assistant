# P3-Derived Language Flow Checklist

Use this checklist to revise a complete research-paper Introduction one step at a time. The focus is language and organization rather than scientific content. The main checks are:

1. Preserve content and diagnose the original flow.
2. Choose verb tenses according to meaning and current relevance.
3. Link sentences explicitly through overlap, pro-forms, close joining, or clear signals.
4. Use transition signals only when the logical relation is accurate.
5. Choose passive, active, or non-human subjects according to style and claim ownership.
6. Shape paragraphs around one clear function with informative entry sentences.
7. Integrate the final Introduction for coherence and consistency.

## Step 1: Preserve Content and Diagnose Language Flow

### Check

- Segment the Introduction into paragraphs and sentences.
- Identify each paragraph's role: background, prior research, gap, present work, contribution, or paper organization.
- Mark all claims that must be preserved: citations, numbers, author names, dates, technical terms, abbreviations, variables, and quoted text.
- Diagnose, but do not yet substantially rewrite, tense problems, weak links between sentences, ambiguous pronouns, overused transitions, unstable `we/our`, and paragraph-focus problems.

### Revision Strategy

- Keep the draft close to the original unless light formatting cleanup is necessary.
- Create a diagnostic map so later edits remain traceable.
- Record any source ambiguity instead of silently changing meaning.

### Examples

| Original-style text | Step-1 diagnosis | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| `Smith found that X increases Y. This is important.` | `This` is ambiguous unless it clearly refers to the finding. | Ambiguous pro-forms weaken sentence linkage. |
| `Many studies investigated X. Therefore, Y remains unclear.` | `Therefore` may be illogical if no causal relation is stated. | Result signals require an identifiable cause. |
| `We know that X is difficult. We evaluated a new method.` | `We` may shift from people generally to the paper's authors. | Claim ownership may become unstable. |
| `The method was proposed. It improves accuracy.` | Check whether `improves` is established fact, reported finding, or current claim. | Tense choice changes evidential meaning. |
| A 320-word paragraph moves from background to aims. | Possible split point before the aim statement. | One paragraph may be carrying multiple functions. |

## Step 2: Revise Verb Tense Choices

### Check

- Treat tense as a meaning choice, especially when describing previous research.
- Use past simple when reporting what a particular study did or found without presenting it as a current established truth.
- Use present simple for established facts, accepted generalizations, definitions, current paper statements, and claims the writer presents as generally valid.
- Use present perfect for research trends, accumulated work, or current gaps that remain relevant now.
- Do not move a sentence into present simple if doing so overstates the reliability or generality of the finding.
- Do not move a current gap into past simple if the point is that the gap still exists.

### Revision Strategy

- Check every literature-review sentence for its function:
  - Study-bound finding: `Smith et al. reported that X increased Y.`
  - Accepted fact or definition: `X increases Y under Z conditions.`
  - Current research trend: `Recent studies have examined X in Y.`
  - Current gap: `Limited attention has been paid to X.`
- Explain every tense change in the method report.
- If the field's current consensus is uncertain and no target literature is provided, choose the more conservative tense.

### Examples

| Rough or problematic version | Step-2 revision pattern | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| `Smith et al. find that pressure increased as temperature rose.` | `Smith et al. found that pressure increased as temperature rose.` | Reports a specific completed study. |
| `Smith et al. found that pressure increases as temperature rises.` | Keep as present simple only if the finding is treated as established. | Present simple presents the relation as generally valid. |
| `Little attention was paid to X.` | `Little attention has been paid to X.` | Present perfect frames the gap as still relevant. |
| `Several studies examined X recently.` | `Several recent studies have examined X.` | Present perfect emphasizes accumulated recent research. |
| `The algorithm reduced noise in all images.` | `The algorithm reduced noise in the tested images.` | Keeps a study-bound result from becoming universal. |
| `This study investigated X.` | `This study investigates X.` | Introductions commonly describe the present paper in present tense. |
| `It is unclear how X affected Y.` | `It remains unclear how X affects Y.` | Signals a current unresolved issue if the source supports it. |

## Step 3: Strengthen Sentence-to-Sentence Linkage

### Check

- Inspect the beginning of each sentence and ask how it connects to the previous sentence.
- Use overlap linkage by repeating a key term early in the next sentence when clarity is needed.
- Use pro-forms such as `this method`, `these systems`, or `such an approach`, but keep the noun consistent with the original term.
- Avoid bare `this`, `these`, `it`, or `they` when the referent could be unclear.
- Join two very closely related sentences with a semicolon only when the combined sentence remains readable.
- Monitor sentence length. Very long sentences reduce readability; in STEMM writing, a moderate average length is usually easier to follow than repeated long sentences.

### Revision Strategy

- Prefer lexical overlap for important concepts.
- Replace ambiguous pronouns with explicit noun phrases.
- Use semicolons sparingly for cause-result, explanation, or continuation when two short sentences are too tightly connected for a full stop.
- Split overloaded sentences before adding more transitions.

### Examples

| Weak linkage | Step-3 revision pattern | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| `Deep models require large datasets. This limits use in clinics.` | `Deep models require large datasets. This requirement limits their use in clinics.` | Clarifies what `this` refers to. |
| `The framework combines A and B. It improves robustness.` | `The framework combines A and B. This framework is designed to improve robustness.` | Keeps the referent explicit and avoids unsupported certainty if needed. |
| `Several sensors collect signals. These methods are inexpensive.` | `Several sensors collect signals. These sensors are inexpensive.` | Uses the same noun category instead of shifting from sensors to methods. |
| `The model failed on noisy data. The training set was small.` | `The model failed on noisy data; the training set was small.` | Semicolon signals a close explanatory link if the relation is clear. |
| `X affects Y. Y affects Z. Z determines performance.` | `X affects Y, and Y in turn affects Z. This relationship can determine performance.` | Builds a chain of repeated terms. |
| `This is important.` | `This limitation is important because it affects the reliability of Y.` | Names the referent and explains relevance. |
| `They are easily detected.` after several plural nouns | `These impurities are easily detected.` | Removes pronoun ambiguity. |

## Step 4: Correct Transition Signals and Logical Relations

### Check

- Check every explicit signal word and classify its function:
  - Cause: `because`, `due to`, `as a result of`, `in view of`.
  - Result: `therefore`, `consequently`, `hence`, `thus`, `as a result`.
  - Contrast or difference: `however`, `whereas`, `while`, `by contrast`, `in contrast`.
  - Unexpectedness or concession: `although`, `even though`, `despite`, `nevertheless`, `nonetheless`, `yet`.
  - Addition or listing: `in addition`, `moreover`, `furthermore`, `also`, `second`.
  - Topic transition: `with regard to`, `regarding`, `with respect to`, `turning to`.
- Use `therefore` or `consequently` only when the result follows from a clear cause.
- Use `moreover` only when the new sentence performs the same function as the previous sentence.
- Use `although`, `despite`, or `nevertheless` when the point is unexpected relative to the previous information.
- Avoid starting every sentence with a signal; repeated explicit signals can make prose jerky.
- Avoid `as`, `since`, and `while` when their time meanings could confuse the intended relation.
- Do not use `conversely` or `on the contrary` for simple difference unless the second point is truly opposite.

### Revision Strategy

- Replace inaccurate signals with the signal matching the real relation.
- Delete a signal when lexical overlap or sentence order already makes the relation clear.
- Add missing information before a result signal if the causal link exists only in the writer's mind.
- Prefer repetition linkage when a signal would be formulaic.

### Examples

| Problematic signal | Step-4 revision pattern | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| `X is inexpensive. Therefore, Y is accurate.` | `X is inexpensive. In addition, Y is accurate.` | Cost does not directly cause accuracy. |
| `The method is accurate. Moreover, it is robust.` | Keep `Moreover` if both sentences list advantages. | The second sentence adds the same type of information. |
| `The method is accurate. Moreover, it fails on noisy data.` | `The method is accurate; however, it fails on noisy data.` | The second sentence contrasts with the first. |
| `While X was tested, Y improved.` where time and contrast are both possible | `Whereas X was tested under A conditions, Y was tested under B conditions.` | Avoids ambiguity when contrast is intended. |
| `Since the model was trained, accuracy improved.` when cause is intended | `Because the model was trained on larger datasets, accuracy improved.` | Avoids confusion between time and cause. |
| `Some methods use A; conversely, other methods use B.` | `Some methods use A; by contrast, other methods use B.` | `Conversely` implies an opposite relation, not just difference. |
| `However` begins three consecutive sentences. | Keep only the signal that marks the real contrast and use overlap for the others. | Prevents over-emphatic, mechanical flow. |

## Step 5: Refine Passive/Active and Subject Choices

### Check

- Identify sentences near the end of the Introduction that state what the paper does or presents.
- Decide whether each sentence should use:
  - Authorial active: `we investigate`, `we evaluate`, `our results suggest`.
  - Passive: `X was measured`, `Y was evaluated`.
  - Non-human subject: `this study investigates`, `this paper presents`, `Section 2 reviews`.
- Check the target journal or nearby target articles if provided. Some fields prefer more passive style; others accept `we`.
- Keep `we/our` referents stable. Avoid switching among the authors, the field, and people generally.
- Use passive when the actor is obvious, irrelevant, or less important than the process or object.
- Use non-human subjects when they clarify claim ownership without overusing `we`.

### Revision Strategy

- Replace general-human `we know` with `it is known that`, `previous research suggests that`, or a specific cited claim.
- Replace unstable `our knowledge` with `current understanding`, `the existing literature`, or `to the best of our knowledge` only when appropriate.
- Use `this study` or `the present work` for aims and contributions when the journal style is unknown.
- Keep active voice for author actions when ownership is useful and consistent.

### Examples

| Rough or risky version | Step-5 revision pattern | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| `We know that X affects Y.` | `It is generally accepted that X affects Y.` | Avoids `we` meaning everyone. |
| `We investigated X. We know little about Y.` | `We investigated X. Current understanding of Y remains limited.` | Keeps authorial `we` separate from field-wide knowledge. |
| `The experiment was performed to evaluate X.` | `We performed the experiment to evaluate X.` | Active voice is clearer if author action matters and style permits. |
| `We present a model. We review previous work. We organize the paper as follows.` | `This paper presents a model, reviews previous work, and is organized as follows.` | Non-human subject reduces repetitive `we`. |
| `A new algorithm is proposed in this paper.` | `This paper proposes an algorithm for X.` | Avoids heavy passive and gives the paper clear agency. |
| `Our method is considered effective.` | `The method is designed to improve X.` | Avoids vague passive attribution and unsupported evaluation. |
| `Section 2 is reviewed.` | `Section 2 reviews existing methods.` | Uses a non-human subject naturally. |

## Step 6: Improve Paragraphing and Entry Sentences

### Check

- Each paragraph should have one dominant function.
- The first sentence should help the reader predict the paragraph's topic, function, or direction.
- Avoid clusters of single-sentence paragraphs unless the genre clearly expects them.
- Avoid overlong paragraphs that shift from one idea to another without a clear break.
- As a practical reference point, research-article paragraphs often cluster around 150-170 words; many paragraphs below 80 words or above 230 words may signal a paragraphing problem. Treat this as guidance, not a rigid rule.
- Check whether the first sentences of all paragraphs form a readable map of the Introduction.

### Revision Strategy

- Merge short paragraphs that share the same function.
- Split long paragraphs when the topic, function, or rhetorical move changes.
- Add or revise entry sentences so each paragraph opens with a clear focus.
- Keep paragraph order logical for the reader: background, prior research, gap, present work, and optional organization.
- Use entry phrases when useful:
  - `An alternative approach is...`
  - `Another key feature is...`
  - `In order to examine this issue,...`
  - `On the basis of this finding,...`
  - `The first evidence for this is...`
  - `This distinction is important because...`
  - `To address this question,...`
  - `With regard to...`

### Examples

| Paragraphing issue | Step-6 revision pattern | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Three one-sentence paragraphs all describe prior methods. | Merge them into one paragraph with an entry sentence: `Previous studies have developed three main approaches to X.` | Creates one coherent literature unit. |
| A long paragraph begins with background and ends with the current study's aim. | Split before the aim: `To address this limitation, this study...` | Separates gap/background from present work. |
| A paragraph starts with a detail before the topic is clear. | Begin with `A major limitation of these approaches is X.` and then give the detail. | Gives the reader a paragraph map. |
| The first sentence says `This is also important.` | Revise to `This limitation is important because it affects Y.` | Makes the paragraph focus explicit. |
| A paragraph contains method comparison plus study objectives. | Keep method comparison in one paragraph and move objectives to a new final paragraph. | Preserves one function per paragraph. |
| First sentences do not form a readable skim map. | Revise paragraph openings so they state topic progression. | Supports reader skimming and section coherence. |
| A paragraph lists unrelated facts. | Group facts by concept and move unrelated material to a more appropriate paragraph. | Reduces information dumping. |

## Step 7: Integrate Final Flow and Consistency

### Check

- Read the revised Introduction from start to finish and ensure it flows as a complete section.
- Check that tense choices remain consistent after linkage and paragraph edits.
- Check that signal words still match their logical relations after sentences have moved.
- Check that `this`, `these`, `it`, and `they` still have clear referents.
- Check that every paragraph has one dominant function and an informative opening.
- Check that the final present-work paragraph clearly states what the paper does without changing the source claim strength.

### Revision Strategy

- Remove duplicated wording created by overlap linkage.
- Replace excessive explicit signals with repeated key nouns where possible.
- Keep citations next to the claims they support.
- Align terminology across paragraphs.
- Verify that the final document contains only the revised Introduction, while the method report preserves all step drafts, reasons, and examples.

### Examples

| Final-flow issue | Step-7 revision pattern | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| `This approach` appears after two possible approaches. | `The graph-based approach...` | Names the referent precisely. |
| Present perfect gap sentence was moved after present-work sentence. | Move the gap before the present-work sentence or revise the transition. | Maintains Introduction logic. |
| A paragraph has `however` after a sentence that already uses `although`. | Delete one contrast marker or restructure. | Avoids double-signalling the same relation. |
| Current-study sentences alternate between `we`, `this study`, and passive. | Choose one dominant convention and revise for consistency. | Improves claim ownership and style. |
| First sentences produce this skim map: background, aim, literature, gap. | Reorder or revise paragraphs to background, literature, gap, aim. | Restores reader-friendly progression. |
| A revised sentence says `proves` where the source said `suggests`. | Restore `suggests` or another appropriately hedged verb. | Preserves evidence strength. |
| The final document includes examples or comments. | Remove them from the revised-document file and keep them only in the method report. | Maintains the required two-output separation. |
