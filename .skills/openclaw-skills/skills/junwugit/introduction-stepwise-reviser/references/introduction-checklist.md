# P2-Derived Introduction Checklist

Use this checklist to revise a complete research-paper Introduction one step at a time. The central progression is:

1. Establish the significance of the research area.
2. Describe previous and/or current research with precise contribution verbs.
3. Identify the gap, problem, limitation, or research opportunity.
4. State what the present paper does and why the approach is useful.
5. Integrate the draft so the Introduction reads as one coherent section.

## Step 1: Source Preservation and Rhetorical Map

### Check

- Segment the Introduction into paragraphs and, where helpful, sentences.
- Identify each paragraph's main rhetorical role: background, importance, prior research, limitation, opportunity, present study, contribution, or paper organization.
- Mark citations, numbers, named methods, technical terms, and claims that must be preserved exactly unless grammar requires a local adjustment.
- Do not rewrite substantively in this step; create a stable baseline for later revisions.

### Revision Strategy

- Normalize only obvious formatting issues if needed.
- Keep the original wording as the draft after this step unless structural cleanup is necessary.
- Use this map to prevent later edits from adding unsupported claims or removing necessary context.

### Examples

| Original-style text | Step-1 handling | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| `Deep learning is used in medical imaging. Many studies discuss segmentation.` | Label as significance plus prior-research material. | The first sentence signals the field; the second begins literature context. |
| `However, the method is slow and not stable.` | Label as gap/problem. | `However` and the negative evaluation indicate a limitation. |
| `This paper proposes a lightweight model for edge devices.` | Label as present-work statement. | The sentence names the current paper and its action. |
| `Smith et al. (2021) measured X in 120 samples.` | Preserve author, year, verb, number, and object. | Citation and quantitative scope are evidence-bearing details. |
| `The rest of this paper is organized as follows.` | Label as optional organization sentence. | This belongs after the present-work move if retained. |

## Step 2: Establish Significance

### Check

- The opening should make the topic's importance clear before moving into narrow methods or results.
- Significance can be based on prevalence, growing interest, practical value, theoretical importance, technical usefulness, social relevance, or an unresolved challenge.
- Avoid empty claims such as `very important` unless the sentence explains why.
- Do not invent dates, statistics, or global scope. Use only evidence already present in the source.

### Revision Strategy

- Strengthen the first paragraph by turning vague importance claims into specific context statements.
- Use topic-first sentence patterns such as:
  - `X plays a central role in Y.`
  - `X has become an important approach for Y.`
  - `Recent work has increasingly focused on X because Y.`
  - `X remains a major challenge in Y.`
  - `The rapid development of X has created new opportunities for Y.`
- If the source gives statistics, keep the numbers and add the date only if the source provides it.

### Examples

| Weak or rough version | Step-2 revision pattern | Why it improves the Introduction |
|---|---|---|
| `X is important in many areas.` | `X plays a central role in several areas of Y, including A and B.` | Replaces vague importance with a clearer domain and examples. |
| `People have studied X for years.` | `X has attracted sustained research attention because it influences Y.` | Links research attention to a concrete reason. |
| `There are more applications of X now.` | `The use of X has increased in recent years as Y has become more widely adopted.` | Connects growth to an observable driver without overclaiming. |
| `Y is a problem for industry.` | `Y remains a practical challenge for industry because it affects cost, reliability, or scalability.` | Makes the significance explicit and functional. |
| `This technology is popular.` | `This technology has become a widely used approach for Y due to its efficiency and flexibility.` | Converts a casual claim into academic phrasing, provided the source supports the advantages. |
| `The study of X is useful.` | `Understanding X is essential for explaining Y and improving Z.` | Clarifies the scientific purpose of studying X. |

## Step 3: Synthesize Previous and Current Research

### Check

- Prior literature should be described by what researchers actually did or contributed.
- Replace repetitive generic verbs such as `did`, `talked about`, `looked at`, `showed`, or `found` when a more precise verb fits.
- Group studies by method, object, contribution, or limitation where possible.
- Preserve citations and avoid implying consensus, priority, or causality unless the source supports it.

### Revision Strategy

- Use precise research-activity verbs:
  - Investigation: `examined`, `investigated`, `explored`, `assessed`, `evaluated`.
  - Method or system creation: `developed`, `designed`, `introduced`, `proposed`, `implemented`.
  - Measurement and analysis: `measured`, `calculated`, `estimated`, `modelled`, `monitored`.
  - Evidence and interpretation: `demonstrated`, `revealed`, `reported`, `identified`, `confirmed`, `suggested`.
  - Review and synthesis: `reviewed`, `compared`, `categorized`, `discussed`.
- When several studies share a function, synthesize them instead of listing them mechanically.

### Examples

| Rough version | Step-3 revision pattern | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| `Many researchers did work on X.` | `Previous studies have examined X from the perspectives of A, B, and C.` | Replaces an empty verb with a meaningful research action and grouping. |
| `Smith showed a method for detecting Y.` | `Smith et al. proposed a method for detecting Y.` | `Proposed` describes a methodological contribution more precisely. |
| `Lee found the performance of the system.` | `Lee et al. evaluated the system's performance under Z conditions.` | Clarifies the action and the evaluation context. |
| `Several papers talked about the problem.` | `Several studies have discussed the causes of the problem and assessed possible solutions.` | Uses contribution verbs and specifies the literature's focus. |
| `They used data to get results.` | `They analyzed dataset A to estimate B.` | Identifies the method and output instead of using generic wording. |
| `Recent studies show X is better.` | `Recent studies suggest that X can improve Y under specified conditions.` | Preserves caution and avoids overgeneralization. |
| `Research has many methods.` | `Existing approaches can be broadly categorized into rule-based, statistical, and learning-based methods.` | Creates a useful synthesis frame. |

## Step 4: Articulate the Gap, Problem, or Research Opportunity

### Check

- The Introduction should explain what remains incomplete, unclear, inefficient, disputed, insufficiently studied, or practically difficult.
- Decide whether the gap is a criticism of existing work, a limitation in scope, an unresolved question, a methodological obstacle, or a research opportunity.
- Use contrast signals carefully: `however`, `although`, `while`, `despite`, `nevertheless`.
- Use modal language for opportunities when appropriate: `may`, `might`, `could`, `would`.
- Avoid unsupported claims such as `no studies have...` unless the source explicitly justifies that scope.

### Revision Strategy

- Convert vague dissatisfaction into a specific limitation:
  - `However, existing approaches remain limited by X.`
  - `Despite these advances, Y is not yet well understood.`
  - `Few studies have examined X in the context of Y.`
  - `The role of X in Y remains unclear.`
  - `This limitation raises the question of whether X can Z.`
  - `Further work is needed to clarify X.`
- If the source provides no defensible gap, state the limitation conservatively in the method report and avoid inventing one in the final text.

### Examples

| Rough version | Step-4 revision pattern | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| `But the old methods are bad.` | `However, existing methods remain limited by high computational cost and unstable performance.` | Replaces unsupported judgment with specific limitations. |
| `Nobody has studied this.` | `To date, limited work has examined this issue in Y settings.` | Avoids an absolute claim unless the evidence supports it. |
| `The mechanism is not clear.` | `The mechanism by which X affects Y remains unclear.` | Specifies the relation that is unclear. |
| `This area needs more work.` | `Further work is needed to determine whether X generalizes to Y.` | Turns a generic need into a testable opportunity. |
| `Previous approaches cannot solve it.` | `Previous approaches have not fully addressed X, particularly under Y conditions.` | Narrows the limitation and avoids overstatement. |
| `The results are different.` | `Although prior studies report inconsistent results, the sources of this variation remain underexplored.` | Connects inconsistency to a research opportunity. |
| `The method is slow.` | `The method can be time-consuming when applied to large-scale datasets.` | Adds condition and scope rather than making a blanket criticism. |

## Step 5: State the Present Work

### Check

- The Introduction should clearly state what the present paper does: objective, object of study, method or approach, and expected contribution.
- The present-work statement should respond directly to the gap or problem established in Step 4.
- Use `this study`, `this paper`, `the present work`, or `we` consistently with the target journal style and the user's original convention.
- Do not claim novelty, superiority, robustness, accuracy, or success unless the source states and supports it.

### Revision Strategy

- Use action-forward patterns:
  - `To address this gap, this study investigates X.`
  - `This paper proposes a method for Y.`
  - `We evaluate X using Y.`
  - `The objective of this study is to determine whether X affects Y.`
  - `The present work compares A and B under C conditions.`
  - `This approach enables Y by Z.`
- If the source includes a paper-organization sentence, place it after the aims and contribution statement.

### Examples

| Rough version | Step-5 revision pattern | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| `So we did this study.` | `To address this gap, this study investigates X in Y.` | Links the study directly to the gap and states the object. |
| `Our paper talks about a model.` | `This paper proposes a model for predicting Y from X.` | Replaces conversational wording with a precise contribution. |
| `We want to know if it works.` | `We evaluate whether X improves Y under Z conditions.` | States the evaluative objective and context. |
| `The study has a new method.` | `The present work develops a method that combines A with B.` | Explains the method without unsupported novelty language. |
| `This method is better.` | `The method is designed to improve Y while reducing Z.` | States intended advantage without claiming proven superiority unless results support it. |
| `This paper includes four parts.` | `The remainder of the paper is organized as follows: Section 2 describes A, Section 3 presents B, and Section 4 discusses C.` | Makes the organization sentence concrete. |
| `We solve the problem.` | `We seek to address this problem by developing and evaluating X.` | Avoids overclaiming before results are presented. |

## Step 6: Integrate Flow and Final Consistency

### Check

- The revised Introduction should move logically from broad significance to prior research, from prior research to the gap, and from the gap to the present work.
- Transitions should clarify relationships without adding claims.
- Tense should match function: established knowledge often uses present tense; specific completed studies often use past tense or present perfect; the present paper often uses present tense.
- Citations should remain attached to the claims they support.
- The final text should read as a complete Introduction, not a checklist.

### Revision Strategy

- Smooth paragraph openings and endings.
- Remove redundant claims created by stepwise revision.
- Align key terms across the Introduction.
- Keep hedging and certainty levels consistent.
- Ensure the gap and present-work statements use the same problem frame.

### Examples

| Draft issue | Step-6 revision pattern | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| `X is important. X is also useful. X has many benefits.` | `X plays an important role in Y because it supports A and B.` | Consolidates repetition into one focused significance sentence. |
| `Many studies examined X. However, this paper studies X.` | `Although previous studies have examined X, its role in Y remains unclear. To address this gap, this paper investigates...` | Creates a clear literature-to-gap-to-study sequence. |
| `Smith proposed A. Lee proposed B. Wang proposed C. The gap is Y.` | `Prior work has proposed A, B, and C; however, these approaches have not fully addressed Y.` | Synthesizes a list and connects it to the gap. |
| `This study will show X.` | `This study examines whether X is associated with Y.` | Avoids promising a result in the Introduction unless already established. |
| `The current research proposed...` | `This study proposes...` | Uses consistent present-work phrasing. |
| `However` appears at every transition. | Use varied transitions such as `despite these advances`, `nevertheless`, or `while prior work...`. | Improves flow while preserving contrast. |
| A citation was moved away from its claim. | Reattach the citation to the sentence containing the supported claim. | Maintains evidence integrity. |
