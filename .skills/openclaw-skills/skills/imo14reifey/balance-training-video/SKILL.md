# Physical Therapy Clinic Video Marketing Skill

## Overview

This skill helps physical therapy practices, sports rehabilitation centers, orthopedic physical therapy clinics, neurological rehabilitation centers, and occupational therapy practices create video content that demonstrates clinical expertise, communicates care quality, and drives new patient referrals and direct patient inquiries. Physical therapy marketing faces a complex environment: most patients come through physician referrals, but direct access and patient-driven selection are growing rapidly. Video content that builds genuine trust with both potential patients and referring physicians strengthens both channels simultaneously.

## Industry Context

The US physical therapy market exceeds $50 billion annually with over 80,000 practices. Key market dynamics:
- Physician referral is still the primary patient source, but direct access is growing
- Patient satisfaction and outcomes directly affect online reviews and referral patterns
- Specialty areas (sports, pediatric, neurological, pelvic floor, vestibular) drive premium positioning
- Telehealth physical therapy emerged strongly post-2020
- The aging US population creates growing demand for orthopedic and balance-related PT

## Video Types for PT Clinics

### 1. Clinic Introduction and Environment Tour
The physical therapy space: equipment, treatment tables, exercise areas, waiting room. Reduces first-appointment anxiety for new patients.

### 2. Therapist Introduction
Each therapist's credentials, specialties, treatment philosophy. Patients increasingly research their therapist before or after referral.

### 3. What to Expect at Your First Appointment
Demystifying the PT intake process: the evaluation, the goal-setting conversation, what treatment might look like. Reduces no-shows and improves preparation.

### 4. Condition-Specific Treatment Education
Post-surgical rehabilitation, rotator cuff injury, lower back pain, ACL recovery. Condition content reaches patients who are researching their specific injury.

### 5. Exercise and Home Program Demonstrations
The exercises prescribed between sessions shown correctly. Improves home program compliance and reduces re-injury.

### 6. Patient Outcome Stories
Real patients describing their recovery journey: the injury, the prognosis they received, the work they did in PT, and where they are now.

### 7. Technology and Equipment Showcase
Specialized equipment: aquatic therapy pools, Pilates reformers, blood flow restriction therapy, dry needling. Shows clinical capabilities.

### 8. Sports Rehabilitation Content
ACL return-to-sport protocols, concussion rehabilitation, sports-specific movement patterns. Reaches athletes and coaches.

### 9. Pediatric Physical Therapy
Children's developmental conditions, torticollis, scoliosis monitoring. Specialized content for parents researching pediatric PT.

### 10. Referring Physician Content
Content specifically designed to educate and inform referring physicians about your specialties and approach. LinkedIn-appropriate professional content.

## Script Templates

### Template 1: First Appointment Guide (60 seconds)

**The Unknown (0-8s):**
[Therapist on camera, welcoming]
"If you have never been to physical therapy before, here is what to expect at your first appointment — so you can arrive prepared and confident."

**The Evaluation (8-32s):**
"Your first appointment is primarily a comprehensive evaluation. Your therapist will take a thorough history of your condition, when it started, what makes it better or worse, and what your goals are for recovery. They will then perform a physical examination — movement testing, strength assessment, and hands-on evaluation of the affected area."

**The Plan (32-50s):**
"At the end of your first visit, your therapist will explain what they found and what your treatment plan will look like. You will leave with a clear understanding of your diagnosis, your goals, your expected timeline, and what your home exercise program will include."

**CTA (50-60s):**
"[Clinic name]. [Website]. We accept most major insurance plans and offer direct access appointments without a referral."

### Template 2: ACL Recovery Patient Story (90 seconds)

**The Injury (0-15s):**
[Patient on camera — a high school or college athlete]
"I tore my ACL in September during a volleyball game. Non-contact — I planted and felt something give. I knew immediately."

**The Surgery and the Fear (15-35s):**
"Surgery was December. Before I started PT, I was scared about two things: the pain, and whether I would actually get back to 100 percent. My surgeon's words were 'most people get back to sport.' I needed more than 'most.'"

**The PT Journey (35-65s):**
"[Therapist name] worked with me from week two post-op through return to sport nine months later. The progression was clear — I always knew what I was working toward and why. The hardest part was the mental side. She helped with that too."

**The Return (65-80s):**
"I played my first collegiate game eleven months post-surgery. I had a double-double. There is a picture of me landing and pivoting that my dad framed — because the PT program specifically trained that landing mechanics pattern."

**CTA (80-90s):**
[Therapist on camera]
"We specialize in ACL and orthopedic sports rehabilitation. Free phone consultation to discuss your specific case. [Website]."

## Advanced Strategy: Building Physician Referral Relationships Through Content

The PT practice that builds genuine professional relationships with referring physicians grows sustainably:

Evidence-based content: Research summaries, clinical guidelines, outcome data relevant to conditions in which you specialize. This content on LinkedIn reaches physicians who appreciate clinical rigor.

Outcomes reporting: Sharing de-identified patient outcome data (with appropriate permissions) demonstrates clinical effectiveness to referring physicians who want to see evidence that their patients are well-served.

Specialty education: Deep-dive content on less-commonly understood specialties (pelvic floor PT, vestibular rehabilitation, dry needling) educates physicians about referral opportunities they may not be fully utilizing.

The PT practice that becomes a trusted clinical resource for referring physicians — not just a referral destination — generates the most loyal and highest-volume referral relationships.

## Specialty Spotlight Content Strategy

PT specialties create distinct audience segments:

Pelvic Floor Physical Therapy: One of the most underutilized PT specialties due to stigma and lack of awareness. Educational content that destigmatizes pelvic floor conditions (incontinence, pelvic pain, post-partum recovery, prolapse) reaches a massive underserved population. Video content specifically about pelvic floor PT conditions and treatments goes viral consistently in the women's health community.

Vestibular Rehabilitation: BPPV (benign paroxysmal positional vertigo) and other vestibular conditions are commonly mismanaged. Educational content explaining the Epley maneuver, dizziness causes and treatments, and the role of vestibular PT reaches patients who have been managed with medications without resolution.

Geriatric and Falls Prevention: The aging population is the fastest-growing PT market. Content about balance training, fall risk reduction, and functional independence for older adults reaches a large, medically-engaged audience and their adult children who often manage healthcare decisions.

Neurological Rehabilitation: Stroke recovery, Parkinson's disease, multiple sclerosis, traumatic brain injury. Content in this specialty reaches patients with long-term complex needs who become long-term engaged clients.

Pediatric PT: Developmental delays, torticollis, scoliosis monitoring, sports injuries in youth. Content for parents of children with these conditions reaches a highly motivated, research-intensive audience.

Each specialty has a distinct content voice and audience. Building a specialty content library creates multiple audience segments with distinct needs and referral networks.

## Patient Compliance and Outcomes

PT effectiveness depends significantly on patient compliance with home programs. Content improves outcomes:

Home exercise video library: Each exercise in your typical home programs filmed and available for patients to access. Reduces improper form, increases compliance, and demonstrates care continuity.

Progress tracking content: Helping patients understand how to self-assess improvement — range of motion milestones, strength testing, functional movement patterns — empowers patient engagement.

The compliance conversation: Educational content about why consistent home exercise matters to recovery outcomes. Patients who understand the evidence for home compliance do their exercises at higher rates.

Patient compliance improves outcomes. Better outcomes generate better reviews and more referrals. The practice that improves compliance through content creates a measurable clinical and business advantage.

## Insurance and Access Education

Many potential patients delay PT due to insurance confusion or cost concerns:

Insurance navigation content: How PT benefits typically work, what "deductible" and "co-pay" mean for PT access, how to check your benefits before your first appointment.

Direct access content: In most states, patients can access PT without a physician referral. Content explaining this directly generates new patient inquiries from people who did not realize they had immediate access.

Cash-pay value proposition: Content explaining the value of PT as a non-surgical alternative to conditions that might otherwise require expensive interventions. The comparison to the cost of surgery, injections, or long-term medication often makes PT the obvious value option.

Financial access content builds the relationship with patients who want PT but are uncertain about cost — reaching them before they decide PT is not affordable.

The compounding effect of content investment in physical therapy is particularly powerful because the content serves multiple audiences simultaneously: potential patients, current patients (compliance), referring physicians, and the wider professional community. Every piece of content does multiple jobs indefinitely.


## Measuring PT Practice Content ROI

Key metrics for PT video content effectiveness:

Referral source tracking: Ask every new patient how they heard about the practice. Separate 'physician referral' from 'physician referral after viewing our website/social media.' The latter is growing rapidly as physicians research PT practices online before referring.

Direct access patient percentage: Track the percentage of new patients who came without a physician referral. As content builds direct recognition, this percentage should grow.

New patient inquiry volume trend: Baseline before consistent content, then track monthly after starting. 6-12 months of consistent content should show measurable inquiry growth.

Patient review correlation: Clinics with high-quality outcome-focused video content typically receive more and better online reviews, because patients arrive with appropriate expectations and leave impressed. Track star rating and review volume alongside content activity.

Return referral rate from specific physicians: If a specific physician increases their referrals after you start publishing evidence-based clinical content on LinkedIn, that is an attributable content effect worth tracking.

The PT practice that measures these metrics and attributes them to specific content investments makes better content decisions and builds a continuously improving marketing operation.

## The Community Health Resource

The physical therapy practice that positions itself as the community health education resource builds the most sustainable brand:

Community education events: Workshops on back pain prevention, sports injury risk reduction, falls prevention for seniors. These events generate content and build community relationships simultaneously.

School and team partnerships: High school sports team partnerships generate consistent student-athlete referrals. Content documenting these partnerships reaches the parent community.

Corporate wellness: Ergonomic assessment, back pain prevention workshops for corporate clients. Content about these programs reaches HR and operations professionals.

Public health content: Seasonal injury prevention (shoveling injuries, repetitive strain from home projects, gardening injuries), age-appropriate exercise guidance, condition awareness campaigns.

The PT practice seen as the community health resource generates trust that converts indefinitely. When a community member needs PT — for themselves or for someone they love — the practice they have been following for health education is the first call they make.
