# Coding Bootcamp Video — AI Video Marketing for Coding Bootcamps & Tech Training Programs

The coding bootcamp value proposition is simultaneously the most compelling and the most difficult to communicate in education marketing: a 12-16 week program that turns a teacher, a barista, or a marketing manager into a software engineer earning $75,000+. The evidence for this transformation exists — graduate stories, hiring partner relationships, placement statistics — but statistics require trust that hasn't been established yet. Video creates that trust. The graduate who sits on camera and says "I was making $38,000 as a retail manager. I graduated 6 months ago. I'm a frontend engineer making $82,000 and I love going to work" converts ten times more effectively than a placement rate statistic. Coding Bootcamp Video captures these stories and turns them into enrollment.

## 1. Industry Context

### Market Size & Landscape
- The U.S. coding bootcamp market generates approximately **$1.2 billion** in annual revenue (Course Report 2025), with **600+ active bootcamp programs** across all formats.
- Approximately **33,000 students** graduate from U.S. coding bootcamps annually, with market growth of **8-12% YoY** despite increased competition from online learning platforms.
- Average full-time bootcamp tuition: **$13,500-$16,000**; premium programs: **$17,000-$20,000+**; part-time and online programs: **$7,000-$12,000**.
- **Income share agreements (ISAs)** allow graduates to defer tuition until employed — programs with ISAs convert price-sensitive applicants at significantly higher rates and require specific marketing video to explain the model.
- The average coding bootcamp graduate **salary uplift**: from approximately **$40,000** pre-bootcamp to **$68,000-$85,000** post-bootcamp, representing a 70-100% salary increase — the most compelling ROI story in education.
- **Job placement rates** vary from 60% to 95%+ across programs — this metric is the primary differentiator for informed applicants, and video that shows actual graduates in their new roles makes placement claims credible.
- The competitive landscape has intensified: **App Academy, Flatiron School, General Assembly, Hack Reactor, Lambda School (BloomTech), Galvanize**, and hundreds of regional programs compete for the same career-changer demographic.
- **Online-only bootcamps** accelerated dramatically during 2020-2022 and have permanently expanded the market — programs without strong video presence lose to online competitors who have built sophisticated video marketing funnels.
- **Employer partnerships** are increasingly the primary differentiator — bootcamps with named hiring partner relationships (Microsoft, Google, Accenture, regional tech employers) convert significantly better when those partnerships are shown in video.
- The **demographic shift**: bootcamp applicants skew older (average age: 28-32), more career-experienced, and more financially cautious than traditional university students. They require more evidence, more social proof, and more risk-reduction content — all best delivered through video.

### Why Video is Critical for Coding Bootcamp Marketing
1. **Career-changers need to see the transformation**: The applicant considering a coding bootcamp is betting their savings, their time, and potentially their family's stability on a program they've never experienced. A graduate who looks directly at the camera and says "It was the hardest thing I've done and the best decision I've made" is the most powerful sales tool a bootcamp can produce.
2. **Outcome credibility**: Job placement statistics are easy to inflate. Video of graduates in their new roles — filmed at their desks, describing their responsibilities, explaining what the bootcamp prepared them for — makes placement claims credible in a way that statistics cannot.
3. **Curriculum demonstration**: Applicants cannot evaluate code quality from a course description. Video showing actual student projects, code on a screen, a capstone demo day — these communicate technical rigor to the applicant (and the applicant's skeptical partner) who wants to know the program is serious.
4. **The partner conversation**: Many bootcamp applicants are making a decision with a spouse, parent, or partner who is also evaluating the risk. Video that addresses the skeptic directly — showing hiring partner companies, salary outcomes, and graduate confidence — converts the household, not just the applicant.
5. **Competitive differentiation**: The bootcamp applicant researches multiple programs simultaneously. The program with the most compelling video content — not the most detailed website — typically wins the application. Video is the conversion tool that moves an informed, motivated applicant from "considering" to "applied."
6. **ISA and financial model explanation**: Income share agreements are genuinely confusing to applicants. A clear, conversational video explaining how ISA works, when payments begin, what the cap is, and what happens if you don't get a job within a certain timeframe converts ISA-hesitant applicants who would otherwise default to tuition-required competitors.

### Customer Decision Journey
- **Trigger event**: Job dissatisfaction, layoff, career ceiling, exposure to a developer's work, salary research, friend who completed a bootcamp
- **Research phase**: Google search ("coding bootcamp [city]", "is coding bootcamp worth it") → Course Report / SwitchUp comparison → program website → video content review → admissions call
- **Decision timeline**: Average time from initial search to application submission: **4-8 weeks**; average time from application to enrollment decision: **1-3 weeks**
- **Decision factors**: (1) Job placement rate and salary outcomes, (2) Curriculum and technology stack, (3) Teaching quality and support, (4) Cost and financing options, (5) Format (full-time, part-time, in-person, online), (6) Alumni community and network
- **Where video intervenes**: Video content encountered during the research phase — especially graduate testimonials and outcome showcases — is the single highest-converting touchpoint in the bootcamp enrollment funnel. Programs that invest in video content report 40-60% higher inquiry rates than those without.

## 4. Script & Content Guidelines

### Coding Bootcamp-Specific Writing Rules

**DO:**
- Lead with the transformation, not the curriculum: "She was a middle school teacher earning $42,000. She's a backend engineer at a fintech startup earning $88,000. She's 31." The before/after story is more powerful than any description of what was taught.
- Use specific numbers: salary before and after, weeks to job offer, number of job applications before first offer, cohort size, placement rate with methodology disclosed. Specificity builds credibility; vagueness destroys it.
- Address the skeptic (often the applicant's partner or parent): "You're probably wondering whether this is real. Here's what our graduates are doing right now. Here are the companies they work for." The household decision requires the skeptic to be convinced.
- Show the work: actual student projects on screen, code in a terminal, a deployed web application — technical evidence that the program is serious.
- Acknowledge the difficulty honestly: "This is the hardest thing you'll do in 16 weeks. The people who succeed are the ones who come in knowing that and commit to it anyway." Honest difficulty acknowledgment reduces post-enrollment dropout rate.
- For ISA content, use plain language and be explicit about the downside scenario: "If you don't get a job within [X months], you owe nothing for [Y period]." ISA fear is usually rooted in not understanding what happens if things go wrong.

**DON'T:**
- Don't use average salary statistics that aren't clearly defined (median vs. mean, responders only vs. all graduates, specific timeframe). Inflated placement claims have damaged industry credibility across the board; your claims must be verifiable.
- Don't film testimonials where the graduate reads from a script — stilted, scripted testimonials actively undermine credibility. Let graduates speak in their own words.
- Don't neglect the technical credibility content — applicants with engineering backgrounds or partners with technical knowledge need to see that the curriculum is legitimate.
- Don't make curriculum-specific promises that depend on rapidly changing technology — tech stacks evolve; be careful about claims that lock the bootcamp to a specific version of tools that may be outdated.
- Don't target content exclusively at the career-changer — recent graduates, career switchers within tech, and professional development seekers represent distinct segments with distinct needs.

### Top Performing Content Formats

**The 90-second graduate transformation video**: Structure: (1) Where they started — job title, salary, frustration or ceiling [20 sec]; (2) Why they chose the bootcamp — specific reasons, fears acknowledged [20 sec]; (3) The experience — one thing that was harder than expected, one thing that was better [20 sec]; (4) Where they are now — job title, company, salary if willing, how they feel [30 sec]. This format, executed authentically, is the single highest-converting video asset a bootcamp can produce.

**The demo day showcase**: 90 seconds showing actual student projects from the most recent cohort — 4-5 projects, 15-20 seconds each, with the student explaining what they built and what problem it solves. Technical confidence on display. This content converts technically sophisticated applicants and convinces skeptical partners that the program produces real skills.

**"A week in the bootcamp" day-by-day content series**: Short-form content (30-45 seconds each, 5 episodes) showing Monday through Friday of a typical bootcamp week. Authentically captures the culture, the difficulty, the camaraderie, and the learning — the content that helps applicants self-select appropriately and reduces dropout from misaligned expectations.

**The hiring partner spotlight**: 60-90 seconds featuring a hiring manager from a recognizable company explaining why they recruit from the bootcamp, what skills graduates bring, and how they perform relative to traditional CS graduates. This format is extraordinarily powerful for B2B (corporate training) and for converting applicants who need employer credibility to justify the investment.

## 6. Compliance & Safety

### Education Consumer Protection
- The FTC has issued guidance on income share agreements and deferred tuition — ISA marketing must accurately represent terms, caps, and conditions.
- Job placement rate claims must use a clearly disclosed methodology. The coding bootcamp industry has faced significant scrutiny for inflated placement statistics; accurate, methodology-disclosed claims are both ethical and strategically superior.
- "Average salary" and "median salary" must be clearly distinguished and accurately sourced. Using median of self-reported respondents is legitimate only when disclosed as such.

### FERPA and Student Privacy
- Graduate testimonials require written consent for use of name, image, and career information in marketing.
- Salary information shared in testimonials must be explicitly consented to — some graduates are comfortable sharing salary ranges but not specific figures.

### State Licensing Requirements
- Coding bootcamps operate under varying state educational licensing requirements. Marketing representations about "degree-equivalent" education or transferable academic credit must be accurate per applicable state law.
- VA-approved programs must maintain compliance with VA educational benefit requirements in all marketing representations.

### Financial Aid Advertising
- Programs that accept Title IV federal student aid must comply with applicable regulations in marketing representations about financial aid availability.
- ISA terms must be disclosed fully and accurately, including all conditions that trigger payment obligations.
