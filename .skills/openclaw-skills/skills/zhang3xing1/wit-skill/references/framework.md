# WIT Framework

## Goal
Move from vague idea to practical decision in two stages:
1. Break the problem open
2. Make the decision

WIT is not generic brainstorming. It is a guided narrowing process.

---

## Recommended facilitation sequence

### Step 0 — Frame the session
Start by stating the immediate objective in one sentence:
- Are we validating whether this is a real problem?
- Are we trying to choose a direction?
- Are we trying to reduce scope?
- If this is a note integration / merge / rewrite request, what concrete decision is the merge supposed to support?

If the user is unclear, default to problem clarification first.

### Step 0.5 — Integration-intent check
Use this only when the user asks to merge, consolidate, rewrite, or fold one note into another.
Ask one tight question before doing the edit:
- Is this merge for reframing the product, narrowing v1, preparing a PRD, aligning language, or making a roadmap decision?

Purpose:
Prevent WIT from collapsing into pure note-moving when the real job is still decision clarification.

---

## Phase 1 — Break the problem open

### 1. Reality check
Ask:
- What exact problem do you believe exists?
- Who experiences it?
- How do you know it is real?
- What have you directly observed?
- What is still assumption?

Purpose:
Prevent solving an imagined problem.

### 2. Status quo
Ask:
- What do people do today instead?
- What workaround already exists?
- Why is that workaround insufficient?
- Is the pain frequent, expensive, risky, or emotionally sharp?

Purpose:
Ground the need in current behavior rather than abstract preference.

### 3. Specific painful scenario
Ask:
- In what exact moment does the problem hurt most?
- Who is most desperate for relief?
- What concrete job are they failing to get done?
- What is the smallest real-world scenario where this matters?

Purpose:
Avoid broad personas and find the sharpest entry point.

### 4. Framing check
Ask:
- If we ignore the proposed feature, what job is the user really hiring this for?
- Is the current framing too broad, too narrow, or pointed at the wrong object?
- Are we actually discussing a tool, a workflow, a service, or a system?
- What would be a more truthful one-sentence framing?

Purpose:
Challenge the first framing before committing to it.

### 5. Narrowest wedge
Ask:
- What is the smallest useful wedge into this problem?
- If only one slice could be solved first, which one creates the most leverage?
- What would make the first user say “this alone is worth it”?

Purpose:
Find an entry point small enough to build and test.

### 6. Observation and surprise
Ask:
- What has surprised you so far?
- What did users do that contradicted expectations?
- What facts changed your original idea?

Purpose:
Anchor thinking in reality instead of narrative.

### 7. Assumptions to test
Ask:
- Which assumption matters most?
- Which assumption is most uncertain?
- What can be tested fastest?
- What result would kill this idea?

Purpose:
Turn ambiguity into explicit validation targets.

### Phase 1 synthesis checkpoint
Before moving on, synthesize:
- the problem in one sentence
- the target user in one sentence
- the current status quo in one sentence
- the corrected framing in one sentence
- the wedge in one sentence
- the biggest assumption in one sentence

If these cannot be stated clearly, remain in Phase 1.

---

## Phase 2 — Make the decision

### 8. Worth doing now?
Ask:
- Why now?
- What happens if this waits?
- Relative to everything else, is this actually high priority?
- Is this the next best use of time, money, and attention?

Purpose:
Decide whether the opportunity deserves current resources.

### 9. Smallest viable move
Ask:
- What is the smallest version worth shipping or testing?
- What can be removed without destroying the core value?
- What is the first version, not the final vision?

Purpose:
Prevent overbuilding.

### 10. Explicit non-goals
Ask:
- What are we deliberately not doing now?
- What attractive but distracting ideas must be excluded?
- What will wait until the first wedge proves useful?

Purpose:
Control scope by naming exclusions.

### 11. Decision threshold
Ask:
- Do we already know enough to decide?
- Are we asking for more research because we need it, or because we fear commitment?
- What specific decision must be made in this round?

Purpose:
Convert open discussion into a decision point.

### 12. Execution handoff
Ask:
- What is the next concrete action?
- Who owns it?
- What evidence will tell us this round worked?
- What signal triggers the next revisit?

Purpose:
Make the decision operational.

### Phase 2 synthesis checkpoint
Before ending, state explicitly:
- the decision state
- the non-goals
- the next step
- the success signal

Do not end with “needs more thinking” unless a specific blocking unknown is named.

---

## Compression rule
If time is short, compress to these five prompts:
1. What real problem is observed?
2. What do people do today?
3. Is the current framing wrong?
4. What is the narrowest wedge?
5. What should we do now, and what are we not doing?

## Anti-patterns to catch
- Abstract market language with no concrete user or scene
- Broad platform ambition before one useful wedge exists
- Treating assumptions as facts
- Asking for more research without a decision threshold
- Ending with options but no choice
- Defending the user's first framing instead of testing it
