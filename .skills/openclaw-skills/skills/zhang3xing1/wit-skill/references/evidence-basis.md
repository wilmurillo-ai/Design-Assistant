# Evidence Basis

## Why this framework exists
WIT combines two different but complementary strengths:
- early-stage problem clarification
- disciplined decision and scope control

## What Phase 1 is based on
Phase 1 is based on discovery-style questioning similar to “office hours” style prompts:
- demand reality
- status quo
- desperate specificity
- narrowest wedge
- observation and surprise
- future-fit / assumption framing

The underlying logic:
- start from lived pain, not from a proposed solution
- separate observation from imagination
- reduce broad market narratives into a sharp first use case
- identify the key assumption before building too much

This phase protects against:
- fake demand
- overly abstract personas
- solving a story rather than a problem
- building broad systems before proving a narrow need

## What Phase 2 is based on
Phase 2 is based on decision-oriented product thinking in the style associated with 37signals:
- make decisions with enough information, not perfect information
- narrow scope aggressively
- define what not to do
- favor practical, shippable increments over grand plans
- reduce complexity before adding process

The underlying logic:
- once the problem is credible, the main risk becomes overbuilding and indecision
- product quality often improves when scope becomes smaller and clearer
- explicit non-goals are part of the decision, not a side note

This phase protects against:
- scope creep
- pseudo-research used to delay commitment
- premature platform thinking
- vague execution with no owner or next step

## Why the combination works
Used alone, discovery-style prompts can leave a team with good questions but no decision.
Used alone, decision-style prompts can assume the problem is already valid.

WIT therefore sequences them:
1. Verify the problem is real and sharp enough.
2. Then force a constrained decision about what to do now.

## Summary
WIT is designed to answer two different risks in order:
- **Risk 1: solving the wrong problem**
- **Risk 2: solving the right problem in the wrong way**
