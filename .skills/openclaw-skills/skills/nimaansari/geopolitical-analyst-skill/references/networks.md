# Actor Networks & Alliance Structures

## How to Use This File

In proxy conflicts, local actors are secondary. Analyze patron networks first — they hold the real escalation levers. Use this file to map the network behind a local conflict.

## Current Patron-Proxy Networks (Active 2024–2026)

### Iran — Regional Network

**Patron:** Islamic Republic of Iran (IRGC-Quds Force primary coordinator)
**Strategic objective:** Reduce US and Israeli military presence in the region; maintain influence through non-state partner forces; avoid direct state-to-state conventional war

| Actor | Location | Type | Capability | Current Status |
|-------|----------|------|-----------|----------------|
| Hezbollah | Lebanon | NSA (state-capacity) | HIGH — 150,000+ rockets, ground forces | Active — Lebanon front |
| Houthis (Ansar Allah) | Yemen | NSA | MEDIUM — drones, missiles, naval | Active — Red Sea campaign |
| Islamic Resistance in Iraq (PMF factions) | Iraq | NSA/PROXY | MEDIUM | Active intermittently |
| Hamas (partial) | Gaza/West Bank | NSA | MEDIUM | Active — reduced Iran coordination post-Oct 2023 |
| Palestinian Islamic Jihad | Gaza | NSA | LOW-MEDIUM | Iran-directed more than Hamas |
| Syrian government | Syria | STATE (client) | LOW (degraded) | Dependent on Iran/Russia |

**Network dynamic:** Iran coordinates but does not command. Each actor has local agenda. Hezbollah is most reliable; Houthis most autonomous.

**Escalation trigger:** Direct attack on Iranian territory or IRGC leadership = network-wide activation.

## Alliance Structures

### NATO (North Atlantic Treaty Organization)
**Article 5:** Attack on one = attack on all (collective defense)
**Current activation state:** Heightened — Ukraine war context
**Membership:** 32 states (North America + Europe)
**Key internal tensions:** Burden-sharing disputes; Turkey's independent foreign policy posture; divergent threat perceptions between eastern and western members; questions about US commitment under shifting administrations
**Contested framing:** NATO describes itself as a defensive alliance. Russia, China, and several Global South states frame NATO expansion as an offensive encirclement strategy. Both framings are present in international discourse.

### SCO (Shanghai Cooperation Organisation)
**Members:** China, Russia, India, Pakistan, Iran (2023), Belarus (2024)
**Nature:** Security/economic coordination body — not a collective defense alliance
**Combined population:** ~50% of world population
**Key dynamic:** India-Pakistan simultaneous membership creates structural tension; limits military operationalization; primarily a diplomatic coordination forum
**Contested framing:** Western governments characterize SCO as an authoritarian bloc. Member states characterize it as a multipolar alternative to Western-dominated institutions. Both framings are documented.

### CSTO (Collective Security Treaty Organization)
**Members:** Russia, Belarus, Kazakhstan, Kyrgyzstan, Armenia (suspended participation), Tajikistan
**Nature:** Russia-led collective defense organization; Article 4 mutual defense clause
**Key tension:** Failed to respond to Azerbaijan-Armenia 2020–23 conflict — Armenia subsequently suspended participation, which is a significant credibility test
**Note:** NATO has also faced credibility questions (Article 5 non-invocation debates, burden-sharing failures); credibility gaps affect all alliance structures, not only non-Western ones

### Arab League
**Nature:** Political coordination body; not a military alliance
**Effective capacity:** Limited — used primarily for diplomatic signaling and norm-setting
**Key dynamic:** Member state rivalries (Qatar-Saudi, Syria suspension) limit unified action

### African Union
**Relevant mechanisms:** APSA (African Peace and Security Architecture), ACIRC (rapid reaction force)
**Coup response:** AU norm — automatic suspension of coup governments; inconsistently applied
**Key dynamic:** Growing tension between AU coup suspension norms and popular support for some coup governments (Sahel), reflecting broader questions about democratic legitimacy vs. governance effectiveness

## Network Analysis Questions

For any conflict, answer these before concluding:

1. **Who is the patron on each side?** (If no patron: pure local conflict, different dynamics)
2. **How tight is the patron-proxy command relationship?** (Tehran → Hezbollah is tight; Tehran → Houthis is looser)
3. **Does the patron have an off-ramp?** (Can they de-escalate without losing face?)
4. **Are two patrons on a collision course, or managing competition?** (Managed competition = lower escalation risk than unmanaged)
5. **What would trigger patron direct involvement?** (The red line that changes the conflict type entirely)
6. **Which patron has more to lose from escalation?** (That patron is the more likely de-escalator)
