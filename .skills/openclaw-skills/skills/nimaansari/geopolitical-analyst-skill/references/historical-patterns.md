# Historical Conflict Patterns

## How to Use This File

Match the current situation to the closest archetype. State the match explicitly in output.
Divergences from the archetype are often more analytically valuable than the match itself.

**Neutrality note:** These archetypes are drawn from comparative conflict studies. "Resolution" in this context means cessation of active armed conflict or political crisis — not a judgment that one outcome was more legitimate or just than another. A military victory, a negotiated partition, and a frozen conflict are all documented as resolution types without preference.

---

## SECTION 1: Civil Conflict Archetypes

### CP-01: Protest-to-Civil War Escalation
**Canonical cases:** Syria 2011, Libya 2011, Yemen 2011
**Onset pattern:**
1. Economic grievance + political exclusion (years of pressure)
2. Triggering incident (police killing, election fraud, corruption revelation)
3. Peaceful mass protests — security force overreaction
4. Military defections begin — army fractures along ethnic/sectarian lines
5. Armed opposition groups form from defectors + criminal networks
6. External powers choose sides → proxy war structure emerges
7. State fragmentation

**Key pivot point:** Military defection rate. If >15% of officer corps defects within 60 days of onset, civil war is highly probable.

**Average duration:** 7–12 years (internationalized); 2–4 years (contained)

**Resolution pathways:**
- Military victory by one side (rare — usually >5 years)
- Negotiated partition / power-sharing
- Exhaustion ceasefire with frozen conflict
- External intervention ending conflict (Korea model — indefinite freeze)

**Divergence signals to watch:** If military remains unified = lower escalation ceiling. If external powers show restraint = shorter duration probable.

---

### CP-02: Coup Attempt / Military Intervention
**Canonical cases:** Turkey 2016, Bolivia 2019, Myanmar 2021, Niger 2023
**Onset pattern:**
1. Civil-military tension accumulates (political interference in promotions, corruption investigations, ideological conflict)
2. Small conspirator group acts — speed is essential for coup plotters
3. First 6 hours decisive: control of communications, presidential palace, airport
4. Mass civilian response determines outcome in democratic societies
5. International community response locks in or reverses outcome

**Key variable:** Coup plotters' control of communications infrastructure within first 2 hours. If state TV is broadcasting the civilian government — coup likely failing.

**Average duration to resolution:** 24–72 hours (success or failure determined quickly)

**Resolution pathways:**
- Coup succeeds → military junta, constitution suspended
- Coup fails → purge of military + democratic consolidation (or authoritarian consolidation)
- Partial coup → negotiated power-sharing (unstable)

---

### CP-03: Ethnic / Sectarian Mobilization
**Canonical cases:** Rwanda 1994, Bosnia 1992–95, Iraq post-2003, CAR ongoing
**Onset pattern:**
1. Long-standing ethnic/sectarian tension (structural)
2. Political transition or security vacuum removes restraining force
3. Elite entrepreneurs mobilize ethnic identity for political gain
4. Trigger event coded as inter-group attack (real or fabricated)
5. Security dilemma: each group arms because the other is arming
6. Violence becomes self-sustaining — moderates lose influence

**Early warning signal:** State media begins ethnic/sectarian categorization of events that previously had no such framing.

**Average duration:** 3–15 years; tends to recur in 10–20 year cycles

**Resolution pathways:**
- Physical separation (partition, population transfer)
- Power-sharing agreement (fragile — Bosnia model)
- Hegemonic peace (one group dominates — Rwanda post-1994)
- International protectorate (Kosovo model)

---

### CP-04: Insurgency / Counter-Insurgency
**Canonical cases:** Afghanistan, Colombia, Philippines (Mindanao), Mali
**Onset pattern:**
1. State legitimacy deficit in specific region (neglect, extraction, cultural exclusion)
2. Armed group establishes presence, provides shadow governance
3. State responds with military force → alienates civilian population further
4. Insurgency becomes self-financing (drugs, kidnapping, taxation)
5. Externalization: diaspora funding, neighboring state sanctuary

**Viability threshold:** Insurgency that survives past 5 years typically cannot be militarily defeated without political accommodation.

**Average duration:** 10–30 years

**Resolution pathways:**
- Negotiated settlement with political integration (Colombia FARC model)
- Insurgency military defeat (rare; requires cutting off sanctuary + financing)
- Transformation into political party (long track)
- Fragmentation into criminal enterprise (worst outcome — no political solution available)

## SECTION 2: Interstate Conflict Archetypes

### IC-01: Territorial Escalation
**Canonical cases:** Falklands 1982, Kargil 1999, Georgia 2008, Ukraine 2014, Azerbaijan-Armenia 2020–23
**Onset pattern:**
1. Disputed territory with symbolic / resource value
2. Domestic political pressure on one side → leader needs demonstrable action
3. Military probe / limited action tests opponent response
4. Response determines next step: capitulation, negotiation, or escalation
5. Escalation ladder: skirmish → limited war → full war (each step harder to reverse)

**Nuclear shadow:** When one party has nuclear weapons, escalation ceiling is structurally different. Conventional military victory becomes strategically dangerous for the winning side.

**Average duration:** Days to weeks (limited territorial war); years (internationalized)

**Resolution pathways:**
- Military fait accompli accepted (Crimea 2014 model)
- Negotiated territorial swap
- Status quo ante restored (Falklands model)
- Frozen conflict along new line of control (most common)

---

### IC-02: Proxy War Structure
**Canonical cases:** Yemen, Syria, Libya, Lebanon (ongoing), Korea (historical)
**Onset pattern:**
1. Local conflict provides entry point for external powers
2. External powers begin with material/financial support
3. Escalation: advisory → direct training → combat support → direct involvement
4. Conflict becomes vehicle for great power competition
5. Local actors lose agency as patron interests dominate
6. Resolution requires patron agreement, not local agreement

**Key analytical insight:** In proxy wars, analyze patrons first. Local actors are secondary. The conflict ends when patrons decide it should.

**Resolution pathways:**
- Patron negotiation (rarely happens without exhaustion)
- One patron abandons client → rapid collapse
- External power direct intervention ends conflict (changes conflict type)
- Indefinite freeze / partition

---

### IC-03: Economic Coercion / Sanctions Spiral
**Canonical cases:** Iran 2012–present, Russia 2014+, North Korea ongoing, Cuba ongoing
**Onset pattern:**
1. Sanctioning state(s) cite a triggering behavior (the legitimacy of this framing is itself contested between parties)
2. Initial targeted measures (individuals, entities)
3. Target state does not change behavior → measures expand to sectoral
4. Target state adapts (alternative trade networks, new partners, domestic substitution)
5. Sanctioning coalition faces internal compliance costs and defection pressure
6. Equilibrium: measures cause economic stress but rarely produce stated policy objectives

**Contested framing:** Sanctioning states frame measures as legitimate coercive diplomacy. Target states and many Global South governments frame the same measures as economic warfare and violations of sovereignty. Both framings are documented in the international record.

**Effectiveness window:** Economic pressure is highest in first 6–18 months before adaptation occurs.

**Resolution pathways:**
- Negotiated agreement where both parties accept partial concessions (Iran JCPOA model)
- Coalition defection collapses the measures
- Indefinite coexistence with declining effectiveness (Cuba, North Korea — decades)
- Target state changes policy for unrelated reasons (attribution to sanctions rarely provable)
- Government collapse / political instability — documented but extremely rare as a direct causal outcome of sanctions alone; typically requires compounding factors (military defeat, mass unrest, elite defection)

## SECTION 3: Political Crisis Archetypes

### PC-01: Election-Related Violence
**Canonical cases:** Kenya 2007, Zimbabwe 2008, Belarus 2020, Venezuela ongoing
**Onset pattern:**
1. Pre-election: opposition suppression, media restrictions, rule changes
2. Election day: irregularities reported
3. Results announcement: dispute over legitimacy
4. Mass protests vs. security crackdown
5. International community position determines incumbent's calculation
6. Military choice: enforce results or stay neutral/defect

**72-hour rule:** If mass protests sustain past 72 hours without violent dispersal, incumbent's position significantly weakened.

**Resolution pathways:**
- Power-sharing agreement (Kenya 2008 model)
- Incumbent consolidates through repression (Belarus 2020 model)
- Incumbent falls through military defection (Georgia 2003 model)
- Prolonged standoff (Venezuela model)

---

### PC-02: Economic Crisis → Political Destabilization
**Canonical cases:** Argentina 2001, Greece 2010–15, Lebanon 2019–present, Sri Lanka 2022
**Onset pattern:**
1. Currency crisis and/or debt default
2. Austerity measures imposed (IMF or voluntary)
3. Mass protests — economic grievance transitions to political demand
4. Government falls (or survives through repression)
5. Political vacuum → fragmentation or authoritarian consolidation

**Early warning signals (pre-crisis):**
- Currency depreciation > 20% in 12 months
- Foreign reserve depletion to < 3 months import cover
- Debt service > 25% of government revenue
- Capital flight by domestic elite

**Resolution pathways:**
- IMF program + reform (painful but stabilizing — 3–5 years)
- Default + restructuring (Argentina model — 2–10 years)
- Currency collapse + hyperinflation (Zimbabwe/Venezuela model — decade+)
- External bailout (Gulf states, China — strategic dependencies created)

## SECTION 4: Regional Escalation Archetypes

### RE-01: Cross-Border Spillover
**Canonical cases:** Syrian war → Turkey/Jordan/Lebanon; Ukraine → Moldova/Baltic states
**Pattern:** Conflict in one state generates: refugee flows, armed group sanctuary, economic disruption, and political contagion in neighbors.

**Spillover risk factors:**
- Shared ethnic/sectarian population across border
- Existing territorial dispute with conflict state
- Weak state capacity in neighboring country
- Active armed groups with cross-border networks

---

### RE-02: Great Power Confrontation via Crisis
**Canonical cases:** Cuban Missile Crisis 1962, Taiwan Strait crises, South China Sea standoffs
**Key dynamic:** Neither party wants direct war, but neither wants to back down — crisis management becomes as important as the underlying issue.

**Escalation risk peaks:** During military exercises, naval incidents, and unexpected operational contact.

**De-escalation mechanism:** Back-channel communication between principals (not proxies). Absence of direct communication channel = highest escalation risk.

## Pattern Matching Checklist

When matching current situation to archetype:

- [ ] What is the conflict type? (civil, interstate, political crisis, hybrid)
- [ ] Who are the external actors and what archetype does their involvement match?
- [ ] What stage of the archetype are we in? (onset, escalation, peak, resolution)
- [ ] What are the key pivot variables for this archetype?
- [ ] Where does current situation diverge from the archetype?
- [ ] What does the historical resolution pathway suggest about timeline?
- [ ] What is the archetype's "point of no return" — and have we passed it?
