# Credible Commitment & Commitment Devices

## Purpose

Saying "I will do X" is cheap talk. Actors say things they don't mean all the time.

**Credible commitment** is: **Making it costly to break a promise so people believe you.**

Examples:
- US places troops in South Korea — now withdrawal = abandonment (costs domestic/alliance capital)
- Russia annexes Crimea — can't reverse without massive loss of face
- Iran enriches uranium — proves weapons program is real cost

This module helps you:
- Identify when promises are credible vs cheap talk
- Understand why some threats are believed and others aren't
- See how actors create leverage through commitment

---

## What Makes a Commitment Credible?

### Method 1: Sunk Cost / Irreversibility
**Mechanism:** Make the action costly to reverse

**Examples:**
- Weapons test (can't undo proving capability)
- Troop deployment (withdrawal = public humiliation)
- Territorial annexation (reversing = loss of face)
- Public statement (retraction = weakness signal)

**Detection:** Actor does something they couldn't easily undo
**Credibility level:** HIGH (they've paid a cost)

---

### Method 2: Delegation / Hostage
**Mechanism:** Put decision in someone else's hands

**Examples:**
- Bind yourself to military subordinate (general can't ignore war order without mutiny)
- Stake political capital (if promise breaks, you lose office)
- Place family member in danger
- Sign binding treaty (breaking it = international law violation)

**Detection:** Actor has delegated to someone who'll enforce the promise
**Credibility level:** HIGH (cost of breaking > cost of following through)

---

### Method 3: Institutional Constraint
**Mechanism:** Make promise legally/institutionally binding

**Examples:**
- Treaty obligation (breaking = international legal consequence)
- Constitutional requirement (president can't act against constitution)
- Democratic procedure (leader must get parliament approval; can't act unilaterally)

**Detection:** Promise is written into law/institutions
**Credibility level:** MEDIUM-HIGH (depends on institutional strength)

---

### Method 4: Reputation Cost
**Mechanism:** Make it so breaking promise ruins future credibility

**Examples:**
- Business deal: break contract once, no one trades with you
- Diplomatic commitment: break promise, no one believes your future commitments
- Military threat: if threat fails, you're seen as weak

**Detection:** Actor has made many similar promises; reputation is stake
**Credibility level:** MEDIUM (depends on actor's past record)

---

### Method 5: Audience Cost
**Mechanism:** Make breaking promise costly domestically

**Examples:**
- Leader makes public commitment → backing down = domestic political loss
- Democracy makes promise → parliament must ratify; hard to break
- Nationalist leader makes nationalist promise → breaking it = voted out

**Detection:** Promise made publicly; domestic audience aware
**Credibility level:** MEDIUM (depends on how much audience cares)

---

## Red Flags: Cheap Talk (Not Credible)

### Signal 1: No Sunk Cost
- Actor makes promise but could easily reverse it
- No institutional constraint
- No reputational stake

**Example:** "We don't want war" (said by every government, costs nothing)

### Signal 2: Actors Have Incentive to Break Promise
- Promise is costly to follow through
- Actor benefits if they break it
- They only committed because they thought you'd believe them

**Example:** State promises not to weaponize space, but developing anti-satellite weapons secretly

### Signal 3: No Enforcement Mechanism
- If promise is broken, no consequence
- Other actor can't force compliance
- Mutual benefit evaporates

**Example:** Non-binding agreement with no verification

## Commitment Analysis Template

```markdown
## Credible Commitment Assessment: [Actor] - [Promise]

### The Promise
**What is the commitment?** [Specific statement]
**Who made it?** [Actor]
**To whom?** [Other actor(s)]
**When?** [Date]

### Credibility Mechanisms Present

**Sunk costs made?**
- [ ] Irreversible action taken (weapons test, troop deployment, annexation, public statement)
- What: [Describe]
- Cost to reverse: [HIGH / MEDIUM / LOW]

**Delegation/hostage?**
- [ ] Decision delegated to someone who must enforce it
- Who: [Military? Subordinate? Ally?]
- Cost of not enforcing: [What happens to enforcer if promise breaks?]

**Institutional constraint?**
- [ ] Promise is legally binding (treaty, law, constitution)
- Binding mechanism: [Which institution? How binding?]
- Enforcement: [Who enforces if broken?]

**Reputation cost?**
- [ ] Actor has track record on similar promises
- Past record: [Do they keep or break commitments?]
- Reputational stake: [How much does credibility matter for future?]

**Audience cost?**
- [ ] Promise made publicly; domestic audience aware
- Audience size: [Small elite / broad public?]
- Domestic cost if broken: [Election loss? Policy reversal? Protest?]

### Credibility Score
**Mechanisms present:** [Count]
**Overall credibility:** CREDIBLE (4+ mechanisms) / SOMEWHAT CREDIBLE (2-3) / CHEAP TALK (0-1)

### Likelihood of Following Through
**If commitment is costly:** [Probability actor follows through]
**If circumstances change:** [Would actor break commitment?]
**Escape route for actor:** [Is there a face-saving way to break the promise?]

### Implication for Analysis
[Does actor's commitment change likelihood of [outcome]?]
```

## Escalation & Counter-Commitment

When two actors make competing commitments:

**Scenario:** State A says "we will defend X at all costs"
State B says "we will take X at all costs"

**Both have made credible commitments.** Now what?

**Options:**
1. One side backs down (loses credibility but avoids war)
2. Both escalate to war (expensive but maintains credibility)
3. Find face-saving compromise (both retreat partially)

**How to analyze:**
- Who has MORE at stake (reputationally)?
- Who can afford the war more?
- Is there a compromise both can accept?
- Who has more credibility to lose?

---

## When Commitment Fails: Signaling Vulnerability

Sometimes actors make commitments they CAN'T actually follow through on:

**Example:** Small state says "we will defend this territory against invasion"
- Limited military
- No allies
- Impossible to actually defend

**This is cheap talk.** Investors/allies know it can't be followed through.

**Detection:**
- Commitment is incredible (no mechanism, no sunk cost)
- Actor's capabilities don't match promise
- No one believes it

**Implication:** Actor is bluffing, and everyone knows it.
