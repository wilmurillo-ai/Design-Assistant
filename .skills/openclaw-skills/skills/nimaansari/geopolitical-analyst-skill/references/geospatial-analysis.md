# Geospatial Dynamics: Terrain, Logistics, and Strategic Geography

## Purpose

Military analysts always think spatially. Most political analysts don't. This is a mistake.

Geography shapes:
- **Military balance** (terrain favors defense/offense differently)
- **Economic viability** (trade routes, chokepoints, resources)
- **Governance capacity** (state reach: cities vs remote regions)
- **Refugee flows** (where people can flee)
- **Alliance patterns** (proximity creates security interests)

Geospatial analysis makes the map part of the analysis, not just background.

---

## Key Geospatial Dimensions

### Dimension 1: Terrain and Military Advantage

#### Mountain Warfare
**Advantage:** Defender (small force can hold terrain)
**Typical ratio:** 1 defender : 3 attackers needed
**Examples:** Afghanistan, Yemen, Caucasus, Andes

**For analysis:**
- If conflict is in mountains, expect prolonged stalemate
- Attacker needs 3x force superiority to win
- Irregular warfare likely (smaller units, longer duration)
- Supply lines are critical (narrow mountain roads)

#### Urban Warfare
**Advantage:** Defender initially, then attacker if overwhelming force deployed
**Typical ratio:** 1 defender : 5-10 attackers in dense urban
**Examples:** Gaza, Ukraine cities, Syria cities

**For analysis:**
- High casualty rates (both civilian and military)
- Extreme civilian displacement
- Very difficult for attackers to consolidate gains
- If attacker takes city, still faces insurgency

#### Open Terrain (steppe, desert, plain)
**Advantage:** Attacker with mechanized force
**Typical ratio:** 1 attacker : 1 defender in mobile warfare
**Examples:** Russia-Ukraine open terrain, North Africa, Arabian Peninsula

**For analysis:**
- Rapid maneuver possible
- Battle lines shift quickly
- Air power and artillery more decisive
- Defender needs air cover or superior numbers

### Dimension 2: Logistics and Supply Lines

#### Chokepoint Control
**Definition:** Geographic locations where goods MUST pass (narrow strait, mountain pass, river crossing)

**Strategic value:**
- Controller can tax / monitor / block all trade
- Military forces at chokepoint can control vast territory
- Loss of chokepoint is strategic disaster

**Global chokepoints:**
- **Strait of Hormuz** (Persian Gulf): 20% of world oil passes through
- **South China Sea straits** (Malacca, Sunda): 40% of world trade
- **Suez Canal**: 12% of world trade
- **Panama Canal**: 5% of world trade
- **Turkish Straits** (Bosporus): All Black Sea trade
- **Bab el-Mandeb** (Red Sea): Major shipping chokepoint

**For analysis:**
- Control of chokepoint = geopolitical leverage
- Threat to chokepoint = major great-power concern
- Blockade of chokepoint = act of war equivalent

#### Supply Route Vulnerability
**For any military force:**
1. Map the supply lines (how do they get food, fuel, ammunition?)
2. Identify chokepoints on those lines
3. Calculate how long they can fight if supply cut
4. Assess enemy's ability to cut supplies

**Examples:**
- Ukraine: Russia controls supply lines through Belarus → Ukraine must use longer NATO routes
- Gulf states: Supply lines pass through Strait of Hormuz → Iran leverage
- Taiwan: Supply lines pass through First Island Chain → US guarantee critical
- Israel: Supply lines from US pass through Mediterranean → Egypt's Suez control matters

### Dimension 3: Resource Geography

#### Concentrated Resources
**Definition:** Critical resource located in few places

**Examples:**
- **Oil**: 60% in Middle East + Russia/Central Asia
- **Natural gas**: 40% in Middle East + Russia
- **Rare earths**: 70% in China
- **Wheat**: Top 5 exporters = Russia, Ukraine, France, Argentina, Germany
- **Phosphate**: Morocco controls 70% of reserves
- **Lithium**: Chile, Argentina, Australia, China control 90%

**For analysis:**
- Resources are leverage (can be cut off)
- Resource-dependent states are vulnerable to coercion
- Resource exporters have geopolitical influence
- Resource scarcity drives conflict (water in arid regions)

#### Agricultural Zones
**Definition:** Geographic regions where food is produced

**Vulnerability:**
- War disrupts planting/harvest → food crisis within months
- Blockade of food exports → starvation possible
- Climate stress hits certain latitudes more than others

**Examples:**
- Ukraine wheat: 10% of global trade → Ukraine war → global food crisis
- East Africa rainfall: Dependent on monsoon patterns → drought → famine
- Indian monsoon: Shifts in strength create regional famines

### Dimension 4: Population Distribution

#### City-Centric Control
**Principle:** State controls major cities or it doesn't exist

**Implication:**
- Controlling 80% of land area is meaningless if you don't control cities
- Population concentration in few cities = vulnerability (capital city = regime vulnerability)
- Remote regions often ungoverned even in stable states

**Examples:**
- Syria: Assad controls major cities (Damascus, Aleppo) but much of countryside is insurgent/Kurdish
- Afghanistan: Government controls Kabul and major cities; countryside is Taliban
- Democratic Republic of Congo: Government controls Kinshasa (capital) but much of countryside is ungoverned

**For analysis:**
- Wars that don't control cities usually lose (irregular forces can't hold territory)
- Cities = where power is; countryside = where insurgency hides
- Refugee flows go city → city (then to borders if war in cities)

#### Displacement Patterns
**Principle:** Refugees follow geography (easier routes, less hostile areas)

**Examples:**
- Syrian refugees: Flow through Turkey (neighbor) not overland to Europe (easier route through neighbor)
- Ukrainian refugees: Flow west (away from Russia)
- Afghan refugees: Flow to Pakistan/Iran (not overland across mountains)

**For analysis:**
- Refugee flows reveal conflict geography (which direction is "safe"?)
- Hosting countries become stressed (Turkey has 4M Syrian refugees)
- Refugee routes become geopolitical (countries can close borders)

### Dimension 5: Border Dynamics

#### Disputed Borders
**Definition:** Territory whose sovereignty is contested

**Examples:**
- Kashmir (India-Pakistan-China)
- Crimea (Russia-Ukraine)
- Golan Heights (Israel-Syria)
- East Jerusalem / West Bank (Israel-Palestine)
- South China Sea islands (China-Vietnam-Philippines-others)

**For analysis:**
- Disputed borders = potential conflict sources
- Control of disputed territory = legitimacy (whoever controls it de facto is recognized)
- Border disputes often become permanent (easier to maintain status quo than change it)

#### Militarized Borders
**Definition:** Border with significant military presence / fortifications

**Examples:**
- DMZ Korea: Most militarized border in world
- India-Pakistan line of control: Heavy fortification
- Israel-Syria Golan Heights: Significant military presence

**For analysis:**
- Militarized borders = high alert / potential for incident
- Demilitarization = trust signal (rare)
- Military buildups on borders = tension signal

### Dimension 6: Strategic Depth

#### Definition
Distance from enemy border to critical infrastructure

**Examples:**
- Russia: Vast depth (thousands of km to Moscow from Western Europe)
- Ukraine: No depth (Russia 50-500 km from Kiev)
- Israel: Minimal depth (hostile neighbors close)
- Germany: Moderate depth historically

**For analysis:**
- Low strategic depth = vulnerability to rapid attack
- High strategic depth = defensive advantage
- Invasion requires either overwhelming force (Germany 1940) or enemy allowing it (Ukraine 2022)

## Geospatial Analysis Checklist

When analyzing any conflict or region:

- [ ] **Terrain**: Identify advantages (mountain = defender, open = attacker)
- [ ] **Supply lines**: Map them; identify chokepoints; assess duration without supply
- [ ] **Resources**: What's at stake? Oil? Water? Food? Rare earths?
- [ ] **Population**: Where are the people? Where would they flee?
- [ ] **Cities**: Who controls them? That's who controls the state
- [ ] **Borders**: Which ones are disputed? Which are militarized?
- [ ] **Strategic depth**: Can defenders slow attackers through distance?
- [ ] **Logistics chain**: Can attacker sustain offensive far from home?
