# Civil-Military Relations & Military Loyalty

## Purpose

Militaries are not neutral actors. They have interests, factions, grievances.

Understanding civil-military dynamics means predicting:
- Will military remain loyal to civilian government?
- When will military intervene in politics?
- What triggers military coups?
- How stable is civilian control?

---

## Factors Determining Military Loyalty

### Factor 1: Payment & Treatment
**Question:** Are soldiers paid on time? Are conditions acceptable?

**Loyal if:** Regular pay, decent conditions, career advancement
**Disloyal if:** Unpaid soldiers, poor conditions, no advancement

**Signal:** Soldiers demanding back pay = military disloyalty risk

### Factor 2: Institutional Autonomy
**Question:** Does military have institutional independence or is it controlled by leader?

**Stable if:** Military has institutional autonomy; follows legal orders
**Unstable if:** Leader purges officers; military becomes personality cult around leader

**Signal:** Officer purges = military becoming unreliable

### Factor 3: Ethnic/Sectarian Composition
**Question:** Does military reflect population or is one group dominant?

**Stable if:** Diverse officer corps; no dominant faction
**Unstable if:** One ethnic/religious group controls military; others marginalized

**Signal:** Military dominated by one group = potential ethnic tensions

### Factor 4: Power Within Government
**Question:** How much power does military have in government?

**Stable if:** Military limited to defense role; civilian government controls
**Unstable if:** Military has veto over civilian decisions; military sees itself as guardian

**Signal:** Military issuing political statements = military sees itself as political actor

### Factor 5: Patron-Client Structure
**Question:** Is military loyal to government or to specific faction/leader?

**Stable if:** Loyalty to institution (military as whole)
**Unstable if:** Loyalty to personality (specific leader); military would switch allegiance

**Signal:** Military chief's personal popularity > government popularity = factional loyalty

## Coup Risk Indicators

### Early Warning Signs

**Sign 1: Military Public Statements**
- Military issuing political statements (unusual)
- Military criticizing civilian government
- Military claiming "duty to protect nation" (hint at intervention)

**Sign 2: Officer Purges / Reassignments**
- Civilian government removing military officers
- Military removing officers loyal to government
- Loyalty tests increasing

**Sign 3: Military Unit Movements**
- Units moved from barracks to capital
- Elite units repositioned
- Security forces increased readiness

**Sign 4: Coalition Building**
- Military leaders meeting with opposition
- Military signaling neutrality in government crisis
- Military refusing direct orders (indicating disloyalty)

**Sign 5: Popular Support for Military**
- Public opinion favoring military intervention
- Military seen as more legitimate than government
- Opposition calling for military intervention

---

## Coup Probability Model

Coup likely when:
- Military grievances HIGH (unpaid, purged, disrespected)
- Government weakness HIGH (losing support, delegitimized)
- Civilian control LOW (government can't enforce orders on military)
- Public support for coup MEDIUM-HIGH
- External powers NEUTRAL or encouraging
