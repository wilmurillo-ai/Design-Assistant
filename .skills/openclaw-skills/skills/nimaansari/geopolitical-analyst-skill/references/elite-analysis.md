# Elite Factionalism & Internal Government Conflict

## Purpose

Governments are not monoliths. They're coalitions of competing factions, each with:
- Different strategic interests
- Different constituencies
- Different international patrons
- Different institutional bases (military, security, economic, diplomatic)

When internal factions are in conflict, it's the **leading indicator** that foreign policy will shift. Military coups, sudden policy reversals, and unexpected escalations often correlate with internal factional conflict becoming acute.

This module detects visible signals of elite factionalism — often months before major policy changes.

---

## Factional Structure Template

Most governments have 4-6 major factions:

### 1. Security/Military Faction
**Base:** Military establishment, intelligence services, security apparatus
**Interests:** Military budgets, territorial control, strategic posture, deterrence capability
**Patron:** Often strongest patron state, or independent if military is powerful
**Risk tendency:** Often more hawkish; views security through military lens

### 2. Economic/Business Faction
**Base:** Large corporations, oligarchs, business associations, finance sector
**Interests:** Trade access, sanctions relief, business partnerships, currency stability
**Patron:** Can be multiple patrons with economic leverage
**Risk tendency:** Often more dovish; wants to avoid disruption to business

### 3. Ideological/Party Faction
**Base:** Political party in power, ideological allies, civil society partners
**Interests:** Ideology (nationalism, socialism, islamism, liberalism), domestic legitimacy, international reputation
**Patron:** Often other states sharing ideology
**Risk tendency:** Variable (depends on ideology)

### 4. Diplomatic/Elite Faction
**Base:** Foreign ministry, international institutions, diplomatic corps, technocrats
**Interests:** International relationships, reputation, diplomatic solutions, economic integration
**Patron:** Often Western powers if faction is pro-engagement
**Risk tendency:** Usually more dovish; views problems as negotiable

### 5. Regional/Local Factions
**Base:** Regional power brokers, ethnic/sectarian leaders, local strongmen
**Interests:** Regional autonomy, resource control, influence over security forces
**Patron:** Often other countries' regional allies
**Risk tendency:** Often more parochial; willing to sacrifice national interests for regional gain

### 6. Succession/Leadership Faction
**Base:** Potential successor to leader, allies positioning for future
**Interests:** Weakening current leader, positioning themselves, proving capability
**Patron:** Sometimes foreign powers betting on successor
**Risk tendency:** Often unpredictable; willing to take risks to appear strong

## Visible Signals of Factional Conflict

### Signal 1: Public Disagreement Between Officials
**What it looks like:**
- Two officials make contradictory policy statements
- One official criticizes another publicly
- Officials make statements in different order of priority

**Example signals:**
- Defense minister says "military readiness critical"; foreign minister says "diplomacy primary"
- Security chief says threat level HIGH; civilian government says situation stable
- Regional governor contradicts central government on policy

**Confidence:** MEDIUM (some disagreements are theater; some are real)

**Timeline:** 2-4 weeks before major policy shift

### Signal 2: Personnel Changes / Purges
**What it looks like:**
- Unexpected minister firing/resignation
- Military chief of staff suddenly replaced
- Multiple officials from one faction fired together
- Personnel changes concentrated in specific ministries

**Example signals:**
- Defense minister fired; replaced with ally of current leader
- Central bank governor removed; replaced with different economic philosophy
- Three military generals reassigned (purge of one faction)
- Ambassador recalled (factional position on foreign policy)

**Confidence:** HIGH (personnel changes reflect power struggle)

**Timeline:** 1-8 weeks before policy change

### Signal 3: Security Force Repositioning
**What it looks like:**
- Military units moved to unexpected locations
- Elite units (presidential guard, special forces) repositioned
- Officers transferred between different commands
- Loyalty checks (units tested for reliability)

**Example signals:**
- Paratroopers moved from border to capital (preparation for potential domestic action)
- Special forces loyalty tested in unusual exercise
- Regional commanders replaced with loyalists
- New military appointments concentrated in capital

**Confidence:** HIGH (military repositioning is preparation for action)

**Timeline:** 1-4 weeks before coup attempt or major political move

### Signal 4: Media Tone Shift Toward Internal Targets
**What it looks like:**
- State media suddenly critical of specific official/ministry
- Criticism becomes personal (corruption charges, incompetence)
- Media campaign coordinated (multiple outlets, same narrative)
- Intensity increasing (from mild criticism to harsh attacks)

**Example signals:**
- Official A criticized in state TV → Removed within 2 weeks
- Ministry suddenly accused of corruption → Budget cut or leadership changed
- Coordinated media attack on security chief → Chief fired within month
- Regional governor attacked in capital media → Governor removed

**Confidence:** MEDIUM-HIGH (media campaigns often precede personnel changes)

**Timeline:** 1-3 weeks before personnel change

### Signal 5: Factional Leader Visibility Changes
**What it looks like:**
- Factional leader suddenly more/less visible in public
- Leader appears at unexpected events
- Leader makes unusual public statements
- Leader's travel patterns change (visiting unexpected allies)

**Example signals:**
- Military chief suddenly appears at civilian ceremonies (unusual)
- Business faction leader meets with government officials (rare)
- Ideological faction leader makes statement on foreign policy (unusual domain)
- Succession-faction leader visits key allies (positioning signals)

**Confidence:** MEDIUM (visibility changes sometimes indicate positioning)

**Timeline:** 2-8 weeks before major move

### Signal 6: Policy Contradictions Emerging
**What it looks like:**
- Announced policy contradicted by actual implementation
- Policy A announced, then modified toward opposite direction
- One ministry's policy contradicts another's
- Statements from government vs. parliament contradictory

**Example signals:**
- Sanctions announced, then quietly lifted (economic faction wins)
- Military operation planned, then called off (dovish faction wins)
- Trade deal signed, then implementation blocked (protectionist faction wins)
- Parliament passes law; executive doesn't implement (institutional conflict)

**Confidence:** HIGH (policy contradictions indicate genuine factions fighting)

**Timeline:** Concurrent with factional fighting; policy incoherence is symptom

### Signal 7: Unusual Meetings / Coalition Building
**What it looks like:**
- Factional leaders meeting who normally don't meet
- New alliances being formed (visible through meetings)
- Diplomatic outreach to external supporters
- Meeting intensity increasing (frequency of closed-door meetings)

**Example signals:**
- Military chief meets with business oligarchs (unusual coalition)
- Regional governors coordinate (unusual alliance against center)
- Opposition leaders meet with government officials (negotiation/defection)
- Diplomatic faction chief travels to foreign capitals (seeking external support)

**Confidence:** MEDIUM (meetings sometimes are theater, sometimes real)

**Timeline:** 2-8 weeks before factional outcome determined

### Signal 8: Institutional Conflict / Checks and Balances Stressed
**What it looks like:**
- Parliament challenges executive (or vice versa)
- Constitutional court makes unexpected ruling
- Agencies refuse to implement government order
- Mutual accusations of illegality/overreach

**Example signals:**
- Parliament refuses budget proposed by executive
- Court blocks government order
- Military refuses to execute civilian government order
- Intelligence agency leaks information undermining government

**Confidence:** HIGH (institutional conflict is real conflict)

**Timeline:** Ongoing; indicates how polarized elite is

## Factional Conflict Severity Scale

### Level 1: Normal Disagreement (Stable)
- Different factions have different interests (expected)
- Disagreements are negotiated within government
- No public contradictions
- Personnel stable
- No institutional conflict

**Implication:** Foreign policy stable; no sudden shifts expected

### Level 2: Visible Tension (Caution)
- Public statements show disagreement
- Media criticism emerging
- Occasional personnel changes (minor)
- Institutional tension visible but not crisis

**Implication:** Watch for policy incoherence; prepare for minor shifts

### Level 3: Active Conflict (Alert)
- Explicit public disagreement
- Media campaigns against officials
- Personnel changes concentrated in specific ministry/faction
- Policy contradictions emerging
- Unusual meetings/coalition building

**Implication:** Major policy shift likely within 4-12 weeks; may include personnel changes

### Level 4: Acute Crisis (High Alert)
- Multiple officials fired/resign
- Security force repositioning visible
- Institutional conflict escalating
- Factional meetings openly occurring
- Government policy incoherent / contradictory

**Implication:** Coup attempt, succession crisis, or major policy reversal possible within 2-8 weeks

### Level 5: Breakdown (Emergency)
- Government paralyzed
- Security forces fractionally loyal
- Constitutional crisis
- Violence possible

**Implication:** Regime change possible; foreign policy completely unpredictable

## Integration with Main Workflow

**Triggered:** In Step 5 (Actor Mapping), when assessing state-level actors

**Input:**
- News reports of official statements / policy shifts
- Personnel tracking (official biographies, ministry changes)
- Media tone analysis (who's being attacked?)

**Output:**
- Factional map (who's who, what do they want?)
- Conflict severity assessment (stable or crisis?)
- Foreign policy prediction (what will happen when factions compete?)
- Early warning signals (what to watch for?)

**Use case:** Analysts who need to understand why governments act unpredictably; why policy suddenly shifts; whether foreign policy change is temporary (factional victory) or permanent (structural shift).
