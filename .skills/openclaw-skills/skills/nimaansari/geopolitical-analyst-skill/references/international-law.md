# International Law Reference

## Purpose

International legal status affects actor behavior, coalition-building, and the range of permissible responses. An ICC arrest warrant changes a leader's travel behavior. A UNSC resolution creates or removes legal cover for military action. A Geneva Convention breach triggers specific legal obligations.

This reference provides a lookup framework — not legal advice. Always note when legal determinations are contested between parties.

---

## Key Legal Instruments and Their Analytical Relevance

### UN Security Council (UNSC)

**What it does:** The only body with authority to authorize military force, impose binding sanctions, and create peacekeeping missions under UN Charter Chapter VII.

**Analytical relevance:**
- Chapter VI resolution = non-binding; calls for negotiation
- Chapter VII resolution = binding on all member states; authorizes coercive measures
- Chapter VII + "all necessary means" = explicit authorization for military force
- Veto by permanent member (US, UK, France, Russia, China) = blocks any resolution

**Key lookup:**
- UNSC resolutions: https://www.un.org/securitycouncil/content/resolutions
- Active sanctions regimes: https://www.un.org/securitycouncil/sanctions/information
- Current peacekeeping missions: https://peacekeeping.un.org/en/missions

**When to check:**
- Before assessing whether a military intervention is internationally authorized
- When evaluating sanctions — distinguish UN-mandated (binding) vs unilateral (contested)
- When a state claims international legal justification for force

### International Criminal Court (ICC)

**Jurisdiction:** War crimes, crimes against humanity, genocide, crime of aggression
**Coverage:** 124 states parties. Notably NOT members: US, Russia, China, India, Israel, Iran, Saudi Arabia

**Analytical relevance of arrest warrants:**
- ICC warrant = leader **cannot travel** to any of 124 member states without risk of arrest
- Creates diplomatic isolation: meetings must happen in non-member states
- Signals international community's legal framing of the conflict
- Does NOT prevent military operations or governance at home

**Current warrant status:** Check https://www.icc-cpi.int/situations-and-cases

**Key distinction:** An ICC investigation ≠ an ICC warrant ≠ an ICC conviction. These are different stages with different analytical implications.

**Non-member caveat:** When the US, Russia, China, India, or Israel is the subject of alleged violations, ICC jurisdiction typically does not apply — note this explicitly in assessments.

---

### International Court of Justice (ICJ)

**Jurisdiction:** Disputes between states; advisory opinions to UN bodies
**Analytical relevance:**
- ICJ genocide ruling = internationally recognized legal determination (use "genocide" as label only after this)
- ICJ provisional measures = legally binding orders to states during proceedings
- ICJ advisory opinions = not binding but carry significant political/diplomatic weight

**Active cases:** https://www.icj-cij.org/cases

---

### Geneva Conventions and International Humanitarian Law (IHL)

**Four conventions + two protocols** governing conduct of war. Customary IHL extends to non-signatory states.

**Key principles (analytical reference):**
| Principle | Meaning | Violation Indicator |
|-----------|---------|-------------------|
| Distinction | Must distinguish combatants from civilians | Attacks on civilian infrastructure, hospitals |
| Proportionality | Civilian harm cannot be excessive relative to military advantage | Casualty ratios, strike patterns |
| Precaution | Must take feasible precautions to minimize civilian harm | Warning practices, weapon selection |
| Prohibition on torture | No torture under any circumstances | Detention practices |
| Protected persons | Prisoners of war, wounded, medical personnel | Treatment of POWs, hospital attacks |

**Analytical use:**
- Assess whether reported conduct constitutes likely IHL violations
- IHL violations → trigger obligations on third states (non-recognition, aid restrictions)
- Systematic IHL violations → potential ICC jurisdiction

**Note:** IHL applies to ALL parties in a conflict — state and non-state actors alike. Apply the same legal standard regardless of actor.

### UN Charter Article 51 (Self-Defense)

Every state has the right to individual or collective self-defense "if an armed attack occurs" — but:
- Must be reported to UNSC immediately
- Must be proportionate
- Must be necessary
- Right ends when UNSC takes measures to restore peace

**Analytical relevance:** Most military interventions are justified under Article 51. Assess whether:
1. An "armed attack" actually occurred (contested in many cases)
2. The response is proportionate to the attack
3. UNSC was notified

### Responsibility to Protect (R2P)

**Not legally binding** but politically influential doctrine:
- States have a responsibility to protect their populations from genocide, war crimes, ethnic cleansing, crimes against humanity
- If a state fails this, international community has responsibility to act
- Only authorized through UNSC Chapter VII — unilateral R2P intervention has no legal basis

**Analytical use:** When states invoke R2P for intervention, assess whether UNSC authorization exists. R2P rhetoric without UNSC authorization = contested legal basis.

---

### Territorial Integrity vs. Self-Determination

These two principles are in direct tension and both are in the UN Charter:

| Principle | Supported by | Applied to |
|-----------|-------------|-----------|
| Territorial integrity | Most states (especially multiethnic ones) | Kosovo (against), Crimea (against), Taiwan (against) |
| Self-determination | Peoples seeking independence | Kosovo (for), Crimea (for by Russia), Palestine (for) |

**Key analytical point:** There is no consistent international legal resolution to this tension. States selectively invoke whichever principle supports their interests. Document which parties invoke which principle in any given dispute.

## International Law Lookup Workflow

When assessing a conflict or action, answer:

```
1. Is there a UNSC resolution covering this situation?
   YES → Note chapter (VI or VII), what it authorizes, when passed
   NO → Note absence of UNSC mandate

2. Is there an active ICC investigation or warrant?
   YES → Note stage, implications for actor behavior
   NO → Note whether affected states are ICC members

3. Does the conduct described potentially violate IHL?
   YES → Note which principle, note it applies to ALL parties equally
   NO → Note IHL compliance

4. Are there active UN sanctions?
   YES → Distinguish UN-mandated (binding) vs unilateral (contested)
   NO → Note absence of binding international sanctions

5. Are legal bases being contested between parties?
   YES → Document each party's legal framing — do not adjudicate
   NO → Note apparent consensus
```

## Framing Rule

International law is interpreted differently across political and legal traditions:
- Western liberal tradition: emphasizes ICC, human rights law, R2P
- TWAIL (Third World Approaches to International Law): emphasizes sovereignty, self-determination, historical injustice
- Russian/Chinese tradition: emphasizes UN Charter sovereignty, non-interference, UNSC primacy

When legal arguments are made, note which tradition they draw from. Do not privilege one legal tradition as the correct interpretation unless a binding legal determination (ICJ, ICCruling) has been made.
