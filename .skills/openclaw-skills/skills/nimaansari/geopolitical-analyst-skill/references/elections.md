# Election Calendar & Political Transition Reference

## Why Elections Matter for Geopolitical Analysis

Elections are the **most predictable source of geopolitical inflection points**. They shape:
- **Incumbent risk tolerance** — leaders with elections approaching take different risks than those recently re-elected
- **Military adventurism** — conflicts are started, escalated, or ended on electoral timelines
- **Economic policy** — populist measures, subsidy restoration, and currency manipulation cluster before elections
- **Repression cycles** — opposition crackdowns intensify before contested elections
- **Alliance signaling** — governments signal to external patrons before transitions that might change policy

**Rule:** Always check election calendar before finalizing any political risk assessment.

## Analytical Framework: Election Risk Assessment

For any upcoming election, assess across five dimensions:

### Dimension 1: Competitive Threat Level
```
HIGH threat → Incumbent genuinely at risk of losing
MEDIUM threat → Incumbent likely to win but opposition can cause problems
LOW threat → Outcome predetermined or token opposition only
```

**Behavioral prediction by threat level:**
- HIGH competitive threat → increased repression, economic populism, possible foreign crisis manufacturing, higher likelihood of post-election dispute
- MEDIUM → selective targeted repression, media control, vote manipulation at margins
- LOW → minimal electoral violence, possible choreographed opposition for legitimacy

### Dimension 2: Institutional Integrity
```
STRONG institutions → Electoral commission independent, courts will enforce results, military stays out
WEAK institutions → Commission controlled by incumbent, courts politically compromised, military loyalty uncertain
```

**Key signal:** Has the electoral commission been restructured or its leadership changed in the 12 months before the election?

### Dimension 3: Post-Election Dispute Risk
High when:
- Competitive threat is HIGH + institutional integrity is WEAK
- Previous election was disputed
- Opposition has mobilization capacity (civil society, diaspora funding)
- International observers likely to issue critical reports
- Incumbent has signaled unwillingness to accept loss

### Dimension 4: Economic Policy Pre-Election Cycle
Watch for in the 3–6 months before polling:
- Subsidy restoration or expansion
- Government wage increases (public sector)
- Currency stabilization measures (even unsustainable ones)
- Infrastructure announcement sprees
- Reduction of visible repression (temporary)

These measures often create post-election fiscal stress → economic policy reversal after vote → social unrest.

### Dimension 5: External Actor Involvement
- Which external patrons prefer which outcome?
- Are there reports of campaign financing from external sources?
- Is there a competing foreign power that would benefit from the challenger winning?
- Post-election: will external patrons recognize or reject the result?

## Election Outcome → Foreign Policy Change Risk Matrix

| Election Type | Foreign Policy Change Risk |
|--------------|--------------------------|
| Incumbent re-elected, strong mandate | LOW — policy continuity |
| Incumbent re-elected, weak mandate | MEDIUM — may need external distraction |
| Opposition wins, smooth transition | MEDIUM — new government signals, relationship resets |
| Opposition wins, transition contested | HIGH — uncertainty period, patron reassessment |
| Coup following disputed election | VERY HIGH — all bets off |
| Election postponed or cancelled | HIGH — institutional crisis signal |

## Geopolitically Significant Election Patterns

### The "Rally Around the Flag" Mechanism
Leaders facing low approval ratings or competitive elections sometimes initiate or escalate foreign conflicts to boost domestic support.

**Preconditions:** Low approval + competitive election + available target + manageable escalation risk
**Historical cases:** Falklands (1982 — though disputed as deliberate), various territorial disputes timed to election cycles

**Detection:** Look for foreign policy escalation in the 3–6 months before a competitive election where the incumbent is polling badly.

### Alliance Policy Reversal Risk
When an election brings a new government with different foreign policy orientation:
- New government may withdraw from alliance commitments
- Troops may be pulled back, basing agreements renegotiated
- Sanctions policies may shift (new government may normalize with sanctioned state)
- Examples: 2019 Argentina pivot, various Sahel governments post-coup abandoning French ties

### The Lame Duck Window
Between election result and new government taking power:
- Incumbent may sign agreements, make appointments, or take military action that binds successor
- Intelligence services and military may position themselves with new leadership
- External actors may try to lock in agreements before transition

## Post-Election Assessment Template

After an election result, run this check before updating any existing assessment:

```
1. Did result match pre-election expectation? YES / NO / PARTIAL
   → If NO: revise patron network calculations, alliance commitment assumptions

2. Is the result being contested? YES / NO
   → If YES: enter high-uncertainty window; apply dispute risk framework

3. What foreign policy signals has winning party/candidate sent?
   → Map signals against: alliances, sanctions policy, territorial disputes, armed conflicts

4. Who are key foreign policy appointments in new government?
   → Appointments often signal policy direction more accurately than campaign rhetoric

5. Update tracking_log.jsonl with election outcome and foreign policy delta
```
