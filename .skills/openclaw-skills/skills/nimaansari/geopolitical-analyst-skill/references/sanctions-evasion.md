# Sanctions Evasion & Counter-Sanctions Strategy

## Purpose

When sanctions are imposed, targets don't just accept them. They adapt, evade, and counter.

This module maps:
- How do sanctioned actors evade?
- What evasion methods are available?
- How effective is evasion?
- What counter-evasion strategies work?

---

## Evasion Methods

### Method 1: Proxy Trading
**How it works:**
- Sanctioned actor trades through third countries
- Third country buys goods, re-exports to target
- Direct trade is illegal; indirect trade appears legal

**Example:**
- Iran buys oil through brokers in non-aligned countries
- Russia uses shell companies in Kazakhstan, UAE, Turkey
- North Korea uses Chinese intermediaries

**Detection difficulty:** MEDIUM (requires tracking supply chains)
**Effectiveness:** HIGH (difficult to block all proxies)
**Cost to evader:** Medium (markup costs, broker fees)

---

### Method 2: Cryptocurrency & Alternative Finance
**How it works:**
- Sanctioned actor uses cryptocurrency for transactions
- Transfers value without using sanctioned banking system
- Hard to trace; operates globally

**Example:**
- Iran uses cryptocurrency for oil sales
- Russia explores crypto for sanctions evasion
- North Korea uses crypto for cryptocurrency theft

**Detection difficulty:** VERY HIGH (crypto transactions hard to trace)
**Effectiveness:** MEDIUM (limited scale, new technology)
**Cost to evader:** Low (once infrastructure built)

---

### Method 3: Black Market & Smuggling
**How it works:**
- Illegal goods smuggling around blockade
- Official channels cut off; illegal channels flourish
- Often more expensive but available

**Example:**
- Venezuela smuggles oil despite sanctions
- Syria evades energy sanctions through smuggling
- North Korea uses smuggling networks

**Detection difficulty:** HIGH (underground, hard to track)
**Effectiveness:** MEDIUM (limited volume, high cost)
**Cost to evader:** High (smuggling premiums, risk)

---

### Method 4: Barter & In-Kind Trade
**How it works:**
- Direct exchange of goods (goods for goods, not cash)
- Bypasses financial sanctions
- Old method, effective

**Example:**
- Iran exchanges oil for goods directly
- Russia offers energy in exchange for food
- China uses barter with sanctioned countries

**Detection difficulty:** MEDIUM (visible if tracked)
**Effectiveness:** MEDIUM (limited to willing partners)
**Cost to evader:** Medium (inefficient exchange rates)

---

### Method 5: Sanctions-Compliant Supplier Diversification
**How it works:**
- Target finds non-sanctioning countries as suppliers
- Legal trade with countries not enforcing sanctions
- Creates alternative supply chains

**Example:**
- Russia sources semiconductors from India, China, Kazakhstan
- Iran gets oil equipment from non-sanctioning suppliers
- North Korea trades with Chinese, Russian willing partners

**Detection difficulty:** LOW (legal trade, visible)
**Effectiveness:** HIGH (addresses real needs)
**Cost to evader:** Medium (less efficient suppliers, longer supply chains)

---

### Method 6: Asset Restructuring & Offshoring
**How it works:**
- Move assets to countries outside sanction jurisdiction
- Create shell companies in non-sanctioning countries
- Hide real ownership

**Example:**
- Russian oligarch's assets moved to UAE, Turkey
- Iran moves corporate assets to countries without US jurisdiction
- Officials hide wealth in non-sanctioning countries

**Detection difficulty:** HIGH (requires financial investigation)
**Effectiveness:** HIGH (assets protected from seizure)
**Cost to evader:** Low (lawyers, offshore fees)

---

### Method 7: Sanctions Busting Technologies
**How it works:**
- Develop ways to produce sanctioned goods domestically
- Substitute technologies for sanctioned imports
- Reduces dependence on external supply

**Example:**
- Russia develops domestic chip production (slow, limited)
- Iran produces pharmaceuticals domestically
- Russia uses Soviet-era technology instead of modern imports

**Detection difficulty:** LOW (visible if detected)
**Effectiveness:** LOW-MEDIUM (domestic production usually inferior)
**Cost to evader:** HIGH (retooling, inefficiency)

---

## Counter-Evasion Strategies

### Counter 1: Secondary Sanctions
**Mechanism:** Sanction countries/companies that trade with target

**Example:** US threatens sanctions on companies that help Russia
**Effectiveness:** MEDIUM-HIGH (companies avoid sanctions)
**Cost:** May break coalition (other countries resent secondary sanctions)

### Counter 2: Supply Chain Monitoring
**Mechanism:** Track goods through multiple countries to detect proxying

**Example:** US monitors oil shipments to detect Iranian oil
**Effectiveness:** MEDIUM (detects some, not all)
**Cost:** Significant intelligence/diplomatic cost

### Counter 3: Enforcement Through Payment Systems
**Mechanism:** Use control of major payment systems (SWIFT, dollar system) to block transactions

**Example:** Russia partially cut from SWIFT; limits transaction capability
**Effectiveness:** HIGH (very difficult to circumvent)
**Cost:** Requires control of payment infrastructure

### Counter 4: Restrict Dual-Use Goods
**Mechanism:** Control export of goods that could have multiple uses

**Example:** Semiconductor export restrictions (can be civilian or military use)
**Effectiveness:** MEDIUM (difficult goods to control globally)
**Cost:** May hurt sender's own industry

### Counter 5: Increase Coalition Enforcement
**Mechanism:** Constant diplomatic pressure to prevent third countries from trading with target

**Example:** US pressure on UAE, Turkey not to trade with Russia
**Effectiveness:** MEDIUM (some countries comply, some don't)
**Cost:** Significant diplomatic capital

## Key Insight

**Sanctions are a race between:**
- Sender's ability to block evasion routes
- Target's ability to develop evasion methods

If target is adapting faster than sender is blocking, sanctions are failing.

If sender is blocking faster than target can adapt, sanctions are working.
