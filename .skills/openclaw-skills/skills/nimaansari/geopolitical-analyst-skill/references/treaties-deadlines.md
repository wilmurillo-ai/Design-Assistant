# Treaties, Agreements & Deadline Tracking

## Purpose

Ceasefires, peace agreements, arms control treaties, and nuclear deals have specific terms, review windows, and expiry dates. These are **predictable future inflection points** — some of the most reliable short-term forecasting opportunities in geopolitical analysis.

Missing a treaty deadline is a common analytical failure. This reference provides the framework for tracking them.

---

## Agreement Types and Their Analytical Significance

### Ceasefire Agreements
**Why they matter:** Most ceasefires are fragile. They have:
- Monitoring mechanisms (or lack thereof) that predict durability
- Violation thresholds that, when crossed, trigger re-escalation
- Expiry or renewal windows that concentrate political pressure

**Durability predictors:**
| Factor | More Durable | Less Durable |
|--------|------------|-------------|
| Third-party monitoring | Strong international presence | Self-monitored |
| Prisoner exchange completed | Yes | No |
| Territorial lines defined | Clearly marked | Ambiguous |
| Political process linked | Yes — ceasefire tied to talks | No — pure military pause |
| Mutual exhaustion | Both sides depleted | One side resting/rearming |
| External patron support | Patrons both want pause | One patron pushing for resumption |

**Violation tracking:** A ceasefire with escalating minor violations is the leading indicator of imminent collapse. Track violation frequency, severity, and attribution.

### Peace Agreements
**Key components to track:**
- Implementation timeline and milestones
- Which provisions are being implemented vs. stalled
- Who has verification authority
- Spoiler groups excluded from the agreement
- International guarantor commitments

**Warning signs of failure:**
- Implementation milestones missed without consequence
- Spoiler groups attacking implementation process
- Guarantor states disengaging
- Key provision (elections, disarmament, transitional justice) repeatedly delayed
- Political party formed from armed group fails to win elections → returns to violence

### Arms Control / Nuclear Agreements

**Active/Recent Major Agreements:**

| Agreement | Parties | Status | Key Dates |
|-----------|---------|--------|-----------|
| JCPOA (Iran nuclear) | Iran, US, UK, France, Germany, Russia, China | Effectively suspended (2018 US withdrawal, Iran countermeasures) | Sunset clauses 2025–2031 |
| New START | US, Russia | Suspended by Russia (2023) | — |
| CTBT (nuclear test ban) | 186 signatories | Not in force (requires specific states to ratify) | Ongoing |
| NPT (non-proliferation) | 191 states | In force; review conferences every 5 years | 2026 review |
| INF Treaty | US, Russia | Terminated (2019) | — |
| Open Skies Treaty | Multiple | US withdrew (2020); Russia suspended (2021) | — |
| Chemical Weapons Convention | 193 states | In force | Ongoing |
| Mine Ban Treaty (Ottawa) | 164 states | In force; US, Russia, China not party | Ongoing |

**JCPOA sunset clauses (analytically important):**
- 2025–2026: Restrictions on uranium enrichment levels expire
- 2031: All remaining restrictions expire
- Each expiry creates a decision point for all parties regardless of current agreement status

### Status of Forces Agreements (SOFAs) and Basing Agreements

Foreign military presence is governed by bilateral agreements with specific terms:
- SOFAs can be terminated with notice periods (typically 1 year)
- Basing agreement termination = strategic repositioning signal
- Recent examples: Niger (terminated French/US presence 2023–2024), Mali (expelled French forces)

**What to track:** When a host government gives notice to terminate a foreign military presence, map the strategic implications for regional power balance immediately — the vacuum is filled faster than most expect.

## Deadline Tracking Framework

When analyzing a conflict with active agreements, check:

```
1. Is there an active ceasefire agreement?
   → When was it signed? By whom? Who monitors it?
   → Is it being violated? Frequency/severity trend?
   → Does it have an expiry date or renewal window?

2. Is there an active peace process?
   → What are the implementation milestones?
   → Which have been met / missed?
   → When is the next scheduled review or milestone?

3. Are there relevant arms control agreements?
   → Which parties are bound?
   → Are there sunset clauses approaching?
   → Is there compliance dispute?

4. Are there basing/SOFA agreements?
   → Are any at risk of termination?
   → If terminated, what fills the vacuum?

5. Are there economic agreements with political dimensions?
   → Trade agreements with political conditions
   → Debt arrangements with governance conditionality
   → Aid agreements with performance milestones
```

## Agreement Violation Escalation Ladder

When monitoring an active ceasefire, violations escalate in predictable stages:

```
Stage 1: Minor technical violations (individual soldiers, unclear lines)
  → Normal; monitor frequency

Stage 2: Significant violations with denial (both sides blame each other)
  → Ceasefire under stress; increase monitoring

Stage 3: Acknowledged violations with justification ("response to provocation")
  → Ceasefire seriously degraded; prepare for collapse scenario

Stage 4: Open offensive action with ceasefire nominally in place
  → Ceasefire is collapsed in practice; formal declaration likely follows

Stage 5: Formal declaration that ceasefire is over
  → Return to open conflict; assess new military situation
```

**Key signal:** Transition from Stage 2 to Stage 3 (from denial to justification) is the most reliable leading indicator of collapse.
