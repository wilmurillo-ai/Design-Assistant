# Dopamine Design — Motivation Architecture for ADHD

> The science and practice of designing for dopamine-driven motivation systems.
> This is the UX philosophy file — why things are built the way they are.

---

## The Core Problem

Neurotypical motivation systems run on a relatively stable background of dopamine and norepinephrine that allows for consistent, sustained effort even on boring or aversive tasks.

ADHD motivation systems don't. The regulatory circuitry is different — not broken, different. Effort requires more neurochemical activation to fire. "Just try harder" is like telling someone to run faster without giving them legs.

The design implication: **motivation isn't something to be lectured about. It's something to be engineered.**

---

## What Actually Works for ADHD Motivation

### 1. Interest (Novelty + Engagement)
The ADHD brain lights up for new, interesting, or engaging tasks. Routine and repetition deplete dopamine faster.

**Engineering tactics:**
- Gamify: frame tasks as challenges, levels, or games
- Rotate: vary the environment, tools, or approach regularly
- Connect: link boring tasks to interesting contexts ("answer emails while listening to this album")
- Novelty injection: introduce something new about a stale task

### 2. Urgency (Deadline-Driven Activation)
Real or perceived time pressure creates neurochemical urgency that enables initiation.

**Engineering tactics:**
- Create micro-deadlines within larger tasks ("have a draft by 2pm, not by end of week")
- Use timers (Pomodoro, sprints) to create bounded urgency
- Body doubling creates social urgency
- Commit out loud to create accountability urgency

### 3. Challenge (Problem-Solving Activation)
The brain's reward circuit activates for challenging problems — not too easy (boring) and not too hard (anxiety-inducing). The Goldilocks zone.

**Engineering tactics:**
- Reframe tasks as puzzles to solve
- Set a "beat your best time" frame
- The challenge can be artificial ("can I do this in 10 minutes?")

### 4. Passion / Personal Significance
Connection to deep values or personal identity activates sustained motivation differently than external rewards.

**Engineering tactics:**
- Connect tasks to reasons that matter to the specific person
- "Why does this actually matter to you?" is a genuine question, not a coaching cliché
- Passion windows should be protected and leveraged, not just appreciated

## The 5-Minute Deal

**What it is:** The offer to do just 5 minutes of a task, with explicit permission to stop after.

**Why it works:**
- Removes the pressure of "I have to do all of this"
- ADHD initiation is the hard part — once started, momentum often carries
- It's a commitment the brain can actually accept
- The worst case (they stop after 5 minutes) still produced 5 minutes of progress

**How to use it:**
"Just 5 minutes. If it still sucks after 5 minutes, we stop. But usually it gets easier once you're in it."

**Only works when honest:** If you say "just 5 minutes" and then immediately pile on more, users learn not to trust the deal. Mean it.

## Reward Scheduling

**Variable ratio reinforcement** (rewards at unpredictable intervals) is more motivating than fixed-ratio (every time). This is why games are more compelling than checklists.

**Engineering tactics:**
- Celebrate wins unexpectedly, not formulaically
- Vary the size and type of celebration
- Surprise is dopamine-triggering

**Don't:**
- Reward the same way every time ("great job!" after every single small action becomes meaningless)
- Create reward systems so complex they require their own management

## Streak Psychology

**What works:** Short streaks with explicit break allowances. Knowing that breaking a streak doesn't mean starting over.

**What doesn't:** Punishing streak breaks. Creating shame around inconsistency.

**The ADHD-specific approach:**
- "We're going for average behavior, not perfect streaks"
- Distinguish between consistency (most days) and perfection (every day)
- "You've done this 4 out of the last 7 days. That's a real pattern."
- Reset streaks without ceremony. Yesterday was yesterday. Today is today.

**Minimum viable streak:** One day. The goal is to do something again, not to do it forever without interruption.

## Celebration Mechanics

Celebration is not optional. It's functional — celebration provides the dopamine hit that associates task completion with reward, which makes future completion more neurologically accessible.

**Rules for good celebration:**
1. **Specific, not generic.** "You made the three phone calls you'd been avoiding for a week" not "great job."
2. **Proportional but generous.** A dentist call deserves celebration. Opening a document deserves acknowledgment.
3. **Immediate.** Delay weakens the association.
4. **Varied.** Repeated identical celebration becomes background noise.

**Types of celebration:**
- Verbal (enthusiastic, specific acknowledgment)
- Streak reference ("3 days in a row")
- Historical callback ("remember when this felt impossible?")
- Progress visualization ("you're halfway through the week's tasks")

## Dopamine Breaks (Not Just Rest Breaks)

The ADHD brain doesn't just need rest — it needs dopamine recharging between high-demand tasks.

**Dopamine break ≠ scrolling your phone** (passive consumption can actually deplete certain people)

**Better dopamine breaks:**
- Short walk (movement recharges attention)
- Music (specific dopamine release)
- Brief social interaction (if energizing, not depleting)
- Something that uses a different modality (physical after mental)
- Something with a clear end state (quick game level, not open-ended browsing)

**Engineering:** Build dopamine breaks INTO plans, not just rest periods. Name them as recharges.

---

## Progressive Difficulty

Tasks should start easy and get harder — not because it's pedagogically correct, but because the win at the beginning builds dopamine momentum.

**"Easiest first" principle:**
In task-chunker output: always identify the EASIEST step first, not the most logical. Get a win. Build momentum. The order can flex.

---

## Interest-Based Engagement vs. Discipline-Based

**Neurotypical model:** I will do this task through sustained willpower and discipline.

**ADHD model:** I will do this task because it's interesting enough, urgent enough, challenging enough, or meaningful enough to fire my motivation circuitry.

**The design implication:** Don't try to make ADHD users more disciplined. Make the environment more interesting, more urgent, or more meaningful. Work with the operating system, not against it.

---

## Immediate vs. Delayed Rewards

ADHD brains show steeper temporal discounting — the gap between "reward now" and "reward later" is felt more intensely than average. The future feels less real.

**Engineering tactic:** Make future rewards feel more present.
- "How will you feel when this is done?" (make the relief concrete now)
- "What's the first thing you'll do after this?" (anchor post-task reward)
- Build in immediate small rewards alongside larger delayed ones
