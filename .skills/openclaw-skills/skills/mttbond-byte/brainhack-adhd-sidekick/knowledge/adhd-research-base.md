# ADHD Research Base

> Evidence base for design decisions. The agent uses this to explain why something works when asked.
> Not exhaustive — curated for operational relevance.

---

## Key Statistics

**Prevalence:**
- ADHD affects ~5-8% of children globally (APA estimates ~5%, some studies higher)
- ~2-5% of adults meet criteria; many are undiagnosed
- Diagnosis rates have increased, reflecting better recognition, not increased incidence

**Persistence:**
- 50-80% of ADHD children continue to meet criteria as adults
- Adult presentation often looks different: less hyperactivity, more inattention and executive dysfunction

**Heritability:**
- Among the most heritable psychiatric conditions: ~70-80% heritability
- Having a parent with ADHD gives children approximately 8x higher risk

**Co-occurring conditions:**
- ~50% of ADHD adults have at least one co-occurring condition
- Most common: anxiety disorders, depression, learning disabilities, substance use disorders
- This matters: treating only the ADHD without the co-occurring condition often underperforms

**Outcomes:**
- Unmanaged ADHD is associated with lower educational attainment, more frequent job changes, relationship difficulties, and higher accident rates
- These outcomes are from executive function deficits, not intelligence
- With appropriate support and management, outcomes normalize significantly

## Why Body Doubling Works

**What the research shows:**
- One survey study (Zylowska et al.) found 75% of adults with ADHD reported performing better with another person present
- The mechanism: external social presence activates a different attention circuit than self-directed attention
- This is why ADHD students often do homework in coffee shops — ambient social presence, not stimulation

**Limitation:** Most evidence is survey-based and anecdotal. The controlled studies are limited but consistent with the self-report data.

**The design implication:** Body doubling is one of the most consistently reported ADHD strategies. It works whether the person is physically present, on video, or virtually present (text-based). The agent as body double is functionally valid.

## Why Shame Makes ADHD Worse

**Neurological basis:**
ADHD already creates significant shame through years of "not trying hard enough," underperformance relative to perceived potential, and social feedback about being unreliable or disorganized.

Shame activates the threat response system, which actively suppresses prefrontal cortex function — the exact brain region already compromised by ADHD.

**The loop:** ADHD → underperformance → shame → reduced PFC function → more ADHD symptoms → more shame.

**The design implication:** Zero-shame design isn't just philosophically nice. It's functionally necessary. Shame literally makes ADHD worse.

## CBT for ADHD — What the Research Shows

**Adult ADHD and CBT:**
- Multiple RCTs (Safren et al., 2005; Solanto et al., 2010; Emilsson et al., 2011) show CBT specifically designed for ADHD executive function produces meaningful symptom reduction
- Effect sizes typically moderate — not a cure, a useful tool
- Works best as an adjunct to medication, not a replacement
- Most effective elements: behavioral activation, task planning, cognitive restructuring for ADHD-specific distortions

**What this means for the agent:**
CBT-informed approaches (reframing distortions, behavioral activation, implementation intentions) are evidence-based and appropriate to incorporate — at psychoeducation level, not clinical therapy.

## Implementation Intentions Research

**The finding** (Gollwitzer, 1999, and extensive follow-up research):
Forming a specific "when-then" plan dramatically increases follow-through on intentions.

Vague: "I'll exercise more" → ~30% follow-through
Specific: "I'll go for a 20-minute walk at 6pm on Monday, Wednesday, and Friday" → ~70-80% follow-through

**ADHD-specific application:**
The say-do gap in ADHD is partly an implementation planning gap. Adding the specifics (when, where, what first action) substantially closes it.

**Design implication:** Never accept vague commitments. "I'll work on it this week" → "When specifically? What's the first thing you'd do?"

## Time Blindness Research

**Malone & Ratey (2002):**
ADHD adults show impaired ability to estimate duration, reproduce intervals, and anticipate future time. They literally don't experience time the same way.

**Barkley's research:**
Frames ADHD partly as a disorder of time — difficulty connecting present actions to future consequences in a felt, motivating way. The future isn't real in the same neurological sense.

**Design implication:** Never say "just remember" about deadlines. Build external time cues: timers, anchored deadlines, regular check-ins. Don't ask ADHD users to rely on internal time awareness — augment it externally.

## The Interest-Based Motivation Model

**Barkley (2012) and others:**
ADHD executive function requires one of four neurological activators: interest, urgency, challenge, or passion. Without one, initiation often doesn't occur — not because of lack of effort, but because the circuit doesn't fire.

**This is why ADHD students can play video games for 8 hours but can't study for 30 minutes.** It's not about the difficulty of the task. It's about whether the task generates enough neurochemical activation.

**Design implication:** Motivation isn't a character trait in ADHD — it's a neurochemical event. Design tasks to be more interesting, urgent, or challenging. Don't lecture about discipline.

## Rejection Sensitive Dysphoria (RSD)

**Source:** Dr. William Dodson (2019, ADDitude Magazine) — coined the term and characterized the phenomenon.

**What it is:** An extreme emotional sensitivity to perceived rejection, criticism, failure, or teasing. Experienced as intense, overwhelming emotional pain that can feel like a sudden mood crash.

**Prevalence:** Estimated ~99% of ADHD adults experience this to some degree. Often the most impactful symptom for quality of life and relationships.

**ADHD connection:** RSD appears to be neurologically linked to ADHD — not a co-occurring condition, but a feature of the same underlying dysregulation. The same circuits that cause time blindness and task initiation deficits also affect emotional regulation.

**Design implication:** Build zero-shame culture. Never frame feedback as criticism when a reframe is possible. Be aware that a seemingly mild comment can land much harder than intended.

## Minimum Viable Routines

**Research basis:**
Habit formation research (Lally et al., 2010) found habits take an average of 66 days to form — but the range was 18-254 days. Perfection streaks are not required.

**Missing a day:** In the Lally study, missing one day did NOT significantly impact long-term habit formation. The "never miss twice" principle has some empirical backing.

**ADHD-specific:**
Standard habit formation research assumes neurotypical baseline consistency. ADHD brains have more variable days, higher likelihood of interruption, and steeper emotional responses to breaking patterns.

**The design implication:** Build minimum viable versions of everything. The 2-step routine that happens 70% of the time beats the 10-step routine that happens 20% of the time.
