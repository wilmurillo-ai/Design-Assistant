# ADHD & Executive Function — Operational Reference

> Not a textbook. An operational guide the agent uses to make better decisions in conversation.

---

## What Executive Function Actually Is

Executive function is the brain's management system — the prefrontal cortex processes that govern how we plan, start, regulate, and complete goal-directed behavior. ADHD primarily disrupts this system.

Key point: **ADHD is not a knowledge deficit. It's a performance deficit.** People with ADHD often know exactly what they should do. The gap is between knowing and doing.

## The Six Domains

### 1. Working Memory
**Clinical definition:** The ability to hold and manipulate information in mind while using it.

**What it looks like in ADHD:**
- Forgetting what you were doing mid-task
- Losing your train of thought while someone is still talking
- Can't hold a multi-step plan in mind while executing step one
- Reading a page and retaining nothing
- Walking into a room and forgetting why

**Performance implications:**
- Writing tasks become much harder (track argument + write simultaneously)
- Following multi-step instructions is genuinely difficult, not selective
- Context-switching causes greater information loss

**Design response:**
- Externalize working memory: write it down, say it back, repeat it
- Keep plans simple enough to fit on one screen
- Confirm understanding before moving on

### 2. Task Initiation
**Clinical definition:** The ability to begin a task or activity without undue prompting or delay.

**What it looks like in ADHD:**
- Knowing you need to do something for hours before starting
- Paralysis on tasks that feel too big, too vague, or too uncertain
- Starting only when deadline pressure is acute (crisis mode)
- "Warming up" for hours before actual work begins

**The neurological reason:**
ADHD brains require one of four activators to engage executive function:
- **Interest** (the task is novel or engaging)
- **Urgency** (deadline is imminent)
- **Challenge** (the task is a puzzle to solve)
- **Passion** (deeply connected to personal values or identity)

Without one of these, the initiation circuit doesn't fire. This is not laziness.

**Design response:**
- Name the first physical action ("open the file," not "work on the report")
- Use the 5-minute deal to lower activation energy
- Create artificial urgency or novelty when possible
- Never say "just start" — the starting IS the problem

---

### 3. Time Blindness
**Clinical definition:** Impaired sense of time passing; difficulty estimating duration; inability to feel urgency of future deadlines.

**What it looks like in ADHD:**
- "That's not due for a week" (feels like forever) suddenly becomes "that was due yesterday"
- Consistently underestimating how long tasks take (by 50-200%)
- Hyperfocus making hours feel like minutes
- Inability to feel the urgency of something that isn't happening NOW

**The ADHD time model:** Two time zones exist — Now and Not Now. Past and future don't have the same felt reality as the present.

**Design response:**
- Anchor to clock time, not relative time ("20 minutes" not "quick")
- Always add 50% to stated time estimates
- Break future deadlines into present actions
- Time-blocking makes time concrete and visible
- Regular external time cues help (timers, check-ins)

---

### 4. Emotional Dysregulation
**Clinical definition:** Difficulty modulating emotional responses; intense emotional reactions relative to perceived stimulus.

**What it looks like in ADHD:**
- Rejection Sensitive Dysphoria (RSD): intense emotional pain from perceived rejection or failure
- Frustration escalating faster than neurotypical peers
- Shame spirals after mistakes that seem disproportionate
- Emotional flooding that shuts down executive function entirely
- Difficulty recovering from emotional states quickly

**Key concept — Rejection Sensitive Dysphoria (RSD):**
Not every ADHD person experiences RSD, but for those who do, perceived rejection (criticism, correction, perceived disappointment) triggers a pain response that can be overwhelming. Not manipulation — a real neurological response.

**Design response:**
- Zero shame, always
- Acknowledge emotion before problem-solving
- Don't pathologize big feelings — normalize them
- Validate without amplifying
- Build in recovery time after emotional events

---

### 5. Flexible Thinking
**Clinical definition:** The ability to shift cognitive set — to adapt plans, tolerate change, consider multiple perspectives, and think around obstacles.

**What it looks like in ADHD:**
- "If X is broken, my whole plan is broken" — difficulty finding workarounds
- Stuck on one approach even when it's not working
- Difficulty tolerating ambiguity or changing instructions
- "Done" = all steps in planned order, can't skip or reorder
- Over-investment in a particular plan, difficulty letting go

**Design response:**
- Build in "if this breaks, here's Plan B" explicitly
- Minimum viable versions of every plan
- Validate that the original plan made sense
- Never just say "try something else" — offer the specific alternative

---

### 6. Planning and Organization
**Clinical definition:** The ability to look forward, sequence steps, manage complexity, and translate intention into action.

**What it looks like in ADHD:**
- Strong vision, weak execution pathway
- Knowing WHAT but not HOW to start
- Projects that are 80% done indefinitely
- Can't see the steps between "I should do this" and "it is done"
- Poor calibration between effort and time (underestimating complex tasks, overestimating simple ones)

**Design response:**
- Always ask "what would your hands do first?" not "what's your plan?"
- Break to physical, observable actions
- Celebrate partial completion as completion, not failure
- Identify "good enough" explicitly before starting

## The Say-Do Gap

**What it is:** The consistent difference between ADHD users' intentions and their actions.

Research consistently shows ADHD adults accurately predict their ideal behavior but systematically fail to execute it — not because of motivation but because of executive function gaps.

**Common manifestations:**
- "I'll definitely do it tonight" → 40% actually do
- "I'll do it in the morning when I'm fresh" → morning-self has no memory of evening-self's plan
- "This will only take an hour" → takes three
- "I work better under pressure" → true AND costs quality

**Implementation intentions work:**
Vague: "I'll work on the essay this weekend"
Specific: "I'll open my laptop at 2pm Saturday at the kitchen table and write for 20 minutes"

The more specific the implementation intention, the more likely execution. Date + time + place + first action.

## Overwhelm Thresholds

These are hard limits — not suggestions:

| Rule | Threshold |
|------|-----------|
| Options presented at once | Max 3 |
| Tasks in a day plan | Max 5 |
| Steps in a task breakdown | Max 7 (default); 10 in teeny-tiny mode |
| Minutes per task chunk | 15 max (default); 5 in teeny-tiny mode |
| Sentences in a maintenance message | Max 3 |
| Questions per message | Max 1 |

**Why these numbers matter:** ADHD working memory is smaller. Presenting 8 options doesn't give 8x information — it causes analysis paralysis. Less is genuinely more.

## Hyperfocus

**What it is:** A state of intense, sustained focus on an engaging task. Often misunderstood as evidence that ADHD isn't "real" — "they can focus when they want to."

**The reality:** Hyperfocus is not controlled voluntary attention. It's the brain's reward circuit locking onto a high-stimulation task and refusing to release. It can be incredibly productive OR incredibly destructive (hours lost to gaming, social media, rabbit holes).

**Productive hyperfocus:**
- Don't interrupt unless harmful
- Protect the conditions that enable it
- Surface a check-in at natural break points

**Problematic hyperfocus:**
- Gentle surface, not hard interruption
- "Hey — it's been [X] time. You want to keep going or take a break?"
- Never shame it; it's not a choice

## Medication Awareness

**On medication:**
- Peak focus window typically 2-4 hours post-dose
- Schedule hardest initiation tasks in this window
- Afternoon dose may extend window (if applicable)

**Off medication or unmedicated:**
- Interest-based engagement becomes more important
- External structure and accountability more critical
- Body doubling more effective
- Expectations adjusted proportionally — not zero, but lighter

**Agent config:** Medication status in USER.md. Adjust approach accordingly.

## Key Statistics (for agent citation)

- ADHD affects ~5-8% of children, ~2-5% of adults globally
- Up to 80% of ADHD children continue to meet criteria as adults
- ADHD is one of the most heritable psychiatric conditions (~80% heritability)
- Adults with ADHD are 3x more likely to have a co-occurring mood disorder
- Untreated ADHD is associated with significantly lower educational and employment outcomes — not because of intelligence, but executive function
- CBT specifically targeting ADHD executive function shows meaningful improvement in trials
- Body doubling has anecdotal support and emerging research backing — one study showed 75% of participants performed better with a body double present
