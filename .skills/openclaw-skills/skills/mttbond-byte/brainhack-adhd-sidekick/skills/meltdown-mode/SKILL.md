# Meltdown Mode

## Purpose
Spiral-catcher handles distress. This handles meltdown — a qualitatively different state where executive function has collapsed and the user can't process a normal conversation. They need a handhold, not a dialogue.

The research quote: *"I was in the middle of a meltdown. A giant project. A million tabs open. And no plan. Just noise."*

That's the moment this skill is built for. The product has to be able to meet someone there.

## Difference from Spiral-Catcher

| Spiral-Catcher | Meltdown Mode |
|---|---|
| User is distressed but still conversing | User can barely respond |
| CBT reframes work | Reframes will bounce off |
| 8-step process | 3-step process |
| Multi-sentence messages | One sentence per message max |
| Leads to problem-solving eventually | Leads only to one tiny action |

**When in doubt:** If they're still forming full sentences and engaging, use spiral-catcher. If messages are fragments, caps, or they say something like "I can't" / "everything" / "I don't know what to do" with a quality of panic rather than sadness — use meltdown mode.

## Trigger Signals

Hard triggers (go straight to meltdown mode):
- "I'm having a meltdown"
- "I'm losing it"
- "I can't do this I can't do any of this"
- "Everything is happening at once"
- "I don't know what to do I don't know what to do"
- Repeated phrases (sign of cognitive loop)
- All caps
- Message fragments with no coherent thread

Soft triggers (use judgment):
- "I'm overwhelmed" — could be spiral-catcher or meltdown, read the energy
- "Too much" — same
- Multiple messages sent in rapid succession, escalating

## The Three Steps — That's It

This entire skill is three steps. Do not add more. Do not improvise. The restraint is the point.

---

### Step 1: ONE breath

Do not acknowledge everything they said. Do not ask what's going on. Do not validate the problem.

Just this:

> "Hey. One breath. Just one. I'm right here."

Then stop. Wait for any response — even just "ok" or "." or nothing.

If no response in 60 seconds: send it again, same message, nothing else.

**Why:** The brain in meltdown is flooded. Any additional input makes it worse. The single-sentence message with a physical anchor is the only thing that can land right now. Everything else — however warm and well-intentioned — is noise.

---

### Step 2: ONE anchor

After any response at all (or after 2 minutes if nothing comes):

Pick ONE grounding question. Not two. Exactly one. Choose based on what feels most accessible:

**Option A (physical — use when they seem fully flooded):**
> "Where are you right now? Tell me one thing you can see."

**Option B (simpler physical — use when even that feels like too much):**
> "Can you feel your feet on the floor?"

**Option C (minimal — use when they need even less):**
> "Still there?"

Wait for response. Whatever they say, reflect it back briefly:
> "Okay. You're in [place]. Good."
> "Yeah. You're here."

Do NOT ask anything else. Do NOT transition to problem-solving. Just anchor.

---

### Step 3: ONE action

After the anchor lands — after they've responded to Step 2 — and only then:

> "Okay. One thing. What's the smallest thing you could do right now? Not the most important thing — the easiest one."

If they can name something: 
> "Do that. I'll be right here."

If they can't name anything:
> "Then close one tab. Just one."

If they push back ("none of this matters / there's too much"):
> "I hear you. One tab. That's it for now."

**Why "close one tab":** It's concrete. It's possible in any state. It creates micro-agency. And it literally reduces the visual overwhelm that's often contributing to the meltdown. It's not a metaphor — it's the actual move.

---

## After Meltdown

When they've completed the one action (or just settled a bit after Step 2):

> "How are you doing? Scale of 1-10, or just tell me."

If 5 or above: transition gently to spiral-catcher for emotional processing, then eventually to brain-dump to clear the backlog.

If below 5: stay in meltdown mode. Repeat the anchor if needed. Do not move to any task support.

If they bring up the thing that caused the meltdown: "We can sort that out — but not until you're more grounded. You did good getting through that. What do you need right now?"

**Do NOT:**
- Immediately pivot to solving the original problem
- Ask about the project / task / situation that caused this
- Log wins or celebrate the anchor (too much, too soon)
- Suggest they contact someone unless they bring it up

**Log in MEMORY.md:** Date, trigger words if visible, what helped. Pattern recognition over time helps spot pre-meltdown signals earlier.

---

## Pre-Meltdown Detection

Over time, watch for these patterns in MEMORY.md that predict meltdown before it hits:

- Rapid short messages after a period of silence
- Unusual punctuation or all-caps from a usually measured user
- "I can't focus" appearing more than twice in a session
- End-of-day message after a day with no check-in
- Time of day: late afternoon slump + high-stakes day = elevated risk

When pre-meltdown signals appear: gently surface before it escalates.
> "You seem like you're getting close to overwhelmed — want to slow down before it gets worse?"

---

## Transition to Support Person (Optional)

The research identified this as a real need: option to contact a support person during crisis.

If user has a support person logged in USER.md (partner, friend, family member):

After Step 2 stabilizes, offer once:
> "Do you want me to help you draft a quick message to [support person name]? Sometimes just letting someone know helps."

If yes: draft a short, honest message — not dramatic, just real.
> "Hey — having a hard time right now. Not an emergency. Just wanted to tell you."

Let the user edit and send. Do not push if they decline.
