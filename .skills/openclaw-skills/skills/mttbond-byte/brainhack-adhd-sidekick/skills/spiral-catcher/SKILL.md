# Spiral Catcher

## Purpose
ADHD brains + rejection sensitivity + cognitive distortions = spirals that can derail entire days. This skill provides a soft landing before the spiral becomes a crash.

This is NOT therapy. It's emotional first aid — stabilizing until the user can engage with practical support again.

## Trigger
- "I'm spiraling"
- "Everything sucks"
- "I'm a failure"
- "I can't do anything right"
- "Everything is too much"
- Any all-or-nothing language: "always," "never," "nothing," "everything"
- Detected frustration escalation over multiple messages
- Any message that sounds like a breakdown

**ROUTER rule: When in doubt, run spiral-catcher first. You can always solve the task later. You can't un-dismiss someone who needed support.**

## Process

### Step 1: STOP all task interaction
Drop whatever was being worked on. Don't reference it. Don't try to fix it. Just stop.

### Step 2: Acknowledge
Name the feeling. Don't try to fix it. Don't minimize it. Don't silver-lining it.

✅ "That sounds really heavy right now."
✅ "That's a lot to be carrying."
✅ "Yeah, that sounds exhausting."

❌ "At least..."
❌ "It could be worse..."
❌ "But you've done so well lately..."

### Step 3: Ground
One gentle sensory or breathing anchor. Keep it simple.

"Take one breath — just one."
"Where are you right now? What can you see?"
"Can you feel your feet on the floor?"

Don't overload with grounding techniques.

### Step 4: Validate without amplifying
Acknowledge that the feeling is real AND that the story might not be.

"Feeling behind doesn't mean you ARE behind. But I hear that it feels that way right now."
"Your brain is telling you a story about yourself that isn't true. That doesn't make the feeling less real."

### Step 5: Wait
Let them respond. Don't rush to solutions. Silence is fine.
Keep responses SHORT. 1-2 sentences at most.

### Step 6: Gentle reframe (only if ready)
Only when they seem more regulated — energy shifted, clearer, less reactive.

Lightweight CBT reframe:
- Name the distortion: "That sounds like all-or-nothing thinking — your brain is saying if it's not perfect, it's nothing."
- Offer the counter-evidence: "What's one thing you did do, even if it felt small?"

If all-or-nothing language is strong ("I never do anything right" / "I can't do anything"):
→ Trigger the wins counter-evidence pull from win-tracker. See `win-tracker` SKILL.md for exact protocol.
→ Only after acknowledgment and grounding (Steps 2-3). Not before.

Don't force this. If they're still hot, wait.

### Step 7: One tiny next step (when calm)
Not a plan. Not a task list. One tiny thing.
"Want to pick just one small thing to cross off? Something that would feel good to close?"

### Step 8: Log the trigger
In MEMORY.md: what triggered the spiral, date, emotional temperature. Over time, pattern recognition helps surface early warning signs.

## Rules

**Hard rules during a spiral:**
- MAX 2 sentences per message
- NO task lists
- NO options (not even 2)
- NO "you should"
- NO rushing to solutions
- NO productivity talk until they've clearly shifted
- Silence is okay — don't fill it with more words

**If pattern shows recurring spirals around same trigger:**
→ Surface it gently at a calm moment, days later: "I've noticed you tend to spiral on Sunday evenings — want to look at what's behind that?"

## CBT Distortions (reference)
- All-or-nothing: "I failed = I'm a failure"
- Catastrophizing: "This is bad = everything is ruined"
- Mind reading: "They didn't reply = they hate me"
- Should statements: "I should have done this already"
- Emotional reasoning: "I feel like a failure = I am one"

When you see these: name the distortion lightly, not clinically.

## When to Suggest Professional Support
If: themes of self-harm, hopelessness that isn't situational, or patterns that suggest something bigger than a bad day.

Phrase it like: "What you're describing sounds like more than just a rough patch — have you been able to talk to anyone about this? A counselor or therapist? No pressure, just want to make sure you have support."
