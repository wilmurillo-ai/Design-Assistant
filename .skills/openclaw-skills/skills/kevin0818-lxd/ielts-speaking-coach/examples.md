## Example 1: Part 1 — Work & Studies (Band 6.0)

**Input**: "I am a student. I study business. It is good. I like my school because it is big and has many people."

**Part**: 1 | **Topic**: work_study

### Score Breakdown

| Criterion | Band | Evidence |
|-----------|------|----------|
| Fluency & Coherence | 6.0 | Short, simple sentences with minimal linking. No discourse markers. Coherent but lacks development. ~20 WPM equivalent for the brevity. |
| Lexical Resource | 5.5 | Very basic vocabulary ("good", "big", "many people"). No idiomatic expressions or collocations. Repetitive structure. |
| Grammar | 6.5 | Simple structures are error-free (S-V agreement correct, articles correct). However, no complex structures attempted — only simple SVO patterns. |
| Pronunciation | 6.0 | (Estimated: median of FC/LR/GRA — no audio) |

**Overall Band**: 6.0

### Grammar Corrections

No grammatical errors found. The sentences are correct but simple.

### Vocabulary Upgrades

| Original | Alternatives | Reason |
|----------|-------------|--------|
| "It is good" | "I find it quite rewarding", "It's been really engaging", "I'm really enjoying it" | When discussing studies, experiential expressions convey genuine opinion better than generic "good". |
| "big" | "fairly large", "pretty spacious", "quite sizeable" | Campus descriptions benefit from more precise size collocations with spoken hedges. |
| "has many people" | "has a really diverse student body", "is quite vibrant", "has a real buzz about it" | University descriptions use community-oriented collocations rather than counting "people". |

### Model Answer (Band 7.0)

Well, I'm currently studying business at university, and to be honest, I'm really enjoying it. The campus is quite sizeable, and there's a really diverse student body, which I think makes the whole experience more interesting. I suppose what I like most is that I get to meet people from all sorts of backgrounds.

### Study Tips

1. **Extend your answers**: Part 1 expects 2-4 sentences, but add reasons and examples. Use "because", "which means", "for instance".
2. **Use discourse markers**: Start with "Well," or "To be honest," to sound more natural.
3. **Replace generic adjectives**: Swap "good/nice/big" for topic-specific collocations.

## Example 2: Part 2 — Describe a Trip (Band 7.5)

**Input**: "I'd like to talk about a trip I took to Japan last year. It was actually my first time visiting Asia, and I have to say, the cultural differences really blew me away. The architecture was incredible — I mean, the attention to detail in temples and traditional buildings was unlike anything I'd seen before. And the food, well, it was out of this world. I tried ramen at this tiny little place in Tokyo, and it was hands down the best I've ever had. The people were incredibly polite too, which made the whole experience feel very welcoming. If I had the chance, I'd definitely go back. It's one of those places that really stays with you."

**Part**: 2 | **Topic**: narrative / travel

### Score Breakdown

| Criterion | Band | Evidence |
|-----------|------|----------|
| Fluency & Coherence | 7.5 | Long turn sustained without noticeable effort. Good use of discourse markers ("actually", "I mean", "well", "I have to say"). Logical narrative progression. |
| Lexical Resource | 7.5 | Idiomatic expressions: "blew me away", "out of this world", "hands down", "stays with you". Topic-appropriate collocations for travel narrative. Some less common items used effectively. |
| Grammar | 8.0 | Wide range: conditionals ("If I had the chance"), relative clauses ("which made"), past perfect ("I'd seen"), comparatives ("the best I've ever had"). No errors detected. |
| Pronunciation | 7.5 | (Estimated: median of FC/LR/GRA — no audio) |

**Overall Band**: 7.5

### Grammar Corrections

No errors found.

### Vocabulary Upgrades

| Original | Alternatives | Reason |
|----------|-------------|--------|
| "incredible" | "absolutely stunning", "genuinely breathtaking" | Travel architecture descriptions benefit from more vivid sensory collocations. |
| "incredibly polite" | "exceptionally courteous", "remarkably hospitable" | Describing people in cultural contexts, "hospitable" captures warmth better than "polite". |

### Model Answer (Band 8.5)

I'd love to tell you about a trip I took to Japan last year — it was my first foray into Asia, and honestly, it completely exceeded my expectations. What really struck me was the meticulous attention to detail everywhere you looked, particularly in the traditional architecture. The temples had this sense of quiet elegance that I found genuinely moving. And the food — well, where do I even begin? I stumbled upon this hole-in-the-wall ramen place in a back street of Tokyo, and it was hands down the most flavourful meal I've ever had. What really topped it off was how welcoming everyone was. There's this innate sense of respect in Japanese culture that makes you feel completely at ease. I'd go back in a heartbeat — it's one of those rare places that leaves a lasting impression on you.

### Study Tips

1. **Vary sentence openers**: You start several sentences with "The..." — try fronting ("What really struck me was...") or inversions.
2. **Deepen description**: Add more sensory detail — sounds, smells, textures — to push from 7.5 toward 8.0.

## Example 3: Part 3 — Technology and Society (Band 6.0)

**Input**: "Technology is important. It helps us do many things. Computers and phones are useful. But sometimes it is bad because people play games all day. I think technology will be more important in the future."

**Part**: 3 | **Topic**: abstract / technology

### Score Breakdown

| Criterion | Band | Evidence |
|-----------|------|----------|
| Fluency & Coherence | 5.5 | Very short turn for Part 3. Simple listing without development. Only one connector ("But"). No abstract discussion or argumentation. |
| Lexical Resource | 5.5 | Generic vocabulary ("important", "useful", "bad", "many things"). No topic-specific collocations for technology discussion. No academic or analytical vocabulary. |
| Grammar | 6.5 | Simple structures correct. One complex attempt ("because people play games") but limited range. No conditionals, passives, or relative clauses. |
| Pronunciation | 5.5 | (Estimated: median of FC/LR/GRA — no audio) |

**Overall Band**: 6.0

### Grammar Corrections

No grammar errors found. The sentences are correct but simple; Part 3 would benefit from more complex structures (conditionals, passives, relative clauses).

### Vocabulary Upgrades

| Original | Alternatives | Reason |
|----------|-------------|--------|
| "important" | "indispensable", "pivotal", "integral to modern life" | Technology discussion requires analytical vocabulary that conveys degree of significance. |
| "helps us do many things" | "has streamlined countless aspects of our daily lives", "has revolutionized how we communicate and work" | Abstract discussion demands specific examples rather than vague generalizations. |
| "bad" | "counterproductive", "has its drawbacks", "can be a double-edged sword" | Evaluative discussion vocabulary should convey nuance, not binary good/bad. |
| "play games all day" | "become excessively reliant on screens", "struggle with digital addiction" | Analytical framing of social issues uses academic collocations for Part 3. |

### Model Answer (Band 7.5)

From my perspective, technology has become pretty much indispensable in modern life. I mean, it's fundamentally changed the way we communicate, work, and even think about things. Take smartphones, for instance — they've basically put the entire world at our fingertips, which is remarkable when you think about it. That said, I do think there's a flip side. I've noticed that a lot of people, especially younger generations, have become quite dependent on their devices, and it can actually hinder real social interaction. So I suppose it's a bit of a double-edged sword. Looking ahead, I'd say technology will only become more embedded in our daily routines, but hopefully we'll find a better balance between embracing innovation and maintaining genuine human connection.

### Study Tips

1. **Develop arguments**: Part 3 requires "why" and "how" — not just "what". Use "This is because...", "The reason for this is...", "What this means is...".
2. **Use hedging language**: "I'd argue that", "It could be said that", "To some extent" — these show analytical thinking.
3. **Give specific examples**: Replace "many things" with concrete instances to demonstrate lexical resource.
