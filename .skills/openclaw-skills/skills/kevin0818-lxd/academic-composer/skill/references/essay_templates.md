# Essay Templates

Reference templates for Phase 1 (outline generation) and Phase 2 (essay expansion). The LLM should adapt these structures to the user's specific topic and requirements.

---

## 1. Argumentative / Persuasive

**Purpose:** Defend a thesis with evidence and counterargument handling.

**Structure (with source mapping):**

```
1. Introduction (1 paragraph)
   - Hook: surprising statistic, provocative question, or brief anecdote
   - Context: 2-3 sentences of background
   - Thesis statement: clear, arguable claim
   - Sources: [S_] for hook statistic if applicable

2. Body Paragraph 1 — Strongest argument
   - Topic sentence (claim)
   - Evidence: [S_] — specific finding, data point, or quote
   - Evidence: [S_] — corroborating source (optional)
   - Analysis: explain WHY the evidence supports the claim
   - Transition

3. Body Paragraph 2 — Supporting argument
   - Topic sentence (claim)
   - Evidence: [S_] — specific finding
   - Analysis: connect to thesis

4. Body Paragraph 3 — Counterargument + rebuttal
   - Acknowledge opposing view: [S_] — source for counterargument
   - Refute with evidence: [S_] — source for rebuttal
   - Reinforce original thesis

5. Conclusion (1 paragraph)
   - Restate thesis in new words
   - Synthesize key points (do NOT just summarize)
   - Closing thought: implication, call to action, or broader significance
```

**Word count:** 1000-1500 words typical
**Citation style:** APA, MLA, or Chicago depending on user preference
**Register:** Formal academic; third person unless otherwise specified

## 2. Expository / Analytical

**Purpose:** Explain a topic, process, or concept in depth without arguing for a position.

**Structure (with source mapping):**

```
1. Introduction
   - Define the subject or concept
   - State the scope (what will be covered)
   - Thesis: analytical claim or framing question

2. Body Paragraph 1 — Aspect/dimension A
   - Topic sentence
   - Explanation: [S_] — key source for this dimension
   - Examples: [S_] — supporting data or case study
   - Connect to thesis

3. Body Paragraph 2 — Aspect/dimension B
   - Topic sentence
   - Explanation: [S_] — key source
   - Connect to thesis

4. Body Paragraph 3 — Aspect/dimension C
   - Topic sentence
   - Explanation: [S_] — key source
   - Connect to thesis

5. (Optional) Body Paragraph 4 — Implications or applications
   - Sources: [S_] for real-world application data

6. Conclusion
   - Summarize the analysis
   - Answer the framing question or confirm the analytical claim
   - Suggest further inquiry or relevance
```

**Word count:** 800-1200 words typical
**Register:** Neutral, objective, informative
**Key rule:** No personal opinion unless the prompt asks for evaluation

## 3. Compare and Contrast

**Purpose:** Examine similarities and differences between two or more subjects.

**Structure — Block method (with source mapping):**

```
1. Introduction
   - Introduce both subjects
   - State the basis for comparison: [S_] — framework or prior comparative study
   - Thesis: what the comparison reveals

2. Subject A — Full analysis
   - Criterion 1: [S_] — source for Subject A data
   - Criterion 2: [S_]
   - Criterion 3: [S_]

3. Subject B — Full analysis (same criteria)
   - Criterion 1: [S_] — source for Subject B data
   - Criterion 2: [S_]
   - Criterion 3: [S_]

4. Synthesis
   - Direct comparison of findings
   - Which criteria show similarity vs. difference: [S_] — interpretive framework
   - What the pattern reveals

5. Conclusion
   - Judgment or insight from the comparison
   - Broader significance
```

**Structure — Point-by-point method (with source mapping):**

```
1. Introduction + thesis

2. Criterion 1: Subject A [S_] vs. Subject B [S_]
3. Criterion 2: Subject A [S_] vs. Subject B [S_]
4. Criterion 3: Subject A [S_] vs. Subject B [S_]

5. Conclusion
```

**Word count:** 1000-1500 words typical
**Key rule:** Use consistent criteria; do not discuss features for one subject that you ignore for the other

## 4. Narrative / Reflective

**Purpose:** Tell a story or reflect on an experience with analytical depth.

**Structure:**

```
1. Opening scene
   - Vivid, specific moment that hooks the reader
   - Sensory details (sight, sound, texture)
   - Establish stakes or tension

2. Background / Context
   - What led to this moment
   - Relevant personal or situational context

3. Rising action / Development
   - Events unfold chronologically or thematically
   - Internal thoughts and reactions
   - Dialogue if appropriate

4. Turning point / Insight
   - The moment of change, realization, or key event
   - What shifted in understanding

5. Reflection / Conclusion
   - What was learned
   - How the experience connects to a broader theme
   - Avoid cliched "lesson learned" endings — aim for nuance
```

**Word count:** 800-1200 words typical
**Register:** First person; conversational but literate
**Key rule:** Show, don't tell. Concrete details over abstract statements.

---

## General Guidelines (all types)

- **Paragraph length:** 4-8 sentences. Avoid single-sentence paragraphs in academic writing.
- **Transitions:** Use logical connectors between paragraphs, not mechanical "Furthermore" / "Moreover" patterns.
- **Voice:** Vary sentence length and structure. Mix short declarative sentences with longer complex ones.
- **Vocabulary:** Prefer precise, plain words over inflated vocabulary (avoid: delve, intricate, tapestry, pivotal, underscore, landscape, foster).

## Source Mapping Rules

- Replace `[S_]` placeholders with actual source IDs from the Source List (e.g., `[S1]`, `[S3]`)
- Every body paragraph should cite at least one source
- The introduction may cite a source for the hook (statistic, finding) but is not required to
- The conclusion should not introduce new sources
- Narrative/reflective essays may have fewer citations
- See `skill/references/citation_formats.md` for in-text citation and reference list formatting
