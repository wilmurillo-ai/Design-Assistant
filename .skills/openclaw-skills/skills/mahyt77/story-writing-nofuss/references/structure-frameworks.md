# Story Structure Frameworks

Choose the framework that best fits your story. Each provides a different lens for organizing plot and pacing.

---

## 1. Three-Act Structure

The classic Western narrative structure.

### Act 1: Setup (25%)
- **Introduce** the ordinary world
- **Establish** the protagonist's want vs. need
- **Inciting Incident** disrupts status quo
- **Plot Point 1** commits hero to journey

### Act 2: Confrontation (50%)
- **Rising action** with obstacles
- **Midpoint** shifts context (false victory/defeat)
- **Complications** escalate
- **All Is Lost** moment

### Act 3: Resolution (25%)
- **Climax** - final confrontation
- **Resolution** - new equilibrium
- **Denouement** - aftermath

**Best for:** Commercial fiction, thrillers, adventure, most genre fiction.

---

## 2. Save the Cat (15 Beats)

Blake Snyder's screenwriting method, adaptable to novels.

| Beat | % | Purpose |
|------|---|---------|
| Opening Image | 0% | Before snapshot |
| Theme Stated | 5% | Lesson hinted |
| Set-Up | 1-10% | Status quo |
| Catalyst | 12% | Inciting incident |
| Debate | 12-20% | Hesitation |
| Break into Two | 20% | Commitment |
| B Story | 22% | Relationship begins |
| Fun and Games | 20-50% | Promise of premise |
| Midpoint | 50% | Stakes raised |
| Bad Guys Close In | 50-75% | Pressure mounts |
| All Is Lost | 75% | Lowest point |
| Dark Night | 75-80% | Processing loss |
| Break into Three | 80% | Solution found |
| Finale | 80-100% | Final battle |
| Final Image | 100% | After snapshot |

**Best for:** Commercial fiction, screenplays, fast-paced stories.

---

## 3. Hero's Journey (Monomyth)

Joseph Campbell's mythic structure, refined by Vogler.

1. **Ordinary World** - Hero's normal life
2. **Call to Adventure** - Challenge presented
3. **Refusal of the Call** - Hesitation, fear
4. **Meeting the Mentor** - Guidance received
5. **Crossing the Threshold** - Commitment to journey
6. **Tests, Allies, Enemies** - Learning the new world
7. **Approach** - Nearing the goal
8. **Ordeal** - Central crisis, death/rebirth
9. **Reward** - Seizing the sword
10. **The Road Back** - Return journey begins
11. **Resurrection** - Final test, transformation
12. **Return with Elixir** - Boon for ordinary world

**Best for:** Epic fantasy, adventure, mythic storytelling, character transformation arcs.

---

## 4. Fichtean Curve

In medias res structure with escalating crises.

- **Start in action** - No lengthy setup
- **Series of crises** - Each more intense
- **Climax** - Peak confrontation
- **Falling action** - Quick resolution

**Best for:** Thrillers, mysteries, literary fiction, stories that benefit from immediate tension.

---

## 5. Kishōtenketsu (Eastern Structure)

Traditional East Asian four-act structure.

1. **Ki (Introduction)** - Establish characters/world
2. **Shō (Development)** - Expand without conflict
3. **Ten (Twist)** - Unexpected turn, recontextualization
4. **Ketsu (Conclusion)** - Synthesis, harmony

**Key difference:** No direct conflict required; meaning comes from juxtaposition.

**Best for:** Slice of life, literary fiction, horror, stories emphasizing mood over plot.

---

## Choosing Your Framework

**Ask yourself:**

1. **What genre am I writing?** (Match framework to reader expectations)
2. **Is my story character-driven or plot-driven?** (Hero's Journey vs. Fichtean)
3. **Do I want conflict or contrast?** (Western vs. Eastern structure)
4. **Am I writing to market?** (Save the Cat for commercial, Three-Act for general)

**Remember:** Frameworks are tools, not rules. Adapt as needed.
