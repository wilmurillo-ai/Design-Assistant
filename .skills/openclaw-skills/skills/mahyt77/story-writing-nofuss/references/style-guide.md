# Writing Style Guide

Standards and best practices for quality writing.

---

## Core Principles

### Show, Don't Tell
**Instead of:** "He was angry."
**Write:** "His knuckles whitened around the steering wheel."

**Instead of:** "She felt sad."
**Write:** "She stared at the rain-streaked window, not seeing the city lights beyond."

### Active Voice
**Passive:** "The ball was thrown by the boy."
**Active:** "The boy threw the ball."

**Exception:** Use passive when the receiver is more important than the doer, or when the doer is unknown.

### Strong Verbs
**Weak:** "He walked quickly to the door."
**Strong:** "He rushed to the door."

**Weak:** "She said loudly."
**Strong:** "She shouted."

---

## Common Issues to Avoid

### Filter Words
Words that distance the reader from the character's experience:

- felt
- saw
- heard
- noticed
- realized
- watched
- looked
- seemed
- appeared

**Instead of:** "She felt the cold wind on her face."
**Write:** "The cold wind bit her cheeks."

### Adverbs
Often indicate weak verbs. Use sparingly.

**Instead of:** "He ran quickly."
**Write:** "He sprinted."

**Instead of:** "She said quietly."
**Write:** "She whispered."

### Crutch Words
Words that add little meaning:

- very
- really
- just
- somehow
- suddenly
- actually
- basically
- literally

**Instead of:** "It was very cold."
**Write:** "The air froze in her lungs."

### Telling Emotions
**Instead of:** "He was nervous."
**Write:** "He checked his watch for the third time in a minute."

**Instead of:** "She was happy."
**Write:** "A smile broke across her face before she could stop it."

## Dialogue

### Tags
- Use "said" and "asked" most of the time (they become invisible)
- Use other tags (whispered, shouted, muttered) sparingly
- Often better: use action beats instead of tags

**Good:**
"I don't know." She turned away, hiding her face.

**Okay:**
"I don't know," she whispered.

**Avoid:**
"I don't know," she whispered quietly. (redundant)

### Natural Speech
- People interrupt each other
- Use contractions (don't, can't, won't)
- Vary sentence length
- Include pauses, hesitations

**Instead of:**
"I do not know what you are talking about. I have never been to that location."

**Write:**
"I don't... I've never been there."

### Attribution
- Place tags where natural pauses occur
- Vary placement (beginning, middle, end)
- Don't over-explain who's speaking if it's clear

## Pacing

### Sentence Length
- **Short sentences** for action, tension, impact
- **Medium sentences** for normal narration
- **Long sentences** for description, reflection, complex ideas

**Example:**
He froze. The door creaked open. Shadows stretched across the floor. In the silence, he could hear his own heartbeat, a frantic drum against his ribs, each beat counting down the seconds until something—anything—happened.

### Paragraph Length
- **Short paragraphs** (1-3 sentences) for dialogue, action, emphasis
- **Medium paragraphs** (4-6 sentences) for normal narration
- **Long paragraphs** (7+ sentences) for description, exposition (use sparingly)

### Chapter Length
- Vary chapter length for pacing
- Short chapters can create momentum
- Long chapters can allow deep immersion
- End chapters with hooks (questions, revelations, decisions)

## Point of View

### First Person (I/me)
- Immediate, intimate
- Limited to what narrator knows/experiences
- Strong voice required
- Common in YA, mystery, literary

### Third Person Limited (he/she/they)
- Most flexible
- Can follow one character per scene/chapter
- Allows some distance while maintaining intimacy
- Most common in commercial fiction

### Third Person Omniscient
- God-like perspective
- Can show multiple characters' thoughts
- Can provide commentary
- Difficult to master; can feel distant

### Tense
- **Past tense:** Most common, feels traditional
- **Present tense:** Immediate, cinematic, common in YA/thrillers
- **Choose one and stick to it**

## Description

### Sensory Details
Use all five senses:

**Sight:** "The sky bruised purple with coming storm."
**Sound:** "Rain tapped a hesitant rhythm on the roof."
**Smell:** "The air smelled of wet earth and ozone."
**Touch:** "The fabric was rough against her skin."
**Taste:** "Metallic fear coated her tongue."

### Specificity
**Vague:** "He drove a car."
**Specific:** "He drove a '67 Mustang, cherry red with a dent in the left fender."

**Vague:** "She lived in a house."
**Specific:** "She lived in a Craftsman bungalow with peeling blue paint."

### Integration
Weave description into action:

**Instead of:** "The room was large. There was a fireplace. Books lined the walls."
**Write:** "She crossed the room, her footsteps echoing on the hardwood. Firelight danced across leather-bound books that lined the walls from floor to ceiling."

## Common Genre Conventions

### Fantasy/Sci-Fi
- **World-building:** Show through character experience
- **Magic/tech:** Establish rules, follow them
- **Names:** Make them pronounceable, memorable
- **Exposition:** Avoid info-dumps

### Mystery/Thriller
- **Pacing:** Keep it moving
- **Clues:** Plant them early, payoff later
- **Suspense:** Withhold information strategically
- **Twists:** Must be fair (reader could have guessed)

### Romance
- **Chemistry:** Show through interaction, not just description
- **Conflict:** Internal and external obstacles
- **Emotion:** Deep point of view
- **Satisfaction:** Ending must feel earned

### Literary
- **Language:** Attention to prose quality
- **Theme:** Explored through character and plot
- **Character:** Depth over plot sometimes
- **Voice:** Distinct, consistent

### YA
- **Voice:** Authentic teen perspective
- **Pacing:** Faster than adult
- **Themes:** Coming of age, identity, firsts
- **Length:** Typically 60,000-80,000 words

## Revision Checklist

### First Pass: Big Picture
- [ ] Plot makes sense
- [ ] Character arcs complete
- [ ] Pacing works
- [ ] Theme clear but not preachy

### Second Pass: Scene Level
- [ ] Each scene has purpose
- [ ] Conflict in every scene
- [ ] Show vs. tell balanced
- [ ] Dialogue natural

### Third Pass: Sentence Level
- [ ] Strong verbs
- [ ] Minimal adverbs
- [ ] Active voice
- [ ] Varied sentence structure

### Fourth Pass: Polish
- [ ] Spelling/grammar
- [ ] Consistency (names, details)
- [ ] Read aloud for rhythm
- [ ] Beta reader feedback addressed

## Quick Reference

### Do
- ✓ Use specific details
- ✓ Vary sentence length
- ✓ Show emotions through action
- ✓ Read dialogue aloud
- ✓ End chapters with hooks

### Don't
- ✗ Overuse adverbs
- ✗ Tell emotions directly  
- ✗ Use clichés
- ✗ Info-dump
- ✗ Head-hop (change POV mid-scene)

### Red Flags
- "Suddenly" (show the suddenness)
- "Very" (use stronger word)
- "Began to" (just do the thing)
- "Felt" (show the feeling)
- "There was" (often weak opening)

## Exercises

### 1. Filter Word Hunt
Find all instances of "felt," "saw," "heard," etc. Rewrite without them.

### 2. Adverb Elimination
Find adverbs. Can you replace verb+adverb with a stronger verb?

### 3. Dialogue Tags
Replace dialogue tags with action beats.

### 4. Sentence Variety
Analyze a page: count sentences, average length, variation.

### 5. Sensory Audit
Pick a scene. Mark each sense used. Add missing ones.

---

*Good writing is rewriting. Use this guide, but develop your own voice.*
