## Free Indirect Discourse (FID)

One of literary fiction's most powerful tools — the narrator adopts the character's
voice and perspective *without* announcing it with "she thought" or "he felt".

**Direct thought:** *She thought: I can't keep doing this.*
**FID:** *She couldn't keep doing this. She simply couldn't.*

The reader is now inside the character's head without a quotation mark in sight.
FID allows irony: the narrator can subtly undercut the character's logic while
appearing to relay it faithfully. It also creates intimacy — the reader is
given access they didn't ask for.

**Common failure mode:** Authors switch in and out of FID without control,
creating POV instability. If one paragraph is FID and the next is omniscient
narrator, the reader feels unsettled without knowing why.

## The Iceberg Principle (Hemingway / Carver)

The surface of a scene carries only a fraction of the meaning. The weight is
underwater: backstory, subtext, implication, the conversation that is not
happening but is present in every line.

**Technique:** Write the scene as if the characters know far more than they're
saying. Then cut the parts where they say it.

**Test:** Can you delete a line of dialogue and have the reader feel its absence
even though they never read it? If yes, the iceberg is working.

## The Late Arrival of the Key Information

Readers process information in sequence. If the most important fact in a scene
arrives first, everything after is anticlimactic. Reverse it: withhold the
central fact, let the scene build around its absence, then deliver it.

This is not the same as a twist. It's a structural choice about *when the reader
is ready* to receive a piece of information — after they've been made to want it.

## Scene vs. Summary

**Scene:** Real-time, moment-by-moment, sensory. Creates intimacy, tension, presence.
**Summary:** Compressed time, overview, transition. Creates distance, pace, movement.

Literary fiction errors:
- Summarising moments that should be scenes (the confrontation told in retrospect)
- Scenifying transitions that should be summary (three pages of a character driving)

The rule: any moment where emotion changes in the character — or where the
reader's understanding fundamentally shifts — deserves scene treatment.

## The Significant Object

Chekhov's gun is the famous version. The deeper principle: objects in literary
fiction carry thematic and emotional weight. They are not decorations.

The broken watch, the mother's dress, the specific brand of cigarette — these
objects become containers for meaning across the narrative. The reader holds
them in their memory. When they reappear, they bring their whole history with them.

**Technique:** Identify 2–3 objects in your story that could carry this weight.
Introduce them casually. Return to them at key moments. Let them mean more
each time.

## Comedy in Dark Material

The laugh in a difficult scene is not a tonal error — it is a release valve
that actually *deepens* the difficulty. It lets the reader back in before
the next blow lands.

**The mechanism:** Gravity → unexpected specificity → recognition → laugh → 
renewed attention.

The specific detail that makes someone laugh at a funeral (the deceased's
terrible taste in music, the cousin who can't stop eating canapés) is not
disrespectful — it is *true*, and truth at a funeral is the most powerful
thing you can put on the page.

**What doesn't work:** Comedy that arrives because the author is uncomfortable
with the scene's darkness and reaches for relief before earning it.

## Dialogue as Subtext Engine

Most dialogue in first drafts says what it means. Literary dialogue almost
never does.

**Principle:** Characters say what they can *afford* to say given the social,
emotional, and power context of the conversation. What they can't afford to
say drives the scene.

**Technique:** Write the scene where the characters say exactly what they mean.
Then replace each line with something that *implies* the same thing. The
subtext version should create more tension and feel more true.

**Dialogue tags:** Said and asked are invisible. Any other tag (hissed, whispered,
declared) pulls attention to itself. Use sparingly, and when you do, choose
the tag for what it adds, not just as variety.

## Endings

Literary endings don't resolve — they *recontextualise*. The reader doesn't reach
the final page with a sense of completion so much as a shift in how they see
everything that came before.

**The test:** After the final line, does the reader want to go back to the
beginning? If yes, the ending has done its job.

**What to avoid:** The retrospective narrator explaining what it all meant. The
image that is too perfectly symbolic. The last line that is trying to be
the last line. The best endings feel like they were always there, waiting.
