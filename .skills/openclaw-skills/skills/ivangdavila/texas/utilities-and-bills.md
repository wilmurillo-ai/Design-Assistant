# Utilities and Bills — Texas

Use this file for electricity, water, internet, gas, trash, and recurring household-cost questions.

## Electricity Is Not One Texas Story

- Much of Texas has retail electric choice, but municipal utilities and co-ops do not.
- Before comparing plans, identify whether the address is in a deregulated territory, city utility, or co-op.
- In competitive areas, use official plan-comparison tools instead of trusting teaser rates.

## Billing Reality

Texas monthly costs often swing because of:
- summer air-conditioning load
- winter storm backup habits
- water or irrigation use
- tolls and fuel tied to location choice
- internet monopoly or limited-fiber pockets

## Move-In Utility Checklist

1. Confirm electric territory and whether plan shopping is even allowed.
2. Confirm water and trash provider or whether they are bundled with rent or HOA.
3. Check fiber availability before signing a lease if remote work matters.
4. Ask about deposits, disconnect windows, and transfer timing.

## Power Rules

- Compare total expected bill, not only cents per kWh headline marketing.
- Ask whether the home is old, shaded, well-insulated, all-electric, or exposed to afternoon heat.
- If outage tolerance is low, bring in backup-power planning from `weather-and-emergencies.md`.

## Internet Rules

- Address-level availability beats brand reputation.
- For remote workers or students, ask about upload speed, outage history, and whether cell coverage can serve as backup.

## Common Mistakes

- Assuming every Texas address can choose its electricity provider.
- Recommending a cheap suburb without estimating power, water, commute fuel, and tolls together.
- Ignoring internet availability until after the lease is signed.
- Treating summer utility spikes as a one-time anomaly instead of a recurring cost driver.
