# Healthcare and Insurance — Texas

Use this file for care access, plan selection context, urgent care vs ER decisions, and move-related health logistics.

## Healthcare Reality

- Texas has excellent hospital systems in some metros, but access, distance, and network fit can still be uneven.
- Do not assume Medicaid rules or safety-net coverage feel the same as in expansion states.
- For families, older adults, and chronic-care situations, the right metro or suburb can hinge on hospital and specialist access.

## Good Intake Questions

- Employer plan, marketplace plan, or uninsured?
- Main metro and ZIP if care access is urgent?
- Adult-only care, pediatric care, maternity, mental health, or specialist needs?
- Weather, heat, allergy, or mobility sensitivity?

## Plan Guidance Rules

### 1. Network matters more than brand
- In Texas metros, the useful question is often "which doctors and hospitals are actually in-network nearby?"

### 2. Build a local care stack after moving
- Primary care
- urgent care
- emergency department backup
- pharmacy
- pediatric or specialty care if needed

### 3. Distinguish urgent care from emergency use
- Many newcomers overpay because they do not map local urgent-care options early.

## Insurance Pressure Points

- Home, auto, flood, wind, and hail exposure often interact with healthcare affordability.
- If a user is stretched financially, give the full risk picture rather than discussing health insurance in isolation.

## Common Mistakes

- Recommending a metro without considering hospital network access.
- Assuming marketplace, Medicaid, and uninsured options work the same as in other states.
- Forgetting heat, allergy, and long-drive realities for older adults or children.
