# Customs and Biosecurity — Chile

## Why This Matters

Chile is unusually strict about animal and plant biosecurity. Treat the SAG declaration as a real compliance step, not a formality.

## SAG Declaration Basics

- If carrying animal or plant products, declare them.
- Honey and other bee products are especially sensitive for Chile and for Rapa Nui.
- Even if an item seems harmless, declare first and let SAG decide.
- "I forgot" is a bad strategy; undeclared food can lead to fines or confiscation.

## Typical High-Risk Items

| Category | Default advice |
|----------|----------------|
| Fresh fruit and vegetables | Declare; usually avoid bringing them |
| Meat, dairy, cured products | Declare; often restricted |
| Seeds, plants, soil, wood | Declare; high scrutiny |
| Honey and bee products | Avoid for Rapa Nui; declare everywhere |
| Packaged snacks with no animal/plant issues | Usually lower risk, but still check labels |

## Aduana Practical Notes

- Use Chilean customs guidance for duty and personal-item limits.
- Keep receipts for high-value goods if carrying cameras, drones, or luxury purchases.
- If carrying large amounts of cash, declare it when required.
- Public customs guidance highlights a declaration threshold above USD 10,000 for cash or equivalents.

## Arrival Discipline

1. Separate any food or natural products before landing.
2. Fill the declaration honestly.
3. Keep medicines in original packaging when possible.
4. Do not pack camping food carelessly if combining Chile with another country.

## Tourist Mistakes to Prevent

- Bringing fruit for the plane and forgetting it on arrival.
- Assuming "sealed" always means exempt.
- Carrying trekking food with meat or dairy into Patagonia without checking it.
- Ignoring the stricter environmental logic of Rapa Nui.
