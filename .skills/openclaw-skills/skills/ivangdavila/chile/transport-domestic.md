# Transport Domestic — Chile

## Core Reality

Chile works as a long corridor. Domestic flights save time; buses expand reach; rail is useful but limited compared with Europe.

## Mode Selection Grid

| Mode | Best for | Watch-outs |
|------|----------|------------|
| Domestic flights | Atacama, Patagonia, long north-south jumps | Baggage fees, weather delays, airport transfer overhead |
| Long-distance buses | Budget travel, overnight corridors, central and southern mainland | Comfort quality varies, night arrival fatigue |
| Rail | Central corridor day moves | Limited network, not a whole-country solution |
| Shared transfers / tours | Calama-San Pedro, Torres access, airport-town moves | Fixed schedules, less flexibility |

## Santiago Airport to City

- Official airport buses make central-city transfers cheaper than taxis.
- Red Movilidad also operates airport-linked service from La Cisterna.
- For late arrivals or heavy luggage, compare trusted transfer or ride-hail options.
- Protect the first night: do not land late and immediately start a long domestic chain.

## Practical Route Advice

- Santiago plus coast: bus or car is easy; no flight needed.
- Santiago to Atacama: fly to Calama, then transfer to San Pedro.
- Santiago to Puerto Montt / Puerto Varas: flight-heavy model wins unless the traveler wants slower surface travel.
- Santiago to Punta Arenas / Puerto Natales: flights are the normal time-saving move.

## Rail Reality

- Rail is most relevant in central Chile rather than for nationwide trip design.
- Use rail when the route actually benefits from it, not because "train sounds nicer."
- For Chile-wide planning, rail is a supplement, not the backbone.

## Booking Logic

1. Lock the long jump first.
2. Then lock the transfer that makes that flight useful.
3. Then place hotels.
4. Then add tours or park days.

This avoids owning a cheap ticket that breaks the rest of the chain.
