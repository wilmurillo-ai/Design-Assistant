# Food Guide

## Denmark Food Logic

Denmark food planning works best when the user chooses the dining style first: bakery and coffee, smorrebrod and lunch culture, modern Nordic, seafood coast meals, or casual budget support.

## What Denmark Does Well

- Bakery culture, breakfast, and coffee breaks
- Smorrebrod and lunch-led eating
- Seafood in coast and island regions
- Seasonal produce and design-forward dining in bigger cities
- Food halls and market-style eating in city breaks

## Match Food to Region

- Copenhagen for range, reservations, and top-end dining
- Aarhus for strong city dining without full-capital saturation
- Bornholm and coasts for produce, smoked fish, and relaxed seasonal meals
- Funen for slower food, farm-shop rhythm, and local produce

## Practical Advice

- Book notable restaurants earlier than users expect in Copenhagen and peak-summer destinations.
- Use bakeries, lunch, and supermarkets intelligently to control daily spend.
- Small-town and island dinner options can close earlier or narrow outside peak dates.
- Dietary restrictions are manageable, but users should not assume every remote stop has equal choice.

## Trap Avoidance

- Planning every dinner spontaneously in a high-demand period.
- Assuming all Danish dining is expensive fine dining.
- Treating beach or island towns like they will have city-level late-night choice.
- Ignoring lunch as a high-value part of the Denmark eating rhythm.

## Planning Rule

Use food to shape the day, not only to fill gaps. In Denmark, the right meal strategy can improve budget, pacing, and place selection at once.
