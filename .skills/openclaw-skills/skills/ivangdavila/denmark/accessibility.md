# Accessibility

## Best-Fit Trip Shapes

- Copenhagen and Aarhus are usually the strongest bases for low-mobility travelers because transit, sidewalks, and service density are better.
- Odense can also work well for a smaller-city version of the same logic.
- Rugged coast, cliffs, dunes, and some historic streets need more pre-checking than city users assume.

## Transport Accessibility

- DSB publishes disability services and station assistance, so rail can be a strong choice when arranged properly.
- Metro and major-city transit are often easier than parking-first plans in dense cores.
- Road-trip value depends on step-free lodging, parking distance, and whether viewpoints actually match the user's mobility reality.

## Attraction and Nature Logic

- Museums, design sites, and central-city districts are easier to deliver accessibly than cliffs, dunes, and beach-heavy days.
- Mons Klint, Thy, and some Bornholm experiences can still work, but route and surface detail must be checked first.
- Harborfront, waterfront, and town-center experiences often deliver better value than forcing rugged-nature goals that do not fit.

## Practical Advice

- Ask what "accessible" means for this user: wheelchair, low stamina, short walking tolerance, sensory constraints, or stroller overlap.
- Protect bathroom, seating, transfer, and weather-shelter logic in every plan.
- Keep same-day transfer chains short when assistance, ramps, or luggage are involved.

## Common Mistakes

- Selling famous nature viewpoints without checking terrain and distance.
- Treating every old-town surface as easy just because the city is compact.
- Choosing a remote scenic stay with poor access to meals and backup plans.
- Letting parking shape the trip more than actual usable experiences.

## Planning Rule

Accessible Denmark planning should maximize ease and dignity, not just theoretical access. If an experience is technically possible but exhausting, it is probably the wrong call.
