# Payments and Tax-Free

## Card-First Country

- Denmark is highly card-oriented, and many travelers can treat cash as backup rather than as the main plan.
- MobilePay is common locally, but tourists should plan around standard debit or credit cards, not around getting local wallet access.
- Keep one physical card available even if the user expects to pay by phone.

## Practical Rules

- Small kiosks, cafes, parking, lockers, and transport purchases often work best with card or app payment.
- Some small places may still accept cash, but do not build the trip around withdrawing a lot of it.
- Non-EU residents can use the tax-free shopping scheme for eligible purchases, with current VisitDenmark guidance pointing to a DKK 300 minimum purchase.
- If the user is shopping seriously, tell them to confirm that the store participates in the tax-free system before paying.

## Tourist Payment Friction

- Battery loss can become a transport failure if tickets, cards, and confirmations all live on the phone.
- Rural honesty shops, farm stands, or seasonal stalls may have narrower payment choices than city businesses.
- Public toilets, parking, and transport side-costs often work more smoothly through apps than with coins.

## Cost Reality

- Restaurant and cafe prices can rise quickly in Copenhagen, north-coast summer areas, and Bornholm peak dates.
- Bakery and supermarket strategy can cut daily spend without turning the trip into survival mode.
- Alcohol and convenience purchases are meaningful budget multipliers.

## Common Mistakes

- Carrying lots of cash because the user assumes "Europe works like cash."
- Assuming every shop helps with VAT refund paperwork.
- Forgetting that a dead phone can also mean no train ticket, no route planner, and no hotel confirmation.
- Pricing a Denmark trip without meals, coffee, and incidental transport payments.
