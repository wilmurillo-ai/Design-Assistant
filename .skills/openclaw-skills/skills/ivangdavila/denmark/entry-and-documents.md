# Entry and Documents

Use this file before booking anything non-refundable.

## Core Logic

1. Confirm nationality and residence status.
2. Check whether the user enters Denmark as an EU or EEA or Swiss traveler, a visa-free short-stay visitor, or a visa-required traveler.
3. For non-EU short stays, verify passport-validity and Schengen-day-count rules before the itinerary is locked.
4. If the route also includes Sweden, Germany, or wider Schengen travel, check the whole legal path rather than Denmark alone.

## Practical Rules

- Denmark applies Schengen entry logic, so the legal answer often depends on the entire Schengen trip, not only the Denmark dates.
- EU, EEA, and Swiss travelers usually use a valid national ID card or passport.
- Many non-EU short-stay visitors need a passport issued within the last 10 years and valid for at least 3 months beyond planned Schengen departure.
- As of March 9, 2026, the EU Entry/Exit System is already in rollout, so some non-EU travelers may see biometric border processing at external Schengen borders.
- As of March 9, 2026, ETIAS is still not operational; official EU guidance points to a later 2026 start, so confirm status before travel.

## Ask These Before Advising

- What passport is being used?
- Is Denmark the only Schengen country or part of a longer Schengen route?
- Total planned days in Schengen?
- Any previous refusal, overstay issue, or dual-nationality complexity?
- Is the user entering by air, rail, ferry, or car from another country?

## Common Mistakes

- Treating Denmark entry as separate from the rest of the Schengen trip.
- Assuming visa-free means document-free.
- Discovering passport-validity issues after booking summer islands or non-refundable city stays.
- Ignoring that residency, work, or study questions stop being tourism advice.
