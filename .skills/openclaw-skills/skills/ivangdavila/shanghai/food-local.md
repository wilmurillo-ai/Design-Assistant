# Shanghai Local Food Guide

## Core Local Dishes
- Xiaolongbao: thin wrapper soup dumplings, best eaten immediately.
- Shengjianbao: pan-fried soup buns with sesame and scallion.
- Cong you ban mian: scallion oil noodles, simple but iconic.
- Hongshao rou: red-braised pork, rich and slightly sweet.
- Lion's head meatballs: braised pork balls in light broth.
- Kaofu: braised wheat gluten, common cold starter.

## Seasonal Must-Try
- Hairy crab (usually autumn): steamed, eaten with vinegar and ginger.
- River shrimp and eel dishes: common in Jiangnan-style restaurants.
- Spring bamboo shoots: frequent in set menus during spring.

## Breakfast Playbook
- Soy milk + youtiao at neighborhood breakfast counters.
- Cifantuan (rice roll with fillings) near metro exits.
- Jianbing exists, but Shanghai breakfast leans more toward buns and rice rolls.
- Go before 08:30 for better quality and shorter lines.

## Where To Explore Local Food
- Huangpu: classic old-city style spots and long-running local brands.
- Xuhui: polished Shanghainese restaurants with easier English support.
- Jing'an: mix of traditional kitchens and modern local concepts.
- Putuo and Yangpu: less touristy, often better value.

## Ordering Tips
- Balance dishes: 1 cold starter, 1 braise, 1 greens, 1 soup, 1 starch.
- Local flavor is often mildly sweet; ask for less sugar if needed.
- For two people, 3-4 dishes is usually enough.
- Soup dumplings lose quality quickly, so eat them first.

## Common Mistakes
- Ordering only famous dishes and skipping seasonal specials.
- Choosing highly rated places far away instead of good nearby options.
- Going too late; many local kitchens close earlier than bars.
- Assuming all local food is heavy; many dishes are light and delicate.

## Quick Local Meal Template
- Lunch: noodles + one cold side.
- Dinner: one steamed dish, one braised dish, one vegetable dish, rice.
- Add dessert only if the restaurant is known for it.
