# Food Guide — United Kingdom

## Regional Food Compass

| Region | Signature style | What to prioritize |
|--------|------------------|--------------------|
| London | Global dining depth, modern British, pub and market mix | Neighborhood-led eating, not landmark strips |
| South England | Seafood, pub lunches, afternoon tea, farm-shop culture | Coastal towns and historic-market towns |
| North England | Pies, roast culture, curry scenes, hearty pub food | Strong value outside capital pricing |
| Scotland | Seafood, game, whisky context, baking | City bistros plus rural produce |
| Wales | Lamb, coastal seafood, bakery and market tradition | Pair scenic routes with destination pubs |
| Northern Ireland | Seafood, soda bread, pub culture, modern Belfast dining | Build around market and neighborhood hubs |

## Restaurant Planning Rules

- In London, Edinburgh, and other high-demand cities, reserve headline places early.
- In small towns and rural regions, Sunday and early-week opening patterns matter more than rating apps.
- Pub kitchens can stop earlier than visitors expect outside major cities.

## Pub Strategy

- Distinguish between food-led gastropubs, historic drinking pubs, and chain pubs.
- If the user wants a meal, confirm service times and whether table booking matters.

## Market and Casual Food Rule

- Food halls and covered markets are high-value for mixed groups and dietary variety.
- They also work well as weather backup when a sightseeing day turns wet.

## Tourist Traps to Flag

- Eating every meal inside the most famous attraction zone
- Assuming every historic pub serves memorable food
- Ignoring reservation pressure in London and Edinburgh peaks
- Treating UK dining as only fish and chips plus full English

## Practical Rule

Anchor one meaningful meal a day, then fill the rest with flexible local options. UK travel goes better when dining fits the route instead of fighting it.
