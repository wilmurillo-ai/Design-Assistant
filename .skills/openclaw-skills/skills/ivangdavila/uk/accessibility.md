# Accessibility — United Kingdom

## Planning Priorities

- Choose fewer bases with cleaner station-to-hotel routing.
- Verify accessibility details directly with lodging and attraction operators, not only listing tags.
- Build buffer time into major transfer days.

## Rail and Station Support

- Passenger assistance is available across much of the National Rail system, but advance notice can materially improve the experience.
- Not every historic station has equally simple step-free routing.

## London and Major Cities

- London can be highly workable if step-free routing is planned in advance rather than assumed.
- Use station-specific accessibility information, not just line-level assumptions.
- Bus-first movement can sometimes be easier than Underground changes.

## Historic Buildings and Streets

- Castles, old inns, cobblestones, stair-heavy museums, and heritage districts often create the real friction.
- Offer modern-access alternatives nearby instead of pretending every iconic site is equally accessible.

## Nature and Coast Strategy

- Coastal paths, moors, and upland routes vary sharply in practical accessibility.
- For scenery-first users, prioritize accessible promenades, viewpoints, gardens, and heritage rail rather than defaulting to hikes.

## Practical Rule

Accessibility planning should be route-first: station, pavement, venue entry, toilets, and return path. One inaccessible handoff can break an otherwise good day.
