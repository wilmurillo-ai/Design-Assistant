# Accommodation Strategy — United Kingdom

## Base Selection Rules

- In London, pay for time savings first: strong Tube or Elizabeth line access beats marginal nightly savings.
- In smaller historic cities, staying inside the walkable core usually improves the trip more than chasing chain hotels on the edge.
- In scenic regions, choose one strong base with parking or rail logic instead of one-night hops.

## What To Optimize By Destination

| Destination type | Better accommodation logic |
|------------------|----------------------------|
| London | Zone 1-2 edge or direct fast line into central areas |
| Bath, York, Edinburgh | Walkable historic center with luggage-friendly access |
| Cotswolds villages | Parking and dinner options nearby matter more than "most famous village" branding |
| Lake District, Highlands, North Wales | Base near the routes you actually plan to drive or hike |
| Belfast and smaller UK cities | Core center or rail-adjacent base usually beats airport-area savings |

## Practical Filters

- Confirm lift access in old buildings if luggage or mobility matters.
- Check whether breakfast is worth it; many UK stays charge extra for a meal that nearby cafes beat.
- Watch for narrow check-in windows in guesthouses and inns.
- In rural areas, confirm whether dinner is available nearby without driving at night.

## Peak Season Advice

- Summer weekends, school holidays, and bank-holiday periods can move rates sharply in Edinburgh, Bath, Cornwall, and the Cotswolds.
- August in Edinburgh and the Highlands needs earlier booking than many first-time visitors expect.
- December city breaks also price up fast because of Christmas markets and seasonal demand.

## Trap Prevention

- Do not stay near a London airport unless the airport is the point of the trip.
- Do not book remote cottages without a transport plan and grocery plan.
- Do not choose lodging in scenic areas by postcard value alone; road access, parking, and dinner options shape the experience.
- Do not underestimate stairs, uneven floors, and smaller rooms in historic UK properties.
