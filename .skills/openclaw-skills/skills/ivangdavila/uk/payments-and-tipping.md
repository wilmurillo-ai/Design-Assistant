# Payments and Tipping — United Kingdom

## Card and Cash Reality

- Cards and phone payments are standard across most of the UK.
- Cash is still useful for edge cases, but not as the default strategy.
- Keep one physical backup card and a small cash reserve.

## Tipping Baseline

| Context | Typical expectation | Notes |
|--------|----------------------|-------|
| Casual cafes, pubs at counter | Usually none | Round up only if it feels right |
| Sit-down restaurant | Often optional | Check whether service charge is already added |
| Taxi | Small round-up | Not a U.S.-style percentage culture |
| Hotel help or porter | Optional small tip | Only for clear service |

## Service Charge Rule

- In London and tourist-heavy cities, restaurants often add a service charge automatically.
- Always check the bill before adding more.
- If service was weak, the user can ask to review or remove the service charge.

## Payment Friction to Flag

- Some pubs split between table service and bar ordering, which affects when payment happens.
- Small businesses may prefer card over cash, not the reverse.
- Dynamic currency conversion is usually a bad deal; pay in pounds.

## Cash Withdrawal Logic

- Use bank ATM networks where possible.
- Avoid airport and tourist-area exchange counters when a card works.
- If the user needs cash, withdraw a modest amount rather than overloading on notes.

## VAT and Pricing Reality

- Consumer shelf pricing normally includes VAT.
- Restaurant and hotel bills are less about surprise tax and more about optional or automatic service charges.

## Practical Rule

Tell users to think in "final bill plus transport" rather than U.S.-style tax-and-tip math. UK pricing is simpler, but London hospitality and transport choices still move the real total.
