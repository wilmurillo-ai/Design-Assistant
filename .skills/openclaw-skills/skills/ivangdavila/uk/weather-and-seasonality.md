# Weather and Seasonality — United Kingdom

## Macro Seasonal Strategy

| Season | Strong fits | Main risks |
|--------|-------------|------------|
| Spring | Cities, gardens, shoulder travel, southern routes | Rain swings, cool evenings, school-holiday demand |
| Summer | Coast, festivals, Highlands, parks, long daylight | Peak prices, crowding, occasional heat spikes |
| Autumn | Cities, pubs, food trips, foliage, lower crowds | Shorter daylight, wetter countryside |
| Winter | Christmas cities, museums, pubs, shorter city breaks | Storms, dark afternoons, transport disruption |

## What UK Weather Actually Means

- The main issue is not constant heavy rain; it is variability.
- A good trip usually depends on layering, footwear, and backup indoor plans.
- In Scotland, Wales, and coastal or upland routes, wind and visibility can matter as much as rain.

## Best Windows by Trip Type

| Trip type | Better window |
|-----------|---------------|
| London and city culture | March-June, September-November |
| Highlands and long scenic drives | Late spring to early autumn |
| Cornwall and coastal southwest | Late spring to early autumn |
| Christmas markets and festive trips | Late November to December |

## Summer Rule

- Summer is the easiest season for long daylight and mixed-region trips.
- It is also the hardest season for value and availability.
- If the user wants August Edinburgh or Cornwall, say "book early" by default.

## Winter Rule

- Winter works for London, major museums, theater, and pub-forward city breaks.
- It is weaker for ambitious countryside movement and short-day scenic touring.

## Weather-Aware Planning

- Keep one indoor-heavy backup day per week
- Protect the final departure leg from storm exposure
- For hiking or coast days, check same-day forecasts and warnings
