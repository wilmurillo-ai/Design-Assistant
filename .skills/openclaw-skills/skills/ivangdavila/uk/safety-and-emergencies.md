# Safety and Emergencies — United Kingdom

## Essential Numbers

- `999` for urgent police, fire, ambulance, or coastguard emergencies
- `112` also works for emergency services
- `111` for urgent but non-emergency NHS medical advice
- `101` for non-emergency police matters

## Overall Safety Pattern

- The UK is broadly straightforward for tourists, but risk concentrates in petty theft, nightlife judgment, transport disruption, and weather-exposed outdoor plans.
- Major cities require the same phone and bag awareness as other European capitals.

## City Safety Habits

- Keep phones secured near station exits, curbside, and busy nightlife streets.
- Use licensed taxis or trusted ride options late at night.
- Save hotel and return-transport details offline before going out.

## Nature and Coast Safety Habits

- Coastal cliffs, tides, and sudden weather shifts are more operational risks than crime in many leisure areas.
- In parks and highlands, daylight, rain, and wind can change a walk quickly.
- Build earlier turn-back decisions than users would in sunnier climates.

## Transport Disruption Rule

If rail strikes, engineering works, storms, or flooding disrupt a route:
1. Rebuild around one stable base
2. Drop low-value day trips first
3. Protect the final pre-departure night near the departure city or airport

## Health and Medical Friction

- NHS access exists for urgent needs, but tourist coverage and charging rules vary by situation.
- Travel insurance still matters, especially for non-urgent treatment, repatriation, and trip disruption.

## Incident Response Rule

If a traveler feels unsafe or a route becomes unstable:
1. Move to a staffed, populated place
2. Use official transport or venue staff for support
3. Rebuild the itinerary conservatively instead of trying to save every booking
