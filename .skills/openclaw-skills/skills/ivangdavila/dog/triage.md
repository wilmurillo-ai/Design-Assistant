# Dog Triage

## Emergency Now

Escalate to emergency veterinary care now if the dog has:
- trouble breathing, blue or very pale gums, collapse, or unresponsiveness
- seizure, severe weakness, or inability to stand
- major trauma, heavy bleeding, or suspected heat injury
- toxin exposure, swallowed medication, or unsafe food or chemical ingestion
- a swollen abdomen with repeated retching or attempts to vomit that produce little or nothing
- extreme pain, screaming, rigid posture, or sudden abdominal distress

## Same-Day Veterinary Escalation

Push for prompt vet contact if there is:
- repeated vomiting or diarrhea
- fast-moving lethargy or refusal to eat
- coughing that is new or worsening
- limping, pain, or sudden reluctance to move
- a major change in urination, stool, or drinking
- a behavior change that looks abrupt and out of character

## Minimum Intake Questions

Before giving non-emergency guidance, gather only what changes the decision:
- How old and roughly how large is the dog?
- What changed first, and when?
- Eating, drinking, urination, stool, and energy now?
- Any toxins, new foods, trauma, overheating, or strenuous activity?
- Any known conditions, allergies, or current medications?
- Any vomiting, diarrhea, coughing, pain, or swollen abdomen?

## Safe Language

Use language like:
- "This could be urgent because..."
- "I cannot confirm the cause from chat alone."
- "The safest next step is..."

Do not:
- diagnose from one photo or one short description
- suggest human medications
- tell the user to watch and wait through active red flags

## Follow-Up Checks

If the situation is not clearly emergent, support short factual check-ins:
- still drinking and keeping food down or not
- more coughing, less, or none
- walking normally, reluctant, or worsening
- another vomiting or diarrhea episode or none
- same trigger, new trigger, or no trigger
