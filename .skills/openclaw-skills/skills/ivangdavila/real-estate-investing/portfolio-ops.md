# Portfolio Operations and Exit Discipline

The acquisition is only the first decision. Good investing depends on operating quality and knowing when to hold, refinance, or sell.

## Operating fit checks

- can the user self-manage this asset type and market realistically
- if using management, is local manager quality proven
- are turnovers, leasing, maintenance, and collections operationally simple or fragile
- does this asset create concentration in one tenant profile, one block, or one economic driver

An average deal with excellent operations can outperform a "great" spreadsheet deal with weak execution.

## Capex planning

Separate:
- immediate make-ready
- near-term replacements in the next 12-24 months
- longer-cycle items such as roof, exterior, systems, parking, or common areas

Always ask whether the business plan includes enough cash and enough time.

## Exit routes

Know the likely exits before buying:
- stabilized hold for cash flow
- refinance and keep
- sell after cleanup or rent stabilization
- condo or lot split only if legally and operationally verified

If the deal has only one narrow exit, demand a stronger margin of safety.

## Sell-or-hold triggers

Revisit the asset when:
- returns compress below the user's hurdle rate
- market liquidity changes materially
- insurance, taxes, or regulation reprice the economics
- management burden rises above what the thesis allowed
- a better capital use appears and the opportunity cost is real

## Post-mortem discipline

After every closed or rejected deal, record:
- what assumptions were right
- what assumptions were wrong
- which red flags were ignored or overestimated
- how the buy box or reserve rules should change
