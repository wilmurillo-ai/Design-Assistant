# Nature and Right to Roam - Sweden

## Why This Changes the Trip

Sweden is unusually strong for low-friction access to forests, lakes, islands, and trails, but users still need to understand where freedom ends and protected-area rules begin.

## Core Outdoor Framework

| Topic | Practical rule |
|-------|----------------|
| Right to roam | Walking, short rests, and one-night low-impact camping are broadly allowed away from homes and sensitive land |
| National parks | Rules can be stricter than general countryside access |
| Fire and waste | Leave-no-trace behavior is not optional |
| Wildlife and weather | Distance, insects, water, and cold can escalate faster than the map suggests |

## Planning Rules

- Always check if the user wants easy-access nature, hut-based hiking, day hikes, or remote winter activity.
- Distinguish summer archipelago or lake-nature plans from Lapland mountain conditions.
- In shoulder seasons, ask about wet ground, wind, darkness, and gear tolerance before promising scenic hikes.

## National Parks and Outdoor Strategy

- Sweden has strong national-park coverage, but not every trip needs a park-specific route.
- For many users, one accessible nature add-on is better than building the whole trip around remote trail logistics.
- If the user wants Abisko, Kungsleden, or mountain territory, pair ambition with transport and weather realism.

## Tourist Trap to Flag

Users hear "right to roam" and assume it means unlimited camping, fire freedom, or no need to think about private homes, protected areas, and seasonal conditions. Correct that early.
