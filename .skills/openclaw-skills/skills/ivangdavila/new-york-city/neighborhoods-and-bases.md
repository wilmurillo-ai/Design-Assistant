# Neighborhoods and Bases — New York City

Use this file when the user is choosing where to stay, where to live, or which borough fits their routine.

## Quick Fit Map

| Area Pattern | Best for | Watchouts |
|--------------|----------|-----------|
| Lower and central Manhattan | First-time visitors, dense access, office-heavy routines | Highest room and rent pressure, noise, small spaces |
| Upper Manhattan | Better value, transit access, neighborhood feel | Longer crosstown trips, building variation, nightlife fit depends on block |
| Brownstone Brooklyn | Families, classic neighborhood feel, parks, calmer routines | High rent, slower Manhattan access from some areas, stroller-and-stairs reality |
| North Brooklyn | Young professionals, nightlife, food, startup and creative scenes | High prices, weekend noise, subway fragility in some corridors |
| Western Queens | Better value, airport access, practical daily life, food diversity | Patchy nightlife, building-age variation, neighborhood feel changes fast |
| Bronx pockets near strong transit | Value and local culture with city access | Block-by-block variation, longer cross-city trips |
| Staten Island | Space and suburban feel | Ferry dependence, car logic, weak fit for most short stays |

## Borough Choice Rules

### 1. Match the week, not the fantasy
- Ask where the user needs to be at 8 a.m., 11 p.m., and on weekends.
- The best neighborhood is the one that keeps the routine sustainable.

### 2. Transit shape matters more than borough label
- Two neighborhoods in the same borough can feel farther apart than neighborhoods across a river with a better train.
- Always ask about late-night returns, stairs, and transfer tolerance.

### 3. Visitors and residents need different bases
- A hotel base optimized for sightseeing is not automatically a good rental zone.
- A "cool" neighborhood can be a bad fit if the user values quiet, elevators, or airport ease.

## Common Mistakes

- Calling Brooklyn or Queens one thing.
- Choosing by vibe alone without mapping the real commute.
- Treating one subway line as a full mobility plan.
- Assuming a cheaper neighborhood is cheaper after time and transit friction.
