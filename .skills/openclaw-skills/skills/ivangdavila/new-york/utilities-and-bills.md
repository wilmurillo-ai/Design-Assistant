# Utilities and Bills — New York (State)

Use this file for power, gas, heating fuel, internet, water, and recurring-home-cost questions.

## New York Utility Rule

The useful question is not "what is the utility bill?" It is "what kind of building, heating system, and winter exposure am I taking on?"

## What Changes the Bill Most

### 1. Heating type
- Electric heat, gas heat, and fuel-oil or propane setups create very different winter bills and refill logistics.
- In older homes and small towns, the heating system often matters more than the ZIP code average.

### 2. Building efficiency
- Drafty windows, poor insulation, basement moisture, and aging boilers make winter and summer bills swing harder.
- Ask whether the building was updated or whether the user is inheriting a comfort problem.

### 3. Utility territory and service options
- Electric and gas providers vary by region.
- Internet quality, municipal water, septic, and refuse or snow-removal responsibility can vary sharply between city, suburb, and rural settings.

## Good Guidance Pattern

- Identify housing type and heating system first.
- Then estimate the recurring burden and operational hassle.
- Then compare the total monthly setup against a nearby alternative.

## Common Mistakes

- Treating heat as a flat line item instead of the defining winter cost.
- Forgetting snow removal, trash, parking, and internet reliability in lower-density areas.
- Assuming utilities are always included in New York rentals.
- Comparing two homes without checking one has oil heat, septic, or older windows.
