# Weather and Preparedness — New York (State)

Use this file when weather, outages, flooding, or seasonal planning change the answer.

## New York Hazard Pattern

New York does not have one weather problem. The operational mix changes by region:
- downstate rain, coastal storms, flood exposure, and heat waves
- upstate snow, ice, lake effect, wind, and road disruption
- mountain and rural areas with longer outage recovery and tougher winter logistics

## Preparedness Rules

### 1. Winter is an operations problem
- Good advice changes when the user must shovel, drive before roads clear, keep pipes from freezing, or manage school closures.
- Ask whether they have off-street parking, alternate heat, backup work flexibility, and snow-removal responsibility.

### 2. Water risk is not only for dramatic shorelines
- Basements, poor drainage, creeks, river towns, and storm-sewer backups matter.
- Floodplain or drainage history should be checked before saying a home or town is a good fit.

### 3. Summer still matters
- NYC and some inland corridors can be humid and punishing in heat waves.
- Building cooling, shade, walkability, and outage resilience affect comfort and health.

## Travel and Move Planning

- Avoid move-day assumptions that ignore snow, ice, holiday traffic, or storm cleanup.
- For trips, convert map distance into winter-safe daylight and road-condition logic.
- For homes, ask what happens during a two-day outage, not just on a normal day.

## Common Mistakes

- Assuming all New York winters are equally harsh.
- Treating lake-effect zones, coastal zones, and dense urban heat as the same problem.
- Ignoring drainage, basement, and roof questions in older housing.
- Recommending remote scenic areas without discussing storm recovery and medical access.
