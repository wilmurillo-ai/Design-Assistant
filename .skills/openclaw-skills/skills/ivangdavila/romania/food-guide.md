# Romania Food Guide

## The Core Idea

Romanian food is hearty, generous, and better when the user stops chasing gimmicks and leans into soups, grilled meats, baked dishes, and regional produce.

## Dishes Worth Naming

| Dish | What it is | Best context |
|------|------------|--------------|
| Mici | Grilled minced meat rolls | Market or terrace lunch |
| Sarmale | Cabbage rolls with meat and rice | Traditional meal |
| Ciorba | Sour soup, many variants | Everyday starter |
| Mamaliga | Polenta-style staple | Side or full comfort dish |
| Papanasi | Fried dough dessert with cream and jam | Share one after dinner |
| Zacusca | Vegetable spread | Breakfast or starters |
| Placinte | Filled pastries | Bakery or road snack |

## Where The User Usually Eats Best

- markets and casual terraces for mici
- old-school Romanian restaurants for ciorba and sarmale
- guesthouses and rural stays for full meals with less menu theater
- modern city spots in Bucharest or Cluj for cleaner presentations and lighter pacing

## Regional Signals

### Transylvania
- heavier comfort food
- smoked meats
- stronger Austro-Hungarian influence

### Moldavia
- rich soups
- pastries
- strong home-cooking reputation

### Dobrogea
- more fish and seafood logic
- best paired with coast or Delta plans

## Practical Advice

- lunch and dinner timing is easier than in Spain or Italy; do not overcomplicate it
- many strong dishes are filling, so avoid overordering
- ask for the house soup or grill when the menu is huge and generic
- in tourist cores, steer away from places that sell only "Dracula" atmosphere

## Good Fast Wins

- Obor in Bucharest for mici
- a strong local restaurant in Brasov or Sibiu for soup, sarmale, and papanasi
- a guesthouse dinner in rural Transylvania if the user wants the most memorable meal
