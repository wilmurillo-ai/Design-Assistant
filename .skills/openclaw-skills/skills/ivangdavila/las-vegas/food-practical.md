# Practical Dining Guide

## Buffets: The Full Truth

### The Golden Age Is Over
Classic Vegas buffets (cheap, unlimited, mediocre) are mostly dead. What survived the pandemic is either premium or gone.

### Still Standing (and Worth It)

**Bacchanal Buffet (Caesars)**
- **Price**: $75-90 (varies by day/meal)
- **Quality**: High — this is fine dining in buffet form
- **What's good**: Crab legs, prime rib carving, diverse cuisines
- **Strategy**: Go for weekend brunch, skip weekday lunch
- **Verdict**: Worth it once for the experience

**Wicked Spoon (Cosmopolitan)**
- **Price**: $55-75
- **Quality**: Good — more curated, less overwhelming
- **What's good**: Individual portions, brunch items
- **Vibe**: Trendy, younger crowd
- **Verdict**: Best value premium buffet

**Sterling Brunch (Bally's)**
- **Price**: $100+
- **Quality**: High-end
- **What's good**: Champagne, caviar, prime cuts
- **When**: Sundays only
- **Verdict**: Special occasion splurge

### Skip These
Most other casino buffets have declined in quality. Research current reviews before committing.

### Buffet Strategy
- **Go hungry but not starving** (you'll overeat and regret it)
- **Scout first** — walk the entire buffet before plating
- **Prioritize high-value items** — seafood, carving stations
- **Skip the filler** — bread, basic salads, fried stuff
- **Pace yourself** — this is a marathon

## Steakhouses: A Buyer's Guide

### Understanding Vegas Steakhouse Tiers

**Top Tier ($150-250/person)**
- SW Steakhouse (Wynn)
- CUT (Palazzo)
- Bazaar Meat (SLS)
- Jean Georges Steakhouse (Aria)

**Premium ($100-150/person)**
- STK (Cosmopolitan)
- Gordon Ramsay Steak (Paris)
- Carnevino (Palazzo)
- Prime (Bellagio)
- Delmonico (Venetian)

**Value ($70-100/person)**
- Oscar's (Downtown)
- Golden Steer (Off-Strip)
- Herbs & Rye (Off-Strip, happy hour)
- Triple George (Downtown)

### What Makes Vegas Steaks Different
- Competition drives quality up
- Better beef grades available
- Sides are often excellent
- Wine lists are deep
- You're paying for experience, not just meat

### Ordering Strategy
- **Prime vs Wagyu**: Prime is the sweet spot for most. Wagyu is rich—small portions.
- **Dry-aged**: Worth the upcharge for the funk
- **Sides**: Order fewer than you think (portions are huge)
- **Wine**: By-the-glass is often good value; bottles have markup

## Dietary Restrictions

### Vegetarian/Vegan
Vegas has improved dramatically. Best options:

**Vegan-Focused**:
- Vegenation (Downtown) — 100% vegan
- Violette's (Henderson) — Vegan fine dining
- Crossroads (Resorts World) — Upscale vegan

**Best Vegetarian Options At**:
- Wynn properties (Steve Wynn was veg-focused)
- Cosmopolitan restaurants
- Any Japanese restaurant (tofu dishes, veggie rolls)
- Indian restaurants (Green Valley)

### Gluten-Free
- Most high-end restaurants accommodate well
- Call ahead for severe celiac
- Italian restaurants can do GF pasta (ask)
- Sushi is naturally mostly GF

### Kosher
- Limited options
- Hamada (various) has kosher menu
- Check Vegas kosher guides for current options
- Many Strip kitchens can't guarantee kosher

### Halal
- Chinatown has halal options
- Call ahead to verify
- Middle Eastern restaurants (off-Strip) more reliable

### Allergies
- High-end restaurants handle allergies well
- Always disclose at reservation AND to server
- Carry an EpiPen if severe
- Avoid buffets for serious allergies (cross-contamination)

## Tipping Guide

### Standard Tips
| Service | Tip |
|---------|-----|
| Full-service restaurant | 18-22% |
| Fine dining | 20-22% |
| Buffet | 15-18% |
| Counter service | $1-2 or 10-15% |
| Cocktail server | $1-2 per drink |
| Sommelier (separate from server) | 10-15% of wine cost |

### Vegas-Specific
- Vegas servers often work harder (tourist volume)
- Automatic gratuity common for 6+ people
- Check your bill — some restaurants add gratuity

### When to Tip More
- Exceptional service
- Complex dietary accommodations
- Last-minute reservation requests fulfilled
- Server went above and beyond

## Reservations

### How Far Ahead

| Restaurant Type | Booking Window |
|-----------------|----------------|
| é by José Andrés | Day of release (sells out in minutes) |
| Carbone, SW Steakhouse | 2-4 weeks |
| Most celebrity chefs | 1-2 weeks |
| Popular local spots | 3-7 days |
| Buffets | Same day or walk-in |

### Best Booking Platforms
- **OpenTable**: Most restaurants
- **Resy**: Trendy spots (Carbone, etc.)
- **Yelp Reservations**: Some locals spots
- **Direct call**: Always try for special requests

### Getting Hard Reservations
- Book right when window opens
- Try cancellation alerts on apps
- Call the restaurant (sometimes holds tables back)
- Ask hotel concierge (especially if staying at property)
- Lunch is easier than dinner
- Bar seating sometimes available without reservation

### Cancellation Etiquette
- Cancel if you can't make it (others want that table)
- Many charge for no-shows ($25-100/person)
- Same-day cancellation often incurs fee

## Dress Code

### What "Smart Casual" Means in Vegas
- Guys: Collared shirt, nice jeans or slacks, closed shoes
- Women: Anything nice, really
- NO: Athletic wear, flip-flops, tank tops, shorts (at nicer spots)

### What "Business Casual" Means
- Guys: Button-down, dress pants, dress shoes
- Women: Business-appropriate attire
- NO: Jeans (usually), sneakers

### Fine Dining Dress Code
- Guys: Jacket suggested (not always required), no jeans
- Women: Cocktail attire
- **Always check restaurant website**

### Places That Actually Enforce Dress Code
- Joel Robuchon
- Le Cirque
- SW Steakhouse
- Most French fine dining

### Places More Relaxed Than Expected
- Cosmopolitan restaurants
- Most celebrity casual spots
- Chinatown (wear anything)
- Downtown (casual accepted)

## Timing Your Meals

### Best Times for Reservations
- **Lunch**: 11:30am (just opened, not rushed)
- **Early dinner**: 5:30-6pm (easier to get, less crowded)
- **Prime dinner**: 7-8pm (hardest to book, longest waits)
- **Late dinner**: 9pm+ (easier, sometimes tired kitchen)

### When Restaurants Are Slowest
- Tuesday and Wednesday
- Early in the week after holiday weekends
- Summer (July-August)

### When Everything Is Packed
- Saturday night (always)
- Convention weeks (CES in January especially)
- Holiday weekends
- Fight nights / big events

## Money-Saving Tips

### Happy Hour Strategy
Many celebrity restaurants offer happy hour (usually 4-7pm):
- 50% off drinks
- Discounted appetizers
- Same kitchen, lower price

Good happy hours: Herbs & Rye, STK, TAO, Jaleo

### Lunch vs Dinner
Same chef, same kitchen:
- Lunch typically 30-50% cheaper
- Easier reservations
- Shorter waits

### Sharing Strategy
Vegas portions are often huge:
- Split appetizers
- Share entrees (some restaurants charge split fee)
- Desserts are always splittable

### Comp and Rewards
- MGM Rewards / Caesars Rewards members get offers
- Sign up before your trip
- Check app for dining credits

## Alcohol

### Vegas Pour Reality
- Drinks are expensive on Strip ($15-25 cocktails common)
- Wine markup is steep (2.5-4x retail)
- Beer is reasonable at most places

### Saving on Drinks
- Happy hour (significant savings)
- Gambler drinks (technically "free" while playing)
- Off-Strip is cheaper
- Downtown is cheaper than Strip

### Corkage
- Most restaurants charge corkage ($25-75)
- Some don't allow outside wine at all
- Always call ahead
