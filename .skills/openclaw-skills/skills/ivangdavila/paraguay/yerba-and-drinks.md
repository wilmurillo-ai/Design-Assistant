# Yerba and Drinks - Paraguay

Drink culture in Paraguay is a social language. Terere is not a gimmick for tourists; it is part of the day's rhythm.

## What Matters Most

| Drink | Context | Notes |
|-------|---------|-------|
| Terere | Heat, daytime, social pauses | The national default in warm weather |
| Mate or cocido | Cooler mornings, breakfast, rainy weather | More common when the day is not blazing hot |
| Fresh juice | Lunch and casual cafes | Good heat-management option |
| Beer | Social evenings, grills, waterfronts | Cold matters more than complexity |
| Clerico and festive drinks | Summer gatherings | Seasonal and social, not an everyday identity |

## Terere Etiquette

- If someone is sharing terere, the ritual matters as much as the drink.
- Accepting a round is friendly; there is no need to overperform expertise.
- Do not treat it like a one-off exotic sample. It is part of normal daily life.

## How to Use This in Recommendations

- In hot months, build pauses around terere, shade, and lighter daytime pacing.
- If the traveler loves ritual and local habits, mention markets, plazas, and road breaks where terere culture is visible.
- For travelers who dislike shared-drink customs, simply explain the ritual and move on without drama.

## Traps

- Describing terere as just "cold mate" and missing the cultural weight.
- Planning alcohol-heavy afternoons in peak heat.
- Ignoring hydration advice when the traveler is doing long outdoor days.
