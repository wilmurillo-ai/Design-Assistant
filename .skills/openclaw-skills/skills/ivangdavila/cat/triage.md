# Cat Triage

## Emergency Now

Escalate to emergency veterinary care now if the cat has:
- trouble breathing, open-mouth breathing, blue or very pale gums
- collapse, seizure, severe weakness, or unresponsiveness
- major trauma, heavy bleeding, or suspected heat injury
- toxin exposure, swallowed medication, or unsafe plant or chemical ingestion
- repeated retching without producing vomit
- repeated trips to the litter box with little or no urine, especially in a male cat
- extreme pain, rigid posture, or a suddenly swollen abdomen

## Same-Day Veterinary Escalation

Push for prompt vet contact if there is:
- repeated vomiting or diarrhea
- sudden hiding, lethargy, or refusal to eat
- major change in litter box use
- eye injury, limping, or obvious pain
- a fast-moving change in behavior that does not fit the cat's baseline

## Minimum Intake Questions

Before giving non-emergency guidance, gather only what changes the decision:
- How old is the cat, and is it male or female if urinary risk matters?
- Indoor only, outdoor, or both?
- What changed first, and when?
- Eating, drinking, litter output, vomiting, and energy now?
- Any toxins, new foods, new meds, trauma, or recent stress?
- Any known conditions, allergies, or current medications?

## Safe Language

Use language like:
- "This could be urgent because..."
- "I cannot confirm the cause from chat alone."
- "The safest next step is..."

Do not:
- diagnose from one photo or one symptom line
- suggest human medications
- tell the user to wait through active red flags

## Follow-Up Checks

If the situation is not clearly emergent, support short factual check-ins:
- still eating or only sniffing food
- any urine or stool since last update
- more vomiting, less, or none
- hiding more, same, or less
- energy compared with the cat's normal baseline
