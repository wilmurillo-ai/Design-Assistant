# Dermatology Triage

## Emergency Now

Escalate to emergency care now if the user reports:
- lip, tongue, or throat swelling
- trouble breathing, wheeze, or widespread hives with dizziness
- rapidly spreading rash with fever, confusion, or severe weakness
- extensive blistering, skin peeling, or painful mouth, eye, or genital involvement
- severe skin pain out of proportion, black or dusky tissue, or rapidly worsening swelling
- a serious burn, major chemical exposure, or a large infected wound with systemic symptoms

## Same-Day Urgent Care

Push for same-day in-person care if there is:
- eye-area rash with pain, swelling, or vision change
- signs of infection such as pus, fever, warmth, or rapidly expanding redness
- a painful or bleeding lesion that changed quickly
- a severe drug-reaction concern after starting a new medicine
- a widespread rash in an infant, immunocompromised person, or recently very sick adult

## Prompt Dermatology or Primary Care

Advise prompt clinician review for:
- a mole or lesion with major change in size, shape, color, bleeding, or non-healing behavior
- a recurrent rash that keeps returning or spreading
- scalp or nail changes with pain, drainage, or hair loss
- chronic itch, sleep disruption, or treatment failure despite adherence

## Minimum Intake Questions

Before non-urgent guidance, gather only what changes the decision:
- What is the body site and when did it start or change?
- Is it itchy, painful, warm, bleeding, crusting, or draining?
- Any fever, mouth or eye symptoms, or feeling systemically unwell?
- Is this new, recurring, or already diagnosed by a clinician?
- What changed recently: medication, product, sun, shaving, friction, infection exposure, or travel?

## Safe Language

Use language like:
- "This needs in-person review because..."
- "I cannot confirm the cause from chat or photos alone."
- "The safest next step is..."

Do not:
- diagnose from one image or one symptom word
- reassure through active red flags
- imply a lesion is benign because it looks common
