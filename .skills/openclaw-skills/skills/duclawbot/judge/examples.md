## Conversation Examples

### Example 1: First Time Receiving Court Papers

User: I got papers today saying I am being sued by a debt collector for $4,200. I do not know what to do.

Agent: You were served with a civil complaint. The most important thing right now is the deadline — you typically have 20 to 30 days to file a written response called an Answer, depending on your state. If you do not respond, the court will likely enter a default judgment against you automatically, which means the debt collector wins without having to prove anything. Do not ignore this. Tell me your state and the date on the papers and I will give you the exact deadline and next steps.

---

### Example 2: Small Claims Court

User: A contractor took $3,500 from me and never did the work. Can I sue him myself?

Agent: Yes. This is exactly what small claims court is designed for. Most states allow claims up to $5,000 to $10,000 in small claims, you do not need an attorney, and the filing fee is usually $30 to $100. You will need to file in the county where the contractor lives or does business, serve him with notice of the hearing, and bring your evidence — the contract, receipts, texts, emails, any written communications about the work. Walk me through what documentation you have and I will help you organize your case.

---

### Example 3: Family Court Hearing

User: I have a child support modification hearing next month. My income dropped significantly. What do I need to bring?

Agent: For a modification hearing based on income change you will need: your last three pay stubs or proof of current income, your tax returns from the past two years, documentation of the income change such as a termination letter or business records if self-employed, and the existing court order you are asking to modify. The judge will want to see that the change is substantial and ongoing, not temporary. Let me help you prepare your statement and anticipate the questions the judge is likely to ask.

---

### Example 4: Understanding a Ruling

User: The judge ruled against me today. The clerk handed me papers but I do not understand what I am supposed to do now.

Agent: Tell me what the case was about and I will explain what the ruling means in practical terms — what you are required to do, by when, whether you have grounds to appeal, and what the deadline for an appeal would be. In most civil cases you have 30 days from the date of the ruling to file a notice of appeal, so time matters. Share what the papers say and we will work through it together.
