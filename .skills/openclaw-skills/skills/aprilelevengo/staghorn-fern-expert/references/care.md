# Staghorn Fern Care Reference

## Watering
The weight test is the golden rule for watering staghorn ferns: lift the mount and water when it feels light. Use the soak-and-dry method every 7-10 days, submerging the entire mount for 15-30 minutes. Adjust frequency seasonally—summer watering every 4-7 days, winter every 10-21 days. Never follow a fixed schedule; always check the plant first. Staghorn ferns use CAM photosynthesis, meaning their stomata open at night for gas exchange, making evening watering optimal for absorption.

## Light
Staghorn ferns fall into three light categories: High (1500-3000+ fc), Medium (500-1500 fc), and Low (200-500 fc). High light species include P. veitchii (tolerates direct sun), P. wandae, and P. bifurcatum. Medium light species include P. grande, P. superbum, and P. elephantotis. Low light species include P. ellisii, P. stemaria, and P. coronarium. East-facing windows are ideal for most indoor species. For grow lights, use 20-40W LED panels providing 150-300 PPFD for 10-14 hours daily.

## Mounting
Western red cedar is the best all-around mount material due to rot resistance and pH neutrality. Cork bark mimics natural habitat and offers excellent moisture retention. Seven mount materials ranked: cedar > cork > driftwood > hardwood > tree fern > basket > pottery. To mount, attach a sphagnum moss bed to the board, position the fern with crown facing up, and secure with fishing line or staples. Ferns self-attach in 3-8 months, after which you can remove ties.

## Substrate & Potting
Never use regular potting soil—epiphytes require airy, fast-draining media. Sphagnum moss works best for mounted ferns. For potted specimens, use a mix of 50% sphagnum moss, 30% orchid bark, and 20% perlite. This combination ensures proper aeration while retaining adequate moisture. Replace sphagnum moss every 12-18 months as it naturally compresses over time, which can reduce drainage and air circulation around the roots.

## Fertilizer
Use balanced 20-20-20 fertilizer at half strength for safest results. Apply fertilizer to the underside of shield fronds at night when CAM stomata are open for better absorption. Homemade alternatives include banana peel water mixed with Epsom salt. Fertilize monthly during the growing season and stop entirely in winter. Over-fertilizing causes salt burn, typically visible as brown tips on frond edges. Less is more with staghorn fern fertilization.

## Propagation
Pup division has a 90%+ success rate and is best performed in spring. Wait until pups develop 2-3 fronds before separating. Spore growing takes 1-2 years from spore to mountable plant and requires sterile conditions. Colony division works well for large multi-bud plants. Note that staghorn ferns cannot be propagated from leaf cuttings or rooted in water—only division and spores work.

## Indoor Care
Best indoor species include P. bifurcatum (most forgiving), P. hillii, and P. veitchii. East-facing windows provide ideal light conditions for most species. Maintain 50-70% humidity using a humidifier in dry climates. Watch for low humidity during winter months when indoor heating dries the air. Monitor plants closely and adjust watering frequency based on environmental conditions rather than following rigid schedules.

## Winter Care
Three main threats during winter: low humidity, reduced light, and temperature fluctuation. Reduce watering frequency by 50% compared to summer. Supplement natural light to 10+ hours daily using grow lights. Stop fertilizing entirely during dormancy. Species minimum temperatures vary: P. veitchii tolerates light frost, P. bifurcatum survives to -2C (30F), but most tropical species need 15C+ (59F+) to thrive without damage.

## Hanging & Display
Wire baskets offer the simplest mounting method for beginners. French cleats work best for ferns over 10 lbs and allow easy removal for watering. Tree mounting is only viable in USDA zones 9-12 (frost-free regions). Mature specimens can weigh 50-100+ lbs when fully saturated with water. Always use hardware rated for at least 2x the wet weight to ensure safety and prevent mounting failures.

## Anatomy
Staghorn ferns have five main parts: crown (growing point), shield frond (flat basal), bud point, fertile frond (antler-like), and sporangium. Single-bud species (P. grande, P. superbum, P. ridleyi) have one growing point and cannot divide by pups. Multi-bud species (P. bifurcatum, P. hillii, P. veitchii) have multiple growing points and divide easily. Roots attach the plant rather than absorb nutrients. Shield fronds collect water and organic debris for nutrition.

## Elkhorn vs Staghorn
Staghorn refers to P. bifurcatum while Elkhorn refers to P. hillii—both are Australian common names. Frond shape differs: bifurcatum produces more forked fronds while hillii has more upright, compact growth. Growth patterns vary: bifurcatum spreads wider while hillii forms tighter colonies. Cold tolerance differs slightly: bifurcatum tolerates down to -2C (30F) while hillii handles only 5C (40F) minimum temperatures.
