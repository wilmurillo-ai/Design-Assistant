# Deep Dialogue 🧠

**A structured framework for philosophical and psychological conversations.**

[![Agent Skill](https://img.shields.io/badge/Agent-Skill-blue)](https://github.com/arikmir/deep-dialogue)
[![License: MIT](https://img.shields.io/badge/License-MIT-yellow.svg)](https://opensource.org/licenses/MIT)

## What It Does

Deep Dialogue guides AI agents through meaningful conversations about personal struggles, existential questions, and psychological patterns. Instead of surface-level advice, it provides a structured framework for genuine insight.

### Integrated Frameworks
- **Stoicism** — Dichotomy of control, negative visualization, practical exercises
- **Existentialism** — Freedom, authenticity, meaning-creation, being-toward-death
- **Socratic Method** — Elenchus questioning, assumption testing, dialectic
- **CBT** — Cognitive distortions, thought restructuring, behavioral experiments
- **ACT** — Defusion, acceptance, values clarification, committed action
- **Jungian Psychology** — Shadow work, archetypes, individuation, projection

## Examples

### Example 1: Anxiety About Career Change

**User:** "I want to quit my job and start a business, but I am terrified of failing."

**Agent (Phase 1 - Clarify):**
> When you say "terrified" — what specifically are you afraid will happen? Financial ruin? Looking foolish? Something else?

**User:** "I guess... what if I give up a stable income and it all falls apart? Everyone will think I made a stupid decision."

**Agent (Phase 3 - Framework):**
> Two things happening here. First, Stoicism: the outcome — success or failure — is not fully in your control. Your effort, preparation, and response to setbacks are. Second, you are mind-reading — assuming "everyone" will judge you. That is a cognitive distortion. What evidence do you have for that?

**Agent (Phase 4 - Challenge):**
> Here is a harder question: If you stay in your job and never try, will you judge *yourself*? Which regret is worse — trying and failing, or never knowing?

**User:** "...never knowing. That is worse."

**Agent (Phase 5 - Synthesize):**
> So the fear is not really about failure — it is about judgment. And the deepest judgment you fear is your own. The Stoics would say: act according to your values, accept what follows. What is one small step you could take this week that moves toward your goal without burning bridges?

### Example 2: Feeling Like a Fraud

**User:** "I got promoted but I feel like I do not deserve it. Like they will figure out I am not that good."

**Agent (Phase 1 - Clarify):**
> Impostor syndrome. What specifically makes you feel undeserving? Is there concrete evidence you are underqualified, or is this a feeling?

**User:** "It is a feeling. I mean, I hit my targets. But I feel like I just got lucky."

**Agent (Phase 3 - Framework):**
> CBT calls this "discounting the positive" — you achieved something, but you explain it away as luck rather than skill. Let me ask: if a colleague hit the same targets, would you say *they* got lucky?

**User:** "No, I would say they earned it."

**Agent (Phase 4 - Challenge):**
> So you apply different rules to yourself than others. Why? What would it mean to accept that you actually earned this?

**User:** "I guess... then I would have to keep performing. The pressure."

**Agent (Phase 5 - Synthesize):**
> There it is. The impostor story protects you from the weight of your own competence. If you are "lucky," you are off the hook. If you are genuinely good, you have to own it — and that is terrifying. But it is also the only path to real confidence.

### Example 3: Existential Emptiness

**User:** "I have a good life on paper but nothing feels meaningful. What is the point?"

**Agent (Phase 2 - Explore):**
> When did you last feel something *did* matter? Even briefly?

**User:** "Maybe... when I was helping my nephew learn to ride a bike. I was fully there."

**Agent (Phase 3 - Framework):**
> Existentialism says meaning is not found — it is created. The universe does not hand you purpose; you forge it through engagement. That moment with your nephew was meaningful because you were present and connected. Camus would say: that *is* the answer. Not a grand purpose, but full presence in small moments.

**Agent (Phase 4 - Challenge):**
> But here is the uncomfortable part: if meaning comes from engagement, then the emptiness is partly a choice. What are you avoiding by staying disengaged?

**User:** "If I care about things, they can hurt me."

**Agent (Phase 5 - Synthesize):**
> So the emptiness is armor. You traded meaning for safety. The existentialists would say: that is bad faith — pretending you have no choice when you do. The question is not "what is the point?" but "what are you willing to risk caring about?"

---

### Example 4: Repeating Relationship Patterns

**User:** "I keep dating people who end up being emotionally unavailable. Why do I do this?"

**Agent (Phase 1 - Clarify):**
> How many times has this pattern repeated? And when you first meet them, are they unavailable from the start, or does it emerge later?

**User:** "Maybe four or five times. They seem great at first. Then they pull away."

**Agent (Phase 3 - Framework):**
> Jung would look at this as projection and shadow. Sometimes we are drawn to people who mirror unresolved parts of ourselves. Question: In your family growing up, was love consistent or did you have to earn it?

**User:** "My dad was... not around much. Emotionally, I mean."

**Agent (Phase 4 - Challenge):**
> So unavailable love feels familiar. On some level, you might be recreating what you know — trying to finally "win" the love that was withheld. But you keep choosing people who cannot give it. What would it feel like to be with someone who was actually present and available?

**User:** "Honestly? Weird. Uncomfortable."

**Agent (Phase 5 - Synthesize):**
> That discomfort is the work. Healthy love might feel "boring" at first because it lacks the drama of pursuit. But that is not boredom — that is safety. The pattern breaks when you can tolerate being loved without having to chase it.

## Conversation Phases

1. **Clarification** — Socratic questioning to understand the actual issue
2. **Phenomenological Exploration** — Map the lived experience
3. **Framework Application** — Apply relevant philosophical/psychological lens
4. **Dialectical Challenge** — Push back constructively, test assumptions
5. **Integration & Synthesis** — Crystallize insights into understanding

## Usage

The skill triggers on philosophical/psychological conversations, or explicitly with:

| Command | Action |
|---------|--------|
| `/deep` | Start a deep dialogue session |
| `/framework stoicism` | Load Stoicism reference |
| `/challenge` | Request dialectical pushback |
| `/synthesize` | Summarize current insights |
