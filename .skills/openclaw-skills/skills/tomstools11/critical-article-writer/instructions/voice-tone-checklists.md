# Voice & Tone Checklists

## Pre-Writing Checklist

- [ ] Identified primary argument/thesis
- [ ] Gathered supporting evidence/data
- [ ] Considered counterarguments
- [ ] Determined appropriate length (quick take vs. deep dive)
- [ ] Identified target audience and their knowledge level
- [ ] Chosen opening strategy that fits content
- [ ] Planned evidence integration points
- [ ] Selected 3-5 relevant hashtags (if applicable)

## During Writing Checklist

### Tone & Voice
- [ ] Starting with context, ending with sharp observation
- [ ] Using conversational language without sacrificing substance
- [ ] Incorporating strategic humor where appropriate (not forced)
- [ ] Asking rhetorical questions to engage readers
- [ ] Balancing skepticism with fairness

### Structure & Flow
- [ ] Opening hooks reader immediately
- [ ] Each section builds on previous one
- [ ] Using em dashes, colons, and varied sentence length
- [ ] Supporting claims with specific examples
- [ ] Addressing complexity and nuance
- [ ] Conclusion ties back to opening

### Credibility & Accuracy
- [ ] All major claims backed by data or sources
- [ ] Links provided for evidence
- [ ] Acknowledging uncertainty where appropriate
- [ ] No strawman arguments
- [ ] Distinguishing personal experience from industry patterns

### Technical Elements (If Applicable)
- [ ] Technical terms used accurately and explained
- [ ] Industry jargon balanced with accessibility
- [ ] Hashtags relevant and strategically placed
- [ ] Links formatted and functional

## Post-Writing Checklist

- [ ] Read aloud to check rhythm and flow
- [ ] Verify all claims and statistics
- [ ] Check for logical consistency
- [ ] Ensure opening matches conclusion
- [ ] Review for unnecessary repetition
- [ ] Confirm tone matches intended voice
- [ ] Test all links and references
- [ ] Proofread for typos and grammar
- [ ] Verify hashtags are appropriate
- [ ] Consider if anything needs source citation

## Tone Adjustment Guide

### If Your Draft Feels Too Dry
- Add more direct address to reader ("Here's what you're missing...")
- Include rhetorical questions
- Use more em dashes for emphasis
- Inject specific examples with personality
- Consider self-deprecating observation ("I'm no financial expert, but...")

### If Your Draft Feels Too Casual
- Increase specific evidence and data citations
- Develop technical explanations more thoroughly
- Use fewer colloquialisms
- Add more analytical frameworks
- Balance humor with substantive argumentation

### If Your Draft Feels Uncertain
- Clarify your actual position vs. presenting options
- Use stronger evidence or remove unsupported claims
- Acknowledge gaps explicitly rather than hedging
- Differentiate between "I don't know" and "data shows"
- Restructure to lead with strongest arguments

### If Your Draft Feels Aggressive
- Acknowledge legitimate strengths of criticized entity
- Present strongest version of opposing argument
- Soften language while keeping edge ("questionable" vs. "terrible")
- Add nuance sections
- Include "I might be wrong about this" where appropriate

---

**Use these checklists before, during, and after writing to maintain consistency with the critical article style.**
