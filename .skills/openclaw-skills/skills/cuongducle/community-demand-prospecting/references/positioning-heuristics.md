# Positioning Heuristics

Use this reference after market research and before outreach.

## Goal

Choose the clearest angle for how the product should be described in public, especially in early-stage marketing.

Do not try to lead with every strength at once.

## Primary Angles

### Problem-Led

Lead with the pain when:

- the pain is easy to recognize
- users already complain about it in plain language
- urgency matters more than category education

Good for:

- frustrating repeated workflows
- costly failures
- frequent interruptions

### Audience-Led

Lead with the audience when:

- a narrow group has a strong shared identity
- that group already gathers in distinct communities
- the product is not obviously relevant outside that group

Good for:

- role-specific tools
- niche craft workflows
- community-native products

### Comparison-Led

Lead with comparison when:

- the category already has obvious incumbents
- users openly compare options
- the product wins on one or two clear dimensions

Good for:

- alternatives to bloated tools
- cheaper or simpler replacements
- focused substitutes

### Simplicity-Led

Lead with simplicity when:

- existing tools feel overbuilt
- setup friction blocks trial
- the product intentionally does less, but better

Good for:

- utilities
- narrow workflow tools
- lightweight replacements

### Trust-Led

Lead with trust when:

- users care about safety, privacy, reliability, or control
- failures are expensive
- adoption depends on confidence more than novelty

Good for:

- infrastructure tools
- security-sensitive products
- workflow tools with high switching risk

### Workflow-Fit-Led

Lead with workflow fit when:

- users repeat the same job often
- generic tools can technically do it, but poorly
- the product is strongest in a specific repeated scenario

Good for:

- tools with strong job-to-be-done fit
- products that save time in one repeated task

## Selection Rules

Choose the primary angle by asking:

- what users already recognize fastest
- what makes the product easiest to explain
- what most clearly separates it from alternatives

Choose a secondary angle only if it reinforces the first one.

Avoid pairings that blur the message, such as:

- broad audience plus broad problem
- trust plus hype
- comparison plus too many feature claims

## Output Pattern

Return:

- primary angle
- optional secondary angle
- why this angle fits the observed market
- one-line positioning
- what not to lead with
