## 🏛️ Meridian Gate (Wumen / 午门)

### L1 Basic
> "This is the Meridian Gate, the main entrance to the Forbidden City. Built in 1420, it's where the emperor issued imperial edicts and held grand ceremonies."

### L2 Standard
> "The Meridian Gate, built in Ming Dynasty (1420), is the main southern entrance to the Forbidden City. It's named 'Wumen' because it faces south and sits on the north-south meridian axis.
>
> The gate has a distinctive concave shape with five pavilions, earning it the nickname 'Five-Phoenix Tower.' The central archway was exclusively for the emperor—only the empress could use it once, on her wedding day.
>
> Contrary to popular belief, executions did NOT happen here. That's a myth from TV dramas. The actual execution ground was at Caishikou. However, officials who angered the emperor were sometimes caned in the square—a punishment called 'Tingzhang.'
>
> During important ceremonies, the emperor would stand on the tower to review his troops and receive victorious generals."

### L3 Deep
> "**Architecture**:
> The Meridian Gate stands 37.95 meters tall, with a double-eaved hip roof—the highest architectural rank in ancient China. The roof ridge features 9 mythical beasts, second only to the Hall of Supreme Harmony's 10.
>
> **Historical Events**:
> - 1644: Li Zicheng entered through this gate, marking the end of Ming Dynasty
> - 1900: Eight-Nation Alliance held their entry ceremony here
> - 1912: Puyi abdicated but continued living in the Inner Court
>
> **Imperial Edict Ceremony**:
> When the emperor issued important edicts, a elaborate ceremony was held on the tower. Officials would place the edict in a cloud-shaped container, lower it down with colorful ropes, and officials below would receive it on their knees before distributing it nationwide.
>
> **The Tingzhang Punishment**:
> In Ming Dynasty, officials who offended the emperor were publicly caned in the square. In 1519, 146 officials were punished for opposing the emperor's southern tour—11 died from the beating, the largest such incident in history.
>
> **Trivia**:
> - The gate has 81 door nails (9×9), symbolizing the highest imperial rank
> - The gate was struck by lightning in Ming Dynasty and rebuilt
> - The side wings are called 'Goose Wing Towers' due to their shape"

## 👑 Hall of Supreme Harmony (Taihedian / 太和殿)

### L1 Basic
> "This is the Hall of Supreme Harmony, the largest wooden structure in China. Known as the 'Golden Throne Hall,' it's where emperors held their coronation, wedding, and other grand ceremonies."

### L2 Standard
> "The Hall of Supreme Harmony is the grandest building in the Forbidden City and the largest wooden structure in China. Built in 1420 during Ming Dynasty, the current structure was rebuilt in Kangxi's reign (1695) after being destroyed by fire.
>
> The hall stands 35.05 meters tall with 72 massive wooden pillars. The six central pillars are 12.7 meters tall, made of entire nanmu trees covered in gold lacquer with coiled dragons.
>
> Inside, beneath an elaborate caisson ceiling, sits the Dragon Throne—the emperor's golden lacquered chair. This is where the most important ceremonies took place: coronations, imperial weddings, and the announcement of successful candidates in the imperial examinations.
>
> Three times a year (New Year, Winter Solstice, and the emperor's birthday), the emperor would hold court here to receive congratulations from all officials."

### L3 Deep
> "**Architectural Marvel**:
> Dimensions: 63.96m wide × 37.17m deep × 35.05m high, covering 2,377 square meters. It's supported by 72 nanmu pillars, with the central 6 being entire trees over 12 meters tall.
>
> **Three Reconstructions**:
> The hall has been destroyed and rebuilt three times:
> - 1420: Original construction under Emperor Yongle
> - 1440: Rebuilt during Zhengtong reign
> - 1679: Destroyed by lightning, rebuilt 1695 under Kangxi (current structure)
>
> **The 10 Roof Beasts**:
> The roof ridge features 10 mythical beasts—the ONLY building in the Forbidden City with this many. The 10th beast is 'Xingshi,' a monkey-like figure holding a vajra, representing the thunder god. Its purpose: protect against fire and lightning.
>
> **The Dragon Throne**:
> The throne stands 1.8 meters tall, carved with 9 golden dragons. Above it hangs an elaborate caisson ceiling with a bronze mirror (Xuanyuan Mirror). Legend says it would fall on anyone who wasn't the rightful emperor—Yuan Shikai reportedly moved the throne back because he feared this.
>
> **Coronation Ceremony Process**:
> 1. Emperor departs from Palace of Heavenly Purity
> 2. Changes into ceremonial robes in Hall of Central Harmony
> 3. Enters through the back door and ascends the throne
> 4. Imperial edict is read aloud
> 5. All officials perform the three-kneels-nine-prostrations ceremony
> 6. Emperor returns via the same route
>
> **'Golden Bricks' Flooring**:
> The floor is paved with special bricks from Suzhou, ironically called 'golden bricks.' Each brick took 2 years to make: selecting clay, refining, molding, air-drying, firing, and oil-soaking. They cost approximately one tael of gold each.
>
> **Trivia**:
> - The plaque features both Manchu and Chinese text (Manchu on left)
> - Bronze turtle and crane in front symbolize longevity
> - Sundial and grain measure stand on either side
> - The emperor did NOT hold daily court here; daily work was done in Palace of Heavenly Purity or Hall of Mental Cultivation"

## 🏯 Hall of Central Harmony (Zhonghedian / 中和殿)

### L1 Basic
> "This is the Hall of Central Harmony, where the emperor rested before ceremonies in the Hall of Supreme Harmony. It's the smallest of the three main halls."

### L2 Standard
> "The Hall of Central Harmony sits between the Hall of Supreme Harmony and Hall of Preserving Harmony. It's a square building with a single-eaved pyramidal roof topped with a gilded bronze finial.
>
> The emperor would rest here before grand ceremonies, making final preparations to his robes and appearance. Before the Ploughing Ceremony, the emperor would also inspect the sacrificial texts here.
>
> During Qing Dynasty, emperors would view seeds and farming tools here, demonstrating the importance of agriculture."

### L3 Deep
> "**Unique Architecture**:
> Unlike other major halls, this is a square building (24.15m × 24.15m), symbolizing 'centrality and harmony.' The roof is a single-eaved pyramidal design with a gilded bronze finial—unique in the Forbidden City.
>
> **Meaning of 'Zhonghe'**:
> From the Book of Rites: 'Zhong (centrality) is the great foundation of the world; He (harmony) is the universal path.' This reflects the Confucian ideal of balanced, moderate governance.
>
> **Historical Functions**:
> - Ming Dynasty: Resting place before ceremonies
> - Qing Dynasty: Added inspection of sacrificial texts and farming tools
> - Qianlong reign: Imperial lectures (Jingyan) were held here
>
> **The Ploughing Ceremony**:
> Each spring, the emperor would personally plough a field at the Temple of Agriculture to demonstrate the importance of farming. The day before, he would inspect the sacrificial texts and farming tools in this hall.
>
> **Trivia**:
> - Only square-plan major hall in the Forbidden City
> - Bronze finial on top is about 2 meters tall
> - Emperor also came here before Temple of Heaven ceremonies"

## 📜 Hall of Preserving Harmony (Baohedian / 保和殿)

### L1 Basic
> "This is the Hall of Preserving Harmony, where the imperial examinations were held. Behind it is the largest stone carving in the Forbidden City, weighing over 200 tons."

### L2 Standard
> "The Hall of Preserving Harmony is the last of the three main halls, second only to the Hall of Supreme Harmony in size. In Ming Dynasty, it was used for imperial banquets. From Qianlong's reign, it became the venue for the palace examination—the highest level of imperial examinations.
>
> The palace examination was presided over by the emperor himself. Successful candidates were ranked in three tiers: first tier (top three: Zhuangyuan, Bangyan, Tanhua), second tier, and third tier.
>
> Behind the hall lies a massive stone carving, 16.57 meters long, 3.07 meters wide, 1.7 meters thick, weighing approximately 200 tons. It's carved with nine dragons playing with a pearl, symbolizing imperial power."

### L3 Deep
> "**Architecture**:
> Dimensions: 63.96m wide × 37.17m deep × 29.5m high. The roof is double-eaved gable-and-hip, one rank below the Hall of Supreme Harmony's hip roof.
>
> **Meaning of 'Baohe'**:
> From the I Ching: 'Maintain great harmony, and all will benefit.' This reflects the Confucian value of harmony as the highest ideal.
>
> **The Palace Examination**:
> Held here during Qing Dynasty, the process was:
> 1. Emperor ascends the throne in the hall
> 2. Candidates (who passed provincial exams) kneel in the square
> 3. Exam questions, personally written by the emperor, are distributed
> 4. Candidates answer in the square, with one day to complete
> 5. Papers are collected at dusk and graded by officials
> 6. Next day, results are announced in the hall
>
> **The Great Stone Carving**:
> Behind the hall is the largest stone carving in the Forbidden City: 16.57m long, 3.07m wide, 1.7m thick, weighing ~200 tons. Carved during Yongle reign, it features nine dragons playing with a pearl amidst sea and cliffs, symbolizing 'nine dragons protect the empire forever.'
>
> **The Legendary Transport**:
> The stone came from Fangshan, southwest of Beijing. During winter, workers dug 140 wells along the route, poured water to create an ice road, and used thousands of workers to drag it on ice. It took 28 days, over 20,000 workers, and 110,000 taels of silver.
>
> **Historical Events**:
> - 1647: First palace examination held here
> - 1789: Heshen presided over the examination
> - 1895: Kang Youwei passed the examination and became a Jinshi
>
> **Trivia**:
> - Last reconstructed of the three halls (Wanli reign)
> - Top examination scorer (Zhuangyuan) could plant a locust tree here
> - Last Zhuangyuan was Liu Chunlin in 1904"

## 🛏️ Palace of Heavenly Purity (Qianqinggong / 乾清宫)

### L1 Basic
> "This is the Palace of Heavenly Purity, the emperor's residence. The 'Zhengda Guangming' plaque behind the throne hid the secret imperial will naming the successor."

### L2 Standard
> "The Palace of Heavenly Purity was the emperor's residence during Ming and early Qing Dynasty. After Emperor Yongzheng, emperors moved to the Hall of Mental Cultivation, but this palace remained important for ceremonies.
>
> The 'Zhengda Guangming' (Justice and Brightness) plaque was written by Emperor Shunzhi. Starting from Yongzheng, emperors practiced secret succession: the will was written in duplicate, one hidden behind this plaque, one carried by the emperor. After his death, officials would retrieve and compare them to announce the new emperor.
>
> Emperors Kangxi, Yongzheng, Qianlong, Jiaqing, and Daoguang all lived here and handled daily affairs."

### L3 Deep
> "**Architecture**:
> Dimensions: 60m wide × 33m deep × 20m high. The double-eaved hip roof matches the Hall of Supreme Harmony's rank, showing its importance.
>
> **The 'Zhengda Guangming' Plaque**:
> Written by Emperor Shunzhi, the four characters mean the emperor should be upright and光明 (bright/clear). Behind it was where the secret succession edict was hidden.
>
> **Secret Succession System**:
> After the tragic succession struggle among Kangxi's nine sons, Yongzheng invented this system:
> 1. Emperor writes the succession edict in duplicate
> 2. One copy hidden behind the plaque
> 3. One copy carried by the emperor
> 4. After emperor's death, officials retrieve and compare them
>
> This system effectively prevented succession struggles among princes.
>
> **Historical Events**:
> - 1722: Kangxi died at Changchun Garden; Yongzheng succeeded here
> - 1799: After Qianlong's death, Jiaqing handled affairs here
> - 1861: After Xianfeng's death, Cixi launched the Xinyou Coup here
>
> **Architecture Details**:
> - Golden throne similar to Hall of Supreme Harmony, slightly smaller
> - East and West warm chambers: for emperor's reading and rest
> - Moon terrace in front: for small ceremonies
>
> **Trivia**:
> - The palace burned down 14 times; current structure rebuilt in Jiaqing reign
> - Originally had 27 beds; emperor randomly chose each night to prevent assassination
> - Kangxi created the 'Thousand Elderly Banquet' here, hosting seniors over 65 nationwide"

## 🌳 Imperial Garden (Yuhuayuan / 御花园)

### L1 Basic
> "This is the Imperial Garden, where emperors and empresses relaxed. It features ancient cypress trees, rockeries, and pavilions. It's the end of the central axis."

### L2 Standard
> "The Imperial Garden is at the northern end of the Forbidden City, serving as the imperial family's private garden. It features dozens of ancient cypress trees, with the most famous being the 'Linked Cypress'—two trees whose trunks merge, symbolizing eternal love.
>
> The garden includes the Xiuqiu Hill, a rockery made of Taihu stones with a pavilion on top. Emperors would climb here during the Double Ninth Festival. Other structures include the Qin'an Hall, Wanchun Pavilion, and Qianqiu Pavilion.
>
> This is the last stop on the central axis. After visiting, you'll exit through the Gate of Divine Might."

### L3 Deep
> "**Garden Layout**:
> Covering 12,000 square meters (80m north-south, 140m east-west), the garden is symmetrically arranged around the Qin'an Hall, with pavilions and terraces on both sides.
>
> **The Linked Cypress**:
> The most famous tree stands before Qin'an Hall. Two cypress trees merge about 2 meters above ground, forming a natural arch. Legend says couples who take photos here will stay together forever.
>
> **Xiuqiu Hill**:
> Located in the northeast, this 10-meter hill is made of Taihu stones. At the top is the Yujing Pavilion, where emperors climbed during the Double Ninth Festival. The hill has caves you can walk through—cleverly designed.
>
> **Qin'an Hall**:
> The main building, dedicated to the Dark Lord (water god), meant to protect against fire. Originally housed a bronze statue, now gone. Emperors held water god ceremonies here annually.
>
> **Wanchun and Qianqiu Pavilions**:
> These symmetric pavilions have unique designs: circular upper level, square lower level, symbolizing 'round heaven, square earth.' Originally housed clocks, now gone.
>
> **Seasonal Flowers**:
> - Spring: Crabapple, lilac, magnolia
> - Summer: Peony, Chinese herbaceous peony, lotus
> - Autumn: Chrysanthemum, osmanthus
> - Winter: Plum blossoms (potted)
>
> **Trivia**:
> - Cypress trees are 300-500 years old; oldest over 600 years
> - Originally had 160+ decorative rocks; now about 80 remain
> - Emperors often held small banquets here
> - Last emperor Puyi once rode a bicycle here and sawed off some door thresholds"
