# Divorce Coaching Video Marketing Skill

## Overview

This skill helps licensed therapists, psychologists, counselors, marriage and family therapists, life coaches, and mental wellness practices create video content that reduces stigma, builds trust, and drives new client inquiries. Mental health marketing requires particular sensitivity: potential clients may be in vulnerable moments, may face stigma concerns, and are making deeply personal decisions. Video content done with genuine care and clinical sensitivity can make the difference between someone seeking help and not.

## Industry Context

The US mental health services market exceeds $75 billion annually. Market dynamics:
- Demand for mental health services has significantly outpaced provider supply since 2020
- Telehealth has dramatically expanded geographic reach for therapists
- Stigma reduction is both a societal trend and a marketing opportunity
- Specialization (trauma, LGBTQ+ affirming, ADHD, eating disorders) drives referrals
- Insurance billing versus private pay creates distinct marketing approaches

## Ethical Considerations in Mental Health Marketing

Before discussing video content, critical boundaries:
- Never exploit anxiety or crisis for marketing purposes
- Avoid language that over-promises outcomes ("cure," "fix," "heal")
- Do not use before-and-after framing for mental health conditions
- Protect client privacy absolutely — never feature client faces or details without explicit consent
- Comply with professional licensing board marketing guidelines for your state
- Content should reduce barriers to care, not create urgency through fear

## Video Types for Mental Health Practices

### 1. Therapist Introduction (Primary Conversion Content)
Who you are, your training, your theoretical orientation, and what sessions with you feel like. Clients choose therapists based on perceived fit.

### 2. What to Expect in Therapy
Demystifying the therapy process for first-time clients. Reduces the anxiety that prevents people from making the call.

### 3. Specialty Area Education
What is trauma therapy, what is EMDR, what is DBT, what is CBT. Educational content about approaches builds understanding and trust.

### 4. Mental Health Myth Debunking
Common misconceptions about therapy, medication, mental illness, and help-seeking. Reduces stigma and drives inquiry.

### 5. Self-Care and Wellness Tips
Practical mental health strategies: breathing exercises, grounding techniques, sleep hygiene. Builds audience and demonstrates genuine care.

### 6. Telehealth Explanation
What a telehealth therapy session looks like, how privacy is protected, how effective it is. Reduces barrier for first-time telehealth clients.

### 7. Specialty Population Content
Content for specific communities: parents, teens, first responders, LGBTQ+ individuals, professionals. Shows specialization and builds community trust.

### 8. FAQ Content
How long does therapy take? How do I know if I need therapy? How do I choose a therapist? Educational content that addresses the questions preventing people from starting.

## Script Templates

### Template 1: Therapist Introduction (90 seconds)

**Opening (0-12s):**
[Therapist in calm, welcoming office or natural setting]
"I am [Name], and I am a licensed [credential] therapist specializing in [specialty]. I want to tell you what it is actually like to work with me, because I know that choosing a therapist is one of the most personal decisions you can make."

**The Approach (12-45s):**
"I work from a [theoretical orientation] approach, which means that in our work together, [brief explanation of what this means for the client experience]. I believe therapy is a collaboration — you bring your experience and your goals; I bring training, skills, and a genuine commitment to your wellbeing. My job is not to tell you what to do or how to think. It is to help you understand yourself more clearly and develop the skills to move toward the life you want."

**Who I Work With (45-68s):**
"I primarily work with [population description — adults, adolescents, couples, etc.] who are navigating [presenting issues]. I have particular experience with [specialty]. My clients tend to be [relatable description of your typical client — not pathologizing but humanizing]."

**The Practical Information (68-82s):**
"I offer sessions in person at my [city] office and via telehealth for clients in [state]. I see clients [schedule] and currently have [availability description]. For fee information and insurance coverage, please visit my website."

**CTA (82-90s):**
"I offer a free 15-minute consultation so you can get a sense of whether we might be a good fit. Link in bio or at [website]."

### Template 2: What to Expect in First Therapy Session (75 seconds)

**The Anxiety (0-10s):**
"If you have never been to therapy before — or if it has been a while — you might be nervous about what to expect. Here is what actually happens in a first session."

**The Reality (10-50s):**
"First sessions are mostly about information gathering. I will ask about what brings you in, what you have been experiencing, and what you are hoping therapy might offer. You do not have to have everything figured out. You do not have to have a clear answer. People often say 'I am not even sure why I am here' — that is completely okay.

I will ask about your background and history because context matters. We will also talk about logistics: how we will work together, what confidentiality means and its limits, and what to expect from the process.

You will not be pushed to share anything you are not ready to share. You are in charge of how fast or slow this goes. The whole session is a conversation — a chance for both of us to see if this feels like a fit."

**Normalization (50-65s):**
"Many people feel emotional in first sessions — sometimes just because they have finally given themselves permission to show up. That is okay too. There is no wrong way to do your first therapy session."

**CTA (65-75s):**
"Free 15-minute consultation to answer your questions before you schedule. [Website]."

## Building an Ethical Mental Health Practice

The mental health provider who creates consistent, stigma-reducing, genuinely helpful content builds the most trusted health brand in their market. This content serves two simultaneous goals: it reaches people who need help and it reduces the barriers that prevent people from seeking it.

The therapist whose content a future client reads for six months before calling — and who says in the first session "I felt like I already knew you from your videos" — has already done the most important therapeutic work: building enough trust for someone to ask for help.

Protect client privacy absolutely. Build content around education and genuine care. The practice that shows up consistently and ethically in video content will attract clients for as long as the content exists.

## Specialization as Marketing Strategy

In mental health, specialization drives referrals:

Trauma-informed therapy: Content specifically about trauma, PTSD, and trauma-informed approaches reaches clients who have not found understanding elsewhere. "I finally found a therapist who gets it" is the highest-value referral.

LGBTQ+ affirming therapy: Content explicitly welcoming and affirming of LGBTQ+ identities and experiences reaches a community with high need and historically poor service. An affirming therapist who shows up consistently in video content becomes a trusted resource for an entire community.

Perinatal and postpartum mental health: Pregnancy and the postpartum period are high-need times with significant mental health challenges. Content addressing this specific population reaches parents in acute need.

Anxiety and OCD specialization: One of the largest mental health markets. Content about anxiety, panic, and OCD that is clinically accurate and destigmatizing reaches millions of people who are suffering silently.

Eating disorder treatment: High-need, high-acuity, and often underserved. Specialized content in this area reaches clients and their families with high urgency.

## The Consultation Conversion Content

The free 15-minute consultation is the primary conversion mechanism for most therapy practices. Video content that prepares prospects for the consultation increases conversion:

"What to ask in a therapy consultation" — helps prospects know what to say and reduces consultation anxiety.

"Signs this therapist might be right for you" — helps clients identify fit indicators and reduces decision paralysis.

"What happens after you book a consultation" — removes uncertainty from the process that causes prospect drop-off.

The more thoroughly you reduce the anxiety of the unknown, the higher your consultation conversion and the lower your no-show rate.

## The Referral Network in Mental Health

Most therapy practices grow primarily through referral. Video content strengthens the referral network:

Primary care physician relationships: Physicians refer mental health clients every day. A therapist whose expertise and approach is visible through video is more memorable and trustworthy to the PCP who will make the referral.

School counselor relationships: School counselors refer students and families to therapists constantly. Content that demonstrates your work with children and adolescents reaches this important referral source.

Psychiatric prescriber relationships: Psychiatrists who prescribe medication often prefer to refer to therapists for the therapy component. A visible, specialized therapist who communicates effectively is the preferred referral.

Employee Assistance Programs (EAP): Many companies offer EAP services that refer to therapists. A therapist with professional video presence is more likely to be included in EAP networks.

Peer therapist relationships: Therapists refer clients outside their specialty or geographic area to trusted colleagues. Video content that clearly communicates your specialty helps colleagues know when you are the right referral.

## Building a Waitlist Practice

The goal of consistent mental health video marketing is to move from "accepting clients" to "have a waitlist":

Content builds demand that can be managed: A waitlist is not a failure to serve clients — it is a signal of quality and specialization that allows you to see the clients whose needs best match your expertise.

Waitlist management content: Content that helps clients prepare for their first session before they start, maintain between-session practices, and understand the value of the waiting period reduces waitlist drop-off.

Group therapy content: When individual therapy waitlist is long, group therapy content reaches clients who are ready now and provides service to more people with the same therapist time investment.

Premium intensives: Intensive therapy programs (half-day or full-day sessions over a week) serve clients who need immediate focused work and generate significant revenue from a small number of clients.

The mental health practice that builds a waitlist through consistent, ethical, stigma-reducing video content has achieved something rare: more demand than supply of excellent therapeutic services.


## Seasonal Content Considerations

Mental health needs are not uniformly seasonal, but certain times of year drive particular topics:

January: Post-holiday depression, New Year anxiety, seasonal affective disorder
February: Valentine's Day relationship content, loneliness and isolation
March-April: Seasonal transition, spring anxiety, academic stress
May: Mental Health Awareness Month (highest content engagement period)
June-August: Summer anxiety, vacation and social pressure, teen mental health
September: Back-to-school, seasonal transition, academic pressure
October: ADHD Awareness Month, anxiety content
November-December: Holiday stress, family conflict, grief and loss, Seasonal Affective Disorder

May (Mental Health Awareness Month) is the highest-engagement period for mental health content. Content planned for May should be your best and most shareable work.

## Final Thoughts on Ethics and Care

Mental health marketing is unique because the people you are trying to reach are often at their most vulnerable. The content you create should honor that vulnerability:

Every video should serve the viewer, not just market to them.
Every claim should be accurate and appropriately qualified.
Every image and word should treat mental health with the dignity it deserves.
Client privacy is absolute — even anonymized case content requires careful thought.
The most effective mental health marketing is not marketing at all — it is genuine education and stigma reduction that happens to make your practice visible.

The therapist who shows up in video content with genuine care, clinical expertise, and authentic humanity builds the most trusted practice in mental health. That trust is the foundation of everything that follows.


## Video as a Bridge to Care

The most important function of mental health video marketing is not conversion — it is connection. A person who watches your therapist introduction video and thinks 'that is someone I could talk to' has taken the first step toward getting help. Even if they do not call that week, you have reduced the distance between where they are and where they need to be.

That is the unique responsibility and privilege of mental health video content. Used with care, consistency, and genuine concern for the people you serve, it becomes the bridge that brings people from isolation to connection, from suffering to support, from uncertainty to the help they need.

Create content that you would want a struggling friend to find. That is the standard.
