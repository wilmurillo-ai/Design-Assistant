# Aquarium Video Maker — Fish Tank Setup and Aquascaping Videos

The tank has been cycling for six weeks, the water parameters are finally stable, the hardscape took three redesigns because the first looked like a pile of rocks and the second looked like a different pile of rocks and the third looked intentional — which is the entire difference between a fish tank and an aquascape — and the person responsible for this underwater garden is about to discover that the hobby they started "to have something relaxing in the living room" now involves a test kit with seven reagents, a fertilizer dosing schedule more complex than their meal prep, and strong opinions about the nitrogen cycle that they share with anyone who makes the mistake of asking "oh, you have fish?" Aquarium content is YouTube's most meditative yet technically obsessive niche: the videos are visually calming (slow fish, swaying plants, bubbling filters) while the commentary is intensely specific about water chemistry, lighting spectrums, substrate composition, and the ongoing diplomatic negotiations between species that share a 40-gallon glass box. This tool transforms aquarium footage into polished fishkeeping videos — tank setup builds documented from empty glass to finished ecosystem, aquascaping tutorials with hardscape placement principles and plant-selection guides, water-parameter tracking overlays showing the cycle progression, species-profile segments with compatibility charts, maintenance routine walkthroughs with tool demonstrations, and the satisfying time-lapse that compresses three months of plant growth into thirty seconds of visible underwater forest emergence. Built for aquascaping YouTubers building tutorial libraries, fish store owners creating care-guide content, aquarium hobbyists documenting their tank journals, planted-tank competitors showcasing contest entries, marine reef keepers sharing coral propagation progress, and anyone whose relationship with a glass box of water has become unexpectedly meaningful.

## Example Prompts

### 1. Tank Build — Planted Aquarium from Scratch
"Create a 7-minute planted aquarium build video from empty tank to finished scape. Opening (0-15 sec): the empty tank on the stand — clean glass, nothing inside. 'This is 40 gallons of potential and approximately $600 of equipment that hasn't been assembled yet. In three hours, this will be an underwater forest. Here's how.' Equipment overview (15-50 sec): laid out on the table — filter (canister), heater, CO2 system, lighting, substrate, hardscape materials, plants. Each item labeled with name and purpose. 'The equipment list for a planted tank is longer than the equipment list for most hobbies that involve actual danger. This hobby involves water chemistry and still manages to be expensive.' Substrate layer (50-100 sec): the foundation — aquasoil in the tank. 'Aquasoil is the planted tank's secret weapon. It buffers pH, provides nutrients to plant roots, and costs more per pound than premium coffee. The plants don't care about the price. They care about the ammonia it leaches for the first two weeks, which is why we cycle before adding fish.' Show the layering: nutrient base layer, then aquasoil on top. The slope: higher in the back, lower in the front. 'The slope creates depth perception. A flat substrate makes a 40-gallon tank look like a 20-gallon tank. The illusion of depth is the aquascaper's primary tool.' Hardscape placement (100-180 sec): rocks and driftwood — the bones of the scape. 'The rule of thirds applies underwater just as it does in photography. The focal point goes off-center.' Show the main stone placement — try position 1, step back, evaluate. Move to position 2. Step back. 'Aquascaping is 40% placing things and 60% staring at what you placed and moving it two inches to the left.' The driftwood: positioned to create flow and vertical interest. 'The wood should look like it landed here naturally, which requires the most unnatural amount of deliberate placement.' Show the golden ratio overlay on the scape — the focal stone at the intersection point. Planting (180-280 sec): the plants arrive — tissue culture cups and potted specimens. Show the prep: removing rock wool from roots, separating tissue culture into portions. 'Tissue culture plants are pest-free and come in portions that seem small until you realize each cup contains 20-30 individual plants.' The planting process with tweezers: carpet plant (Monte Carlo) inserted in a grid pattern across the foreground — 'One centimeter apart. Yes, it takes an hour. Yes, it's worth it. In 8 weeks this grid becomes a solid green carpet.' Mid-ground: stem plants (Rotala, Ludwigia) planted in groups. 'Plant in groups of the same species. Mixed plantings look messy. Grouping creates impact.' Background: tall stems (Vallisneria, Rotala rotundifolia) creating the back wall. Epiphytes tied to hardscape: Anubias and Bucephalandra attached to rocks and wood with thread. 'These don't go in the substrate. They attach to hardscape and their roots grip the surface over time.' Fill and equipment (280-340 sec): filling with water — slowly, using a plate to disperse the flow. 'Pour onto a plate or the substrate becomes the surface and you start over.' The water cloudy initially. Equipment installation: filter intake and outlet positioned, heater set to 76°F, CO2 diffuser placed, light hung. Turn everything on — the filter starts, bubbles clear, the first CO2 bubbles appear in the diffuser. 'The tank is running. Now we wait.' The cycle (340-380 sec): time-lapse — Day 1 to Week 6. Show the ammonia spike, the nitrite spike, the nitrate rise. Water parameter overlays: 'Week 1: ammonia 4.0 ppm. The bacteria haven't colonized yet.' 'Week 3: ammonia dropping, nitrite spiking. The first bacteria are working, producing nitrite for the second bacteria to convert.' 'Week 6: ammonia 0, nitrite 0, nitrate 20 ppm. The cycle is complete.' Plant growth visible in the time-lapse — the carpet filling in, stems growing toward the light. Fish introduction (380-400 sec): the first fish — acclimated, released. 'The tank has been running for six weeks waiting for this moment. The fish have been at the store waiting considerably less patiently.' Show the fish exploring — the first swim through the hardscape, the first investigation of the plants. 'The fish don't know about the aquasoil, the CO2 system, or the golden ratio. They know there are places to hide and the water feels right. That's enough.' Final reveal (400-420 sec): the finished tank — lights on the optimal spectrum, plants pearling (oxygen bubbles on leaves), fish schooling, the full ecosystem operating. Slow pan across the scape. 'Three hours of building. Six weeks of patience. One ecosystem that will grow, change, and surprise you every day you look at it.'"

### 2. Aquascaping Tutorial — Iwagumi Rock Layout
"Build a 5-minute aquascaping tutorial focused on the Iwagumi rock layout style. Opening: a finished Iwagumi tank — minimalist, powerful, the rocks commanding the space. 'Iwagumi is the hardest aquascaping style because there's nowhere to hide mistakes. No wood to distract. No busy planting to cover flaws. Just rocks, carpet plants, and the courage to leave empty space.' The philosophy (0-30 sec): 'Iwagumi comes from Japanese rock garden tradition. Each stone has a role and a name. The layout follows strict compositional rules that create visual tension and balance.' Diagram overlay: the stone roles — Oyaishi (main stone), Fukuishi (secondary), Soeishi (accent), Suteishi (sacrificial stones that support the composition). Stone selection (30-80 sec): show the stone collection — same type (Seiryu stone), varying sizes. 'All stones must be the same type. Mixing stone types in Iwagumi is like mixing fonts in a résumé — technically possible, aesthetically criminal.' Select the Oyaishi: the largest, most dramatic stone. 'The main stone should be approximately 2/3 the height of the tank. It defines everything.' Show the grain direction — 'All stones must share the same grain direction. They should look like fragments of the same mountain.' Placement (80-200 sec): the Oyaishi goes first — off-center, tilted slightly. 'Never place the main stone straight up. A slight lean creates dynamism. The direction of the lean guides the viewer's eye.' Show the placement on the rule-of-thirds intersection. The Fukuishi: the second stone, placed to complement the main stone. 'The secondary stone echoes the main stone at a different height. It shouldn't compete — it should support.' Show the relationship: similar angle, smaller scale, positioned to create a visual line. The Soeishi: accent stones — smaller, placed to create rhythm. 'The accent stones fill the composition without crowding it. They should feel inevitable, not added.' The Suteishi: the smallest stones, partially buried. 'Sacrificial stones are mostly hidden by the substrate and the carpet plant. They create the foothills of the mountain the main stone represents.' Step back and evaluate: show the layout from eye level — 'This is the only angle that matters. Top-down is for maintenance. Eye level is where the aquascape lives.' Planting (200-260 sec): carpet plant only — Hemianthus callitrichoides (HC Cuba) or Monte Carlo. 'Iwagumi uses one, maximum two plant species. The carpet IS the scape. Everything else is rock.' Show the planting grid around the stones — close to the base, filling every gap. 'Plant tight against the stone bases. The carpet growing up to meet the rock creates the natural transition between earth and mountain.' Final details and water (260-300 sec): fill carefully, equipment on. The finished Iwagumi — clean, minimal, powerful. 'The empty space in an Iwagumi is as important as the filled space. Resist the urge to add. The simplicity is the point.'"

### 3. Maintenance Routine — Weekly Tank Care
"Produce a 4-minute aquarium maintenance routine video. Opening: the tank looking great — 'This tank looks this way because of 30 minutes every Sunday. Here's the routine that keeps an aquarium healthy, the plants growing, and the fish alive.' Water test (0-30 sec): the test kit — pH, ammonia, nitrite, nitrate. Show each test with the color comparison. 'Testing first tells you what the water needs. Changing water without testing is like taking medicine without a diagnosis.' Parameter overlay: the acceptable ranges. 'Ammonia: 0. Nitrite: 0. Nitrate: under 40. pH: whatever your fish prefer, kept stable. Stability matters more than hitting a specific number.' Water change (30-100 sec): the siphon — gravel vacuum technique. 'The siphon does two jobs: removes old water and removes debris from the substrate.' Show the technique: hover above the substrate for planted tanks (don't dig into aquasoil), push into gravel for non-planted tanks. The waste visible in the tube — 'This is fish waste, decaying plant matter, and uneaten food. Removing it prevents ammonia spikes.' Drain 25-30% of the water. Refill: temperature-matched, dechlorinated. 'Match the temperature within 2°F. Cold water into a warm tank stresses fish. Dechlorinator treats the tap water — chlorine and chloramine kill beneficial bacteria.' Show the slow refill — pouring along the glass to avoid disturbing the scape. Plant maintenance (100-160 sec): trimming — stem plants that have reached the surface. 'Cut at a 45-degree angle, 2 inches above the substrate. The remaining stem will branch at the cut point, creating bushier growth.' Show the trim, the cut stems removed. Replanting the tops: 'The cut tops can be replanted — remove the bottom leaves and push the stem into the substrate. Free plants.' Remove dead leaves from Anubias — 'Yellow leaves are normal shedding. Remove them before they decay and spike ammonia.' Carpet maintenance: trim the top layer when it grows too thick. 'A carpet that's too thick lifts from the substrate. Trim the top to keep it compact.' Filter maintenance (160-210 sec): monthly, not weekly — but show it. Open the canister filter. 'Rinse the biological media in tank water, NEVER tap water. Chlorine in tap water kills the bacteria that keep your fish alive.' Show the media rinse — the brown water coming off. 'That brown color is beneficial bacteria colonies. Gross-looking but essential.' Replace the mechanical filter pad — the sponge or floss that catches physical debris. Reassemble, prime, restart. Glass cleaning (210-240 sec): algae scraper on the inside glass. 'Algae on the glass means the tank is healthy — there are nutrients available. It just doesn't look good.' Show the satisfying scrape — the clean line appearing on the glass. Closing: the tank after maintenance — crystal-clear water, trimmed plants, clean glass. 'Thirty minutes. Once a week. That's the cost of a beautiful aquarium. The fish don't know it's maintenance day. They just know the water feels fresh.'"

## Tips for Best Results

1. **Use time-lapses for plant growth and cycling** — Six weeks compressed to 30 seconds shows visible transformation. The AI applies time-lapse to growth and cycle sequences.
2. **Show water parameter overlays during cycling** — Ammonia, nitrite, and nitrate curves tell the cycle story visually. The AI renders parameter graphs when water_params is enabled.
3. **Label every plant species at planting time** — "Monte Carlo" means nothing to beginners without a visual reference. The AI overlays species identification when plant_labels is enabled.
4. **Film at eye level for the final reveal** — Top-down shows layout; eye level shows the scape as a viewer will experience it. The AI positions final shots at tank eye level.
5. **Include the hardscape iteration** — Showing placement, evaluation, and repositioning proves that good aquascapes are designed, not placed once. The AI includes the design-process footage.
