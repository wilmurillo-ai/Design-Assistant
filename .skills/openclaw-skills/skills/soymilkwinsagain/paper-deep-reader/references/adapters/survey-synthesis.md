# Adapter: Survey / Synthesis

Use this adapter when the paper's main contribution is a taxonomy, conceptual synthesis, research map, literature organization, unifying perspective, or broad comparative overview rather than a single new theorem, dataset, system, or algorithm.

## What to prioritize

1. The scope of the survey or synthesis.
2. The organizing axis: taxonomy, historical thread, methodological decomposition, or conceptual unification.
3. What confusion, fragmentation, or duplication in the literature the paper is trying to resolve.
4. Coverage, inclusion criteria, and likely blind spots.
5. Which distinctions in the synthesis are genuinely illuminating rather than merely descriptive.
6. Whether the paper helps the reader reason better about the field, not just remember more papers.

## Questions to answer in the note

- What body of literature, problem family, or research program is being synthesized?
- What organizing principle does the paper use, and why is it better than a flat list of prior work?
- What distinctions, categories, or axes are load-bearing?
- Which parts of the taxonomy seem sharp and useful, and which seem blurry or arbitrary?
- How does the paper choose what to include or exclude?
- What are the major omissions, selection effects, or perspective biases?
- What does the synthesis clarify that would otherwise remain conceptually muddled?

## Minimum insertions into the note

Add or expand these sections:

### Problem setup

- scope of literature or research area covered
- why a synthesis is needed now
- central confusion, fragmentation, or research bottleneck the paper addresses

### Main idea and contribution

- the organizing axis or taxonomy
- the paper's unifying perspective or conceptual decomposition
- what the synthesis changes about how the field should be read

### Technical core

- taxonomy structure, dimensions, or comparison framework
- definitions or conceptual distinctions introduced by the authors
- relationships among major subfamilies of work
- any formal criteria, tables, or decision rules used to separate categories

### Evidence

- coverage of representative papers or lines of work
- whether the taxonomy successfully explains known differences among methods or findings
- examples where the synthesis generates clearer interpretation than prior surveys or ad hoc groupings
- places where the survey's framing seems incomplete, strained, or unstable

### Limitations and failure modes

Include discussion of:

- coverage bias or exclusion bias
- categories that collapse distinct ideas or split essentially similar ones
- perspective dependence of the taxonomy
- how quickly the synthesis may age in a fast-moving literature

## Special reading rules

- Do not mistake breadth for explanatory power.
- Ask whether the taxonomy compresses the field in a way that helps future reasoning.
- Distinguish a useful synthesis from a literature catalog.
- Check whether the organizing axes are mutually coherent, or whether they mix mechanism, application, and evaluation in unstable ways.
- Be alert to selective coverage: what looks like a field-wide map may actually privilege one tradition, benchmark culture, or methodological lens.

## Typical failure patterns to watch for

- taxonomy that is memorable but not analytically useful
- categories that are vague, overlapping, or inconsistently applied
- major omissions that change the apparent structure of the field
- narrative that overstates consensus or progress
- selection of examples that flatters the proposed framing
- synthesis that confuses historical order with conceptual dependency
- recommendations that exceed what the surveyed evidence supports

## Reusable note prompts

- “The synthesis organizes the literature primarily by … rather than …”
- “The most useful distinction introduced here is … because …”
- “This taxonomy clarifies confusion between … and …”
- “A likely coverage or framing bias is …”
- “The survey is most valuable as a map for … but weaker as a guide to …”
