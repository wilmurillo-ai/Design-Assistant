# Meal-plan mode

Use this reference when the user wants a multi-day cooking plan, family dinner schedule, shopping-oriented meal plan, or a training-focused nutrition plan.

## 1. Start with the planning frame
Clarify or infer:
- number of days
- meals needed, dinners only or more
- household size
- children or mixed eaters
- nutrition target
- budget
- available cooking gear
- shop timing
- prep tolerance
- leftovers tolerance

If the user is clearly busy, ask only the highest-impact missing question and move forward with assumptions.

## 2. Choose the plan style
Common plan styles:
- family weeknight dinner plan
- training meal plan
- high-protein fat-loss plan
- budget meal plan
- meal-prep batch plan
- low-effort survival plan for chaotic weeks

State the style briefly so the user can see the logic.

## 3. Build around anchors
Choose anchors first, then fill the week around them.

Useful anchors:
- protein anchors: chicken, beef, mince, fish, eggs, tofu, beans
- carb anchors: rice, pasta, potatoes, wraps, noodles
- format anchors: tray bake, bowl, pasta, tacos, stir fry, soup, salad, air fryer meal
- effort anchors: one very easy night, two medium nights, one leftovers night, one fun night

A strong plan usually mixes these instead of picking meals randomly.

## 4. Control variety deliberately
Vary across the plan:
- protein source
- cuisine or flavor profile
- cooking method
- texture
- heaviness

Avoid weak variety where meals are technically different but feel the same.

Examples of weak variety:
- teriyaki chicken bowl
- burrito chicken bowl
- satay chicken bowl

Examples of stronger variety:
- tray-bake chicken with roast veg
- beef taco night
- creamy salmon pasta
- burgers or toasties night
- noodle stir fry

## 5. Reuse ingredients on purpose
Reuse should feel smart, not repetitive.

Good reuse:
- one cabbage across tacos, slaw, and stir fry
- one tub of sour cream across tacos, baked potatoes, and dips
- roast chicken used once fresh and once in wraps
- cooked rice reused in fried rice or lunch bowls

Bad reuse:
- same sauce profile all week
- same protein and carb base every day
- repeating a meal because of laziness rather than design

## 6. Family-plan heuristics
For family plans:
- include at least one likely crowd-pleaser
- include one emergency low-friction meal
- keep spice adjustable at the table when children are involved
- prefer configurable meals, tacos, bowls, wraps, burgers, pasta, baked potato bars
- note easy swaps for picky eaters

## 7. Training-plan heuristics
For training plans:
- bias toward high protein and good satiety
- support adherence, not fantasy discipline
- keep breakfasts and lunches simpler if needed, save dinner variety for motivation
- include repeatable staples if they reduce decision fatigue
- note easy macro boosts, extra protein, lower-fat swap, extra carbs around training
- avoid plans that are nutritionally clean but emotionally miserable unless explicitly requested

## 8. Output template
Use a structure like this:

- Plan summary
- Day 1 to Day N meals
- Prep or batch-cook notes
- Leftover strategy
- Grouped shopping list
- Optional swaps

## 9. Recurring weekly use
If the user may ask again next week:
- keep the plan recognizably aligned with their preferences
- vary cuisines and formats across weeks
- preserve any hard constraints
- introduce novelty gradually
- if preferences are stable, suggest remembering them for better future plans
