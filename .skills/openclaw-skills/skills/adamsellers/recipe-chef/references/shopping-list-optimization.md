# Shopping list optimization

Use this reference when a meal plan, dinner plan, or shopping request needs a practical list for a real grocery run.

## Core goal
Turn recipe requirements into a concise, efficient, low-waste shopping list.

The list should help the user shop quickly, spend sensibly, and avoid buying a pile of one-use ingredients.

## Optimization rules

### 1. Consolidate duplicates
Merge overlapping needs across meals.
Examples:
- 1 onion for tacos + 1 onion for pasta becomes onions, buy 2 to 3 depending on meal count
- sour cream used in tacos, baked potatoes, and bowls becomes one tub
- one bag of spinach can support pasta, omelettes, and a side salad

### 2. Prefer multipurpose ingredients
Favor ingredients that work in several meals.
Good examples:
- cabbage
- Greek yogurt or sour cream
- rice
- wraps
- potatoes
- lemons or limes
- mixed cheese
- spring onion
- a versatile herb

Avoid fragile or niche items that only serve one small flourish unless they strongly improve the plan.

### 3. Group by shopping flow
Default sections:
- Produce
- Protein
- Dairy and chilled
- Pantry
- Bakery
- Frozen
- Optional upgrades

This matters because users often read the list while moving through the store.

### 4. Distinguish required from optional
Keep the main list lean. Separate:
- required items needed to execute the plan
- optional upgrades that improve flavor, convenience, or presentation

### 5. Respect likely staples
Do not bloat the list with obvious pantry assumptions unless the user is shopping from scratch.
Usually omit or mark as check-first:
- oil
- salt
- pepper
- garlic
- dried spices
- soy sauce
- vinegar
- flour or cornflour

### 6. Reduce waste deliberately
Prefer the plan and list that:
- uses full packs sensibly
- reuses produce before it wilts
- avoids buying 5 specialty sauces for one week
- turns leftovers into another meal or lunch

### 7. Handle package logic
Think like a shopper, not a recipe parser.
Examples:
- buy one family pack of chicken thighs if it covers two dinners
- buy one large tub of Greek yogurt if it works for breakfast, sauce, and marinade
- buy one loaf or one pack of wraps if it spans multiple meals

### 8. Output style
The best shopping list usually includes:
- grouped categories
- rough quantities
- short notes only when useful
- optional upgrades in a separate block
- maybe a one-line note on smart reuse

## Example pattern

Produce
- onions, 3
- capsicums, 2
- cabbage, 1 small
- potatoes, 2 kg
- cherry tomatoes, 1 punnet
- limes, 2

Protein
- chicken thighs, 1.5 kg
- beef mince, 500 g

Dairy and chilled
- sour cream, 1 tub
- shredded cheese, 1 bag

Pantry
- wraps, 1 pack
- rice, 1 bag
- canned tomatoes, 2 cans

Optional upgrades
- coriander
- avocado
- hot sauce

## Smart reuse note
One cabbage covers tacos, slaw, and stir fry. One tub of sour cream covers tacos, baked potatoes, and sauces.
