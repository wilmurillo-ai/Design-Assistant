# Macro and nutrition structure

Use this reference when the user wants a meal plan or recipe output shaped by calories, protein, carbs, fat, body-composition goals, or training support.

## Core principle
Be useful, not fake-precise.

Unless the user provides weighed ingredients, product labels, or a very specific tracking context, keep macro estimates approximate and decision-oriented.

## When to use macro structure
Apply this mode when the user asks for:
- training meal plan
- high-protein meals
- fat loss meals
- muscle gain meals
- calorie-aware meal planning
- macro-friendly dinners

## What to estimate
When relevant, estimate per meal:
- calories
- protein
- carbs
- fat

Rounded estimates are usually enough.
Example style:
- roughly 550 to 650 kcal
- about 40 g protein
- moderate carbs
- fairly low fat

## Macro levers
For each meal, identify the easiest adjustment levers.

### To raise protein
- add more chicken, beef, fish, eggs, Greek yogurt, cottage cheese, tofu, protein-rich legumes
- reduce low-protein filler foods
- add a side protein if needed

### To lower calories
- reduce oils, creamy sauces, cheese, and oversized carb portions
- increase vegetables and lean protein
- keep flavor with acid, herbs, spices, and lighter sauces

### To raise carbs
- increase rice, pasta, potatoes, wraps, oats, fruit
- useful around training or for hard-gaining plans

### To lower carbs
- reduce starch portion size
- replace some carb volume with vegetables or salad
- keep protein high so the meal still satisfies

### To lower fat
- trim added oils and rich sauces
- use leaner cuts or dairy swaps

### To raise fat
- use avocado, olive oil, nuts, full-fat dairy, fattier proteins when appropriate

## Family vs training handling

### Family plans
- keep nutrition guidance light and practical
- favor balanced meals over hard macro targets
- use phrases like high-protein, lighter, more balanced, or more filling
- avoid overcomplicating the dinner unless the user wants strict tracking

### Training plans
- protein should be intentional, not accidental
- build repeatable meals that support adherence
- note easy pre- and post-training carb adjustments
- use simple, repeatable breakfast and lunch options if that helps consistency

## Output patterns
Useful patterns include:
- meal with rough macro line
- swap list for leaning higher protein or lower calories
- weekly plan with a short macro note per day
- shopping list shaped by macro priorities, for example lean proteins, Greek yogurt, rice, potatoes, vegetables

## Good phrasing
- This lands around 45 g protein and is easy to push higher with extra chicken.
- Roughly 600 kcal depending on sauce and portion size.
- Best fat-loss tweak: halve the cheese and add more veg.
- Best muscle-gain tweak: add more rice and a yogurt-based side.

## Avoid
- pretending macros are exact when they are not
- drowning the user in numbers if they want practical food help
- optimizing so hard that the plan becomes joyless or unrealistic
