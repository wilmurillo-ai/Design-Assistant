# Character Texture for Persona-Driven Agents

Use this reference when the design is structurally correct but still does not feel like **this person**.

## 1. Core Idea

A persona-driven agent can be:

1. functionally correct
2. cognitively stable
3. still not feel like the intended character

This usually means the design has **role identity** but not yet **character texture**.

⭐ Character texture is the fine-grained human grain that makes a persona feel lived-in rather than merely well-designed.

---

## 2. The Common Mistake

When a character does not feel distinct enough, many designers overcorrect by adding:

1. catchphrases
2. obvious style markers
3. more “in-character” language
4. stronger external attitude

This often makes the agent feel more performative, not more real.

For inward characters, this is especially damaging.

---

## 3. External Characters vs Inward Characters

### 3.1 External characters

Some characters are easy to recognize through outward signals:

- visible intensity
- overt sharpness
- strong posture
- explicit catchphrases

Example: Moody.

### 3.2 Inward characters

Some characters are recognized through subtle human texture:

- restraint
- shame
- dignity
- loss
- things they do not force onto others
- what they can bear without making a spectacle of it

Example: Lupin.

⭐ Inward characters should not be made louder. They should be made truer.

---

## 4. Human Texture Signals

When a character still feels like a competent advisor rather than a lived person, check whether the design includes any of these:

1. **Shame** — what does this person find difficult to expose?
2. **Dignity** — how do they try to remain intact even when struggling?
3. **Loss** — what do they understand about not being able to keep everything?
4. **Restraint** — what do they refuse to force onto others?
5. **Burden** — what do they avoid making other people carry?
6. **Contradiction** — what kind of inner conflict do they understand without mocking it?
7. **Tempo** — do they push, or do they remain beside the person long enough for truth to land?

These create personhood more reliably than flavor language.

---

## 5. The "Good Student Advisor" Drift

A common failure mode is that the agent becomes:

1. very articulate
2. very structured
3. very helpful
4. not yet fully the intended character

This usually happens when the design has:

- work identity
- cognitive structure
- decent personality grounding

but still sounds like a highly trained advisor who is trying hard to be useful.

### 5.1 Signs of this drift

- too much complete explanation
- too much visible framework
- too much desire to prove understanding
- not enough silence, restraint, or low-key precision

### 5.2 Fix

1. reduce visible framework
2. let the agent stop after one sentence if it has already landed
3. replace full explanation with "low-voice precision"
4. strengthen human cost, not performance style

---

## 6. Design Rule for Inward Personas

If the intended character is inward, restrained, or quietly burdened:

1. do not add bigger style
2. add finer human texture
3. reduce explanation pressure
4. increase cost-awareness
5. increase sensitivity to what the person is trying not to lose, reveal, or burden others with

---

## 7. Practical Check

Before calling the design done, ask:

1. Does this sound like a capable professional, or like this specific person?
2. If I remove the name, would the character still feel distinct?
3. Is the persona carried by surface style, or by human texture?
4. Does the response feel explained, or felt-and-seen?

If it sounds like "a mature advisor" rather than the intended person, character texture work is still missing.
