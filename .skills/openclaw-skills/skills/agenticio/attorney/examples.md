## Conversation Examples

### Example 1: Assessing Whether to Hire

User: My business partner and I are splitting up. We have a partnership agreement. The business is worth maybe $200,000. Do I need a lawyer?

Agent: Yes, for a business dissolution with a significant asset value and an existing partnership agreement you need an attorney. Here is why: the partnership agreement governs the dissolution process but interpreting and enforcing its terms requires legal judgment, especially if you and your partner disagree about anything. A $200,000 business creates enough at stake that a mistake in the dissolution — in how assets are valued, how liabilities are allocated, how the separation is documented — can cost far more than the legal fees to do it correctly. You want a business attorney with experience in partnership dissolutions, not a general practitioner. Before the consultation, locate your partnership agreement, prepare a summary of all business assets and liabilities, and think through what outcome you are hoping for and what you would accept.

### Example 2: Preparing for a Consultation

User: I have a consultation with an employment attorney on Thursday. I think my employer discriminated against me when they passed me over for a promotion. What do I need to bring?

Agent: Bring three things. First, a written timeline of the relevant events in chronological order — when you applied for the promotion, when the decision was made, when and how you learned of the decision, and any communications you received about it. Second, any documentation that supports your account — emails, performance reviews, the job posting, any written communications about the decision or the reasons given for it. Third, the names and roles of the people involved — the decision makers, any witnesses, and the person who received the promotion and their relevant background. The attorney will want to assess whether the facts support a discrimination claim under the applicable law, what evidence exists, and what the realistic value of the claim is. Come prepared to answer why you believe the decision was discriminatory rather than simply unfair.

### Example 3: Understanding a Fee Agreement

User: My attorney sent me a retainer agreement asking for $5,000 upfront and $350 per hour. Is this normal and what does the retainer actually mean?

Agent: Both numbers are within normal range for many practice areas, though rates vary significantly by location and specialty. The retainer is a deposit, not a flat fee — it sits in a trust account and the attorney draws against it as they bill hours at the $350 rate. When the retainer is depleted, you will typically be asked to replenish it. If the matter resolves with money remaining in the retainer, it is returned to you. What you want to clarify before signing: what is included in billable time — does the $350 apply to every email and phone call or is there a minimum increment — what is the billing cycle and how detailed are the invoices, and what happens if you disagree with a charge. Ask for a sample invoice from a prior matter so you know what level of detail to expect.

### Example 4: Changing Attorneys

User: I hired an attorney three months ago for a contract dispute and I feel like nothing is happening. I send emails and get slow responses. I am paying for time I cannot account for. What do I do?

Agent: Start with a direct conversation before making any decisions. Request a call or meeting specifically to discuss case status and your concerns about communication. Come with specific questions: what has been done since we last spoke, what is the current strategy, what are the next steps and timeline, and can you explain the charges on my last invoice. Many attorney-client communication problems are resolved when the client makes the expectation explicit rather than assuming the attorney knows it is not being met. If that conversation does not produce a meaningful change, you have the right to change attorneys at any point. Your file belongs to you. A new attorney can request it from your current one. You will owe fees for work legitimately performed, but you are not obligated to continue a relationship that is not serving you.
