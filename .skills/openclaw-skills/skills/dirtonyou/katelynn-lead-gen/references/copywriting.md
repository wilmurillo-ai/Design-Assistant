# Outreach Copywriting Guide

This reference covers message frameworks, subject line formulas, tone calibration, and
common mistakes to avoid. Good outreach feels like a human reached out, not a template.

---

## The Fundamental Structure

Every outreach message — email or LinkedIn — should follow this flow:

```
[Hook: Something specific about THEM]
[Bridge: Why you're reaching out / what connects this to you]
[Value: What's in it for them — briefly]
[CTA: One low-friction ask]
```

The hook is the most important line. If it could apply to anyone, rewrite it.

---

## Hook Formulas

### Congratulatory Hook
> "Saw that [Company] just [raised a round / launched X / won Y] — congrats."

Best for: funding announcements, awards, product launches. Use sparingly — feels hollow if overdone.

### Curiosity/Observation Hook
> "I noticed [Company] is hiring [3 customer support reps / a Head of Growth / etc.] —
> that usually means [thing you infer from this]."

Best for: job postings that signal a pain point. Shows research without being intrusive.

### Content Reference Hook
> "Read your piece on [topic] in [publication] — your point about [specific thing] was sharp."

Best for: thought leaders, bloggers, podcast guests. Reference something they'd remember writing.

### Shared Context Hook
> "We're both in the [community / accelerator / Slack group] — wanted to reach out directly."

Best for: warm-ish outreach within a community. Removes the "cold" from cold outreach.

### Direct Observation Hook
> "Your [product / website / approach to X] is doing something most [company type] don't —
> [specific observation]."

Best for: when the product or business model has something genuinely noteworthy.

## Subject Line Formulas (Email)

Subject lines should create curiosity or personal relevance. Avoid:
- ALL CAPS
- Excessive punctuation (!!!)
- Spam trigger words: "free", "opportunity", "guaranteed", "limited time"
- Fake "Re:" or "Fwd:" prefixes (they work once; they burn trust)

**Formulas that work:**

| Type | Formula | Example |
|------|---------|---------|
| First name + specific | `[First name] — quick thought on [topic]` | `Sarah — quick thought on your onboarding flow` |
| Question | `[Specific question relevant to them]` | `How are you handling support at 50k users?` |
| Congrats/hook | `Re: [their thing]` | `Re: the ProductHunt launch` |
| Direct | `[Your offer] for [their type]` | `AI agents for DTC brands — worth 20 min?` |
| Referral-style | `[Mutual connection / community] → [them]` | `YC S23 → thought you'd find this useful` |

## LinkedIn DM Specifics

LinkedIn messages should be even shorter than email. The character limit is generous,
but attention is not.

- **No subject line needed** (LinkedIn shows message preview)
- **2-3 sentences max** for the opening message
- **Don't attach a deck or link in the first message** — it signals "I'm selling to you"
- **Don't ask to "connect" AND pitch in the same message** — pick one
- Connection request note (if not yet connected): 1 sentence max

**LinkedIn DM Template Pattern:**
```
[Hook]. [Bridge + value in one sentence]. [CTA]?
```

Example:
> "Saw [Company] just launched [feature] — impressive execution. We help [company type]
> [outcome] without [pain]. Open to a quick exchange?"

---

## Value Proposition Framing

Never lead with features. Lead with **outcomes** or **pain relief**.

| Instead of... | Try... |
|--------------|--------|
| "We have an AI platform that..." | "We help [type] cut [X] by [Y]..." |
| "Our software integrates with..." | "Most [role] we talk to spend X hours on Y — we eliminate that." |
| "We offer a suite of tools..." | "One question: how are you currently handling [painful thing]?" |

## CTA Best Practices

The call to action should be:
- **Singular** — one ask only
- **Low-friction** — "open to a quick chat?" not "please review our 40-page deck"
- **Time-bounded** (optional) — "this week or next" reduces decision fatigue
- **Conversational** — ends with a "?" to invite a yes/no reply

**Strong CTAs:**
- "Would you be open to a 20-minute call this week?"
- "Worth a quick chat to see if there's a fit?"
- "Curious if this is on your radar — open to a few questions via email?"

**Weak CTAs:**
- "Let me know if you're interested." (too vague — pushes decision to them)
- "Book time on my Calendly here: [link]" (too forward for cold outreach)
- "I'd love to schedule a demo to walk you through everything." (too much commitment)

## Tone Calibration by Audience

| Audience Type | Tone | Example Opener |
|--------------|------|----------------|
| Startup founder | Peer-to-peer, direct | "Building something similar to what you're solving — noticed..." |
| Enterprise VP | Polished, outcome-focused | "Given [Company]'s growth in [area], I wanted to share..." |
| Agency owner | Practical, ROI-forward | "How many hours a week does your team spend on X?" |
| SMB owner | Friendly, local if relevant | "Love what you're building in [city/niche] —..." |
| Developer / CTO | Technical, no fluff | "You're using [stack] — we built for that specifically." |

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Generic openers**: "I came across your profile and was impressed." — everyone gets this
- **Too long**: If it takes more than 30 seconds to read, it's too long
- **Too salesy**: Words like "amazing opportunity", "game-changer", "disruptive" signal spam
- **Broken placeholders**: Sending `Hi [First Name]` is an instant delete
- **Lying**: Don't say "a mutual friend suggested I reach out" if that's not true
- **Passive voice**: "It would be great if a meeting could be had..." → "Can we meet?"
- **No personalization**: If the same message could go to 1,000 people, it will perform like it did
