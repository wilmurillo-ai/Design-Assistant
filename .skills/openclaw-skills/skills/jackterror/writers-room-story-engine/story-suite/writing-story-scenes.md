# Writing Story Scenes

Use this skill to turn beats into strong scenes.

This skill assumes some story foundation already exists:
- premise
- protagonist
- major beats
- world pressure

Its job is to create scenes that:
- reveal character through action and choice
- create or intensify conflict
- change the situation
- move the story forward
- expose the world organically

## Core scene principles

- Every scene should turn.
- A scene exists because someone wants something and cannot get it easily.
- Conflict may be external, internal, relational, social, moral, or environmental.
- A scene should reveal character through behavior under pressure.
- Scenes should not explain what action can show.
- If nothing changes, the scene is weak.

## Workflow

Copy this checklist if useful:

```text
Scene Writing Progress:
- [ ] Step 1: Identify the beat function
- [ ] Step 2: Define scene objective and obstacle
- [ ] Step 3: Define pressure and subtext
- [ ] Step 4: Write the turn
- [ ] Step 5: Ensure the ending creates therefore or but
```

## Step 1: Identify the beat function

Before writing, define:
- what story beat this scene serves
- why this scene must exist
- what changes by the end

Possible scene functions:
- desire pursuit
- revelation
- confrontation
- alliance shift
- betrayal
- escape
- setback
- temptation
- decision
- sacrifice
- world exposure through pressure

If the scene does not serve a meaningful function, cut or merge it.

## Step 2: Define scene objective and obstacle

For the primary character in the scene, define:
- what they want right now
- why they want it
- what blocks them
- what they are willing to risk
- what they cannot say openly

If “what do they want?” is vague, the scene is not ready.

## Step 3: Define pressure and subtext

Ask:
- what power imbalance exists?
- what fear shapes the scene?
- what lie or need is being pressured?
- what should remain unspoken?
- how does the world complicate the moment?
- what hidden agenda does each side have?

Subtext matters when:
- characters cannot say what they mean
- status, taboo, fear, or desire distort dialogue
- action and implication carry more weight than explanation

## Step 4: Write the turn

A good scene turns when:
- the power shifts
- new information changes the meaning
- the objective fails
- the objective succeeds at a cost
- an ally becomes a threat
- a threat becomes useful
- the protagonist chooses differently than before
- pressure escalates

The ending of the scene should create the next scene through:
- therefore
- but

Never let a scene merely stop.

## Step 5: Ensure story movement

After drafting, check:
- what changed?
- what did we learn?
- what was risked?
- what pressure increased?
- what part of the world was revealed through conflict?
- what next scene now becomes necessary?

## Scene rules

Each scene should do at least one:
- reveal character through choice
- create conflict
- force a decision
- deliver consequential information
- change relationships
- escalate stakes
- reframe the story

## Scene construction guide

### Objective
What does the main character want in this scene?

### Obstacle
Why can’t they get it easily?

### Pressure
What makes this difficult now?
- time
- status
- danger
- taboo
- fear
- deception
- temptation
- divided loyalties
- public exposure

### Subtext
What cannot be said openly?

### Turn
What changes by the end?

Examples:
- success at a cost
- failure with consequence
- revelation that reframes the scene
- power shift
- alliance shift
- emotional break
- new danger
- irreversible decision

## Dialogue rules

- avoid saying exactly what both characters already know
- let motives collide
- let opinion show
- compress exposition into conflict
- use silence, evasion, threat, ritual, humor, or status shifts when helpful
- dialogue should sound like people pursuing goals, not explaining the outline

## Scene diagnostics

### If a scene feels dead
Check for:
- no objective
- weak obstacle
- no pressure
- no subtext
- no change
- no turn

### If a scene feels overexplanatory
Check for:
- dialogue explaining known facts
- lore replacing conflict
- characters talking without wanting anything
- no hidden agenda

### If a scene feels decorative
Check for:
- atmosphere without story movement
- conversation without pressure
- world detail without consequence
- emotion without decision

## Default output format

### Scene purpose
- beat served
- why the scene exists
- what changes

### Scene setup
- location
- characters present
- scene objective
- obstacle
- pressure
- subtext

### Scene draft
- full scene prose or screenplay-style scene as requested

### Scene turn
- what changed
- what next scene now follows
