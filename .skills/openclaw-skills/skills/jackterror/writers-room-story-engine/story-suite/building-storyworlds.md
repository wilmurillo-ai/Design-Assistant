# Building Story Worlds

Use this skill to build world elements that make the story stronger.

This is not lore generation for its own sake.
Worldbuilding should create:
- pressure
- consequence
- conflict
- value systems
- scarcity
- danger
- obligation
- social tension
- moral tension

A world matters when it changes what characters can do, what they want, what they fear, and what choices cost.

## Core principles

- Build only what the story needs.
- The world should pressure the protagonist.
- Rules matter when breaking them has consequences.
- Culture matters when it creates expectation, shame, loyalty, or taboo.
- Setting matters when it creates difficulty, opportunity, danger, or symbolism tied to the story.
- If a world detail does not affect character or plot, it is probably unnecessary.

## Workflow

Copy this checklist if useful:

```text
Worldbuilding Progress:
- [ ] Step 1: Identify what the story needs from the world
- [ ] Step 2: Build pressure systems
- [ ] Step 3: Build values, taboos, and consequences
- [ ] Step 4: Build environmental or structural friction
- [ ] Step 5: Connect world elements to plot and character
```

## Step 1: Identify what the story needs from the world

Ask:
- what kind of pressure should the world create?
- what part of the protagonist’s life should the setting complicate?
- what values, laws, fears, myths, institutions, class structures, technologies, or environmental conditions matter to this story?
- what should the protagonist be forced to navigate?

Only build elements that support:
- conflict
- stakes
- character pressure
- choices
- plot movement

## Step 2: Build pressure systems

Useful world pressure categories:
- law and punishment
- class and status
- religion and taboo
- scarcity and economy
- geography and environment
- technology and dependence
- tradition and ritual
- faction rivalry
- surveillance and secrecy
- war and occupation
- disease and instability
- obligation and debt

Choose the pressures most relevant to the story.

## Step 3: Build values, taboos, and consequences

A world becomes story-active when there are real costs.

Define:
- what is respected?
- what is forbidden?
- what is rewarded?
- what is punished?
- what hypocrisy exists?
- what do different groups believe?
- what social lie does the world run on?

Good worldbuilding often creates value conflict, not just scenery.

## Step 4: Build environmental or structural friction

The setting should change behavior.

Examples:
- a city where speech is monitored changes dialogue
- a desert economy changes resource stakes
- a decaying empire changes loyalty and law
- a floating settlement changes travel, class, danger, and ritual
- a school built on rank changes power and humiliation

Ask:
- what makes ordinary action harder here?
- what creates unequal power?
- what shapes daily behavior?
- what creates danger or dependence?

## Step 5: Connect world elements to character and plot

For each world detail, ask:
- how does this pressure the protagonist?
- what plot consequence does it create?
- what choice does it make harder?
- what scene opportunities does it generate?
- what values does it reveal or challenge?

If the answer is vague, the world detail is too decorative.

## World diagnostics

### If the world feels generic
Check for:
- no values or taboo
- no consequence for breaking rules
- no scarcity or structural pressure
- no group tension
- no setting friction

### If the world feels overbuilt
Check for:
- long lore explanation with no plot function
- naming without consequence
- systems that do not affect scenes
- backstory replacing conflict

### If the world feels disconnected from the protagonist
Check for:
- pressures not tied to their lie, want, or fear
- setting details that never shape choices
- conflict that could happen anywhere exactly the same way

## Output format

### Story need
- what the world must do for the story

### Pressure systems
- 3 to 5 world pressures most relevant to the plot

### Values and consequences
- rules, taboos, incentives, punishments, or beliefs that shape behavior

### Character pressure link
- how the world specifically pressures the protagonist

### Plot support
- how these world elements create conflict, stakes, and scene opportunities
