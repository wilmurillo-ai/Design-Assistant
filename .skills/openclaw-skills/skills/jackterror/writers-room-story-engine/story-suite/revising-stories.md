# Revising Stories

Use this skill to diagnose what is weak and repair it in the right order.

Revision is not just polishing language.
The goal is to identify the highest-level failure first.

Strong revision usually happens in this order:
1. identify what story is actually on the page
2. diagnose the biggest failure
3. repair story core if needed
4. repair character engine if needed
5. repair causal structure
6. repair scenes and pacing
7. repair climax and ending
8. polish prose last

## Core principles

- Diagnose before rewriting.
- Start with the biggest problem first.
- Fix structure before line edits.
- Fix passivity before dialogue polish.
- Fix story logic before style.
- Theme often becomes clearer in revision.
- Cut what is decorative if it weakens the core.

Copy this checklist if useful:

```text

- [ ] Step 6: Repair scenes and pacing
- [ ] Step 7: Repair climax and ending
```

## Step 1: Identify the real story

Ask:
- what is this story actually about?
- what matters most in it?
- what is the current emotional center?
- what story did the draft accidentally become?
- what promise does the current draft make?
- what kind of ending does the current draft seem built toward?

State:
- real premise
- likely theme
- protagonist arc as currently written
- strongest existing tension
- strongest existing element
- weakest existing layer

Do not revise blindly. First identify what story is actually on the page, not what the writer intended in theory.

## Step 2: Diagnose the main failure

Determine the biggest problem first.

Common categories:

### Audience problem
- the story is technically clear but not compelling
- weak promise
- low emotional investment
- little anticipation or uncertainty
- too much explanation, too little tension

### Story core problem
- the premise is blurry
- the theme is diluted
- the story lacks a strong central tension
- the ending direction is weak or mismatched
- the story is overbuilt and underfocused

### Character problem
- weak want
- weak need
- unclear lie
- unclear ghost
- passivity
- no proactive turn
- flat motives
- weak or generic opinions
- no real contradiction

### Plot problem
- too many and-then beats
- low escalation
- weak consequences
- repetitive middle
- shapeless progression
- causality missing between major turns

### Scene problem
- scenes do not turn
- exposition-heavy scenes
- redundant scenes
- scenes without objective or pressure
- scenes that exist for mood alone

### Ending problem
- climax not testing the core truth
- ending unearned
- resolution by coincidence
- weak emotional payoff
- no visible cost
- no real change

Choose the main problem first. Do not try to fix everything at once.

## Step 3: Repair the story core if needed

If the problem is foundational, rebuild the story core before touching scenes.

Re-evaluate:
- essence
- audience promise
- thematic tension
- ending direction

Ask:
- what is the most economical version of this story?
- what belief, value conflict, or question drives it?
- what is the audience supposed to care about most?
- what final choice, sacrifice, revelation, transformation, or cost should the ending force?

Cut or revise elements that:
- distract from the core
- duplicate the same function
- add scale without meaning
- add lore without story pressure
- weaken the central line

If the core is weak, all later revisions will feel cosmetic.

## Step 4: Repair the character engine

Rebuild:
- want
- need
- lie
- ghost
- spine
- comfort zone
- opposite pressure
- stakes
- arc direction

Check:
- is the protagonist too passive?
- is the protagonist too competent with no vulnerability?
- do they have opinions?
- does the story pressure their comfort zone?
- do they move from reaction toward action?
- does the climax test old lie vs new truth?

Repair by clarifying:
- what they consciously want
- what they are wrong about
- what wound feeds that wrongness
- what force or world pressure exposes it
- what must change by the end

If the character engine is weak, scene rewrites alone will not solve the story.

## Step 5: Repair causal structure

Rebuild the beat chain.

For each major beat, ask:
- what caused this?
- what consequence does it create?
- is the link therefore or but?
- what choice drives it?
- what gets harder?

If a beat exists only because the writer wants it, not because the story causes it, revise it.

Also test:
- can beats be combined?
- are detours weakening the core?
- is the midpoint actually changing direction?
- is escalation visible?
- are stakes increasing?
- are repeated beat functions flattening momentum?

Repair flat structure by:
- strengthening consequences
- increasing costs
- making choices more irreversible
- replacing sequence with causality
- removing rescue coincidences
- simplifying redundant movement

## Step 6: Repair scenes and pacing

For each scene, ask:
- what does the character want?
- what blocks them?
- what changes?
- why must this scene exist?
- is this the most economical version?
- what pressure is operating?
- what part of the world or character does this reveal through action?

Cut or merge scenes that:
- repeat the same function
- only explain lore
- do not create change
- exist only for mood
- delay the real conflict
- let the protagonist remain passive too long
- can be summarized without loss

Strengthen scenes by increasing:
- objective clarity
- obstacle intensity
- subtext
- pressure
- turn at the end
- consequence for the next scene

## Step 7: Repair climax and ending

The ending should:
- test the central lie vs truth
- express the theme through action
- result from choices and consequences
- cost something
- change the character or the meaning of the world

Ask:
- what belief, flaw, or fear must the protagonist face here?
- is the ending earned by earlier setup?
- does the climax force an irreversible choice?
- does the resolution come from character and causality, not luck?
- what emotional afterimage should remain?

Repair weak endings by:
- tying climax directly to the protagonist’s arc
- removing coincidence-based resolution
- increasing visible cost
- clarifying what is won, lost, or transformed
- simplifying if complexity is muddying impact

## Step 8: Recommend the next rewrite pass

After diagnosis and proposed repair, state the next best pass.

Examples:
- foundation rewrite
- protagonist rewrite
- beat restructure
- middle compression
- scene rewrite
- ending rewrite
- dialogue polish after structure is fixed

## Default output format

### Real story on the page
- what the story currently is

### Main failure
- biggest issue and why it matters most

### Repair plan
- story core
- character
- structure
- scenes
- ending

### Recommended next pass
- what to rewrite now and what to leave for later
