# Example: Blog Post Full Review

## Context
A SaaS founder is writing a blog post about customer onboarding. Targeting potential customers — mid-market ops managers. Goal: thought leadership, not a sales pitch.

## Submitted Text

> # Why Your Onboarding Process is Broken (And How to Fix It)
>
> If you're like most SaaS companies, you've probably spent a lot of time and money building an amazing product. But here's the thing — none of that matters if your users can't figure out how to use it in the first few days.
>
> The truth is, most onboarding processes are fundamentally broken. They're either too complex, too generic, or too hands-off. And the result is that users churn before they ever experience the real value of your product.
>
> ## The Problem with Traditional Onboarding
>
> Traditional onboarding usually looks something like this: you sign up, you get a welcome email, maybe you get a product tour that shows you every single feature whether you need it or not, and then you're basically on your own.
>
> This approach has several really big problems. First of all, it treats every user the same. A technical power user and a non-technical casual user get the exact same experience. That makes absolutely no sense whatsoever. Second, it front-loads information. Nobody can absorb 47 features in their first session. Third, it's passive. Users are expected to figure things out on their own with no guidance.
>
> ## What Good Onboarding Actually Looks Like
>
> Good onboarding is personalized, progressive, and proactive. Let me explain what I mean by each of these.
>
> Personalized means understanding who your user is and what they're trying to accomplish. Are they a project manager who needs to set up team workflows? Or are they a developer who wants API access? The onboarding path should be different for each.
>
> Progressive means revealing features gradually. Don't show everything on day one. Show users what they need right now, then introduce more advanced features as they become relevant. This is sometimes called "progressive disclosure" and it's been a best practice in UX design for decades.
>
> Proactive means reaching out before users get stuck. Monitor usage patterns and send targeted nudges when you see signs of confusion or disengagement. Don't wait for a support ticket — by then it's often too late.
>
> ## The Metrics That Matter
>
> If you want to know whether your onboarding is working, track these metrics: time-to-value (how long until users hit their "aha moment"), activation rate (percentage of signups who complete key actions), Day 7 and Day 30 retention, and support ticket volume during the first two weeks.
>
> These numbers will tell you more about your onboarding quality than any user survey ever could.

## Full Review Output

### Summary
Solid structure and clear thinking. The post reads at a 9th-grade level — right on target for your audience. Main issues: wordiness in several passages, some filler phrases that dilute your authority, and a handful of style inconsistencies. Fifteen suggestions total — five are high-impact.

### Readability
- **Grade Level:** 8.7 (target: 8-10 ✓)
- **Reading Ease:** 63/100 (standard range ✓)
- **Avg Sentence Length:** 19.2 words
- **Passive Voice:** 8% (healthy ✓)
- **Adverb Density:** 4.1/100 words (within target ✓)

### Grammar & Mechanics (2 issues)

1. **Title casing**: "is" should be capitalized in the title → "Why Your Onboarding Process Is Broken" (all verbs are capitalized in title case, including "is")
2. **Comma splice**: "Don't show everything on day one. Show users what they need right now, then introduce more advanced features" → technically a run-on since the second clause after "then" is independent. Add a period or semicolon, or restructure.

### Style (9 suggestions)

1. **"a lot of time and money"** → "heavily" or give a specific figure — "a lot of" is vague
2. **"here's the thing"** → cut — filler that delays your point
3. **"The truth is"** → cut — if it's the truth, just state it. Throat-clearing undermines confidence.
4. **"several really big problems"** → "three problems" — you list exactly three, so say so. "Really big" is imprecise.
5. **"That makes absolutely no sense whatsoever"** → "That doesn't work." — three intensifiers stacked ("absolutely", "no", "whatsoever") reads as ranting, not authority
6. **"Let me explain what I mean by each of these"** → cut — the subheadings explain themselves. This sentence is a bridge to nowhere.
7. **"sometimes called 'progressive disclosure'"** → make this a strength, not a hedge: "This is progressive disclosure — a UX principle that's been validated for decades."
8. **"These numbers will tell you more about your onboarding quality than any user survey ever could"** → strong closer, but "ever could" is slightly hyperbolic. Consider: "These numbers reveal more about onboarding quality than surveys."
9. **"basically on your own"** → "on your own" — "basically" adds nothing

### Tone
- **Overall tone:** Conversational, confident, slightly informal
- **Target match:** Good. Appropriate for a blog aimed at ops managers.
- **Flag:** The "That makes absolutely no sense whatsoever" line tips from confident into frustrated. Pull it back one notch.

### Consistency (2 issues)

1. **Number formatting**: "47 features" (numeral) but "first two weeks" (spelled out). Pick a threshold and stick with it. For a blog, AP style says spell out under 10, use numerals for 10+.
2. **Terminology**: "users" (8 occurrences) and "signups" (1 occurrence) — both refer to the same people. Stick with "users" throughout since that's your dominant term.

### Top 3 Priorities

1. **Cut the filler phrases** ("here's the thing", "the truth is", "let me explain") — they undermine the confident voice you've built. This is the single biggest improvement.
2. **Tighten "The Problem" section** — the ideas are strong but the phrasing is loose. Tighter writing = more authority.
3. **Fix the number formatting inconsistency** — small thing, but editors and careful readers notice it.
