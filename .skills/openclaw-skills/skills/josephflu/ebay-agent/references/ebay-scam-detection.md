# eBay Scam Detection Guide

**A practical field guide for identifying and avoiding scams on eBay — for both buyers and sellers.**

eBay is one of the safest online marketplaces thanks to its buyer/seller protection programs, but scammers still operate on the platform. This guide covers the most common scam patterns, the red flags that signal them, and concrete steps you can take to protect yourself. Bookmark this and refer to it whenever something feels off about a transaction.

---

## Part 1: Seller-Side Scam Detection (Protecting Yourself as a Seller)

These are scams where a fraudulent buyer targets you, the seller. They range from social engineering tricks to payment fraud.

### Red Flag 1: Request to Ship to a Different Address

**The scam:** A buyer purchases your item and then messages you asking to ship to an address different from the one on the order. If you comply, eBay's seller protection is void because you did not ship to the confirmed address. The buyer then opens an "Item Not Received" case and wins automatically.

**What to do:** Never ship to any address other than the one shown in the eBay order. If the buyer insists, ask them to cancel the order and repurchase with the correct address. No exceptions.

---

### Red Flag 2: "Can You End the Listing Early for $X?"

**The scam:** A buyer contacts you outside the normal buying flow, offering to buy your item directly — often at a tempting price. The goal is to move the transaction off eBay, where you have no seller protection. They may pay with a fraudulent method, reverse the payment, or simply never pay.

**What to do:** All transactions must go through eBay's checkout. Politely decline and tell them to use the Buy It Now button or place a bid. If they persist, report the message to eBay.

---

### Red Flag 3: Overpayment + Request to Wire the Difference

**The scam:** A buyer "accidentally" overpays (often via a fake payment notification) and asks you to wire or send the difference back. The original payment is fraudulent and will be reversed, leaving you out both the item and the money you wired.

**What to do:** Never send money to a buyer for any reason. If a genuine overpayment occurs, refund it through eBay's system only — never via wire transfer, Zelle, Venmo, or any other method.

---

### Red Flag 4: New Account Shipping to a Freight Forwarder

**The scam:** A brand-new account (zero or very low feedback) purchases a high-value item and ships it to a freight forwarding address. Once the item leaves the country via freight forwarder, you lose all seller protection. The buyer then claims the item was not received or was damaged.

**What to do:** Be cautious with zero-feedback buyers on high-value items. If the shipping address is a known freight forwarder (Google the address), consider canceling the order. eBay's seller protection does not cover shipments to freight forwarders.

---

### Red Flag 5: Pressure for Tracking Before Payment Clears

**The scam:** A buyer demands you ship immediately and provide tracking before eBay confirms the payment. The payment may be pending, fraudulent, or from a compromised account. Once you ship and provide tracking, you cannot recover the item.

**What to do:** Wait for eBay to show the payment as "cleared" or "funds available" before shipping. Legitimate buyers understand standard processing times.

---

### Red Flag 6: Return Fraud (Swap and Return)

**The scam:** A buyer purchases your item, swaps it with a broken or inferior version they already own, and returns the damaged item for a full refund. This is especially common with electronics, game consoles, and brand-name items.

**What to do:**
- Photograph and video-record the item and its serial number before shipping.
- Note the serial number in the listing or your records.
- When you receive a return, check the serial number before issuing a refund.
- If the serial number does not match, contact eBay with your evidence.

---

### Red Flag 7: "Item Not as Described" on Accurately Described Items

**The scam:** A buyer with remorse opens an "Item Not as Described" (INAD) case rather than a return request, because INAD cases force the seller to pay return shipping. Some buyers use this systematically to get free return shipping.

**What to do:**
- Write thorough, honest descriptions with photos of every flaw.
- If a buyer opens an INAD case that is clearly remorse-based, call eBay seller support and explain the situation with your listing photos as evidence.
- Track buyers who do this — eBay's system will eventually flag serial abusers.

## Part 2: Buyer-Side Scam Detection (Protecting Yourself as a Buyer)

These are scams where a fraudulent seller targets you, the buyer.

### Red Flag 1: Price Significantly Below Market (50%+ Off)

**The scam:** A listing for a current-generation iPhone at half the going rate is almost certainly not a lucky find. Scammers list high-demand items at unrealistically low prices to lure quick purchases. The item either does not exist, is counterfeit, or is stolen.

**What to do:** If the price seems too good to be true, it is. Check the seller's feedback and listing history. Compare the price to recent sold listings. Walk away from deals that are dramatically below market.

---

### Red Flag 2: Stock Photos Only with Vague Descriptions

**The scam:** The seller uses manufacturer stock photos (or photos clearly taken from another listing) and provides a minimal or copy-pasted description. They may not actually possess the item, or the item is in much worse condition than implied.

**What to do:** Look for original photos showing the actual item, especially for used goods. Check if the photos appear elsewhere (reverse image search). Ask the seller for additional photos of the specific item. If they refuse or deflect, do not buy.

---

### Red Flag 3: Ships from a Different Country Than Stated

**The scam:** The listing says "Ships from United States" but the item actually ships from China or another country. This results in weeks-long shipping times, potential customs fees, and often a lower-quality or counterfeit item.

**What to do:** Check the item location field carefully. Look at the seller's other listings and feedback — if most reviews mention long shipping times or overseas origins, the listed location is likely misleading.

---

### Red Flag 4: Request for Payment Outside eBay

**The scam:** The seller asks you to pay via PayPal Friends & Family, Zelle, wire transfer, gift cards, or cryptocurrency instead of through eBay's checkout. Payments outside eBay are not covered by eBay's Money Back Guarantee.

**What to do:** Never pay outside eBay. Report the seller immediately. This is a clear violation of eBay policy and a near-certain scam.

---

### Red Flag 5: No Returns + Low Feedback

**The scam:** A newer seller with few reviews and a strict no-returns policy is a high-risk combination. Legitimate new sellers typically offer returns to build trust. The combination of no returns and no track record suggests the seller does not plan to be around if something goes wrong.

**What to do:** Prefer sellers with established feedback (98%+ positive, 50+ reviews). If buying from a newer seller, insist on a listing that accepts returns. eBay's Money Back Guarantee still applies, but returns-accepted listings are easier to resolve.

---

### Red Flag 6: Listing Uses Misleading Keywords

**The scam:** The listing title says "iPhone 15 Pro" but buried in the description it says "case only" or "box only" or "for parts." Scammers rely on buyers not reading the full description.

**What to do:** Always read the full description and look at all photos. Pay attention to words like "box only," "for parts," "read description," or "as-is."

---

## Part 3: General Protection Rules

These apply whether you are buying or selling.

### Always Transact Through eBay

eBay's Money Back Guarantee and seller protections only apply to transactions completed through eBay's checkout system. The moment a transaction moves to email, text, phone, or an external payment method, you lose all protection.

### Document Everything

- Save all messages with the other party.
- Photograph items (serial numbers, condition, packaging) before shipping and after receiving.
- Keep tracking numbers and delivery confirmations.
- Screenshot listings before they can be edited or removed.

### Check Feedback Thoroughly

Do not just look at the percentage — read the actual feedback comments. Look for:
- Patterns of complaints (slow shipping, item not as described, communication issues).
- Feedback age: a seller with 500 reviews from 2019 and none since may be a compromised or dormant account.
- Feedback type: are the reviews from buyers or sellers? A high score built entirely on $1 purchases is less meaningful than one built on relevant transactions.

### Use eBay's Resolution Center

If something goes wrong, open a case through eBay's Resolution Center rather than trying to resolve it solely through messages. The Resolution Center creates a documented trail and gives eBay's support team the context to rule fairly.

### Trust Your Instincts

If a deal feels wrong — unusual pressure, evasive answers, something that does not add up — walk away. There will always be another listing. There is no deal good enough to justify ignoring red flags.
