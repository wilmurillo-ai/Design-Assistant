# Messaging Guide

**The question:** How do you describe the problem in their words - and structure a message that earns the next step?

---

## Why this step matters

Most B2B messaging fails at the first sentence. The sender leads with what they do, not what the buyer feels. The buyer reads "We help companies grow faster with AI-powered GTM" and thinks "sounds like everyone else" and moves on.

The buyer's brain is pattern-matching for one thing: does this person understand my situation? If yes, they lean in. If no, they stop.

A message that works has three parts in sequence:
1. Problem - named in the buyer's language, not yours
2. Mechanism - why this is happening now, what changed
3. Proof - a specific, verifiable result

Get these out of order and the message fails. Lead with mechanism and the buyer has no reason to trust your claim. Lead with proof before establishing the problem and the buyer does not know if it applies to them.

---

## Part 1: Problem - their words, not yours

The problem statement must sound like something the buyer would say to a trusted colleague, not like something a vendor wrote.

**Bad:** "Many professional services firms struggle with inconsistent lead generation."
**Good:** "Most of our clients tell us the same thing: they can sell when they're in the room, but they hate having to create the conversation in the first place."

The second version uses language the buyer has actually thought or said. It references a specific emotional reality, not a category description.

**How to find their words:**
- Read reviews of competitors on G2, Capterra, or similar sites
- Read LinkedIn posts from your target buyer type (what complaints do they voice?)
- Read Reddit threads and industry forums where they are honest
- Review discovery call transcripts or notes - what exact phrases did buyers use?
- Ask your best customer: "How would you describe the problem you had before we worked together?"

**The test:** Read your problem statement out loud. Could your buyer have said this to a friend? If not, rewrite it.

---

## Part 2: Mechanism - why now

The mechanism explains why this problem exists or has gotten worse, and why solving it now matters more than waiting.

A mechanism is not a feature list. It is a structural explanation that makes the buyer nod and think "yes, that's exactly what's happening."

Examples of strong mechanisms:
- "Referrals used to be enough because everyone in your network was still growing. Now your best referrers are managing their own uncertainty, and the referral rate has quietly dropped 40% across most B2B service categories."
- "Your competitors are deploying AI agents that publish content, run outreach, and book meetings 7 days a week at a cost you can't match with a human team. The gap is not a tactical advantage anymore - it is a compounding one."
- "The buyers in your space are doing 70-80% of their research before they contact a vendor. If you are not in the AI search results, the LinkedIn searches, and the category directories where they look, you are invisible before the conversation starts."

A mechanism must be true. If you make up a mechanism, a buyer who knows their industry will catch it and you lose all credibility.

**Questions to find your mechanism:**
- What changed in the buyer's world in the last 12-24 months that made this problem worse?
- What structural shift (technology, market, regulation, competitive) is driving the urgency?
- What are buyers in this category doing differently than they were 3 years ago?
- What is the cost of waiting 6 more months to address this?

---

## Part 3: Proof - specific and verifiable

Proof must be specific and verifiable. Vague social proof is worse than no proof because it signals that you do not have real results to show.

**Weak proof:** "We've helped dozens of companies grow their pipeline."
**Strong proof:** "A CPA firm owner in Chicago went from 0 referrals outside his existing network to 11 booked calls in 47 days. None of them knew him before the first email."

Strong proof contains:
- A specific person or company type (not "a client")
- A specific result (not "significant growth")
- A specific timeframe (not "quickly")
- A mechanism that makes the result believable (what did they do to get there?)

If you do not have strong proof yet, use process proof: "Here is exactly what we do, step by step. You can verify whether this approach would work for you."

---

## Anti-patterns to avoid

**Feature dumping:** Listing what you do instead of what changes for the buyer.
**Category claims:** "AI-powered," "data-driven," "full-service" - these mean nothing because everyone uses them.
**Assumed urgency:** Claiming something is urgent without explaining why. "Don't miss this opportunity" earns skepticism, not attention.
**We-centric framing:** Starting every sentence with "We do..." instead of "You'll be able to..."
**Vague social proof:** "Trusted by hundreds of companies" with no specifics.
**Jargon matching:** Using industry buzzwords that the buyer uses but does not trust in a sales context.

---

## The 3-part message structure

Once you have all three parts, the structure is:

```
[Problem in their words]

[Mechanism - what changed, why it is happening]

[Proof - specific result with context]

[Single, low-friction call to action]
```

The call to action should request one small action: read this, reply yes, take 15 minutes. Not "schedule a 45-minute strategy call." Too much commitment too early.

---

## Questions to answer in writing

1. How would your best customer describe the problem they had before working with you? (Use their words. Quote them if possible.)
2. What structural change in the market makes this problem urgent right now?
3. What is the most specific result you can point to?
4. What is the single sentence that would make your ICP lean in and read the next sentence?
5. What call to action has the lowest friction possible for the first interaction?

---

## Message Summary Template

```
MESSAGE SUMMARY

Problem Statement (their words):
[2-3 sentences. Should sound like something they would say to a colleague.]

Mechanism (why now):
[2-3 sentences. What changed? Why does waiting cost them?]

Proof (specific):
[1-2 sentences. Company type + result + timeframe + credibility mechanism.]

Core Message (combined):
[The 4-part structure above, assembled as a paragraph.]

First-Touch CTA:
[One sentence. One ask. Low friction.]

Anti-patterns I am avoiding:
[List the ones you are most at risk of slipping into.]
```
