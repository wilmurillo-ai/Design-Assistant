# Offer Design Playbook

Build an offer that converts before running any outreach.

An offer is not a service description. It is a structured package -- outcome, mechanism, timeline, risk reversal, and price -- that makes the value so obvious a qualified buyer has almost no rational reason to say no.

Running outreach with a weak offer is the most common reason cold email and sales fail. Fix the offer before fixing the channel.

---

## Prerequisites

- [ ] F1 (Positioning) complete -- ICP defined with buying trigger
- [ ] At least 3 conversations with current or potential clients about their pain
- [ ] You can name the outcome in the buyer's exact words, not your words

If you cannot pass all three: stop. The offer will be built on assumptions. Do F1 first.

---

## What this produces

- Offer statement (outcome + mechanism + timeline + risk reversal in one paragraph)
- Pricing structure (outcome-based, not hourly)
- Offer scorecard (Value Equation score)
- Grand Slam Offer checklist
- Attraction, Upsell, and Continuity offer architecture for your business type

---

## The Four Offer Types (build in this order)

| Type | What it does | Build order |
|------|-------------|------------|
| **Attraction Offer** | Converts strangers to buyers. Low risk, high perceived value entry point. | First |
| **Upsell Offer** | Offered immediately after the first purchase. Most profitable transaction. | Second |
| **Downsell Offer** | Recovers people who said no to the upsell. Lower price, lower scope. | Third |
| **Continuity Offer** | Recurring revenue. Fixes lumpy cash flow. | Fourth |

**Diagnostic:** Which of these four do you NOT have? Build the missing one. Do not skip to a later type before earlier ones exist.

## Step 1: Define the Dream Outcome

The dream outcome is not what you deliver. It is the state the buyer imagines their world in after the problem is solved.

Write three versions. Pick the most visceral one.

Template:
> "[Buyer type] goes from [current painful state] to [desired outcome] in [timeframe] without [effort or sacrifice they dread]."

Examples:
- "A 10-person consulting firm goes from inconsistent $30K months to a full calendar of qualified meetings in 60 days without writing a single cold email themselves."
- "A SaaS founder with 3% trial-to-paid conversion goes to 9%+ in 45 days without rebuilding their onboarding flow from scratch."

Rules:
- Use the buyer's words. If you spoke to clients, their exact language belongs here.
- Make it specific. "More revenue" is not an outcome. "$47K in 90 days" is.
- Make it picture-able. The buyer should be able to see themselves in it.

---

## Step 2: Score with the Value Equation

```
Value = (Dream Outcome x Perceived Likelihood of Achievement)
        / (Time Delay x Effort and Sacrifice)
```

Score each lever 1-5. Target: 18+/20.

| Lever | Score | What to fix if below 4 |
|-------|-------|----------------------|
| Dream Outcome | /5 | Rewrite in buyer's language. Get more specific. |
| Perceived Likelihood | /5 | Add case studies with numbers. Add a guarantee. Name the mechanism. |
| Time Delay | /5 | Create a quick win in the first 7-14 days. Show the first result fast. |
| Effort and Sacrifice | /5 | Remove every step the buyer has to do themselves. Done-for-you beats DIY. |

**If any lever scores below 3: fix that lever before building the offer stack. A weak lever is an objection the buyer will feel but not always name.**

---

## Step 3: Build the Offer Stack

The Grand Slam Offer has six components. All six must be present.

**Component 1: The Outcome**
Verbatim from Step 1. Specific, visceral, in buyer language.

**Component 2: Niche Specificity**
Who exactly is this for? The narrower the niche, the higher the perceived value.

| Generic | Specific |
|---------|---------|
| "Businesses" | "10-30 person B2B service firms" |
| "Get more clients" | "Add 5-8 discovery calls per month" |
| "Marketing services" | "LinkedIn outreach for IT consultancies selling to mid-market CFOs" |

**Component 3: Outcome-Based Price**
Price is a percentage of the outcome value. Target: your price should be 10% or less of the outcome value.

If the outcome is $200K in new revenue: a $20K engagement price is rational.
If the outcome is unclear: pricing is arbitrary and buyers will negotiate or walk.

**Component 4: Risk Reversal**
You bear the risk, not the buyer.

| Type | When to use |
|------|------------|
| Results guarantee | "If we don't hit [metric] by [date], we work free until we do." |
| Money-back guarantee | "If you don't see [result] in [timeframe], full refund." |
| Done-for-you guarantee | "We do it. If it doesn't work, we redo it at no charge." |

**Win Your Money Back structure** (highest converting for productized services):
The client earns their money back by completing prescribed actions within the program. People who earn it back almost never take it -- they apply it to the upsell instead.

Only use if current refund rate is below 5%. Fix the product first if above that.

**Component 5: Scarcity / Urgency (if real)**
Real scarcity converts. Manufactured scarcity destroys credibility permanently.

Real options:
- Cohort timing ("Next intake starts March 1 -- 4 spots left")
- Capacity limit ("We only take 3 new clients per month")
- Price increase ("Current price holds until [date] -- then it goes up")

Do not use countdown timers that reset. Do not manufacture urgency.

**Component 6: The Bonus Stack**
Map the secondary problems the buyer has. Each one is a live objection unless you address it.

Template:
```
Primary problem: [what the core offer solves]
Secondary problem 1: [what stops them from using your solution?]
Secondary problem 2: [what do they still have to do themselves?]
Secondary problem 3: [what could go wrong even if they buy?]
Secondary problem 4: [what is the hidden cost they haven't thought about?]
```

Each secondary problem becomes a bonus, a feature, or part of the guarantee.

---

## Step 4: Write the Offer Statement

One paragraph. All components in it.

Template:
> "We [outcome in buyer's words] for [niche-specific buyer] in [timeframe]. We use [mechanism] so you don't have to [effort they dread]. If we don't hit [specific metric] by [specific date], [guarantee]. [Bonus stack addresses secondary problem]."

Example:
> "We book 5-8 qualified discovery calls per month for IT consultancies selling to mid-market buyers -- without you writing a single email. We build and run the entire outbound system: ICP list, sequence, send, and reply handling. If we don't hit 5 booked calls in the first 60 days, we work at no charge until we do."

Run the Stranger Test: read this to someone unfamiliar with your offer. If they can't explain it back in 15 seconds, rewrite.

---

## Step 5: Build the Pricing Tiers

Minimum: two tiers with a logical progression.

| Tier | What changes | Why it exists |
|------|-------------|--------------|
| Entry | Lower scope, lower risk, faster first win | Converts buyers who aren't ready for the full commitment |
| Full | Complete outcome, full mechanism | Your main offer |
| Premium (optional) | Done-for-you, faster timeline, more access | Anchors the full offer as middle-tier |

**Rules:**
- Each tier must have a clear, logical reason to exist beyond "some people can't afford the top tier"
- The step-up from entry to full must feel obvious: "I got [X] from entry tier, I want [Y] from full tier"
- Never compete on price. Compete on outcome. If buyers compare you on price, your offer isn't specific enough.

---

## Step 6: Define the Attraction Offer

The Attraction Offer is your lowest-risk entry point. Its job is to get strangers to become buyers.

It should:
- Remove all financial risk (free, trial, or money-back)
- Deliver a real result that creates appetite for the full offer
- Be priced low enough that only a crazy person would say no

The Drug Dealer Model: give the strongest hit first. The Attraction Offer is the first hit. Your full offer is what they have to pay for.

The Salty Pretzel Rule: the Attraction Offer should solve a narrow upstream problem that makes your full offer's problem obvious and urgent. If it satisfies them completely, it's too complete.

---

## Step 7: Build the Continuity Offer (if missing)

Lumpy revenue = missing continuity offer. Every service business needs recurring revenue.

Options by business type:

| Business type | Continuity mechanism |
|--------------|---------------------|
| Agency | Monthly retainer (reporting + optimization + new campaigns) |
| Consulting | Monthly advisory call + async Q&A access |
| IT/MSP | Managed service contract (monitoring + support + patches) |
| SaaS | Subscription -- the product IS the continuity |
| Coaching | Monthly group program or cohort |
| Trade business | Maintenance plan, seasonal service agreement |

The continuity offer converts at the highest rate when offered immediately after the client achieves the first win from the core offer.

---

## Offer Readiness Checklist

Before using this offer in outreach or proposals:

- [ ] Dream Outcome written in buyer's exact words
- [ ] Value Equation scored 18+/20 (or specific plan to improve the lowest lever)
- [ ] Niche specificity: a stranger knows exactly who this is for
- [ ] Outcome-based pricing: price is 10% or less of outcome value
- [ ] Risk reversal exists and is real (not hedged)
- [ ] Bonus stack addresses at least 2 secondary problems
- [ ] Stranger Test passed (someone unfamiliar can explain it back in 15 seconds)
- [ ] Attraction Offer exists
- [ ] Continuity Offer exists or has a build date

If any box is unchecked: fix it before running outreach. A weak offer wastes every qualified lead that hits it.
