# LinkedIn Post Guide - Short-Form B2B Content

Short-form posts for LinkedIn. Under 1,300 characters for standard posts. Built to stop the scroll,
earn engagement, and build authority with B2B buyers over time.

---

## Character Limits and Format Targets

| Format | Target Length | When to Use |
|--------|--------------|-------------|
| Standard post | 600-1,000 characters | Daily cadence, thought leadership |
| Long-form post | 1,000-1,300 characters | Story, contrarian argument, deep insight |
| Carousel/document | N/A | How-to content, frameworks, data |
| Thread | 3-5 linked posts | Multi-part argument, step-by-step |

Rule: LinkedIn cuts off the preview at around 210 characters and shows a "see more" prompt.
Everything before that cutoff is your real hook. Write accordingly.

## Hook Formulas (Use One Per Post)

The first line is everything. If it does not earn the click to expand, nothing else matters.
Below are 5 hook formulas with examples and when each works.

### Hook Formula 1: The Contrarian Statement

State something that most people in your ICP's world believe is true - and flip it.

Format: "[Common belief] is [wrong / backwards / costing you]."

Examples:
- "More salespeople will not fix your pipeline problem."
- "Your best clients are probably paying you too little. Not because of market rates - because
  you have not raised prices since you onboarded them."
- "The problem is not that you have too few leads. It is that you have too many conversations
  that should never have happened."

When to use: When you have a clear contrarian position you can defend in the body.
Avoid: Making the contrarian claim too mild. "Some might argue..." is not contrarian. It is hedging.

---

### Hook Formula 2: The Specific Number

Open with a count, percentage, time frame, or dollar figure that is precise enough to be credible.

Format: "[Number] [thing] [outcome or observation]."

Examples:
- "11 of the last 14 service businesses I audited had the same pricing problem."
- "In 90 days, one client went from 3 inbound calls a month to 23."
- "I have had 400+ conversations with B2B founders this year. The same objection comes up in 8 of 10."

When to use: When you have a real observation, data point, or client result to reference.
Avoid: Vague numbers. "Dozens of clients" is weaker than "31 clients."

---

### Hook Formula 3: The Scene Drop

Start in the middle of a specific moment. No setup. No context.

Format: "[Specific scene in present or past tense, under 15 words.]"

Examples:
- "Last Tuesday, a founder showed me his pipeline spreadsheet. It had 200 rows. None of them were
  real opportunities."
- "A client called me in a panic. His biggest referral source had just retired."
- "I spent three hours yesterday reading a business's testimonials. Not one of them said what the
  business actually does for people."

When to use: When you have a real story - from client work, a conversation, or direct experience.
Avoid: Generic scenes. "A business owner told me..." is not a scene. Name the situation specifically.

---

### Hook Formula 4: The Before State

Name the exact situation your reader is in right now, without editorializing it.

Format: "You [are doing / have / keep doing] [specific thing]. [The result or implication]."

Examples:
- "You have 6 clients. 5 of them came from referrals. You have never had to explain your service
  to a stranger."
- "You are booked out 3 months. You also have no idea where the next client after those 3 months
  is coming from."
- "You charge $4,000 per month. Your client would pay $7,000 if you just asked. You have not asked."

When to use: When you know your reader's exact situation and can name it precisely. Recognition
is the fastest path to trust.
Avoid: Making it sound like a takedown. The goal is "they know me," not "I am criticizing you."

---

### Hook Formula 5: The Direct Claim

Open with your thesis. No buildup.

Format: "The reason [X problem] is [real cause, not obvious one]."
Or: "[Thing] does not [what people think it does]. It [what it actually does]."

Examples:
- "The reason most service businesses plateau at $1M is not the market. It is the offer structure."
- "Cold email does not fail because buyers are tired of email. It fails because most cold email
  is about the sender, not the reader."
- "Referrals slow down not because your network shrinks - but because your clients stop knowing
  how to describe you."

When to use: When you have enough credibility with your audience for a bare claim to land.
Avoid: Claims that are too broad to defend in 800 characters.

## Post Formats With Examples

### Format A: Contrarian Insight

Use for: positions that cut against conventional advice in your category.

Structure:
Line 1: Contrarian hook (under 15 words)
Lines 2-4: Why you believe this (evidence or observed pattern)
Lines 5-6: What the conventional approach actually produces
Lines 7-8: What changes when you do the alternative
Last line: Question or invitation

Example:

"Posting every day on LinkedIn will not grow your business.

I have watched 40+ founders do daily posts for 90+ days.

Most of them got more followers. Almost none of them got more clients.

The problem: they optimized for content, not for conversations.

People who book calls do one thing differently. They end every post with a real question
and reply to every comment within 2 hours. That is what builds pipeline - not the post count.

How are you currently converting LinkedIn engagement into conversations?"

---

### Format B: Numbered List

Use for: practical lessons, observations, or patterns that stand on their own as individual insights.

Structure:
Line 1: "[Number] things I have learned from [specific experience]:"
Lines 2-8: Numbered items - one insight per item, 1-2 sentences each
Lines 9-11: Expand on the most surprising item
Last line: Optional - "Which of these matches your experience?"

Example:

"5 things I have learned from auditing service business pipelines:

1. Most founders have 2-3 real opportunities in a pipeline of 20+
2. The ones closest to closing are almost never the biggest deals
3. 'I need to think about it' usually means 'you have not made the value clear'
4. Founders who follow up within 24 hours close 3x more than those who wait a week
5. Price objections in proposal stage almost always started as scope confusion in discovery

The one that surprises people most: #3. Most founders hear 'I need to think about it' and wait.
The right move is to ask: 'What would you need to see to feel confident?' and have that conversation now.

Anything on this list match what you are seeing?"

---

### Format C: Personal Story With a Lesson

Use for: building trust through specific, honest experience sharing.

Structure:
Line 1: Scene-setting sentence (specific, not vague)
Lines 2-4: What happened - details, stakes
Lines 5-6: What you expected vs. what actually happened
Line 7: The lesson in one sentence
Lines 8-9: One practical application for the reader
Last line: Optional question

Example:

"In March, a client fired their entire outbound team. 4 people, $280K in annual payroll.

They replaced the function with a 2-person ops team and a set of automated sequences.

I expected the pipeline to drop. It went up 40% in 60 days.

Why: the 4-person team had been sending 100 emails a day each. Most of them were not qualified.
The automated sequences sent 60 a day total - but only to accounts that matched the ICP exactly.

Volume is not the metric. Qualified volume is the metric.

If you are measuring your outbound by sends, you are measuring the wrong thing."

---

### Format D: Short Hook Post

Use for: quick observations, provocations, or punchy takes. Under 300 characters. High frequency.

Structure:
Lines 1-2: The entire point, stated directly
Lines 3-5: The punchline or brief elaboration
Last line: One-line close or thought

Example:

"You do not have a lead problem.

You have a 'turning conversations into commitments' problem.

Most service businesses have 10-15 conversations a month that could become clients.
The conversion rate is the bottleneck - not the top of the funnel."

---

### Format E: Question-Led Insight

Use for: sparking dialogue by opening with a question that is specific enough to be interesting.

Note: This is the exception to the "never open with a question" rule - it works only when the
question is so specific that it signals expertise by itself.

Structure:
Line 1: The question (specific, not rhetorical)
Lines 2-5: Your answer, briefly
Lines 6-7: What you have seen in practice
Last line: "What is your answer to this?"

Example:

"When was the last time you raised prices?

If the answer is 'when I started,' you are almost certainly undercharging.

Most service business owners I work with find 20-30% of their client base would pay 25-40%
more without hesitation. They just never asked.

The test: quote your next prospect 20% above your current rate. See what happens.

When did you last test your ceiling?"

## Platform Rules and What Performs

### LinkedIn algorithm behavior

- Posts with outbound links in the body get suppressed. Put links in the first comment.
- Posts that generate comments in the first 60 minutes get amplified. Reply fast.
- Dwell time matters more than likes. Posts that make people read longer get distributed more.
- "See more" clicks are tracked. A good hook drives clicks, which drives distribution.
- Native content (text, documents, native video) outperforms external links every time.

### What performs consistently

- Specificity. "I worked with 14 B2B service firms" outperforms "many businesses."
- Personal experience. First-person stories with real stakes outperform general advice.
- Asking one question at the end that is narrow enough to answer in 1-2 sentences.
- Replies in the first hour. Engagement compounds - early replies signal activity.
- Polarizing positions that still respect the reader's intelligence.

### What tanks reach

- Generic advice that applies to everyone (applies to no one)
- Outbound links in the post body
- Posts that start with "I" as the very first word - rewrite to start with the point
- Long unbroken paragraphs on mobile
- Ending with "What do you think?" (too vague to invite a specific reply)
- Using the post as a sales pitch

## Formatting Rules for LinkedIn

- One blank line between every paragraph - mandatory for readability on mobile
- Maximum 3-4 sentences per paragraph; 1-2 is better
- Bold used sparingly - only for the one phrase that is the crux
- No links in the body - LinkedIn suppresses reach; put links in the first comment
- Write "2-3 sentences" not "two to three sentences" - numbers read faster in a feed
- Do not use em dashes or en dashes - hyphens only
- No emojis in the post body unless the writer specifically uses them in their voice
- Hashtags: 3-5 relevant ones at the end, or none. Never mid-post.

## Content Pillar Rotation (Weekly Cadence)

| Pillar | Frequency | Post Types That Fit |
|--------|-----------|---------------------|
| Founder/operator story | 2x/week | Story (Format C), Before State hook |
| Trust and transparency | 2x/week | Contrarian (Format A), Direct Claim hook |
| Proof and results | 1x/week | Numbered list (Format B), Specific Number hook |
| Industry insight | 1x/week | Short hook (Format D), Question-led (Format E) |
| Networking/observation | 1x/week | Scene drop hook, Short hook (Format D) |

Rotate through pillars to avoid pattern fatigue. The same format every day trains readers to skim.

---

## Posting Times (Platform Best Practice)

Best windows for B2B audiences:
- Tuesday, Wednesday, Thursday: 8-10am and 12-1pm in the audience's primary time zone
- Monday: avoid early morning; 10am-noon performs better than 7-8am
- Friday: afternoon posts underperform; post before noon or skip

Consistency beats perfect timing. A post at 9am every Tuesday trains your audience to look for you.

## Before Posting: The Scroll Test

Read your post as if you are scrolling through LinkedIn on your phone. Ask:
- Does the first line make me want to read the second line?
- Is every sentence earning its place?
- Would I share this if a colleague wrote it?
- Does the last line invite a specific, answerable response?

If not, rewrite the first line first. Everything else follows.
