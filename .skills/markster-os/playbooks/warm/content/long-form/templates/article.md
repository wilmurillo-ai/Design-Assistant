# Article Template - Long-Form B2B Content Guide

Use for LinkedIn articles, website posts, newsletters, and long-form guides (800-2,200 words).
This guide covers structure, hook types, section breakdowns with word counts, opening and closing
formulas, CTA options, SEO considerations, and what separates B2B long-form that performs from
long-form that gets ignored.

Variables:
- `[ICP_SITUATION]` - the specific situation your ICP faces
- `[COMMON_BELIEF]` - the thing most people in your category believe or do
- `[COUNTERPOINT]` - your actual position or insight
- `[EVIDENCE]` - experience, client data, or logical argument
- `[PRACTICAL_TAKEAWAY]` - the one thing the reader should do differently
- `[CTA]` - what you want the reader to do next

---

## Hook Types (Use One Per Article)

The hook is the first 50-100 words. It has one job: make the reader take the next step. Below are
five hook types that work in B2B long-form, with examples and when to use each.

### Hook Type 1: The Scene

Drop the reader into a specific moment. No context-setting. No "In today's competitive landscape."
Start inside the situation as if they are already living it.

When to use: When your ICP has a recurring moment of frustration or realization you can name
exactly. The more specific the scene, the wider the recognition.

Example:
"You have been in business for six years. You have nine clients. Eight of them came from referrals.
You have not done real marketing yet because the referrals kept coming. And now they are not. Not
dramatically - just slower. And you are realizing you do not actually know how to generate a meeting
with someone who has never heard of you."

Why it works: The reader thinks "how did they know that?" Recognition creates trust. Trust buys
attention.

---

### Hook Type 2: The Specific Number

Lead with a data point, time frame, or count that is precise enough to be credible and surprising
enough to create a question in the reader's head.

When to use: When you have a real statistic, internal metric, or observation from client work
that challenges a common assumption.

Example:
"83% of the B2B service firms we audited last year had the same problem: their best clients were
paying their oldest rates."

Why it works: Precision signals credibility. Vague claims ("many companies struggle with...")
get scrolled past. Specific numbers stop the scan.

---

### Hook Type 3: The Contrarian Statement

State something your ICP would push back on - but not dismiss. The tension between "that can't be
right" and "wait, is that right?" is what buys the next paragraph.

When to use: When you hold a position that contradicts common advice in your category, and you
have the argument to back it up.

Example:
"Hiring more salespeople will not fix your pipeline problem. It will make it worse."

Why it works: Disagreement is engagement fuel. The reader needs to know why you think that.
Keep the hook short. Save the argument for the body.

---

### Hook Type 4: The Before State

Describe the reader's current reality in precise, unflattering terms - without judgment. The goal
is recognition, not shame. They should feel seen, not criticized.

When to use: When you know the exact situation your ICP is trapped in and most of them do not
have a name for it yet. Naming it is the value.

Example:
"Most service business owners hit $500K and plateau. They are too busy to grow and too small to
delegate. They work more hours than their employees and make less per hour than they did three
years ago."

Why it works: Recognition is the first step to any sale. If the reader nods at the hook, they
will read the rest.

---

### Hook Type 5: The Direct Claim

State your core argument in the first sentence. No wind-up. Works when the claim is strong enough
to carry the piece and specific enough to mean something.

When to use: When you have earned credibility with the audience, or when the claim is so
specific that it signals deep expertise immediately.

Example:
"The reason your referral network is drying up is not because your network is getting smaller.
It is because your clients no longer have the language to describe what you do."

Why it works: Confidence signals authority. Vague openings signal writers who are not sure
what they are arguing. A tight claim up front tells the reader you know where this is going.

## Section Breakdown With Word Counts

For a standard B2B article (1,200-1,800 words), use this section structure. Word counts are targets,
not hard limits. Every section should earn its length.

### 1. Title (0 words in the body, but critical)

Title formulas that work:
- "Why [COMMON_BELIEF] is keeping [ICP type] stuck at [specific problem]"
- "[Number] things I have learned from [number] [ICP type] clients about [topic]"
- "The [specific thing] most [ICP type] get wrong about [topic]"
- "What changes when you [COUNTERPOINT]"
- "The [timeframe] problem: what actually happens when [common action]"

SEO note: front-load the primary keyword or phrase. Search engines weight the first 3-4 words of
a title more heavily. But write for the reader first - if the title is boring, no one clicks.

---

### 2. Opening Hook (80-120 words)

Pick one hook type from above. Apply it.

Rules:
- Do not open with a question. It signals the writer does not have a position yet.
- Do not open with "Most founders..." or "In today's landscape..."
- Do not start with context. Start inside the situation.
- First sentence should be under 15 words.

---

### 3. Setup - The Common Approach (100-200 words)

Describe what your ICP is currently doing, or what the conventional wisdom recommends. Be accurate
and fair. Do not strawman. Your reader may currently be doing this thing, and they need to feel you
understand it before you challenge it.

Structure:
- Name the approach
- Acknowledge why it made sense and why people do it
- One specific example of what it looks like in practice

---

### 4. The Problem With the Common Approach (150-250 words)

Explain specifically why it fails, stalls, or produces mediocre results. This is not a takedown -
it is a diagnostic. Reference patterns you have seen across clients or conversations.

Structure:
- The moment it breaks down (specific, not vague)
- What the failure actually looks like
- Why the conventional solution does not fix it

One specific data point, case study reference, or named pattern per section. "I have seen this
across 40+ clients" is stronger than "many businesses struggle with this."

---

### 5. The Counterpoint or New Angle (150-250 words)

Introduce your actual position. State it directly in the first sentence of this section. This is
the core insight. Everything else is build-up.

Structure:
- State the position directly
- Explain the reasoning in 2-3 sentences
- Provide one piece of evidence: a client situation (anonymized but specific), observed pattern,
  or logical argument that holds up to scrutiny

Avoid hedging. "In my experience, X tends to be true" is honest. "Some people might argue that X
could potentially be true in certain contexts" is noise.

---

### 6. Practical Breakdown (300-500 words)

This is the core of the article. Make the insight concrete and usable. Give the reader a framework,
a sequence of questions, or a step-by-step process they can apply this week.

Format options:
- Numbered steps (best for sequential processes)
- Decision framework: if X then Y (best for diagnostic content)
- Before/after comparison (best for mindset shifts)
- Three-part breakdown (best for concepts with distinct components)

Rules for this section:
- Every item in a list needs one sentence of elaboration minimum
- No item should be generic enough to apply to any industry or any business
- If you write a step, describe what done looks like
- Minimum one specific number, dollar figure, or time frame per 200 words

---

### 7. Closing (100-150 words)

Summarize the insight in 1-2 sentences - not a recap of every point, just the core argument
restated with more weight now that you have built the case. Then bridge to the reader's situation.

Formula:
"[Restate the core position]. For [ICP type], that means [one practical implication]. The question
is not whether to [do the thing]. It is [the real question they should be asking]."

Do not end with "I hope this was helpful" or "Thanks for reading." End on the argument.

---

### 8. CTA (40-80 words)

One action. Make it specific and easy. Match the CTA to where the reader is in the buying journey.

CTA options by intent:

Awareness stage (educate, not pitch):
"If this matches what you are seeing in your business, I write about this every week. Follow me
here for more."

Consideration stage (invite dialogue):
"If you are at this stage and want to think through your specific situation, reply and tell me
where you are stuck. I read every reply."

Conversion stage (direct offer):
"If you want to work through [PRACTICAL_TAKEAWAY] for your own company, [CALENDAR_LINK]. I do a
free 20-minute session on this."

Content gate:
"I wrote a fuller version of this in [LINKED_RESOURCE] - worth reading if you are in the early
stages of building this."

## Opening Formulas (Quick Reference)

| Formula | Example | Best For |
|---------|---------|----------|
| Scene drop | "You have been doing X for Y years. Now Z." | Recognition-based content |
| Specific number | "[stat] of the [ICP] we worked with had [problem]." | Data-led arguments |
| Contrarian claim | "[Common belief] is wrong." | Opinion/thought leadership |
| Before state | "Most [ICP type] hit [milestone] and plateau." | Diagnostic content |
| Direct claim | "The reason [X happens] is not [what they think]." | Expert positioning |

---

## Closing Formulas (Quick Reference)

| Formula | Example | Best For |
|---------|---------|----------|
| Restate + implication | "[Core argument]. For [ICP], that means [thing]." | Most articles |
| The real question | "The question is not [surface question]. It is [deeper one]." | Mindset-shift pieces |
| Pattern observation | "Every [ICP] I have seen solve this did [one thing] first." | Practical advice |
| Stakes statement | "The cost of not solving this is [specific outcome]." | Urgency content |

## SEO Considerations for B2B Long-Form

SEO should inform, not drive. Write for the reader first. Apply these rules after the draft is done.

Title and H1:
- Include the primary keyword phrase in the first 4 words if natural
- Use the exact phrase your ICP types into search, not your internal jargon
- Keep under 60 characters for full display in search results

Subheadings (H2/H3):
- At least one subheading per 300 words
- Use subheadings that describe what the section answers, not clever wordplay
- Include secondary keyword phrases in 1-2 subheadings naturally

Opening paragraph:
- Include the primary keyword in the first 100 words
- Write a sentence that directly answers the article's core question - search engines surface this
  as the meta description

Internal links:
- Link to 2-3 related pieces you have written
- Anchor text should describe the destination, not "click here"

Length guidelines:
- 1,200 words minimum for competitive B2B topics
- 1,800-2,200 words for pillar content you want to rank
- Depth beats length - 1,400 focused words outperform 2,400 padded ones

What does not matter:
- Keyword density percentages
- Exact-match anchor text in every instance
- Writing for crawlers - search engines read for topical authority now, not keyword stuffing

## What Makes B2B Long-Form Work vs. Fail

### What works

Specificity at every level. The ICP, the situation, the numbers, the outcome. Vague content is
forgettable content.

A real position. Articles that hedge everything ("it depends," "there are many approaches") give
the reader nothing to react to. Take a position. You can be wrong. You cannot be boring.

Earned structure. Every section exists because the argument requires it, not because articles
are "supposed to have" a certain number of sections.

One idea per article. The best B2B articles make one argument and make it well. If you find
yourself writing "and another thing..." you have a second article.

Short paragraphs. Maximum 4 sentences. Most should be 2-3. Online readers scan before they read,
and dense paragraphs signal effort.

### What fails

Opening with context instead of content. "In today's rapidly evolving business landscape..."
tells the reader nothing and signals a writer who is not confident in their argument.

Lists without explanation. A list of 7 things with one-sentence bullets is not an article - it is
a slide deck with line breaks.

Generic ICP language. "Business owners" and "entrepreneurs" are not ICPs. "Service business
owners at $500K-$2M revenue who built on referrals" is an ICP. Write for someone specific.

Motivational language. "Unlock your potential." "Transform your business." "Empower your team."
These phrases signal a lack of specificity. Replace with the actual thing you mean.

The hedge stack. "This might potentially be somewhat helpful in certain situations for some
readers." Every hedge is a confidence withdrawal. Make a claim. Own it.

Passive constructions that avoid accountability. "It has been found that..." Who found it?
"Results were seen..." By whom? Name the actor.

## Notes on Tone

- Write the way you talk. Contractions are fine. Formal academic structure is not.
- Do not hedge everything. Take a position.
- Short paragraphs. No paragraph longer than 4 sentences.
- If you are tempted to use "leverage," "synergy," "empower," or "unlock," replace with the actual
  thing you mean.
- Hyphens only - never en-dashes or em-dashes.
- No banned words: leverage, unlock, empower, game-changer, paradigm, optimize, innovative, synergy.
- Lead every section with a statement, not a question.
- Specific numbers in every section.
