# Long-Form Content

Long-form content for B2B audiences: articles, guides, case studies, manifestos, and pillar pages.

Long-form has two distinct jobs. The first is credibility: buyers who research before reaching out need to find something that demonstrates real depth. A LinkedIn post cannot do that. The second is SEO: website articles indexed by search engines generate inbound surface area that compounds over time.

Understanding which job a piece is doing before you write it determines the format, the length, and where it gets published.

---

## What counts as long-form

| Format | Length | Primary Job | Where it goes |
|--------|--------|-------------|---------------|
| Article | 600-1500 words | Credibility, topical authority | LinkedIn, website |
| Guide | 1500-3000 words | Reference material, SEO | Website, repurposed to email |
| Case study | 400-800 words | Social proof, sales enablement | Website, linked in outreach |
| Manifesto | 3000-6000 words | Thought leadership, category definition | Substack, Beehiiv, website |
| Pillar page | 3000-5000 words | SEO, authority hub | Website only |

---

## How long-form connects to the GTM system

Long-form sits at the top of the trust-building sequence. It is what buyers read after they have seen a short-form post and want to go deeper. It is what a sales prospect reads before a discovery call to decide if you are credible. It is what shows up in Google when someone searches the specific problem you solve.

In the GOD Engine framework, long-form content most directly serves the **Get** and **Own** bricks -- it generates inbound discovery (Get) and builds the authority asset library that makes warm prospects ready to convert (Own). Long-form is less useful for the **Deliver** brick but can support onboarding documentation and client education.

**The compounding argument:** Short-form generates presence. Long-form generates permanence. A LinkedIn post is visible for 24-48 hours. A well-optimized article or guide is visible for 2-5 years. Businesses that only write short-form are building a presence that decays without constant input. Long-form is how you build assets that work while you sleep.

## Content types in detail

### Articles (600-1500 words)

The workhorse format. One clear argument, one call to action.

Use articles when:
- You have a specific point of view on a common problem your ICP faces
- You want something linkable in cold outreach or follow-up sequences
- You are repurposing a short-form topic that generated engagement and deserves more depth

Articles do not need to be comprehensive. They need to be specific. A 700-word article that makes one argument perfectly outperforms a 1500-word article that meanders through four points.

### Guides (1500-3000 words)

Reference material. Designed to be saved, shared, and returned to.

Use guides when:
- The topic requires step-by-step explanation that cannot be compressed
- You want something that earns links or newsletter mentions
- You are targeting a search-intent keyword with real volume

Guides should be evergreen. Avoid tying guides to current events or tool-specific workflows that will be outdated in 12 months.

### Case studies (400-800 words)

The most underutilized and most underperforming format in B2B. Most case studies are written for the seller, not the buyer.

A case study that works answers three questions in the buyer's mind:
1. Was the situation similar to mine?
2. Is the result specific enough to be credible?
3. What did they actually do?

Structure: situation before - approach taken - specific outcome - lesson. Nothing else.

Anonymize if needed, but "an e-commerce logistics company in California" is not social proof. "David, who runs a 14-person HVAC operation in Sacramento" is. The specificity is the credibility.

### Manifestos (3000-6000 words)

Category-defining pieces. Used to stake a position on a topic at a level of depth no competitor has gone.

Manifestos are not appropriate for all businesses and not appropriate for all stages. Use them when:
- You have a genuinely differentiated perspective that requires length to make properly
- You have an audience that will read 4000 words (usually a warm subscriber base)
- You are willing to publish something that will polarize some readers -- a manifesto without a clear position is just a long article

The `/longform` skill handles manifesto production specifically.

### Pillar pages (3000-5000 words)

SEO-first format. The goal is to rank for a broad, high-value keyword by providing the most comprehensive treatment of a topic available.

Pillar pages link out to supporting "cluster content" (shorter articles on related subtopics). This internal linking structure signals topical authority to search engines.

Pillar pages require keyword research before writing. Publish only on your website -- not on LinkedIn, Medium, or Substack, where the SEO benefit is captured by the platform, not you.

## SEO vs. thought leadership: choosing the right purpose

Every long-form piece serves one of two masters.

**SEO-first:** You are writing to rank for a specific search query. The reader may not know you at all. The piece is structured around what they are searching for, not around your opinions.

**Thought leadership-first:** You are writing to deepen relationships with people who already know you exist. The piece is structured around your perspective and your proof.

The mistake is writing SEO content in a thought leadership voice (too opinionated and personal for cold discovery) or writing thought leadership content with SEO in mind (dilutes the voice, buries the argument).

Decide before you write. Both are valid. Mixing them produces neither.

## Writing process

**Step 1: Start with the insight**

Every long-form piece should have one core insight -- the thing you believe that most people in your category do not say out loud, or the thing you have learned from working with clients that is not obvious from the outside.

Write the insight in one sentence before you write anything else. If you cannot write the insight in one sentence, you do not know what you are trying to say yet.

**Step 2: Build the structure**

A reliable structure for B2B long-form:
- Open with the problem or situation (not a generic industry trend -- a specific scenario the reader recognizes)
- State what most people think or do about it
- Introduce the counterpoint or new angle
- Back it up with evidence (your experience, client data, or a logical argument)
- End with a practical takeaway or decision rule

**Step 3: Write for skimmers first**

Most readers will skim before they read. Use:
- Bold subheadings that communicate the structure
- Short paragraphs (3-4 sentences max)
- A clear opening paragraph that earns the scroll

**Step 4: Add one CTA**

Every piece should have one action you want the reader to take. Follow-up article, reply to you, book a call, or share with someone. One CTA, clearly stated.

## SEO guidance (for website articles and pillar pages)

If you are writing for SEO, include the target keyword in:
- The title
- The first 100 words
- One subheading
- The meta description (if your CMS supports it)

Do not stuff keywords. Write for the reader first. Search engines reward content that earns engagement.

**Internal linking:** When you publish a new article, link to it from 2-3 related existing pieces. When you write a pillar page, link to it from every related cluster article. These internal links pass authority between pages and signal topic clusters to search engines.

## Distribution for long-form

Long-form does not distribute itself. A well-written guide sitting on your website with no backlinks and no promotion gets zero readers.

**For SEO content:**
- Share on LinkedIn with a post that teases the insight (not "I wrote a guide" -- share the actual best point from the piece)
- Submit to newsletters in your niche that curate resources for your ICP
- Reference in cold outreach as proof of expertise ("I wrote a guide on this if useful: [link]")

**For thought leadership content:**
- Publish on LinkedIn natively for audience reach, then host the full version on Substack or your website
- Repurpose into 3-5 short-form posts. Each section of a long-form piece is a standalone post waiting to happen
- Send to your email list even if small -- subscribers who opted in are your warmest readers

---

## What "done" looks like

A long-form piece is done when:
- It has one clear core insight and the full piece serves that insight without exception
- A stranger can skim the subheadings and understand the argument
- There is a specific next step for the reader at the end
- For SEO pieces: the target keyword appears in the title, first 100 words, and at least one subheading
- It has been published and at least 2-3 people in your network have been pointed to it

A piece that is edited but unpublished is not done. Publishing is the job.

---

## Common mistakes

**Writing for yourself, not the reader.** "Here is everything I know about pricing" is not useful. "Here is the one pricing mistake that kills agencies under $3M" is.

**Length without depth.** Padding a 700-word article to 1500 words does not make it a guide. Length should come from the number of real insights, not from adding sentences.

**No distribution plan.** Publish, then promote. Long-form without promotion is a tree falling in an empty forest.

**Updating instead of creating.** Refresh evergreen content when facts change. Do not rewrite a piece that still ranks -- add to it.

**Case studies without numbers.** "We helped them grow" is not a case study. "Revenue per location increased from $340K to $490K in 7 months" is.
