> Part of Markster OS | GOD Engine Layer G5

## What G5 Is in the GOD Engine

The GOD Engine is a nine-brick growth architecture inside ScaleOS. G5 is the paid acquisition layer - the fifth brick. Its position is intentional.

G1 through G4 cover positioning, message, offer, and organic distribution. G5 only activates after those four bricks are structurally sound. Paid is not a growth engine - it is an amplifier. It takes an already-working message and places it in front of more qualified buyers faster.

If G1-G4 are not producing organic leads, pipeline, or referrals, paid spend will not fix that. It will accelerate the failure. Every dollar spent before organic has validated the message is diagnostic spend, not acquisition spend. That is the foundational operating principle of G5.

Paid ads answer one question: "Can we buy speed on a distribution channel that already works at a smaller scale?" If the answer to that question requires proof that does not yet exist, the correct action is to build that proof - not buy impressions.

## Readiness Checklist: Organic Signals Required Before Starting Paid

All seven gates must pass before any dollar of paid budget is committed. No exceptions.

| Gate | Requirement |
|------|-------------|
| ICP proof | 3+ closed clients from one defined segment via non-paid channels |
| Cold message converts | Website or outbound converts cold contacts at 2%+ (not warm network traffic) |
| Unit economics known | LTV:CAC ratio 3:1 or higher, CAC payback under 6 months, from real organic data |
| Offer sales trusts | At least one low-friction CTA (audit, assessment, demo) that has already closed deals |
| Content proof point | 1-2 pieces of content with documented organic engagement or response |
| Tracking live | UTMs, conversion events, and CRM routing configured and tested before any spend |
| Sustainable budget | $1,500/month minimum, fundable for 3 months without pressure to hit revenue |

If your sales team regularly disqualifies leads your marketing sends, the ICP definition needs tightening - not the channel mix. Fix the signal before buying the reach.

## Channel Selection Guide: Google vs LinkedIn vs Meta

The decision depends on budget and deal size - not on where buyers spend time.

### Budget Decision Tree

```
Monthly ad budget?
- Under $1,500   -> DO NOT RUN ADS
                    Fix ICP, message, and page first. Return when budget is real.
- $1,500-$3,000  -> GOOGLE ADS ONLY
                    Long-tail intent keywords + dedicated landing page
- $3,000-$5,000  -> GOOGLE (80%) + META RETARGETING (20%)
                    Google for demand capture, Meta for warming clicked traffic
- $5,000+        -> ALL THREE CHANNELS
                    Google: demand capture
                    LinkedIn: ABM + high-ticket demand generation
                    Meta: retargeting, nurture, lower-AOV audiences
```

### Platform Benchmarks (B2B, 2025-2026)

| Platform | B2B ROAS | Avg CPC | Minimum Monthly Budget | Time to Signal |
|----------|----------|---------|------------------------|----------------|
| Google Ads | 78% | $2.52-$6.00 | $1,500-$2,000 | Days to 2 weeks |
| LinkedIn Ads | 113% | $5.26-$10.00+ | $3,000-$5,000 | 3-4 months |
| Meta Ads | 29% | $1.54 (cold) | $500-$1,000 (retargeting only) | 1-2 weeks |

### Decision Criteria by Deal Size

- Deal value above $10,000: LinkedIn delivers better ROI despite higher CPC. Lead quality and enterprise targeting (6.8 stakeholders per B2B purchase) justify the cost.
- Deal value under $5,000: Meta on volume wins. Cheaper leads compensate for lower intent.
- SMB-targeted services: Google works best. Small business owners search actively and decide quickly.
- Under $3,000/month and LinkedIn only: this is a donation, not a test. LinkedIn spreads too thin below that threshold to generate meaningful data.

## Minimum Viable Paid Test

**Scope:** One channel, one offer, one ICP segment, 30 days, $1,000-$1,500 maximum.

The first $1,500 is learning budget, not revenue budget. Treat it that way.

### The $100 Audience Validation Method (Run Before Any Full Campaign)

Run $100 tests to 3-5 distinct ICP audience segments. Measure CTR, time on site, and bounce rate only - not conversions. Identify which audience is most engaged before building a landing page or committing full budget. This costs $300-$500 and prevents thousands in misdirected spend.

```
Have you run $100/audience across 3 segments?
  No  -> Do that first before committing full budget
  Yes -> Did one segment show 2x+ engagement vs. the others?
    No  -> Redefine ICP, test new segments
    Yes -> Build a dedicated landing page for that segment,
           allocate $1,000-$1,500/month for 30 days
```

### What You Are Testing in Month 1

Not leads. Not revenue. You are testing three things:
1. Does this audience respond to this message at all? (CTR)
2. Does the offer clear a basic friction threshold? (form fills, calls booked)
3. Is the channel capable of reaching this segment affordably? (CPC vs benchmark)

A successful month 1 test produces 3-5 form fills or calls booked and directional signal on all three questions above. That is a pass, not a failure.

## Landing Page Requirements Checklist

Fix the page before buying the clicks. PPC campaigns with dedicated landing pages convert 65% better than campaigns pointing to a homepage.

### Hard Requirements (Non-Negotiable)

- [ ] Headline mirrors the exact ad promise word-for-word (message match)
- [ ] Single CTA above the fold - one action only, no competing paths
- [ ] CTA copy describes what happens next ("Book a 20-minute demo," not "Learn More")
- [ ] Social proof matches the target segment - same industry, same company size
- [ ] No site navigation menu - navigation is attention leakage on a paid landing page
- [ ] Page loads in under 3 seconds with all scripts active (not design mockup speed)
- [ ] Mobile layout tested on a real iPhone Safari and Android Chrome device
- [ ] Conversion tracking confirmed: CTA clicks, form starts, form submits, thank-you page

### Conversion Sequence (Page Structure)

The page must flow in this order:

1. Hero - headline (match the ad), subhead (who this is for, what changes), primary CTA
2. Proof strip - 3-5 logos or trust markers
3. Problem/stakes - what is broken, expensive, or slow in their world today
4. Solution snapshot - what changes in practical terms (not a feature list)
5. How it works - 3 steps or a workflow summary
6. Outcome proof - testimonials with specific numbers, screenshots, use cases
7. Objection handling - setup time, pricing model, fit concerns
8. CTA repeat - ask again once the visitor has built enough confidence to act

### Technical QA Before Launch

- [ ] CRM routing tested: leads route to the correct pipeline stage
- [ ] UTM parameters pass through to CRM (required for attribution)
- [ ] Hidden fields populate correctly
- [ ] Form submits tested end-to-end, not just visually

## The 90-Day Paid Ramp

Calibrated for B2B service firms with $500-$3,000/month budgets starting from zero paid history.

### Days 1-30: Foundation and Setup

**Budget: $0-$1,000 (diagnostic test spend only, no real campaigns yet)**

- Week 1: ICP validation and message freeze. Write the core value proposition in one sentence: pain, outcome, one proof point. If the team cannot agree on this in 90 minutes, ads are not the bottleneck.
- Weeks 1-2: Build one landing page, one offer, one CTA. Install conversion tracking and test every form, routing rule, and UTM before going live.
- Weeks 2-3: Run $100 MVT audience tests on 2-3 keyword themes (Google) or 2-3 audience segments (LinkedIn). Diagnostic only - do not optimize for conversions yet.
- Week 4: Analyze results. Pick one channel, one audience, one offer. Write 3 ad variants. Set month 2 budget.

**Month 1 milestone:** Tracking live, landing page confirmed converting on organic traffic (30-50 non-paid visitors tested), one channel selected with evidence.

### Days 31-60: First Real Spend

**Budget: $1,000-$1,500**

- Launch one campaign: one ad group (Google) or one campaign (LinkedIn/Meta), 3 ad variants, one dedicated landing page.
- Days 1-7: Check CTR and CPC only. Do not make creative changes in the first 7 days - the algorithm is in its learning phase and changes reset it.
- Day 14: First real review. CTR above 1%? CPC within 20% of benchmark? If both yes, let it run. If CTR is below 0.5%, pause the lowest-performing ad and write a new angle.
- Day 30: Full-funnel review. Leads generated, calls booked, conversion rate. Calculate preliminary cost per SQL.

**Month 2 milestone:** At least 10 clicks/day (Google) or 50 impressions/day (LinkedIn). At least 3-5 form fills or calls booked from paid. Direction visible even if economics are not yet clean.

### Days 61-90: Optimize or Pivot

**Budget: $1,500-$2,000**

- If cost per SQL is within 2x your target: expand to 2-3 new ad variants, test a second audience segment, increase daily budget by 20-30%. Do not double budget in one move.
- If cost per SQL is above 2x target after 60 days: run the stop/optimize diagnostic (see below). The problem is almost always one of three things - wrong ICP (traffic but no conversions), wrong offer (clicks but no form fills), or wrong channel (low CTR across everything).
- Day 90: Declare a verdict. Profitable (double down), data-negative (rethink ICP and offer), or channel mismatch (switch platforms). No ambiguous conclusions.

**Month 3 milestone:** Clear directional CAC estimate. A committed decision on whether to scale, optimize, or pivot channel.

## How to Measure ROI with Long B2B Sales Cycles

Stop measuring cost per lead. Measure cost per SQL, pipeline influenced, and CAC payback period.

### Attribution Model by Sales Cycle Length

| Sales Cycle | Recommended Model | Logic |
|-------------|-------------------|-------|
| Under 30 days | Last-touch or U-shaped | Recent interactions dominate conversion |
| 30-90 days | U-shaped (40/20/40) | First touch and close touch equally important |
| 90-180 days | W-shaped (30/30/30/10) | First contact, lead qualification, and last contact each get 30% credit |
| 180+ days | Custom / time-decay | Recency weighting across all touchpoints |

For most B2B service firms with $2,000-$10,000 deal sizes and 60-90 day cycles, W-shaped attribution is the correct default.

### The 4 Metrics That Replace Cost Per Lead

1. **Cost per SQL** - Connect ad platform data to CRM to see which campaigns produce leads that actually enter the pipeline, not just fill a form.
2. **Pipeline Influenced** - Total deal value of opportunities that had at least one paid touchpoint. Formula: Pipeline ROI = [(Total Pipeline Influenced - Marketing Investment) / Marketing Investment] x 100.
3. **CAC Payback Period** - Target 6-12 months for sustainable growth. If payback exceeds your runway, you cannot afford paid acquisition at current deal economics.
4. **LTV:CAC Ratio** - Minimum 3:1 to justify ongoing spend.

### Attribution Window Rule

Your attribution window must match your sales cycle. If average time from first touch to close is 6 months, a 30-day attribution window misses 80% of the journey and will systematically undervalue all top-of-funnel paid activity. Set the window to at least 1.5x your average sales cycle length.

### Practical Tracking Stack

- UTM parameters on every ad (Source / Medium / Campaign / Content)
- UTM data captured in CRM at lead creation
- Deal source and first-touch recorded in CRM at opportunity creation
- Monthly report: Leads by source, SQLs by source, Revenue by source
- Review cadence: Weekly (leading indicators), Monthly (pipeline influence), Quarterly (CAC payback)

## When to Stop vs Optimize a Paid Experiment

Set stop and scale criteria before the campaign goes live. Do not make these decisions reactively.

### Pre-Launch: Define These Four Numbers

- **Target CPA** - cost per SQL or cost per call booked, derived from unit economics
- **Kill threshold** - CPA at 2x target after the learning phase (7-14 days)
- **Scale trigger** - CPA at or below target for 7+ consecutive days
- **Time limit** - 60 days maximum per experiment before forcing a verdict

### Decision Matrix

| Condition | Action |
|-----------|--------|
| CTR below 0.5% after 7 days | Kill that ad creative, write a new hook. The offer and channel may be fine. |
| CTR above 1%, CPC in range, zero form fills | Landing page problem. Pause ads, fix page, relaunch. |
| CPA at 1.5-2x target after 30 days | Optimize: test 2 new creative angles, tighten audience |
| CPA above 2x target after 60 days + 3 creative variants tested | Kill the campaign. Wrong channel or wrong offer. |
| CPA on target, declining week-over-week | Scale: increase budget 20-30% every 7 days |
| CPA stable and profitable for 14+ days | Scale more aggressively (40-50% budget increases) |

### Optimization Sequence (What to Change First)

When performance is below target, test in this exact order:

1. Ad creative (hook, first sentence, format) - most common source of underperformance
2. Audience targeting (tighten ICP, add negative audiences)
3. Landing page (headline, CTA, proof - not design changes)
4. Offer (lower commitment CTA: assessment instead of demo, calculator instead of call)
5. Channel (only if all four above have been tested and failed)

If every experiment fails at the kill threshold, the problem is not the ads. It is the offer, ICP, or positioning. Return to organic channels to re-validate the offer before spending again.

## Common Failure Modes

These are the seven structural failures, ranked by frequency and cost.

**1. Starting before messaging is locked.**
Ads distribute a message. If the message is wrong or unclear, ads scale the failure. Every campaign launched before positioning is clear is a tax on poor process.

**2. Choosing the wrong channel for the budget.**
LinkedIn at $1,000/month is not a test - it is a donation. The pattern: operator reads that LinkedIn is "the B2B platform," spends $800 over 3 weeks, gets 2 clicks, declares paid ads do not work. Budget threshold for LinkedIn is $3,000/month minimum.

**3. Sending paid traffic to the homepage.**
A homepage is built for general visitors. A landing page is a conversion engine. This single mistake accounts for a large share of wasted B2B paid budgets.

**4. No conversion tracking before launch.**
Launching without accurate conversion tracking means optimizing for the wrong metrics. The fix is not expensive - it is simply not prioritized until it is too late.

**5. Killing campaigns too early.**
Spending $500, seeing "disappointing" results, and stopping before the algorithm has data. The platform needs 7-14 days minimum to exit the learning phase. Google needs 30 days minimum on any new campaign. Cutting at 1.2x your target CPA is operational anxiety, not data-driven decision-making.

**6. Expecting 30-day revenue from long sales cycles.**
If your product takes 3 months to close, evaluating a paid campaign on a 7-day window guarantees false negatives. Attribution periods must be set to at least 1.5x your average sales cycle.

**7. Writing ads that try to speak to everyone.**
Ads built to appeal to everyone cause the algorithm to find an audience of nobody. The most specific, polarizing opening line - one that attracts a precise buyer and repels everyone else - produces lower CPL and better lead quality than a broad, benefit-heavy headline.
