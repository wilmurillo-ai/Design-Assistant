# B2B Cold Email Sequence Template

7-touch sequence over 25 days. Each email is independent -- never reference previous attempts. The prospect encounters each message in a different context and mental state.

Word count: 50-75 words ideal. 100 maximum. 30 minimum. Under 50 words for mobile-first industries.

Read aloud before scheduling. If it sounds like a template, it still is.

---

## Variables

- `[COMPANY]` -- prospect company name
- `[ICP_TYPE]` -- their business category (accounting firm, IT firm, consulting firm, etc.)
- `[CITY]` -- city or metro area (the Neighbor Effect: local reference lowers defenses)
- `[PAIN_OBSERVATION]` -- one specific thing you observed about their situation
- `[DATA_POINT]` -- a specific number relevant to their situation (cost, ratio, benchmark)
- `[PROOF_DETAIL]` -- a brief result from a client in a similar situation (company type + outcome + timeframe)
- `[YOUR_NAME]` -- your name

---

## Tone: Read Your Buyer's Title Before Writing

The job title determines the entire tonal strategy.

**Founder Mode** (Founder, Owner, CEO of smaller firm, Partner)
- Psychology: emotionally fused with the business. Cares about survival, vision, existential threats.
- Tone: direct, blunt, visceral. High-level strategic. No corporate speak.
- Triggers: "stop bleeding cash," "fix the bottleneck," "before you scale this mistake"

**Manager Mode** (VP, Director, Head of, Manager)
- Psychology: hired to execute a function. Cares about KPIs, career safety, budget adherence, looking good to their boss.
- Tone: professional, safety-oriented, metrics-focused.
- Triggers: "streamline the workflow," "reduce compliance risk," "gives you the data to make the case"

Writing a Founder Mode email to a Manager feels reckless. Writing a Manager Mode email to a Founder feels bureaucratic. Check the title first.

## Subject Line Rules

| Rule | Why |
|------|-----|
| 1-3 words, all lowercase | Mimics internal email. Bypasses the "marketing blast" schema. |
| Never include the prospect's name | Looks automated. |
| Never include your company name | Sounds like an ad. |
| Avoid promotional words (boost, growth, save, free) | AI spam filters are trained on these. |

**Subject line frameworks:**

| Type | Example | Psychological trigger |
|------|---------|----------------------|
| Neighbor | "chicago question" | In-group bias (local = trusted) |
| Peer | "idea for [company]" | Mimics a colleague |
| Curiosity | "question about your role" | Zeigarnik effect (must resolve) |
| Internal | "pipeline" / "revenue" / "retention" | Internal camouflage -- reads like a colleague |
| Anti-subject | "intro" / "update" / "hello" | Teflon against spam filters -- all pressure on first line |

Lowercase frequently outperforms title case in practitioner cold-email testing. Validate this with your own list before treating it as a rule.

## Email Construction

Four lines. One idea. No more.

```
Line 1 -- The Hook: specific observation only they would recognize
Line 2 -- The Bridge: quantify the problem connection
Line 3 -- The Proof: specific peer company + specific metric. Never "companies like yours."
Line 4 -- The CTA: one question. Context-driven (see matrix below).
Line 5 -- PS (optional): personal observation that didn't fit above. +35% reply rate.
```

---

## CTA Selection Matrix

Match the CTA to your research depth and where they are in the sequence.

**When you have strong research:**
- "Worth exploring?"
- "Make sense to discuss?"
- "Open to seeing how [peer company] did it?"

**When research is limited:**
- "Is this even a priority?"
- "Am I off base?"
- "Relevant or not?"

**When they've opened multiple times or clicked a link (engagement signal):**
- "Tuesday or Thursday?"
- "15 minutes this week?"
- "Should we talk?"

**When breaking up:**
- "Should I stop reaching out?"
- "Priority or not?"
- "Yes or no?"

**Value-first CTA (any touch):**
- "Want the framework?"
- "Should I send the case study?"
- "Interested in the data?"

The CTA is the last line. Nothing after it. Text after the CTA adds friction.

## E1 -- Day 0: Research-Heavy Opener

**Subject:** [city] question

**Body (Founder Mode):**

[COMPANY] keeps coming up when I look at [ICP_TYPE]s in [CITY].

Most firms your size are hitting the same wall -- [PAIN_OBSERVATION]. [DATA_POINT] of that is usually invisible until it compounds.

[PROOF_DETAIL: "One [ICP_TYPE] I worked with fixed this in 90 days and added [outcome]."]

Worth a look?

[YOUR_NAME]

**Body (Manager Mode):**

Came across [COMPANY] while working with a few [ICP_TYPE]s in [CITY] on [PAIN_AREA].

Most [role titles] I talk to are dealing with [PAIN_OBSERVATION]. The gap is usually [specific process or metric].

[PROOF_DETAIL: "We helped [company type] get [specific metric] in [timeframe]."]

Relevant to what you're working on this quarter?

[YOUR_NAME]

**Notes:**
- First line must be specific to them. The "Neighbor Effect" works because it signals you're not blasting everyone.
- Inference hook: use industry + title to infer their likely pain. "Most [ICP_TYPE]s in [CITY]" is a probabilistic cold read. If you're right, instant authority. If slightly off, they correct you -- that's still a reply.
- No company name, no offer, no pitch in the CTA.

---

## E2 -- Day 3-4: Completely Different Angle

**Subject:** re: [city] question

**Body:**

Different angle --

[A new pain point unrelated to E1. Efficiency, competitive, or risk. Never reference E1.]

[One observation or data point on this new angle.]

[PROOF_DETAIL if not used in E1, or a different result from the same client.]

[Short CTA: "Is this on your radar?" or "Relevant?" or "Am I off base?"]

[YOUR_NAME]

**Notes:**
- Angle shift is mandatory. Repeating the same pain = template pattern = deleted.
- Three progression options: Efficiency (E1) -> Competitive (E2) -> Risk (E3). Or: Problem (E1) -> Cost of delay (E2) -> Social proof (E3).
- Keep it shorter than E1. Under 60 words.

---

## E3 -- Day 7-8: Pure Value, No Ask

**Subject:** [relevant industry term or benchmark]

**Body:**

[A specific insight, framework, or piece of data they can use -- even if they never reply.]

[One sentence connecting it to their situation.]

[Soft value CTA: "Want me to send the full breakdown?" or "Should I send the case study?"]

[YOUR_NAME]

**Notes:**
- This email earns reciprocity. You gave something. The ask at the end is light.
- The insight must be genuinely useful, not a teaser for your product. Givers gain.
- This is the highest-leverage email for enterprise buyers and long-consideration deals.

---

## E4 -- Day 12: Case Study or Competitive Insight

**Subject:** [industry term] / [specific metric]

**Body:**

[Company type similar to theirs] was dealing with [pain you surfaced in E1 or E2].

They went from [before state] to [after state] in [timeframe]. The one thing that changed: [mechanism in one sentence].

[Direct tie to their company/situation.]

[CTA: "Open to seeing the breakdown?"]

[YOUR_NAME]

**Notes:**
- Name the peer company if you can. Specific > vague. "A Chicago accounting firm" is better than "one of our clients." A named firm is better still.
- The mechanism (how it worked) is what earns credibility. Don't just state the result.
- Match the peer company to their company type, size, and problem as closely as possible.

---

## E5 -- Day 17: Cost of Inaction

**Subject:** [metric] / [timeframe]

**Body:**

Quick math --

[The cost of not solving [PAIN_OBSERVATION] over 6-12 months: lost revenue, wasted spend, or compound problem.]

[What changes if they fix it. One number or outcome.]

[CTA: "Worth a conversation?" or "Does this math land?"]

[YOUR_NAME]

**Notes:**
- Loss aversion framing outperforms benefit framing in high-ticket B2B. Show what they are actively losing, not just what they could gain.
- The math must be real. Use their industry benchmarks, not invented numbers.
- This touch works best for deals above $10K where the cost of inaction is material.

---

## E6 -- Day 21: Authority or Insight Position

**Subject:** [trend or industry term]

**Body:**

[A point of view on their industry -- a trend, a shift, something they probably haven't heard framed this way.]

[Connect it to the problem you've been circling. One sentence.]

[Soft open: "Curious if you're seeing the same thing." or "Worth a quick conversation?"]

[YOUR_NAME]

**Notes:**
- Founder Mode buyers respond to conviction. Manager Mode buyers respond to safety and evidence.
- This is not another pitch. It's a peer-level industry observation. The ask is soft.
- If they have engaged with any previous email (opened multiple times, clicked), swap the CTA for a direct meeting ask: "Tuesday or Thursday?"

---

## E7 -- Day 25: Professional Breakup

**Subject:** re: [original subject]

**Body:**

Last one from me --

I've reached out a few times and haven't heard back. Either the timing is wrong, or this isn't relevant to where you are right now.

Either way, I'll stop here. If pipeline ever becomes a pressing problem, you know where to find me.

[YOUR_NAME]

**Notes:**
- Under 50 words. The brevity is the point.
- Do not apologize. "Last one from me" is sufficient.
- Do not add a Calendly link or ask for anything. This is a clean exit.
- Breakup emails often generate replies because they remove all pressure and give a face-saving exit.
- "You know where to find me" signals organization and professionalism -- not desperation.

## Follow-Up Strategy Options

Choose one approach per sequence. Do not mix within a sequence.

**Approach 1: Angle Shift**
Each email attacks a completely different pain point. Never mention previous emails.
- E1: Efficiency angle
- E2: Competitive angle
- E3: Risk angle
- E4+: Cost, social proof, inaction

**Approach 2: Value Stack**
Each email adds new insight or resource, building reciprocity.
- E1: Problem + peer proof
- E2: Industry data relevant to them
- E3: Specific framework or tool (genuinely useful)
- E4+: Case study, cost of inaction, breakup

**Approach 3: Pattern Interrupt**
When standard sequences aren't working on a high-value target.
- "This is email 4. Clearly missing something. What is it?"
- "I've been wrong before. Is [problem] not actually an issue for you?"
- "Quick honesty check -- am I completely off-base here?"

## Sequence Rules

| Rule | Requirement |
|------|------------|
| Each email is independent | Never reference previous emails. They encounter each in a different mental state. |
| Subject lines | 1-3 words, lowercase. Never promotional language. |
| Word count | 50-75 words ideal. 100 maximum. |
| Plain text only | HTML formatting signals mass email to spam filters and recipients. |
| Soft CTA | Permission to decline increases reply rate over pressure. |
| One idea per email | Multiple points dilute focus. |
| PS line | Use whenever there's a personal observation that didn't fit. +35% reply rate. |
| Colleague test | Read aloud. If it sounds like a template, it still is. |

---

## What Does Not Belong in Cold Email

- Any internal system names or jargon the prospect would not recognize
- Specific pricing before discovery
- Multiple asks in one email
- "I'd love to connect" or "Let's hop on a call" (passive and imprecise)
- HTML formatting, logos, or tracked images
- Fake personalization ("I've been following your company closely")
- "Hope this finds you well" / "My name is X and I'm from Y"
- "Run something by you" (signals pitch incoming)
- "Just following up" (creates resistance)
- Calendly link before any prior engagement
- Over-personalization (5+ specific references feels invasive -- 2-3 is the sweet spot)

## Benchmarks

| Metric | Target | Action if below |
|--------|--------|----------------|
| Open rate | 30%+ | Deliverability problem. Check SPF/DKIM/DMARC. Check warmup. |
| Reply rate | 2-5% | Message problem. Rewrite E1 hook. Check subject line. |
| Meeting rate | 0.25-0.5% | CTA or offer problem. Is the ask too big? |

If open rate is good but reply rate is low: the message is the problem.
If open rate is low: the infrastructure or subject line is the problem.
Fix one variable at a time. Wait for 100 sends before evaluating.
