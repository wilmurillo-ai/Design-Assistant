# Brief Playbook: Rules for Creating Segment Briefs

Read this BEFORE starting any new outbound segment brief.

---

## Why This Exists

The first brief in any new segment typically requires multiple rounds of corrections. Every mistake below has been made in production. This playbook exists so those mistakes are not repeated.

---

## The 12 Rules (non-negotiable)

### Rule 1: Every pain point must map to what you deliver

Research is fuel, not strategy. If a pain point does not connect to something you can actually deliver for the prospect, it does not belong in the brief. Ask for every pain point: "What do we deliver that solves this?" If the answer is vague or indirect, cut it or reframe it.

**Test:** For each pain point, write one sentence starting with "What we deliver:" If you cannot, the pain point does not belong.

### Rule 2: The offering is broader than just growth

Prospects want to work less AND grow. Automation is part of the offer -- not just client acquisition. Every segment brief must address both:
- Growth (outbound, content, pipeline, new clients)
- Automation (onboarding, client communication, operational sequences, upsell)

The sprint leads with growth. The retainer expands into automation. Both must be in the brief.

### Rule 3: Downsell chain is mandatory

Never pitch a high-ticket offer cold without a downsell ladder. Conservative buyers especially need lower-commitment entry points. Every brief must include:
- Free diagnostic (primary CTA)
- Free interactive scorecard (for those who will not book a call)
- Low-cost tier (a deliverable they can use themselves)
- Mid-tier (a done-for-you mini-project -- live and running when delivered)
- Lite retainer (for those not ready for the full engagement)

### Rule 4: Know the industry calendar

Every industry has a busy season, slow season, and buying season. The brief must acknowledge this:
- When is their crunch time?
- Does it make sense to email during crunch or after?
- The campaign should not be built around the season -- just acknowledge it.
- Default CTA is always "let's talk now" unless you decide otherwise.

### Rule 5: Revenue now, not later

Never default to "let's schedule for later." The default CTA is "let's talk now." If the prospect says they are too busy, then offer to schedule later. That is reply handling, not the outbound pitch.

### Rule 6: Use universal titles

Not every industry uses the same titles. Do not assume "Managing Partner" or "CEO" or "Founder." Use "owner" as the default -- it is universal across solo practitioners, small firms, and partnerships. Only use specific titles when the context clearly calls for it.

### Rule 7: Do not name specific tools in the offer

Do not say "HubSpot pipeline setup" or brand-name outreach tools. Say "CRM" or "pipeline tracking" or "outbound system." The prospect might use different tools, and naming yours creates confusion. Determine their stack during the diagnostic.

### Rule 8: Downsell offers must be clearly different

Names, scope, and value must be obviously distinct. If two offers sound similar, rename them.

**Test:** If someone reads both names, can they immediately tell the difference?

**Pattern that works:**
- Lower tier = ingredients (data, templates, guides). They execute.
- Higher tier = running system (built, configured, live). They just respond to results.

Name them differently: "Pack" vs "Sprint," "Kit" vs "Install," "Guide" vs "Build."

### Rule 9: The real difference between offers must be obvious

Do not just vary the price. Vary what the prospect has to do:
- Low tier: they get materials and do the work themselves
- Mid tier: they get a working system and just handle responses
- Full engagement: you do everything

This effort gradient must be clear in the brief.

### Rule 10: Each email needs a different reason to reply

NOT the same CTA rephrased multiple times. Each email should have a genuinely different ask:
- E1: soft question (no offer)
- E2: different asset or insight (scorecard, observation)
- E3: first diagnostic mention (earned by now)
- E4: conversation starter (not a pitch)
- E5: breakup (last offer)

The diagnostic appears a maximum of 2 times. Any single phrase or CTA appears a maximum of 2 times. The copywriter owns the exact language -- the brief sets guardrails, not scripts.

### Rule 11: All copy must be in the prospect's language

The brief is internal strategy. The emails must sound like one business owner talking to another in that industry. Every industry has its own vocabulary:
- CPAs: "practice growth," "client base," "advisory," "engagement"
- Lawyers: "practice development," "matter," "billable"
- Trades: "jobs," "customers," "shop" (not "deals," "clients," "office")

**Test:** "Would a [CPA/lawyer/HVAC owner] forward this to their partner?" If not, the language is wrong.

**Anti-patterns:** No "pipeline," "net-new," "GTM," "ICP," "orchestration," "growth hacking," "funnel," or any marketing jargon. Ever. In any prospect-facing email.

### Rule 12: Include the case study

If you have a case study in the same industry or a closely related one, it MUST be prominently featured -- not buried in a one-line proof point. Include:
- Full metrics (before/after)
- The testimonial quote if available
- Public URL if available
- Which pain point each metric supports
- Usage rules (which emails, how to frame it, what NOT to do)

For segments where you do not have a direct case study, map the closest one and explain the bridge. Example: a CPA case study and a financial advisor share the same structural problem -- referral-dependent, owner doing everything, no system.

## Brief Creation Checklist

Before presenting any segment brief, verify:

- [ ] Every pain point has a "What we deliver:" line
- [ ] Both growth AND automation are addressed
- [ ] Downsell chain is included with clear distinctions
- [ ] Industry calendar is acknowledged (but does not dominate)
- [ ] Default CTA is "let's talk now"
- [ ] Titles are universal/correct for the industry
- [ ] No specific tool names in offers (say "CRM" not a brand name)
- [ ] Downsell names are clearly different from each other
- [ ] Effort gradient is obvious (DIY vs done-for-you vs full service)
- [ ] Each email has a different reason to reply
- [ ] All prospect-facing language is industry-native, zero marketing jargon
- [ ] Case study is featured with full metrics and usage rules
- [ ] Psychographic profile includes: fear, desire, mode, keywords, hook angles, tone, what NOT to say
- [ ] Research data has specific numbers and sources (not generic claims)
- [ ] Busy season timing is addressed in a dedicated section

---

## How to Use This Playbook

1. **Before starting a new segment brief:** Read this entire file.
2. **At offering-design stage:** Decide word count cap (default 110 words per email) and confirm CTA tone for this segment.
3. **While writing the brief:** Use the checklist above.
4. **Before presenting:** Run through all 12 rules and verify compliance.
5. **After E1 is approved:** Create a segment-specific Style Bible by copying the master Style Bible and filling in segment-specific fields (case study line, metrics, data sources, forbidden words, CTA names).
6. **For E2-E5:** Use the Style Bible structure and only swap the pain point, data source, and CTA. Do not start from scratch.

---

## Segment-Specific Adaptations

Each segment needs its own:
- Pain points (mapped to offer)
- Psychographic profile
- Industry vocabulary
- Calendar awareness
- Case study mapping (direct or bridged)
- Downsell naming (industry-appropriate)

The STRUCTURE and RULES are identical across all segments. This playbook is the constant.
