# Discovery Call Script

A structured 45-minute framework for qualifying prospects and surfacing the real problem before presenting any solution.

This is a conversation guide, not a word-for-word script. Keep it on a second screen during the call. Use the questions to stay on track, not to read aloud.

---

## Before the Call (10 minutes of prep)

- [ ] Research the company: what they sell, to whom, how they currently get clients
- [ ] Research the contact: their background, how long they have been in the role, what they have said publicly
- [ ] Identify your qualifying criteria from F1: what must be true for this to be a good fit?
- [ ] Know your red lines: what would make you decline this engagement?
- [ ] Pull any prior interaction context: what email did they respond to, what language did they use in their reply?

---

## Opening Frame (60 seconds)

Set the agenda before they do. Most calls drift into a pitch immediately. Take control early.

> "Thanks for making time. Before I tell you anything about what we do -- I want to understand your situation first. I have about six questions. At some point I'll share what we do and whether it applies to your situation. Does that work?"

Then: "What made you agree to this call? What were you hoping to get out of it?"

Their answer tells you their awareness level and urgency. A vague answer ("just curious") means they are early stage. A specific answer ("I lost a key client last month") means urgency is real.

---

## Core Discovery Questions

Work through these in order. Do not rush. Let them talk -- the longer they talk, the more you learn and the more they commit to the problem.

**Q1 -- Current state:**
> "Walk me through what client acquisition looks like for you right now. Where does new business actually come from?"

Follow-up probe: "Is that intentional, or did it just evolve that way?"

What to listen for: referral dependency, single sources of pipeline, owner-driven sales with no system behind it.

---

**Q2 -- What is working:**
> "What is working well about how you currently get clients? What would you not want to change?"

Follow-up probe: "How long has that been the primary driver?"

What to listen for: their anchor. Understanding what they trust helps you frame what you offer without threatening it.

---

**Q3 -- What is not working:**
> "What is not working as well as you would like? Where does the process break down?"

Follow-up probe: "When was the last time that felt like a real problem?"

What to listen for: specific moments of pain -- a slow quarter, a lost client, a hire that did not work out, a campaign that failed. Specifics matter more than generalizations.

---

**Q4 -- History of attempts:**
> "What have you tried before to fix that? What happened?"

Follow-up probe: "Why did it not stick?"

What to listen for: vendor burns, failed hires, abandoned tools. This is critical -- it tells you what objections are coming and what trust you need to rebuild.

---

**Q5 -- Cost of inaction:**
> "If nothing changes over the next 12 months, what does that look like for you? What does the business look like if you stay on the same path?"

Follow-up probe: "Has there been a point in the last year where that felt close to happening?"

This is the most important question. Do not rush past it. The more vividly they describe the cost of staying stuck, the more urgency you have to work with. You will reference their exact words later.

---

**Q6 -- Decision-making:**
> "When you've made decisions like this before -- bringing on a service partner or making a significant operational change -- how did that typically work? Who else is involved?"

Follow-up probe: "Is there someone else who would need to be part of this conversation before anything moves forward?"

What to listen for: whether they are the actual buyer, whether there is a gatekeeper, whether the timeline depends on an external event.

---

## Qualification Checkpoint

Before moving to the offer, confirm these silently. If any are missing, keep the conversation but do not push for a close today.

| Criterion | What to Look For |
|-----------|-----------------|
| Right business type | B2B service firm, 5-50 person team |
| Revenue and maturity | Enough traction to afford a retainer, not pre-revenue |
| Real pipeline pain | Named specifically, not just acknowledged abstractly |
| Loss event | Something that happened in the past 12 months that made the pain concrete |
| Actual buyer | Owner or founder, not a coordinator or middle manager |

If all five are present: transition to your offer.
If missing loss event or buyer authority: nurture, do not push.

---

## Transition to Offer (2 minutes)

Only transition after they have described the need in their own words. Use their language, not yours.

> "What you're describing -- [use their exact phrase about the problem] -- is exactly the situation we're built for."

Then give the outcome statement in one sentence. Describe what changes, not what you do.

> "We build the outreach and content infrastructure that creates new client conversations consistently -- without you personally having to run it."

Then: "Would it be useful to walk through how that works?"

Do not pitch the full scope. One sentence on what you do and one question about whether they want to hear more.

---

## Objection Handling

**"I already have someone doing marketing."**

> "Good. What are they producing right now in terms of new conversations? What's the output?"

Wait for the answer. Most of the time the output is thin. Then: "We're not a replacement -- we're the system that gives whoever you have a lot more to work with."

---

**"We're not ready yet / the timing isn't right."**

> "What would need to be in place for the timing to be right?"

Get a specific answer. Not "by Q3" -- "we need to close two contracts first" or "I need to sort out the team structure." Then ask: "When does that typically resolve?"

Do not accept vague timing objections. Push for specificity -- it reveals whether the objection is real or a brush-off.

---

**"We've been burned by agencies before."**

> "What happened? What did they promise and what actually occurred?"

Let them describe it fully. Then: "That's a fair concern. Here's the difference: [describe your model with specifics on control, approval loops, and what they see before anything goes out]. What would make you feel like this is different from that experience?"

---

**"What does this cost?"**

Give a real number immediately. Do not hedge with "it depends" -- that reads as evasion. You can qualify after giving the number.

> "The entry point is [your price]. That covers [brief scope in one sentence]. Most clients that are a fit find that the first result more than justifies it -- but let me ask you a few more things to make sure it actually applies to your situation."

Then continue discovery. Do not close before you have confirmed qualification.

---

## Close and Next Step

Do not end the call without a defined next action.

**If the call went well and they qualify:**

> "Based on what you've described, I think there's a real fit here. The next step would be a proposal -- I can have that to you by [specific date]. Does it make more sense to walk through it together, or would you prefer to review it first and then talk?"

**If they are not ready:**

> "It sounds like [specific barrier] needs to be sorted first. When does that typically change?"

Always get a date. Not "let's stay in touch" -- a specific date. "Let's talk again on [date]" is a real next step. "I'll follow up" is not.

---

## What to Track During the Call

Write these down during the conversation. They go directly into the proposal.

- The exact words they used to describe their problem
- The specific consequence or cost they named
- What they have tried before and why it did not work
- Their timeline or external trigger (if any)
- Who else is involved in the decision
- Their most important priority right now

---

## After the Call (within 24 hours)

- [ ] Log notes in CRM: situation, cost they named, next step, close timeline
- [ ] Send a brief confirmation email: "Good to talk -- as we discussed, I'll send the proposal by [date]."
- [ ] Draft the proposal using their exact language from the call
- [ ] If they raised a specific objection, address it directly in the proposal -- do not leave it for later

---

## What Not to Do

- Do not start with a pitch. The call loses trust immediately.
- Do not skip the cost-of-inaction question (Q5). Urgency is built there, not in the product description.
- Do not accept "I need to think about it" without a date. That is not a next step.
- Do not offer a discount to close. Address the real objection instead.
- Do not run over time without asking. Respect their calendar.
