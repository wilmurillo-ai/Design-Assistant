# Objection Handling Library

These are not scripts to memorize and recite. They are frameworks for finding the real objection under the stated one -- because the stated objection is almost never the real one.

**Rule before responding to any objection:** Pause. Then ask one clarifying question. The first objection is usually a test or a habit ("I need to think about it" is not a real objection -- it is a delay mechanism). The clarifying question surfaces the actual blocker.

---

## How to use this library

1. Identify the objection category (price, timing, incumbent, trust, authority, vague stall)
2. Use the clarifying question first
3. Use the response framework only after the real objection is named
4. Always end with a specific next step -- never let a handled objection die without a clear action

---

## Price Objections

### "That's more than we budgeted."

**Clarifying question:**
"What were you expecting to invest in solving this?"

**Do not:** Lower the price immediately. Doing so signals that your price was arbitrary.

**Response framework:**
1. Acknowledge: "That's fair -- this is a meaningful investment."
2. Reframe to outcome value: "You mentioned [specific cost/problem they named in discovery]. What does that cost you per month if it continues?"
3. Reframe to comparison: "What would it cost to hire someone internally to handle this?" or "What have you already spent trying to solve it?"
4. If they need a lower entry point: offer a scoped version at lower price. Never discount the full offer.

**Script:**
> "I hear you. Can I ask -- when you said you're losing [cost they named], that's [X per year]. This engagement is [price]. What would make it feel like the numbers worked?"

---

### "It's too expensive."

**Clarifying question:**
"Compared to what?"

**Why:** "Too expensive" without a comparison point is a reflex, not a real objection. The question forces them to name what they're comparing to. Then you can address the actual comparison.

**Response frameworks by comparison type:**

| They're comparing to... | Response |
|-----------------------|----------|
| Doing it themselves | "What would it cost in time, at your hourly rate, to get the same result? What would you NOT be doing during that time?" |
| A cheaper competitor | "What's different about their offer? Are they guaranteeing the same outcome?" |
| Doing nothing | "What does this problem cost you every month it continues? The price of this engagement pays for itself in [timeframe] if [outcome]." |
| An arbitrary number | "Where does that number come from?" -- find the real budget constraint |

---

### "We don't have budget right now."

**Clarifying question:**
"When does your budget cycle reset, and what would need to be true for this to be in next cycle's budget?"

**Key distinction:** Is this "no budget exists" or "this is not a priority"? They are different problems.

**If no budget exists:**
> "I understand. When does your next planning cycle happen? I want to make sure you have everything you need to build a case internally. Can I send you a one-pager with the ROI math?"

**If not a priority:**
Address the priority problem, not the budget problem. The real objection is "I don't believe this is important enough." Go back to the cost of inaction.

## Timing Objections

### "Not the right time."

**Clarifying question:**
"What would need to be different for the timing to be right?"

**Their answer is your roadmap.** Set a specific follow-up date tied to the condition they name.

**Response framework:**
1. Don't argue with timing
2. Get the specific condition: "So if [condition] changes, this would move forward?"
3. Confirm the follow-up: "I'll reach back out on [date] -- does that work?"
4. Ask the real question: "Is timing the only thing, or is there something about the fit you're still evaluating?"

---

### "We just started with someone else."

**Clarifying question:**
"What are you hoping they'll accomplish in the first 90 days?"

**Do not:** Counter-pitch against the incumbent directly. You don't know enough about their arrangement.

**Response framework:**
1. Respect the decision: "That makes sense -- you needed to move forward."
2. Find the gap: "Is there still an area where the problem persists that they aren't covering?"
3. Position as complement, not replacement: "A lot of our clients work with us alongside their existing vendors for [specific function]. Would that kind of arrangement be worth a conversation?"
4. Stay in touch: "Would it be helpful if I checked back in 90 days to see how it's going?"

---

### "We're in the middle of a big initiative right now."

**Clarifying question:**
"What's the initiative, and is this problem something that needs to be solved before, after, or during it?"

**Often the real issue:** They're worried adding another thing will overload the team.

**Response:**
> "Totally understand. What if we scoped a smaller first phase that doesn't require much from your team -- so you're not adding to the plate, but the problem is moving?"

---

## Trust Objections

### "We've tried things like this before and it didn't work."

**Clarifying question:**
"What happened? What did you try?"

**This is the most valuable objection.** Their answer tells you exactly what they need to believe to move forward.

**Response framework:**
1. Ask for the full story: "What did the last attempt look like?"
2. Find the specific failure point: "Was it the approach, the execution, or the fit that was wrong?"
3. Name what's different: "Here's specifically how this is different from what you described: [mechanism difference]."
4. Use social proof that matches their situation exactly: "We've worked with [similar company type] who had the same experience. Here's what changed."

---

### "I'm not sure you can deliver for our industry/size/situation."

**Clarifying question:**
"What specifically would you need to see to feel confident?"

**Response framework:**
1. Don't argue -- ask what proof would satisfy them
2. Match proof to their specific concern: use a case study that matches their company type, size, and problem as closely as possible
3. If no exact match exists: offer a discovery phase or pilot to prove it works in their context
4. Use the guarantee: "If we don't hit [metric] in [timeframe], you don't pay for the next phase."

**Script:**
> "Fair question. What's the closest thing to your situation that you'd need to see a result for? I want to show you something that actually maps to you, not just our best case study."

---

### "How do I know this will work?"

**Clarifying question:**
"What would 'working' look like for you specifically?"

**This defines success criteria.** Get them to name it. Then either confirm you can hit it or scope the engagement to the thing you can guarantee.

**Response:**
> "I want to answer that specifically, not generally. If I could show you [result] for [similar company] in [timeframe], would that be enough proof? Because I have exactly that."

---

## Authority Objections

### "I need to run this by my partner / CFO / board."

**Clarifying question:**
"What are they going to ask about? I want to make sure you have everything you need to walk them through it."

**Never:** "Can I join that call?" as the first response. It signals distrust. Offer to create the tool first.

**Response framework:**
1. Offer to build the internal sell for them: "I can put together a one-pager with the numbers -- the ROI case, the risk reversal, and the implementation timeline."
2. Identify the decision-maker's specific concern: "Does your partner usually focus on the financial side, or more on the risk / implementation side?"
3. Send materials designed for the actual decision-maker, not for your contact
4. Confirm a decision date: "When do you expect to have their input? Should we schedule a call for [date] to talk after that?"

---

### "My partner / CFO / board said no."

**Clarifying question:**
"What was their specific concern?"

**Don't accept vague feedback.** "They said no" is not information. The specific concern is.

**Response:**
> "I understand. Can I ask what the specific concern was? Sometimes there's a misunderstanding I can address directly, and sometimes there isn't -- but I'd rather know which one this is."

---

## Stall Objections (The Most Common)

### "I need to think about it."

**Clarifying question:**
"Of course -- what specifically do you need to think through?"

**Never accept this without clarification.** "I need to think about it" is a stall, not a decision. The clarifying question forces the real objection to surface.

**Common real objections behind this stall:**
- "I'm not sure it will work for me" -- trust objection
- "I can't justify the price internally" -- authority/budget objection
- "I'm comparing you to someone else" -- competitive objection
- "I don't feel urgency right now" -- timing objection

Once the real objection surfaces, use the appropriate section above.

---

### "Send me some information."

**Clarifying question:**
"What specifically would be most useful -- is it the ROI case, how the process works, or examples of results for similar companies?"

**"Send me some information" is a polite exit.** A PDF sent cold to an inbox is rarely read.

**Response framework:**
1. Get specific about what they need: the question above narrows it
2. Send a short, targeted document -- not a deck
3. Set a specific follow-up call: "I'll send that today. Can we schedule 15 minutes on Thursday to walk through it together? That's usually faster than email back and forth."

If they won't commit to a follow-up call, the deal is likely dead. Move it to the long-term nurture sequence.

---

### "Just check back with me in [timeframe]."

**Clarifying question:**
"What do you expect to be different then?"

**Their answer tells you if there's a real opening or not.**

If there's a real condition: "So if [X] happens by [date], this moves forward?" Confirm the follow-up is tied to the condition, not just a date.

If there's no real condition: this is a soft no. Move to long-term nurture. Don't waste prime follow-up bandwidth on a dead deal.

---

## After the Objection is Handled

**Always end with a specific next step.**

Never: "Does that make sense?" (invites vague agreement)
Always: "So here's what I'd suggest for next steps: [specific action with date]. Does that work?"

If they handled the objection but still won't commit to a next step, ask directly:
> "Is there something about this that still doesn't feel right? I'd rather know now than follow up for weeks on something that isn't going to move."

**BAMFAM:** Book A Meeting From A Meeting. Every handled objection should end with a next call on the calendar, a document being sent with a follow-up call scheduled, or a decision date confirmed. No vague "I'll follow up next week."

## Objection Triage

If you're consistently hearing the same objection from multiple prospects, the objection is a signal, not a coincidence.

| Pattern | What it means |
|---------|--------------|
| Price objections on every call | Offer not positioned against outcome value. Fix F2. |
| "We tried this before" on every call | Mechanism not differentiated. Fix the offer story. |
| "Not the right time" repeatedly | No urgency trigger in outreach. Add trigger-based targeting. |
| "Need to talk to my partner" every time | Not reaching the decision-maker. Fix ICP targeting or add decision-maker qualification to discovery. |
| "Send me info" without follow-up | Discovery call not building enough urgency. Review discovery questions. |
