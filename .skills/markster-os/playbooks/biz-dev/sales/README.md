# Sales Playbook

A structured pipeline from first conversation to signed contract.

---

## Prerequisites

- [ ] ICP documented with decision-maker profile and disqualification criteria
- [ ] Outcome statement defined: what the buyer gets, in their language
- [ ] Pricing defined with at least two tiers and a risk reversal
- [ ] Message documented: the problem statement you lead with
- [ ] Proposal template ready to customize (see templates/)

---

## What this produces

- A discovery call framework (questions, structure, qualification signals)
- A proposal structure that sells outcomes, not deliverable lists
- A close framework (how to handle delays, objections, and committee approvals)
- A pipeline tracking template with stage definitions and next-action discipline

---

## Steps

### Step 1: Pre-Call Preparation

Before any discovery call, research the prospect. Arriving prepared is not optional -- it is a competitive signal.

**Company research (15 minutes):**
- What do they sell, who do they sell to?
- What does their current marketing and sales presence look like? (Website, LinkedIn, content)
- What are the obvious gaps in their GTM based on what you can see publicly?
- What is their approximate revenue and team size?

**Person research (10 minutes):**
- Their background and tenure in this role
- What they have written or posted publicly
- What they have done before this role (context for their experience level)

**Know your qualifying threshold:**
- What must be true about their situation for you to want this client?
- What would disqualify them immediately?

Arrive knowing their situation. Do not use discovery time to learn things you could have found in 10 minutes of research.

---

### Step 2: Discovery Call (45 minutes)

Discovery is not a pitch. If you are selling during discovery, you are doing it wrong. The goal is to understand whether and how you can help -- and to surface the cost of the problem continuing.

**Minutes 0-5: Context**
- Why did they agree to the call? What made them respond?
- What do they already know about you? (Calibrates how much context to provide)

**Minutes 5-25: Situation questions**

These questions surface the problem, its history, and its cost:
- "Walk me through what your current [process / pipeline / approach] looks like day to day."
- "What is working well about how you do it now?"
- "What is not working as well as you would like?"
- "How long has this been a challenge?"
- "What have you tried before to fix it?"
- "What happened with that?"
- "If nothing changes in the next 12 months, what does that look like for you?"

The last question is the most important. It surfaces the cost of inaction.

**Minutes 25-35: Implication questions**

These deepen the prospect's awareness of the real cost:
- "When [problem] happens, how does that affect [their team / their clients / their revenue]?"
- "What does it cost you in time or money per month?"
- "How is this showing up in your client relationships or business development?"

**Minutes 35-40: Your solution (brief)**

Only after you fully understand their situation, briefly describe how you help. Frame it in terms of the situation they just described, not a general pitch.

"Based on what you have described, here is what we typically do for [type of business] in your situation: [outcome statement]. Does that align with what you are trying to solve?"

**Minutes 40-45: Next steps**

- "Does what I described make sense for your situation?"
- If yes: "The next step is typically a proposal. I can have that to you by [specific day]. Does that work?"
- If uncertain: "What would make you more confident that this is the right direction?"
- Always leave with a specific date and action. Vague next steps become lost deals.

**Qualifying signals during discovery:**

Green lights (they are a buyer):
- They have a specific timeline or deadline
- They have tried to solve this before (budget has been allocated in the past)
- They mention a specific cost or consequence of the problem continuing
- They ask about pricing without being prompted

Yellow lights (might be a buyer, needs more):
- Vague on timeline: "sometime this year"
- Has not tried to solve it before (problem may not be painful enough)
- The actual decision-maker is not on the call

Red lights (not a buyer right now):
- Cannot articulate why now
- Multiple internal approvals needed with no clear champion
- Budget is not approved and no path to approval

---

### Step 3: Proposal

A proposal is not a scope document. It is a sales document. Send it within 24-48 hours of the discovery call. Longer than that and momentum fades.

**Proposal structure:**

1. **Situation summary** -- demonstrate that you understood their specific situation from discovery. This is not boilerplate. It references what they told you on the call.

2. **The outcome** -- what they will have or achieve as a result of working with you. Outcome, not a list of deliverables.

3. **The approach** -- how you get there. Brief. Enough to give confidence without becoming a free consultation document.

4. **Timeline** -- when they see the first result, when the full outcome is delivered.

5. **Investment** -- the price, presented confidently and tied to the outcome value.

6. **Risk reversal** -- what reduces their risk. A guarantee, a phased commitment, or a diagnostic before full engagement.

7. **Next steps** -- one clear action to say yes.

**Proposal sending rules:**
- Walk them through the proposal on a call rather than just sending a document. Proposals sent without a walkthrough close at a much lower rate.
- Ask for a decision timeline before ending the proposal review call: "When do you expect to make a decision?"
- Follow up within 3 days if you have not heard back.

### Step 4: Close

**When they need to "think about it":**

"Thinking about it" almost always means one of three things: they are not convinced the outcome is real, they need to get internal approval, or the price feels high relative to their confidence in the result.

Ask: "What would make you more confident this is the right move?" Then address what they say directly.

**When they need internal approval:**

Make it easy for them to sell internally. Offer to write a one-pager they can share with their decision-maker. Take the work off their plate. If you let the champion sell without your help, the deal gets diluted.

**When they stall after saying yes:**

Define a deadline. "I can hold this pricing and timeline until [specific date]. After that, I will need to reassess availability." This is not a pressure tactic -- it is an honest constraint.

**When they say no:**

Ask why. Not to argue -- to learn. "Totally understand. Can you tell me what was missing or what made it not the right fit?" Their answer improves your next pitch and sometimes reopens the conversation later.

**Downsell chain:**

When a prospect cannot commit to the full engagement, offer a lower-commitment entry point rather than losing the deal entirely:
- Free diagnostic (primary CTA in outbound, useful for inbound as well)
- Low-cost deliverable tier (materials they execute themselves)
- Done-for-you mini-project (a working system delivered in 1-2 weeks)
- Lite retainer (partial engagement for those not ready to go full)

The effort gradient must be clear: low tier means they do the work, high tier means you do everything.

---

### Step 5: Pipeline Tracking

Track every active opportunity with:
- Company and contact name
- Deal size (estimated)
- Stage: Discovery / Proposal Sent / Proposal Reviewed / Contract Sent / Closed Won / Closed Lost
- Next action and date
- Notes from last conversation

**Review pipeline weekly.** Every deal without a next action date will stall.

Stale deals (no activity for 14+ days) get a follow-up or get closed out. A deal without momentum is not in your pipeline -- it is in your wishful thinking.

## Benchmarks

| Metric | Target |
|--------|--------|
| Discovery-to-proposal rate | 60%+ (if under 60%, qualification process needs work) |
| Proposal-to-close rate | 30%+ (if under 30%, proposal or close process needs work) |
| Days from discovery to proposal sent | Under 48 hours |
| Days from proposal to decision | Under 14 days (set this expectation on the proposal call) |
| Pipeline review cadence | Weekly |
| Deal age before follow-up | 3 days after proposal, 7 days if going quiet |

## Common Mistakes

**Pitching during discovery.** If you describe your solution before fully understanding their situation, you lose the right to customize. Buyers feel pitched, not understood.

**Sending a proposal without a walkthrough call.** Proposals sent over email and not walked through close at a fraction of the rate. Schedule the walkthrough before you send.

**Waiting too long to send the proposal.** Momentum is real. Send within 48 hours. Every day you wait, the urgency they felt on the call fades.

**Leaving discovery without a specific next step.** "I'll follow up next week" is not a next step. "I'll have the proposal to you by Thursday, and we can review it on a call Friday -- does 2pm work?" is a next step.

**Not asking why on a lost deal.** Lost deals are free sales coaching. Ask what was missing. Occasionally this also reopens the conversation.

**Treating "think about it" as a final answer.** It rarely is. Ask what would make them more confident, address it directly, and you will close a meaningful percentage of those deals.

**No downsell path.** When the full engagement is too big for where they are now, have a smaller entry point ready. Not every no is a no forever -- some are a "not at this price or scope right now."
