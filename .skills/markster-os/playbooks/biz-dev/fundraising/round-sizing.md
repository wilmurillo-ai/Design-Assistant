# Round Sizing and Instruments

What each round buys, how much to raise, and which legal instrument to use.

---

## The four VC rounds

### Pre-seed

What it is: the first check from outside your personal network. Usually angels, micro-VCs, or accelerators.

| Element | Typical range |
|---------|--------------|
| Raise amount | $250K-$2M |
| Instrument | SAFE (see below) |
| Valuation | Cap set on SAFE: $5M-$15M (varies by market) |
| What you need to show | Team, market, early signal (paying pilots, LOIs, or compelling traction) |
| What it buys | 12-18 months of runway to reach seed milestones |

Seed milestones (what pre-seed capital needs to produce):
- B2B SaaS: $200K-$1M ARR, clear ICP, repeatable close
- Marketplace: GMV traction, liquidity proof in one geography
- Consumer: LTV:CAC above 3x, D30 retention data
- DevTools: strong developer adoption, early enterprise pilots

---

### Seed

What it is: the first institutional round. Seed funds or early-stage generalist VCs.

| Element | Typical range |
|---------|--------------|
| Raise amount | $2M-$5M (some up to $8M in hot markets) |
| Instrument | SAFE or priced round (see below) |
| Valuation | $8M-$20M post-money |
| What you need to show | PMF evidence, early revenue, repeatable motion |
| What it buys | 18-24 months to reach Series A milestones |

Series A milestones (what seed capital needs to produce):
- B2B SaaS: $1M-$4M ARR, 100%+ YoY growth, NRR above 100%
- Marketplace: $500K+ GMV/month, proven liquidity, retention on both sides
- Consumer: $1M+ ARR, documented LTV:CAC at scale

---

### Series A

What it is: the first major institutional round. Dedicated Series A funds or multi-stage VCs.

| Element | Typical range |
|---------|--------------|
| Raise amount | $5M-$20M |
| Instrument | Priced round (always) |
| Valuation | $20M-$80M post-money |
| What you need to show | Repeatable growth, clear go-to-market, unit economics that work |
| What it buys | 18-24 months of aggressive growth to Series B |

Series B milestones (current bar, as of 2026):
- $4M-$8M ARR (bar raised from pre-2022 levels)
- NRR above 110%
- 2-3x YoY growth
- Multiple quarters of consistent execution

---

### Series B

What it is: scaling round for companies with proven models. Multi-stage VCs or growth-stage investors.

| Element | Typical range |
|---------|--------------|
| Raise amount | $15M-$75M |
| Instrument | Priced round |
| Valuation | $80M-$300M post-money |
| What you need to show | $4-8M ARR, NRR 110%+, clear path to $50M+ ARR |
| What it buys | Hire leadership team, expand internationally, accelerate product |

## SAFE vs. convertible note vs. priced round

### SAFE (Simple Agreement for Future Equity)

The YC standard SAFE is the dominant pre-seed/seed instrument.

How it works: you receive cash now. The investor receives equity later, when a priced round occurs. No valuation is set at signing -- only a cap and/or discount.

Key terms:
- **Cap**: the maximum valuation at which the SAFE converts. A $10M cap means the investor's SAFE converts as if the company was valued at $10M, even if the priced round is at $30M.
- **Discount**: typically 10-20%. If the priced round is at $1/share, the SAFE converts at $0.80/share (20% discount).
- **Pro-rata rights**: the right to invest in future rounds to maintain ownership percentage.

Why founders prefer SAFEs over convertible notes:
- No interest (convertible notes have 6-8% annual interest)
- No maturity date (convertible notes mature in 12-24 months, creating pressure)
- Not debt (SAFEs do not appear as liabilities on the balance sheet)
- Simpler documentation ($500-$2K in legal fees vs. $5K-$15K for convertible notes)

When SAFEs are appropriate: pre-seed and seed rounds under $3M-$5M.

---

### Convertible Note

Debt that converts to equity at the next priced round.

Key terms: principal amount, interest rate (6-8% typical), maturity date (12-24 months), conversion discount (15-25%), valuation cap.

When convertible notes are used: some investors (especially angels and family offices) prefer debt instruments for legal or tax reasons. Less common than SAFEs for early-stage tech startups but still used.

The maturity date is the risk: if you have not raised a priced round before the note matures, the noteholder can demand repayment. Negotiate extension rights before signing.

---

### Priced Round

Equity issued at a set price per share, reflecting an agreed valuation.

Required documents: term sheet, certificate of incorporation amendment, stock purchase agreement, investor rights agreement, right of first refusal agreement, voting agreement.

Legal cost: $15K-$50K depending on complexity.

What investors receive: preferred stock (not common stock). Preferred stock has rights that common stock does not: liquidation preference (they get paid before founders and employees in an acquisition), anti-dilution protection, and sometimes board seats.

When priced rounds are used: Series A and beyond. Sometimes at seed when the round is large enough ($3M+) to justify the legal overhead.

## How much to raise

Rule: raise enough to reach the next clear milestone with 6 months of buffer.

The milestone is the thing that gets you the next round at a better valuation. Raise exactly enough to hit it. Do not raise more -- dilution is permanent, and excess capital creates bloat.

Example:
- Current state: $100K ARR, unproven sales motion
- Next milestone: $1M ARR, repeatable close process
- Monthly burn to get there: $80K/month
- Months needed: 12-15
- Raise: $1.2M-$1.5M (12-15 months of burn + 3 months buffer)

The buffer is mandatory. Fundraising takes 3-6 months. If you run out of runway before closing the next round, you negotiate from weakness.

## Dilution math

Every round dilutes existing shareholders. Track your ownership on a fully diluted basis (including all options, warrants, and convertible instruments).

Typical dilution by round:
- Pre-seed: 5-15% of the company
- Seed: 15-25%
- Series A: 20-25%
- Series B: 15-20%

Cumulative: a founder who raises pre-seed, seed, and Series A may own 40-55% by Series A close, before accounting for employee option pool. The option pool is usually created before each round (further diluting founders), typically 10-15% per round.

Model your cap table before agreeing to any terms. The number that matters at exit is your ownership percentage times the exit value, minus any liquidation preferences.
