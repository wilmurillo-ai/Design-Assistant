# DTC, Consumer Apps, and Consumer Social

Covers: direct-to-consumer physical products, e-commerce brands, consumer mobile apps, B2C subscription products, consumer social platforms.

What unifies these: the buyer is an individual (not a company), there is no sales call, conversion happens in a funnel, and retention determines whether the unit economics work.

---

## What is different about GTM

There is no G3 Book in the traditional sense. Nobody is scheduling a demo to buy a $49/month app or a skincare product. The "sales" function is entirely replaced by:
- Paid social (Meta, TikTok) for acquisition
- The product landing page as the close
- Email/SMS sequences as the follow-up

The GOD Engine still applies but G3 changes completely. G3 here is: ad creative + landing page conversion rate + checkout flow. The metric is not meetings booked, it is cost per purchase (CPP) and ROAS.

The unit economics are brutal if you get them wrong. Paid social CPMs are high. Margins on physical products are thin. If D30 retention is low or LTV is below 3x CAC, you are paying to acquire customers at a loss.

## GOD Engine priorities by stage

| Stage | Priority bricks | What to skip |
|-------|----------------|--------------|
| Stage 0-1 | G1 Find (audience definition), G3 (ad creative + landing page), D1 Deliver (first orders/installs) | G2 organic content -- paid is faster to learn |
| Stage 2 ($100K-500K ARR) | D2 Prove (reviews, UGC, social proof), D3 Expand (repurchase, subscription), O1 Standardize (fulfillment) | -- |
| Stage 3 ($500K-2M ARR) | G2 Warm (influencer, organic content at scale), O3 Instrument (cohort LTV tracking), O2 Automate | -- |
| Stage 4 | Retail channel, international, brand partnerships | -- |

## Key metrics

| Metric | What it measures | Healthy benchmark |
|--------|-----------------|-------------------|
| ROAS (Return on Ad Spend) | Revenue per $1 of ad spend | 3x+ to be viable; 5x+ to be healthy |
| CAC (paid) | Cost to acquire one customer via ads | Must be below LTV/3 |
| D30 retention | % of customers still active at 30 days (apps) or repurchased (DTC) | Apps: 20%+. DTC: 30%+ repurchase at Day 90 |
| Cohort LTV | Revenue per cohort over 6-12 months | Must exceed CAC by 3x+ |
| AOV (Average Order Value) | Revenue per transaction | Higher AOV = better LTV:CAC math |
| Subscription attach rate | % of customers on recurring | Subscriptions dramatically improve LTV |

## Biggest failure modes

1. **Buying growth before knowing LTV.** Running ads before you have real cohort data on D30/D60/D90 retention means you do not know if you are profitable. Wait until you have 90 days of retention data before scaling paid.

2. **Thin margins meeting high CAC.** Physical products with 30-40% gross margins cannot survive $50+ CAC on Meta. Either raise prices, reduce COGS, or find a lower-CAC acquisition channel.

3. **No subscription or repurchase mechanism.** A business that sells once and never sees the customer again has no LTV. Build the repurchase sequence (email, SMS, subscriptions) before scaling acquisition.

4. **Ignoring ad creative quality.** In consumer, creative is the targeting. The best targeting with bad creative fails. Test 10 creative variants before calling the channel broken.

## Fundraising path

VC is possible for consumer if network effects or platform dynamics exist. Most DTC/consumer businesses are better suited for:
- Revenue-based financing once revenue is predictable ($100K+ ARR)
- SBA loans or business lines of credit for physical product inventory
- Inventory financing against purchase orders

Consumer social with genuine virality (organic growth coefficient above 1) is VC-fundable. DTC without a clear brand moat or network effect is typically bootstrapped or debt-financed.
