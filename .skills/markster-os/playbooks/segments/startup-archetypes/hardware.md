# Hardware + Software

Covers: connected hardware with recurring software, IoT devices, physical products with a software layer, smart devices.

What unifies these: a physical product that requires manufacturing and logistics before it can be sold, plus a recurring software component that creates the long-term business model.

---

## What is different about GTM

The supply chain is a GTM constraint. You cannot run G3 Book at scale until you have inventory to fulfill. This means the pre-launch phase looks different from pure software: crowdfunding and pre-orders are a legitimate G2 + cash flow strategy, not just a marketing tactic.

The economics split into two layers:
- Hardware: sold once (or low frequency), often at thin margin or near cost
- Software: recurring subscription, high margin, where the real LTV is

The classic trap is pricing the hardware for margin and killing adoption. The better model is pricing hardware to reduce barrier to entry, monetizing on the software subscription.

## Key metrics

| Metric | What it measures | Healthy benchmark |
|--------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Units shipped | Physical scale | Stage 1: 100-500 units to validate |
| Subscription attach rate | % of hardware buyers converting to software | 40%+ is the target |
| Hardware margin | Revenue minus COGS per unit | Neutral to low is acceptable if software LTV is strong |
| Software MRR | Recurring revenue from subscriptions | This is the real business metric |
| NRR on software | Net revenue retention from software subscribers | 100%+ |
| Return/defect rate | Hardware quality signal | Under 5% to stay operationally sane |

## Biggest failure modes

1. **Underestimating manufacturing lead times.** First-time hardware founders routinely underestimate how long supply chain takes. 6-12 months from design to first batch is normal. Plan fundraising and cash accordingly.

2. **Pricing hardware for margin.** High hardware ASP (average selling price) shrinks the addressable market. Consider subsidizing hardware and capturing value on software.

3. **Skipping crowdfunding.** Crowdfunding validates demand, funds the first manufacturing run, and builds an early user community simultaneously. Most hardware companies that skip it run out of cash before their first order arrives.

4. **No software moat.** Hardware that works fine without the subscription gets abandoned. The software must create genuine ongoing value (data, features, integrations) or customers cancel.

## Fundraising path

Crowdfunding (Kickstarter/Indiegogo) is the standard pre-revenue path: $50K-$2M, validates demand, funds first manufacturing run, no equity.

Pre-seed/seed VC: $1M-$5M. Needs: working prototype, manufacturing partner identified, pre-order traction.
Series A: $5M-$20M. Needs: units shipped, software subscription proven, NRR above 100%.

Note: hardware VC is a smaller universe than software VC. Know which firms invest in hardware before building your list.
