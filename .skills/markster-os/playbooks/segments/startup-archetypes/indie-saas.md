# Indie SaaS and Productized Services

Covers: solo-founder and small-team SaaS products (bootstrapped), productized service packages (fixed scope, fixed price), micro-SaaS tools.

What unifies these: no VC, no sales team, the founder is the product and the brand, and the goal is building a profitable, low-overhead business -- not a venture-scale company.

---

## What is different about GTM

Volume is not the lever. A $50K/yr indie SaaS business with 200 customers at $20/month is a good outcome. The constraints and the strategy are different from VC-backed growth.

G3 Book looks nothing like B2B SaaS cold outreach. There is no SDR, no quota, no sequence tool. Instead:
- Inbound from content (SEO, newsletters, Twitter/X, niche communities)
- Word of mouth from a tight niche
- App stores and directories (ProductHunt, AppSumo, G2, specific vertical directories)

The target is: find a niche specific enough that you can be the obvious best option, and narrow enough that a solo founder can serve it without hiring.

Productized services apply the same logic: one service, fixed scope, fixed price, delivered the same way every time. Removes sales calls, removes custom scoping, removes pricing negotiation.

## GOD Engine priorities by stage

| Stage | Priority bricks | What to skip |
|-------|----------------|--------------|
| Stage 0-1 | G1 Find (hyper-specific niche), G2 Warm (SEO content + community), D1 Deliver (make first customers wildly successful) | Cold outreach -- wrong motion. Paid ads -- CAC too high relative to ACV. |
| Stage 2 ($100K-500K ARR) | D2 Prove (case studies, reviews, G2 ratings), D3 Expand (upsell tiers), O1 Standardize | G1 expansion -- stay narrow until Stage 3 |
| Stage 3 ($500K-2M ARR) | O2 Automate, G2 at scale, D3 Expand | -- |

Note: the fastest path from $0 to $10K MRR for an indie SaaS is a targeted niche with a specific painful problem + inbound content + app store presence. Not cold email.

## Key metrics

| Metric | What it measures | Healthy benchmark |
|--------|-----------------|-------------------|
| MRR | Monthly recurring revenue | $10K MRR is Stage 1 graduation |
| Monthly churn rate | % of customers canceling | Under 2% monthly |
| Organic traffic | Inbound content performance | Primary G2 signal |
| Trial-to-paid rate | Self-serve funnel | 5-15% for well-positioned product |
| NRR | Retention + expansion | 90%+ for a healthy indie SaaS |
| Profit margin | This is the real goal for bootstrap | 60-80% at Stage 2+ |

## Biggest failure modes

1. **Targeting a market that is too broad.** "SaaS for small businesses" is not a niche. "Booking software for mobile dog groomers" is. Broad targeting means you compete against well-funded competitors with massive content and sales teams.

2. **Building before validating.** Spend 2-4 weeks talking to 20 people in the niche before writing a line of code. Do they have the problem? Are they paying for something else to solve it? What words do they use?

3. **Not charging enough.** Indie SaaS products are routinely priced at $9-19/month when the right price is $49-99/month. Low price attracts high churn, low engagement, and the wrong customers.

4. **Ignoring retention.** With a small customer base, a 5% monthly churn rate means you are back to zero in 20 months. Every churned customer at this scale is a meaningful loss.

## Fundraising path

There is no VC path for most indie SaaS -- and that is the point. The goal is profitability, not scale.

Revenue-based financing: accessible at $100K ARR for working capital if needed.
Business line of credit: once you have 2 years of history and profitability.

Most indie SaaS founders should not raise external capital. The constraint is not money -- it is product and distribution.
