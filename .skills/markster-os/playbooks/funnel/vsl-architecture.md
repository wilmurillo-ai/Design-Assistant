# VSL Architecture

A VSL is not a pitch. It is a controlled belief environment. The viewer arrives at the purchase decision through belief transformation, not persuasion pressure.

---

## The Three Frameworks

| Framework | Best for | Mechanism |
|-----------|----------|-----------|
| Brunson - Three Secrets | Affiliate-ready VSLs, $297-$2,000, cold traffic at scale | Belief demolition + value stacking |
| Kern - State, Story, Solution | B2B service positioning, relationship-based sales | Content value as conversion mechanism |
| Kennedy - Results in Advance | High-authority positioning, pre-call warm-up | Problem vacuum creation |

Default for affiliate VSLs: Brunson. Default for B2B service VSLs: Brunson with Kern story structure in the story beat.

---

## The 60-Second Cliff

VSLs lose 40-60% of viewers before minute one.

A stronger hook in the first 15 seconds produced 3x revenue in split tests with identical scripts. The hook is the highest-leverage single optimization in any VSL.

**Conversion rate reality:**
- 3-8% conversion applies to engaged viewers who stayed
- Total traffic to purchase: 1-5% at best
- Always model on the conservative number

## Section-by-Section Structure

### Opening (90 seconds / 150-200 words)

The first sentence has one job: stop the scroll.

Options:
- Pattern interrupt: something unexpected that snaps attention
- Audience qualifier: "If you're a [specific person] doing [specific thing] and hitting [specific wall]..."
- Counter-intuitive claim: a statement that contradicts what they believe

Rules:
- No greetings ("Hi, my name is...")
- No company intro ("We are a marketing firm that...")
- No price in the first 90 seconds

### Problem Agitation (2-3 min / 300-400 words)

Name the problem in the buyer's exact words, not yours. Agitate three dimensions:
1. Financial cost of the problem
2. Emotional cost of the problem
3. Opportunity cost of the problem

Do not present your solution yet. Let the problem breathe.

### Story Arc (3-5 min / 500-700 words)

Six beats in order:

| Beat | What it does |
|------|-------------|
| Struggle | The specific pain before the discovery |
| Failed vehicles | What you tried that did not work (each one is a belief you demolish in the Secrets) |
| Catalyst | The moment that forced a different approach |
| Epiphany | The insight that became the mechanism |
| System | How you turned the insight into a repeatable process |
| Transformation | Specific, measurable result with a real number |

For B2B: end the story on metrics, not emotions.

### Three Secrets - Belief Demolition (2-3 min each / 300-450 words each)

Exactly three. No more (tips into objection-handling territory, reads as defensive). No fewer.

| Secret | Old belief to demolish | Example |
|--------|----------------------|---------|
| Secret 1 - Vehicle | Why the category they tried before failed | "Cold email doesn't work" |
| Secret 2 - Internal | Why they think they personally cannot do this | "I'm not a salesperson" |
| Secret 3 - External | Why they think outside forces prevent it | "My market is too competitive" |

Structure for each secret:
1. Name the old belief
2. Validate why it formed (show you understand their experience)
3. Introduce the new belief
4. Anchor with proof

**The Big Domino:** Identify which single belief, once demolished, collapses all other objections. Lead with that one as Secret 1.

### Repeatability Proof (1-2 min / 200-300 words)

Not just your result. Proof the method works for others.

Each case study needs: company type + specific number + specific timeframe.

At least one case study must mirror your target buyer exactly. If you serve 10-person agencies, your best proof is a 10-person agency result.

### Stack Presentation (2-3 min / 300-400 words)

List everything included. Build perceived value top to bottom before revealing price.

For B2B: use ROI anchors, not retail anchors.
- ROI anchor: "You'd spend $8,000/month on a marketing hire to get this one result alone."
- Retail anchor: "This module retails for $997." (weak for B2B buyers)

Never stack more than 5-6 components. More than that erodes credibility.

### Close - Three Passes in Sequence (2-3 min / 350-500 words)

1. Price reveal with anchoring stepdown (start at a high anchor, step down to real price)
2. Decision reframe (inaction is riskier than action - quantify the cost of not buying)
3. Guarantee immediately before CTA

The guarantee belongs at the end, not the beginning. Placing it earlier signals defensiveness.

## Word Count and Timing

| Section | Words | Time |
|---------|-------|------|
| Opening hook | 150-200 | 1-1.5 min |
| Problem agitation | 300-400 | 2-3 min |
| Story arc | 500-700 | 3-5 min |
| Secret 1 | 300-450 | 2-3 min |
| Secret 2 | 300-450 | 2-3 min |
| Secret 3 | 300-450 | 2-3 min |
| Repeatability proof | 200-300 | 1-2 min |
| Stack presentation | 300-400 | 2-3 min |
| Close | 350-500 | 2-3 min |
| **Total core** | **2,400-4,050** | **17-28 min** |

To reach 30 minutes: add 2-3 client case study blocks (200-300 words each) between the Secrets and the Stack.

---

## Technical Rules

- Never host on YouTube on a conversion page. Use Wistia or Vimeo. YouTube embeds kill conversion.
- Place pattern interrupts at dropout risk points (after each Secret, after the story).
- False close ("if this is not right for you, close the page") screens for committed buyers and raises close rate on those who stay.
- OTO1 fires immediately post-purchase. Opens with identity reinforcement, not a product pitch. The buyer is now a buyer - treat them as one.
